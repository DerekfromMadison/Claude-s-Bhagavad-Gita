# Chapter 14 — Source Notes

*Gunatraya Vibhaga Yoga* — The Yoga of the Three Modes of Nature.

This file logs every quote and citation pulled for Chapter 14, with
confidence flags. Used during Phase 5 self-validation and any later
human validation pass.

---

## Phase 1 — Plan

### Interpretive viewpoints to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara on the gunas as belonging to prakriti,
   not to the witnessing self.
2. **Vishishtadvaita** — Ramanuja on the gunas as real modifications of
   real prakriti, owned by real selves.
3. **Gaudiya / ISKCON Vaishnava** — Prabhupada on the gunas as
   transcendable only through devotion to the personal Lord.
4. **Activist / Karma-yoga** — Tilak / Gandhi on the gunas as a frame
   for moral action, not metaphysical typology.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo on the gunas as the
   psychological economy through which prakriti acts.
6. **Comparative / academic** — Edgerton, Zaehner, Johnson, Patton on
   the philological and historical character of the doctrine.
7. **Literary-universalist** — Mascaró, Miller on the rendering of the
   gunas in modern literary English.

(Seven engaged. Floor: 3. Target: 5–7. Met.)

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

1. **Virtue ethics on character** — Alasdair MacIntyre (*After
   Virtue*) on virtue as practice-formed disposition, set against
   guna-as-temperament.
2. **Modern personality frameworks** — the Big Five (Costa &
   McCrae); brief, with caveats. Used carefully — not claiming
   guna ↔ Big Five mapping.
3. **Buddhist counterpoint** — Bhikkhu Bodhi on the *kilesas* /
   defilements as psychologically descriptive, structurally
   parallel to tamas-rajas-sattva.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

1. **Behavioral genetics on temperament** — Robert Plomin's work on
   heritability of temperamental traits; Mary Rothbart on infant
   temperament dimensions.
2. **Circadian / metabolic effects on cognition** — work by Russell
   Foster and others on chronobiology; Walker on sleep deprivation
   and cognition; metabolic state and decision-making (e.g. ego
   depletion debate, glucose research — to be bounded carefully).

### Anchor verses

- **14.5** — three gunas born of prakriti bind the embodied.
- **14.6–14.8** — characterizations of sattva, rajas, tamas.
- **14.9** — sattva binds to happiness, rajas to action, tamas to
  negligence.
- **14.11–14.13** — signs of dominance of each guna.
- **14.17** — what arises from each: knowledge from sattva, greed
  from rajas, error/delusion from tamas.
- **14.18** — upward / middle / downward trajectories.
- **14.22–14.25** — the marks of the one who has gone beyond the
  gunas (*gunatita*).

For each anchor verse: pull at least three translations.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### A. Anchor verse: 14.5

> Sattva, rajas, tamas — these gunas, O mighty-armed, born of
> prakriti, bind in the body the embodied one, the immutable.

**Translations pulled:**

- **Easwaran (1985/2007)**: *"It is the three gunas born of prakriti
  — sattva, rajas, and tamas — that bind the immortal Self to the
  body."* (Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 14.5, ch. 14, p. ~221 in
  Nilgiri Press 2007 ed.) — confidence: confirmed substance,
  exact wording verified against the Easwaran Penguin/Nilgiri
  edition; **flag: page locator approximate (edition unconfirmed;
  validation pending)**.
- **Mascaró (Penguin 1962)**: *"SATTVA, Rajas and Tamas are the three
  constituents of nature. They appear in the soul of man and they
  bind him to his perishable body."* (Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*,
  14.5, Penguin Classics ed.) — confidence: substance confirmed;
  Mascaró renders gunas as "constituents." **Wording paraphrase-
  level, not verified word-for-word; treat as paraphrase unless
  validated.**
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Lucidity, passion, and dark inertia are the
  qualities that nature creates; they bind the immutable self to
  the body."* (Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War*, 14.5, Bantam ed. 1986, p. ~120) — confidence:
  Miller's signature triplet "lucidity / passion / dark inertia"
  for sattva/rajas/tamas is well attested in her translation;
  **wording approximate; flag for validation**.
- **Prabhupada (*Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1972/1983)**: *"Material
  nature consists of three modes — goodness, passion and ignorance.
  When the eternal living entity comes in contact with nature, O
  mighty-armed Arjuna, he becomes conditioned by these modes."*
  (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 14.5, Bhaktivedanta Book
  Trust) — confidence: confirmed substance, Prabhupada uses
  "goodness, passion, ignorance" consistently. Wording very
  close to the published edition; flag minor for word-by-word
  verification.
- **Sargeant (interlinear)**: roughly: *"Sattva, rajas, tamas — these
  gunas born of prakriti, O mighty-armed one, bind in the body the
  imperishable embodied one."* — confidence: substance confirmed;
  Sargeant's word-by-word edition is the philological check.
  **Treat as paraphrase unless directly checked.**

### B. Anchor verse: 14.6 (sattva)

> Of these, sattva, being stainless, is luminous and free from
> disease; it binds by attachment to happiness and by attachment to
> knowledge, O sinless one.

**Translations pulled:**

- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"Sattva — pure, luminous, and free from
  sorrow — binds us with attachment to happiness and wisdom."* —
  substance confirmed; flagged for exact wording.
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Of these, lucidity, being untainted, is
  luminous and unblemished; Arjuna, it binds one with attachment
  to joy and to knowledge."* — substance and Miller's word
  choices are characteristic; **flag for exact wording**.
- **Prabhupada**: *"O sinless one, the mode of goodness, being
  purer than the others, is illuminating, and it frees one from
  all sinful reactions. Those situated in that mode become
  conditioned by a sense of happiness and knowledge."* —
  substance confirmed.

### C. Anchor verse: 14.7 (rajas)

- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"Rajas is passion, arising from selfish
  desire and attachment. These bind the Self with compulsive
  action."* — substance confirmed; flag for exact wording.
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Know that passion is emotional, born of
  craving and attachment; it binds the embodied self to action."*
  — substance and characteristic Miller wording; flag for
  exactness.
- **Prabhupada**: *"The mode of passion is born of unlimited desires
  and longings, O son of Kunti, and because of this the embodied
  living entity is bound to material fruitive activities."* —
  substance confirmed.

### D. Anchor verse: 14.8 (tamas)

- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"Tamas, born of ignorance, deludes all
  beings through heedlessness, indolence, and sleep."* — substance
  confirmed; wording flagged.
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Know that dark inertia is born of ignorance,
  the delusion of all the embodied; it binds them by negligence,
  indolence, and sleep."* — substance confirmed; "negligence,
  indolence, and sleep" is a Miller signature triplet.
- **Prabhupada**: *"O son of Bharata, know that the mode of
  darkness, born of ignorance, is the delusion of all embodied
  living entities. The results of this mode are madness, indolence
  and sleep, which bind the conditioned soul."* — substance
  confirmed.

### E. Anchor verse: 14.9

> Sattva attaches to happiness, rajas to action, O Bharata; tamas,
> covering knowledge, attaches to negligence.

- **Miller (1986)**: *"Lucidity causes attachment to joy, passion to
  action, Arjuna, but dark inertia, obscuring knowledge, causes
  attachment to negligence."* — flag for exact wording.
- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"Sattva binds us to happiness; rajas binds
  us to action. Tamas, distorting our understanding, binds us to
  delusion."* — substance confirmed.
- **Prabhupada**: *"O son of Bharata, the mode of goodness conditions
  one to happiness; passion conditions him to fruitive action; and
  ignorance, covering his knowledge, binds him to madness."* —
  substance confirmed.

### F. Anchor verse: 14.11–14.13 (signs of dominance)

- **14.11 — sattva ascendant.** All gates of the body are
  illumined by knowledge.
- **14.12 — rajas ascendant.** Greed, activity, undertaking of
  works, restlessness, longing.
- **14.13 — tamas ascendant.** Lack of light, inactivity,
  negligence, delusion.

**Translations pulled:**

- **Mascaró**: substance confirmed across all three; flagged
  paraphrase.
- **Miller**: characteristic phrases preserved; flagged for
  exactness.
- **Easwaran**: substance confirmed.
- **Aurobindo (paraphrase, *Essays on the Gita*)**: Aurobindo
  treats these as descriptions of *psychological dominance*, not
  fixed types. He writes (in substance): *the gunas are not three
  bottles of nature into which souls are poured; they are
  movements that contend in every soul.* — **paraphrase, not
  quotation, unless I can verify the exact line.**

### G. Anchor verse: 14.17

> From sattva, knowledge arises; from rajas, greed; from tamas,
> negligence and delusion and ignorance.

- **Miller**: substance confirmed.
- **Easwaran**: *"From sattva comes understanding; from rajas,
  greed; but the outcome of tamas is confusion, stupefaction, and
  ignorance."* — substance confirmed.
- **Prabhupada**: *"From the mode of goodness, real knowledge
  develops; from the mode of passion, greed develops; and from
  the mode of ignorance develop foolishness, madness and
  illusion."* — substance confirmed.

### H. Anchor verse: 14.18 (trajectories)

> Those abiding in sattva go upward; the rajasic stay in the middle;
> the tamasic, in the lowest mode, go downward.

- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"Those who live in sattva go upwards; those
  in rajas remain where they are. But those immersed in tamas
  sink downwards."* — substance confirmed.
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Men of lucidity rise upward, men of passion
  abide in between, men of dark inertia, in the lowest mode, sink
  down."* — characteristic; flag for exactness.
- **Prabhupada**: *"Those situated in the mode of goodness gradually
  go upward to the higher planets; those in the mode of passion
  live on the earthly planets; and those in the abominable mode
  of ignorance go down to the hellish worlds."* — substance
  confirmed; note Prabhupada's strongly literal cosmographic
  reading.

### I. Anchor verses: 14.22–14.25 (the *gunatita*)

> He who, when sattva, rajas, or tamas arises, does not hate them or
> long for them when absent; who sits as if indifferent, unmoved by
> the gunas, knowing it is the gunas alone that act, who is steady,
> equal in pleasure and pain, equal toward a clod, a stone, or
> gold; equal toward the dear and the unfriendly; equal in praise
> and blame; equal in honor and dishonor; equal toward friend and
> foe; abandoning all undertakings — he is said to have gone beyond
> the gunas.

- **Easwaran (2007)**: *"They live free from the action of the
  gunas. When sattva, rajas, and tamas arise, such people neither
  shun them nor pursue them; unattached, neutral, indifferent —
  knowing it is only the gunas that act, they remain unmoved."* —
  substance confirmed; flag for exact wording.
- **Miller (1986)**: *"Indifferent to lucidity, passion, or dark
  inertia, when they emerge or recede; standing apart from the
  qualities, unmoved, like a witness, knowing only that qualities
  are at work, he stands firm and is unshaken."* — characteristic
  Miller; flag for exact wording.
- **Mascaró (1962)**: *"He who knows that the powers of nature are
  the only doers; who beholds all from on high, beyond the
  storm; who has the same regard for friend and foe, for honour
  and dishonour, for pleasure and pain, for praise and blame —
  that man, in truth, has gone beyond."* — paraphrase-level
  confidence; **flag**.
- **Prabhupada**: *"He who does not hate illumination, attachment,
  and delusion when they are present, nor longs for them when they
  disappear; who is unwavering and undisturbed through all these
  reactions of the material qualities, remaining neutral and
  transcendental, knowing that the modes alone are active... such
  a person is said to have transcended the modes of nature."* —
  substance confirmed; characteristic Prabhupada length and
  cadence.

### J. Commentaries (Phase 2 step 6)

For each engaged tradition, at least one verbatim commentary
passage with full citation. Where verbatim cannot be confirmed,
paraphrase is logged with attribution.

#### J1. Sankara (Advaita)

Sankara's commentary on 14.5 is famously emphatic that the gunas
are properties of *prakriti*, not of the *atman*. The atman, being
free from beginning, is bound only "as if" by the gunas, through
its identification with the body-mind complex.

- **Confirmed substance**, **paraphrase as quotation pending
  validation**: Sankara comments (in substance) that *the
  Self has no real bondage; it is taken to be bound on account of
  its connection with body, mind, intellect, and the modifications
  these undergo through the gunas.* — **flag: paraphrase, not
  quotation, unless I can verify exact translation. Sankara's
  Sanskrit is widely available in English translation by
  A. Mahadeva Sastri (1897) and Swami Gambhirananda (Advaita
  Ashrama, 1984)**.
- For the chapter, I will *cite Sankara through Mahadeva Sastri's
  English rendering* and *paraphrase his interpretation in
  Claude's voice*, naming the text as *Sankara's Bhagavad Gita
  Bhasya, trans. Mahadeva Sastri 1897*. **No direct quotation will
  be presented as Sankara's own English unless I can verify the
  exact wording.** Confidence: paraphrase with citation.

#### J2. Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)

Ramanuja's *Gita Bhasya* on chapter 14 takes the gunas as real
modifications of real prakriti, and argues that the embodied self,
though distinct from prakriti, is bound by the gunas because of
beginningless association.

- **Paraphrase with citation**: Ramanuja, in his *Gita Bhasya*
  (Sanskrit; English trans. Swami Adidevananda, Sri
  Ramakrishna Math, Madras, repr. various) on 14.5, holds — *in
  substance* — that *the gunas, born of real prakriti, really
  bind the real self through real beginningless connection; the
  way out is through real surrender to the real Lord.* —
  **flag: paraphrase, not direct quotation; locator: Adidevananda
  trans., chapter 14 commentary**.

#### J3. Prabhupada (Gaudiya / ISKCON)

Prabhupada's *Bhagavad-gita As It Is* (1972/1983) gives a sectarian
purport for nearly every verse.

- **Verbatim, confirmed**: from the purport to 14.26: *"One who
  engages in pure devotional service, transcending the modes of
  material nature, is at once elevated to the spiritual platform."*
  — Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 14.26 purport, BBT ed.
  — confidence: confirmed substance and very close to wording;
  **flag minor for word-by-word**.
- **Paraphrase**: Prabhupada repeatedly insists that
  transcending the gunas is *only* possible through devotional
  service to Krishna — the philosophical or yogic paths are
  inadequate without this surrender. (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita
  As It Is*, ch. 14 purports, BBT ed.) — confidence: paraphrase,
  substance confirmed.

#### J4. Tilak / Gandhi (activist karma-yoga)

- **Tilak**, *Gita Rahasya* (1915, English trans. B. S. Sukthankar
  1935): Tilak treats the gunas as a frame for character that
  ultimately serves his thesis that the Gita teaches *energetic
  action* over passive renunciation. *Paraphrase, citation:
  Tilak, Gita Rahasya, vol. II, ch. on the gunas (English ed.,
  Tilak Bros, Poona, 1935; edition unconfirmed; validation
  pending).* — confidence: paraphrase.
- **Gandhi**, *Anasakti Yoga* (Gandhi's Gujarati rendering with
  introduction; English trans. Mahadev Desai, *The Gita According
  to Gandhi*, Navajivan Publishing House, 1946). Gandhi, in his
  introduction and in *Discourses on the Gita*, treats the gunas
  as describing the moral atmosphere of the soul and emphasizes
  *sattvic* simplicity and self-restraint. *Paraphrase with
  citation; direct quotation pending verification.*
- For the chapter I will use these as *paraphrase-with-attribution*
  unless I can verify direct lines.

#### J5. Aurobindo (Integral)

Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram Press; first
published serially 1916–1920; collected ed. various). Aurobindo's
chapters on the gunas are extensive; he reads them as the
psychological economy of the soul's movement, not as fixed types.

- **Paraphrase with citation**: Aurobindo argues, *in substance*,
  that the three gunas are not classes of persons but *moods,
  movements, and modes of nature's working* in every soul, and
  that the path beyond is through a self-offering to the Divine
  that does not abolish the gunas but transforms their action.
  (Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, "The Three Gunas," in the
  collected ed.; specific page locator: edition unconfirmed;
  validation pending.) — confidence: paraphrase.

#### J6. Edgerton, Zaehner, Johnson, Patton (academic philological)

- **Edgerton (1944)**, *The Bhagavad Gita*. Edgerton's translation
  is direct, sometimes austere, and his philosophical introduction
  treats the gunas in their Samkhya-philosophical context. He
  notes that the doctrine assumes the Samkhya cosmology in which
  the three gunas are constituent strands of *prakriti*.
  Confidence: paraphrase substance, citation by section, page
  locator pending.
- **Zaehner (1969)**, *The Bhagavad-Gita With a Commentary Based on
  the Original Sources*. Zaehner reads the gunas comparatively,
  with attention to their moral-psychological consequences. He is
  careful not to overstate. Paraphrase with citation.
- **Johnson (Oxford World's Classics 1994)**: Johnson's
  translation of 14.5: *"Sattva, rajas and tamas — those strands,
  born of material nature, bind the imperishable embodied one
  fast in the body, mighty-armed Arjuna."* — confidence:
  substance confirmed; characteristic Johnson use of "strands"
  for gunas; **flag wording for exactness**.
- **Patton (Penguin Classics 2008)**: Patton's translation foregrounds
  the dialogic frame; her chapter 14 reads the gunas as part of
  Krishna's pedagogical ladder for Arjuna. **Paraphrase pending
  verification of exact wording.**

#### J7. Mascaró / Miller (literary-universalist)

Already pulled in anchor-verse section. Mascaró's
"constituents of nature" and Miller's "lucidity / passion / dark
inertia" are the chapter's two main literary alternatives to the
Sanskrit. Both engaged.

### K. Modern counterpoints (Phase 2 step 7)

#### K1. MacIntyre — virtue ethics on character

- **MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (1981/2007, 3rd ed., Univ. of
  Notre Dame Press).** MacIntyre on virtue as a disposition
  formed by practice within a tradition.
- **Verbatim, confirmed substance**: *"A virtue is an acquired
  human quality the possession and exercise of which tends to
  enable us to achieve those goods which are internal to
  practices and the lack of which effectively prevents us from
  achieving any such goods."* (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, ch. 14,
  3rd ed. 2007, p. 191) — confidence: this is one of the most
  cited definitions in the book; substance confirmed; **wording
  flagged for verification against the 3rd ed. p. 191**.
- I will use this to set the gunas — read as *temperament* — into
  conversation with virtue as *practice-formed disposition*.
  MacIntyre's frame complicates any reading of the gunas as
  fixed natural endowment.

#### K2. Big Five / modern personality (Costa & McCrae)

- **Costa, P. T., & McCrae, R. R. (1992).** *Revised NEO Personality
  Inventory (NEO-PI-R) and NEO Five-Factor Inventory (NEO-FFI)
  Professional Manual.* Psychological Assessment Resources.
- **Paraphrase with citation**: the Big Five model — Openness,
  Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism — is
  the modern psychological consensus framework for trait-level
  description. I will *not* claim a guna ↔ Big Five mapping; I will
  use the Big Five as a contemporary point of comparison: like the
  gunas, traits in the Big Five are descriptive *patterns of
  inclination*, not fixed essences; unlike the gunas, the Big Five
  carries no metaphysical claim and no normative ladder
  (sattva-as-better-than-tamas). — confidence: paraphrase, claim
  bounded.

#### K3. Bhikkhu Bodhi — Buddhist counterpoint

- **Bodhi, Bhikkhu (ed. & trans.), *In the Buddha's Words: An
  Anthology of Discourses from the Pali Canon* (Wisdom
  Publications, 2005).** Bodhi's anthology covers the *kilesas* —
  the defilements (greed, hatred, delusion) which the Pali tradition
  treats as the basic forces that distort cognition.
- **Paraphrase with citation**: Bodhi's introductions repeatedly
  emphasize that the Buddha's psychology is *therapeutic* — the
  defilements are diagnosed in order to be uprooted, not in order
  to classify persons. The structural parallel to the gunas is
  striking; the metaphysical commitments are different.
  (Bodhi, *In the Buddha's Words*, ch. 8, "Mastering the Mind,"
  Wisdom 2005; specific page locator: validation pending.) —
  confidence: paraphrase.

### L. Modern science strands (Phase 2 step 8)

#### L1. Behavioral genetics on temperament — Plomin and Rothbart

- **Plomin, Robert.** *Blueprint: How DNA Makes Us Who We Are*
  (MIT Press, 2018). Plomin reviews five decades of behavioral
  genetics on heritability of personality and cognitive traits.
- **Specific finding**: heritability of major personality
  dimensions (the Big Five) is consistently estimated at around
  40–50% across twin studies (Plomin, *Blueprint*, 2018,
  introductory chapters; specific page locator: pending). The
  remaining variance is non-shared environment, not "shared
  family." — confidence: substance confirmed and very widely
  cited; **bound the claim** — heritability is population-level,
  not individual; estimates vary by trait and by method.
- **Rothbart, Mary K.** *Becoming Who We Are: Temperament and
  Personality in Development* (Guilford Press, 2011). Rothbart's
  framework distinguishes temperament dimensions —
  *surgency/extraversion*, *negative affectivity*, *effortful
  control* — observable in early infancy, predictive of later
  personality. — confidence: substance confirmed; specific page
  pending.
- **Use**: I will draw the parallel — that temperamental tendencies
  arrive partly given, are stable but not fixed, and develop
  through environment and practice. This complicates a *fatalist*
  reading of the gunas without invalidating their descriptive use.

#### L2. Circadian and metabolic effects on cognition

- **Foster, Russell G., & Kreitzman, Leon.** *Circadian Rhythms: A
  Very Short Introduction* (Oxford University Press, 2017). Foster
  is a leading circadian neuroscientist. Reviews the neural and
  hormonal control of sleep–wake and its impact on cognition.
- **Walker, Matthew.** *Why We Sleep: Unlocking the Power of Sleep
  and Dreams* (Scribner, 2017). Walker reviews the consequences of
  sleep deprivation on attention, decision-making, and emotional
  regulation. **Bound the claim**: Walker's synthesis has been
  challenged in detail (Alexey Guzey's 2019 critique is
  substantive); I will cite the broad finding that chronic sleep
  loss degrades cognition and emotional regulation, while not
  reproducing specific contested claims.
- **Use**: a reader who would like to "be more sattvic" through
  willpower alone, while sleep-deprived and chronically
  caffeine-cycled, is fighting a metabolic substrate. This is the
  modern-science teeth on the chapter's claim that the gunas
  manifest in *food, sleep, and habit*.
- (Possible secondary: ego-depletion / glucose research — Roy
  Baumeister's original glucose-and-self-control claims have
  failed key replications; I will not cite that line as
  established. **Flag: not used as established science.**)
