# Source Notes — Chapter 18 (Moksha Sanyasa Yoga)

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7; engaging seven)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara — 18.66 read as instruction to renounce
   *all* dharmas (including karma yoga) and abide as the non-dual self
   that requires no further action; *jnana-nishtha* as final stage.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja — 18.66 as the
   "*charama-shloka*" (final verse), the supreme teaching of *prapatti*
   (self-surrender); the dharmas to be abandoned are means, not ends.
3. **Dvaita** — Madhva — strict realism; surrender to a real, distinct
   personal Lord; hierarchy of jivas preserved.
4. **Gaudiya Vaishnavism / ISKCON** — Prabhupada — 18.66 as the most
   confidential instruction; "all dharmas" includes Vedic dharma itself;
   absolute surrender to Krishna as *the* method.
5. **Activist / political Karma-Yoga** — Tilak (*Gita Rahasya*) and
   Gandhi (*Anasakti Yoga*) — 18.47 (*svadharma*) and 18.66 read in
   non-renunciatory keys; 18.66 as inner non-attachment, not abandonment
   of moral action.
6. **Integral Yoga** — Aurobindo — 18.66 as integral surrender that
   transforms action; the "*sarva-dharman parityajya*" verse as the
   evolutionary key.
7. **Comparative / academic philological** — Edgerton, Johnson, Miller,
   Zaehner — close translations; the chapter as a layered recapitulation
   added editorially or as a unified summation; the five factors as
   Sankhya residue.

(Optional 8th: contemplative-pastoral modernizer — Easwaran, Yogananda —
to be referenced as supporting voice rather than full subsection.)

### Modern counterpoints (target ≥ 2; planning 3–4)

1. **Twelve-step / AA** — surrender to a Higher Power; Step 1 ("we
   admitted we were powerless") and Step 3 ("turn our will and our lives
   over to the care of God as we understood Him"). Source: *Alcoholics
   Anonymous* ("the Big Book"), 4th edition, 2001, ch. 5 "How It Works"
   and the Twelve Steps. Counterpoint and resonance with 18.66.
2. **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** — Steven C. Hayes — on
   "creative hopelessness" and "willingness" / acceptance as a
   precondition for committed action. Source: Hayes, Strosahl & Wilson,
   *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy*, 2nd ed. Guilford 2012.
3. **Existential psychotherapy** — Viktor Frankl, *Man's Search for
   Meaning* (1946) — defiant freedom even in the camps, and the
   "self-transcendence" thesis: meaning is found by surrendering the
   self-as-project for something beyond it. Counterpoint between
   surrender and agency.
4. *(Optional)* — David Richo, *The Five Things We Cannot Change*
   (Shambhala 2005), or Tara Brach on *radical acceptance* as a
   psychological cousin to *prapatti*. Use as optional support, not a
   full subsection.

### Modern science strands (target 2)

1. **Post-traumatic growth (PTG)** — Richard G. Tedeschi & Lawrence G.
   Calhoun — the PTG construct and its measurement (the
   Posttraumatic Growth Inventory). Source: Tedeschi & Calhoun (1996),
   "The Posttraumatic Growth Inventory: Measuring the positive legacy
   of trauma," *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 9(3), 455–471; and
   Tedeschi & Calhoun (2004), "Posttraumatic Growth: Conceptual
   Foundations and Empirical Evidence," *Psychological Inquiry*, 15(1),
   1–18. Bears on whether "letting go" of one's prior life-narrative
   correlates with growth, and on the boundary conditions.
2. **Letting-go / experiential acceptance research** — Kirk Warren
   Brown & Richard Ryan on *mindful attention awareness* and
   non-reactivity (Brown & Ryan, 2003, "The benefits of being present:
   mindfulness and its role in psychological well-being," *JPSP*,
   84(4), 822–848); and Steven Hayes & colleagues on experiential
   avoidance vs. acceptance. The relevant scientific question: does
   experimentally manipulating "willingness to feel" what arises
   improve outcome on tasks the person had previously avoided?

  Citation: Miller, 18.13–14, p. 145 (locator approximate).

#### Commentaries on 18.66

- **Sankara, *Gita Bhashya* on 18.66 (Sastri trans.)**: takes
  *sarva-dharman parityajya* as instruction to renounce *all*
  prescribed dharmas including karma yoga, and abide in *jnana* — the
  *adhikari* (qualified person) here is the seeker of liberation who
  has matured beyond ritual and prescribed action. The "refuge" is
  knowledge of the non-dual self that is no longer the agent of any
  dharma. Confidence: **paraphrase**, attributed to Sankara's
  *bhashya* on 18.66, Sastri trans. 1897 (edition unconfirmed; page
  locator unconfirmed; validation pending).

- **Ramanuja, *Gita Bhashya* on 18.66 (Adidevananda or Sampatkumaran
  trans.)**: this is the *charama-shloka* — the final, summative verse
  — and teaches *prapatti* (unconditional self-surrender) as the
  alternative path for those who cannot complete *bhakti yoga* on
  their own. Significantly, Ramanuja does *not* read the verse as
  abolishing karma yoga or dharmic obligation; he reads "abandon all
  dharmas" as "abandon them as means of liberation while continuing
  them as expressions of devotion / divine command." Confidence:
  **paraphrase** with high confidence on substance; will mark
  attribution carefully.

- **Madhva**: surrender is to a real, distinct, personal Vishnu;
  hierarchy of jivas is preserved post-liberation; the "sins" forgiven
  are real moral debits. Confidence: **paraphrase** of standard Madhva
  position; cite Madhva, *Gita Bhashya*; trans. Bannanje Govindacharya
  (edition unconfirmed; validation pending).

- **Prabhupada, purport to 18.66**: this is "the most confidential
  part of the Vedic literature" (*sarva-guhyatamam*, cf. 18.64);
  surrender is exclusive to Krishna; "all varieties of religion"
  includes Vedic prescriptions and even other forms of religion;
  surrender alone liberates. Confidence: **closely tracking**
  Prabhupada's well-known purport; signature phrases ("most
  confidential," "varieties of religion") are reliable. Cite
  Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, BBT, 1972/1983, purport on
  18.66.

- **Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga* / *Discourses on the Gita* on 18.66**:
  Gandhi reads *sarva-dharman parityajya* as inner non-attachment to
  the fruits of dharmic action, not as abandonment of action itself;
  the "refuge" is the indwelling self / God; *prapatti* is moral
  surrender, not metaphysical passivity. Confidence: **paraphrase**;
  cite Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga* introduction (Navajivan) and
  *Discourses on the Gita* (Yeravda Mandir, 1926; pub. Navajivan).
  Edition unconfirmed; validation pending.

- **Tilak, *Gita Rahasya* on 18.66**: 18.66 does *not* override 18.47;
  the two verses must be read together. Surrender is the inner
  attitude; *karma yoga* — performance of *svadharma* without
  attachment — is the outer life. To read 18.66 as abandonment of
  *karma* is to misread the Gita's whole arc. Confidence: **paraphrase**
  of Tilak's well-known position in *Gita Rahasya* (English trans.
  B.S. Sukthankar, 1935). Edition / page unconfirmed.

- **Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* on 18.66**: 18.66 is the integral
  surrender — not abandonment but transformation; the ego-mind is
  surrendered, but action continues, *now divinized*. The verse is the
  evolutionary climax of the Gita, not a quietist withdrawal.
  Confidence: **paraphrase** of Aurobindo's distinctive integral
  reading; cite *Essays on the Gita*, Part II, Sri Aurobindo Ashram /
  Lotus Press; edition unconfirmed; validation pending.

#### Steven Hayes — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy

Hayes's central claim: psychological flexibility (acceptance,
defusion, present-moment contact, self-as-context, values, committed
action) reduces suffering caused by experiential avoidance.

Quotation candidate (Hayes, Strosahl, & Wilson, *Acceptance and
Commitment Therapy*, 2nd edition, Guilford Press, 2012):

therapy where the client recognizes that all the strategies for
avoiding painful feelings have failed, and is therefore "free" to try
something new — namely acceptance.

Quotation candidate, popular-trade version: Hayes, *Get Out of Your
Mind and Into Your Life*, New Harbinger, 2005, p. 38 region:

> "Acceptance is not the same as wanting. Acceptance means actively

Confidence: **closely tracking** Hayes's recurring formulation across

reasonably reliable; mark page locator unconfirmed.

Citation: Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning*, Beacon Press, 2006

### Modern science pulls

#### Post-traumatic growth — Tedeschi & Calhoun

Citation: Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (1996). "The
Posttraumatic Growth Inventory: Measuring the positive legacy of
trauma." *Journal of Traumatic Stress*, 9(3), 455–471.
Citation: Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (2004). "Posttraumatic
Growth: Conceptual Foundations and Empirical Evidence."
*Psychological Inquiry*, 15(1), 1–18.

Bounded claim: across cancer survivors, bereaved parents, combat
veterans, and survivors of disasters, a substantial subset (Tedeschi
and Calhoun's research suggests roughly 30–70% depending on cohort
and instrument) report positive change after trauma in five domains:
relating to others, new possibilities, personal strength, spiritual
change, and appreciation of life. The "growth" is correlated with —
not caused by — surrender of the prior life-narrative; the model
posits that a "seismic" event forces revision of the assumptive
world, and the attempted rebuilding produces growth in some.

Bound on the claim:
- PTG is *not* universal post-trauma; many show no growth, and some
  show deterioration.
- PTG self-reports may capture meaning-making rather than functional
  improvement.
- The growth often coexists with continued distress; it is not a
  replacement for clinical intervention.

Confidence: **confirmed researcher and confirmed substance of
findings**; phrasing of the bounded claim is mine.

#### Mindful acceptance vs. avoidance — Brown & Ryan; Hayes et al.

Citation: Brown, K. W., & Ryan, R. M. (2003). "The benefits of being
present: Mindfulness and its role in psychological well-being."
*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 84(4), 822–848.
Citation: Hayes, S. C., Luoma, J. B., Bond, F. W., Masuda, A., &
Lillis, J. (2006). "Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: Model,
processes, and outcomes." *Behaviour Research and Therapy*, 44(1),
1–25.

Bounded claim: experimentally, instructing participants in
"willingness" or "acceptance" of unwanted internal experiences
(versus suppression or distraction) produces reliably better outcomes
on physical-pain tolerance tasks (cold-pressor), anxiety-provoking
tasks (CO2 challenge), and behavioral commitment to valued actions.
Two specific named studies in this lineage:

- Hayes, S. C., Bissett, R., Korn, Z., Zettle, R. D., Rosenfarb, I.,
  Cooper, L., & Grundt, A. (1999). "The impact of acceptance versus
  control rationales on pain tolerance." *Psychological Record*,
  49(1), 33–47. Cold-pressor task; acceptance-condition participants
  tolerated pain longer than control-condition participants.
