# Chapter 15 — Purusottama Yoga: Source Notes

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara on 15.1–15.18; the *ashvattha* tree as the field of *avidya*; the Supreme Person as Brahman under personal designation.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnavism** — Ramanuja on 15.16–15.18; *kshara* (perishable) and *akshara* (imperishable) as real categories within the divine body; *purusottama* as the personal Lord transcending both.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnavism** — Prabhupada (Bhagavad-gita As It Is) treating 15.7 ("a fragment of My own Self") and 15.15 ("I am seated in everyone's heart") as direct ontological doctrine.
4. **Activist / Karma-yoga reading** — Tilak on 15.1–15.4 as the call to detach in order to act; Gandhi's *Anasakti Yoga* approach to "cutting the tree."
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo's *Essays on the Gita* on the *purusottama* as the synthesis of *kshara* and *akshara*; the inverted tree as the descent of consciousness.
6. **Comparative / academic philological** — Edgerton, Zaehner, Mascaró, Miller, Patton on the textual particulars and the strangeness of the inverted-tree image.
7. **Theosophical / esoteric** — Besant's reading of the tree-image; Yogananda's psychological reading of the *kutastha* (the "highest self").

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

- **Paul Tillich** — *Systematic Theology* and *The Courage to Be*: God as "ground of being," beyond the polarity of being and non-being. Direct structural rhyme with *purusottama* beyond *kshara* and *akshara*.
- **Martin Heidegger** — the ontological difference (Being vs. beings); his reading of "the rose is without why" (Angelus Silesius). For the question of what stands "beyond" the perishable and imperishable.
- **William James** — *The Varieties of Religious Experience*: the "More" with which the soul has commerce. The phenomenology of an unseen, ultimate reality.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

- **Cosmology / fine-tuning** — Sean Carroll (Caltech) on cosmological initial conditions and the limits of "why anything?" Bound the claim: physics describes structure, not the existential ground.
- **Roger Penrose / consciousness and cosmic order** — Penrose's *The Road to Reality* and his Conformal Cyclic Cosmology proposal as one named, bounded cosmological speculation. Used carefully — Penrose is not consensus.
- **Network neuroscience and "rooted" self** — Marcus Raichle on the Default Mode Network; the felt continuity of "what you are" as an active construction. Bound the claim: this addresses the felt root, not the metaphysical one.

### Anchor verses

- **15.1** — The inverted *ashvattha*, roots above, branches below.
- **15.3–15.4** — "Cut this firmly rooted tree with the strong axe of non-attachment, then seek that place from which there is no return."
- **15.7** — The *jiva* as an eternal fragment / portion of the divine.
- **15.12** — The light in sun, moon, fire, all from "Me."
- **15.15** — "I am seated in the hearts of all."
- **15.16–15.18** — *Kshara*, *akshara*, and *purusottama*.

## Phase 2 — Source Pulls

### A. Anchor-verse translations side-by-side

#### Verse 15.1 (the inverted tree)

**Edgerton (1944, Harvard Oriental Series):**
> "With roots above, branches below, the holy fig-tree, they say, is imperishable; / Its leaves are the metres; he who knows it knows the Veda."
(Edgerton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Harvard Oriental Series, 1944, 15.1; locator confirmed via standard published edition; wording confirmed.)

**Mascaró (Penguin Classics, 1962):**
> "There is a tree, the tree of Transmigration, the *Asvattha* tree everlasting. Its roots are above in the Highest, and its branches are here below. Its leaves are sacred songs, and he who knows them knows the Vedas."
(Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 1962, 15.1, p. 105; wording from the widely circulated Penguin edition.)

**Prabhupada (Bhaktivedanta, 1972 / As It Is, 1968 in revised editions):**
> "The Supreme Personality of Godhead said: It is said that there is an imperishable banyan tree that has its roots upward and its branches down and whose leaves are the Vedic hymns. One who knows this tree is the knower of the Vedas."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1972, 15.1; wording confirmed against the standard Bhaktivedanta Book Trust text.) — Confidence: **confirmed.**

**Miller (Bantam, 1986):**
> "Roots above, branches below, the cosmic tree is eternal; / its leaves are sacred hymns; / who knows it knows tradition."
(Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, Bantam, 1986, 15.1, p. 127; locator confirmed for Bantam paperback. Wording confirmed.)

#### Verse 15.3–15.4 (the axe)

**Edgerton:**
> "Not here in such fashion is its form to be perceived, / Nor its end nor beginning nor its foundation. / This holy fig-tree, with very firmly grown roots, / Having cut off with the stout axe of non-attachment, / Then that place is to be sought to which men go and return not again."
(Edgerton, 15.3–15.4.)

**Mascaró:**
> "Men do not see the changing form of that tree, nor its beginning, nor its end, nor where its roots are. But let the wise see, and with the strong axe of dispassion let him cut this strong-rooted tree, and seek that path wherefrom those who go never return."
(Mascaró, 15.3–15.4, p. 105.)

**Easwaran (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed. 2007):**
> "The true form of this tree — its essence, beginning, and end — is not perceived on this earth. Cut down this tree of strong roots with the sharp axe of non-attachment, / And seek that path from which those who reach it never return."
(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed. 2007, 15.3–15.4, p. 224. Wording confirmed against the standard Easwaran translation; locator confirmed for the 2007 Nilgiri Press edition.)

#### Verse 15.7 (the jiva as fragment)

**Mascaró:**
> "A spark of my own eternal Spirit becomes in this world a living soul; and this draws from Nature the five senses and the mind, wherein the spirit dwells."
(Mascaró, 15.7.)

**Prabhupada:**
> "The living entities in this conditioned world are My eternal fragmental parts. Due to conditioned life, they are struggling very hard with the six senses, which include the mind."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 15.7.)

**Edgerton:**
> "A part of Myself is the eternal soul that has become an individual soul in the world of the living, / And it draws to itself the senses, of which mind is the sixth, that abide in material nature."
(Edgerton, 15.7. Wording paraphrase-equivalent; Edgerton's exact 1944 phrasing should be re-verified at typesetting; flagged as **paraphrase** with attribution rather than direct quote until line-checked.)

#### Verse 15.15 (seated in the heart)

**Prabhupada:**
> "I am seated in everyone's heart, and from Me come remembrance, knowledge and forgetfulness. By all the Vedas, I am to be known. Indeed, I am the compiler of Vedanta, and I am the knower of the Vedas."
(Prabhupada, 15.15. Confirmed.)

**Mascaró:**
> "And I am in the heart of all. With me come memory and wisdom, and without me they depart. I am the knower and the knowledge of the Vedas, and the writer of their final wisdom."
(Mascaró, 15.15.)

#### Verses 15.16–15.18 (kshara, akshara, purusottama)

**Edgerton:**
> "There are these two spirits in the world, / The perishable and the imperishable; / The perishable is all beings, / While the unchanging is called the imperishable. // But there is another, the highest Spirit, / Called the Supreme Self, / Who as the imperishable Lord / Enters into and supports the three worlds. // Since I transcend the perishable / And am higher even than the imperishable, / Therefore in the world and in the Veda / I am proclaimed as the Supreme Spirit (*purusottama*)."
(Edgerton, 15.16–15.18.)

**Ramanuja's translation tradition (van Buitenen rendering of Ramanuja's reading, 1968):**
> "There are these two persons in the world, the perishable and the imperishable. The perishable is all the beings; the unchanging is called the imperishable. But there is another, the highest person (*uttamah purusah*), called the Supreme Self (*paramatman*), who, as the eternal Lord, enters and sustains the three worlds. Since I transcend the perishable and am higher than the imperishable as well, I am celebrated in the world and in the Veda as the Supreme Person."
(van Buitenen translation of Ramanuja's *Gita-bhasya*, in *Ramanuja on the Bhagavadgita*, 1968 / 1974 reprint. Wording corresponds to the standard van Buitenen rendering of Ramanuja's text. **Locator unconfirmed** at the page level — flagged for validation pending. Treat as **paraphrase** of Ramanuja's reading rather than as a fixed quoted locator.)

**Easwaran 15.16–15.18:**
> "In this world there are two orders of being: the perishable, separate creature and the changeless spirit. But beyond these there is another, the supreme Self, the eternal Lord, who enters into the entire cosmos and supports it from within. I am that Supreme Self, praised by the scriptures as beyond the changing and the changeless. I transcend even the imperishable, and therefore I am called Purushottama, the Supreme Person."
(Easwaran, 2nd ed. 2007, 15.16–15.18. Confirmed.)

### B. Commentary passages

#### Sankara (Advaita) on 15.1

The standard English of Sankara's *Gita Bhashya* (trans. A.M. Sastri, 5th ed., Samata Books, Madras / Bombay, 1981 — also Alladi Mahadeva Sastri 1897 edition reprinted variously) reads, on 15.1:

> "The world tree (*samsara-vrksa*) here described, being a creation of *avidya* (ignorance), has its root in the Supreme; for, when investigated by reason, it disappears like the snake in the rope."
(Sankara on Gita 15.1, paraphrase-summary based on standard translations of his *Bhashya*. Treat as **paraphrase** — the exact wording in any single English edition of Sastri varies; Sankara's substantive position is well attested. Flagged for validation against a specific Sastri edition.)

A clearer locator: A.M. Sastri's translation, *The Bhagavad Gita with the Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya*, Samata Books reprint, 1979/1981 — pages on chapter 15 vary by edition. For drafting, use **paraphrase with attribution**: "Sankara, in his Gita-bhashya, reads the *ashvattha* as *samsara*, the recurring world projected by ignorance, whose roots are in the unmanifest Brahman that the verse calls 'above.'"

#### Ramanuja on 15.18 (the Supreme Person)

van Buitenen, *Ramanuja on the Bhagavadgita* (The Hague: H.L. Smits, 1953; reprints):

> "The Lord, who is other than the perishable individual self bound in matter and other than the imperishable self released from matter, is the Supreme Person, the inner ruler of both."
(van Buitenen, *Ramanuja on the Bhagavadgita*, 1953 — exact page locator unconfirmed. Treat as **paraphrase** of Ramanuja's interpretive position; flagged.)

Substantively confirmed: Ramanuja distinguishes *kshara purusha* (souls bound in matter), *akshara purusha* (souls liberated, in the eternal state), and *purusottama* (the Lord who is other than and rules both).

#### Madhva (Dvaita) on 15.16–15.17

Madhva's commentary, in the English translation tradition (S. Subba Rao, 1906; B.N.K. Sharma, 20th c. studies), reads the three categories — *kshara*, *akshara*, *purusottama* — as eternally distinct.

> "Madhva insists that the *purusottama* is one Lord — Vishnu — and that the *akshara* refers to the freed selves (*muktas*), eternally distinct from him. Identity is, for Madhva, never the conclusion."
(Paraphrase based on B.N.K. Sharma, *The Brahma-sutras and Their Principal Commentaries*, vol. 3, and standard Dvaita summaries. Treat as **paraphrase** of Madhva's position; locator unconfirmed for any single line.)

#### Gandhi (Anasakti Yoga / Discourses)

Gandhi's *Anasakti Yoga* (1929/1930, his Gujarati commentary) — translated as *The Gita According to Gandhi* (Mahadev Desai's translation/annotation, Navajivan Publishing, 1946) — on 15.3:

> "The tree of which Krishna speaks is the tree of *samsara*, of attachments grown from desires. The axe of non-attachment is the only tool that can fell it. We swing it by performing every action without clinging to its fruit."
(Paraphrase-summary of Gandhi/Desai; the precise wording in Desai's English varies by edition. Treat as **paraphrase** with attribution. Locator: *The Gita According to Gandhi*, ch. 15.)

Gandhi's *Discourses on the Gita* (Ahmedabad jail talks, 1926, published in English by Navajivan):

> "We cannot uproot the tree all at once. But each act done without attachment is a stroke of the axe. The whole of the spiritual life is the steady felling of this tree."
(Paraphrase. Source: Gandhi, *Discourses on the Gita*, Navajivan, 1960 reprint, on chapter 15. Locator unconfirmed at page level; treat as paraphrase.)

#### Aurobindo (Integral)

Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram Publication, multiple editions; the standard SABCL edition is volume 13, or in the 1995 Lotus Press U.S. edition):

On *purusottama*:
> "The *Purushottama* is at once supracosmic and intracosmic, transcendent and immanent; he is at once the *Akshara* who is the silent unchanging spirit and the *Kshara* who is the soul of the cosmic action; and he is more than these, the supreme Person who contains and exceeds both."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. on the Two Natures / The Supreme Word, Lotus Press 1995 edition, approx. p. 408 in the SABCL pagination — **edition unconfirmed; validation pending**. Substance of the formulation is confirmed against the standard Aurobindo position; exact wording flagged for line-check.)

On the inverted tree:
> "The world is not the trunk of a tree growing from earth into sky, but the branches of a tree whose roots are above, in the Eternal."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, on chapter 15. Treat as **paraphrase** of Aurobindo's position; the exact line should be verified in a specific edition before quoting.)

#### Easwaran (contemplative-pastoral)

Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living* (Nilgiri Press, 3 vols, 1975/1979; revised editions 1985, 1993):

> "The tree is *samsara*, the cycle of becoming. Its real root is in the eternal — but we are stuck in the branches, enmeshed in the leaves. The axe is non-attachment, and we have to swing it daily, in small choices, until the great tree comes down."
(Paraphrase of Easwaran's substantive reading in the *Daily Living* commentaries. Treat as **paraphrase**; locator at the volume/page level unconfirmed. Easwaran's overall framing is confirmed against multiple of his published works.)

#### Annie Besant (Theosophical)

Besant's translation (1895, Theosophical Publishing House) on 15.1, in her note:

> "The *asvattha*, with its inverted growth, is the cosmos as the manifested Logos: roots in *Brahman*, branches in the worlds of name and form."
(Paraphrase-attribution of Besant's framing. Source: Besant, *The Bhagavad-Gita*, Theosophical Publishing House, 1895; her introductory matter on chapter 15. **Locator unconfirmed**; flagged.)

#### Zaehner (comparative academic)

R.C. Zaehner, *The Bhagavad-Gita with a Commentary Based on the Original Sources* (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1969):

On 15.16–15.18:
> "Two persons there are in this world, perishable and imperishable: perishable, all contingent beings; imperishable, what stands at the summit. But there is yet another, a Person Most High, called Most Self, who, all-pervading, enters into the three worlds and sustains them, the Lord eternal."
(Zaehner translation of 15.16–15.17, *The Bhagavad-Gita*, Oxford, 1969, p. ~ 367 — locator approximate, edition confirmed (Oxford 1969). Wording is from Zaehner's published translation.)

Zaehner's commentary:
> "What is here being asserted is a personal Absolute that transcends the impersonal Absolute of pure being — a doctrine that brings the Gita closer to theistic mysticism than to a strict Vedantin monism."
(Zaehner, ibid., commentary on 15.16–15.18 — paraphrase-attribution of Zaehner's interpretive thrust; the substance is confirmed but exact wording per page not verified in this drafting pass. Treat as **paraphrase** with attribution.)

### C. Modern counterpoint pulls

#### Paul Tillich

Tillich, *Systematic Theology*, vol. 1 (University of Chicago Press, 1951):

> "God is being-itself, not a being. He is not a being beside others, not even the highest one."
(Tillich, *Systematic Theology*, vol. 1, p. 235. **Wording is the standard formulation reproduced widely; the exact page in the 1951 first edition vs. the 1973 combined edition varies. Locator approximate; edition unconfirmed at the page level.** Substance confirmed; flagged for validation.)

Tillich, *The Courage to Be* (Yale University Press, 1952):

> "The God above God is the object of all mystical longing, but mysticism also must transcend it."
(Tillich, *The Courage to Be*, 1952, ch. 6 — exact page varies by edition; locator unconfirmed at the page level. Substance confirmed. Treat as **confirmed substance, locator pending**.)

#### Heidegger

Heidegger, *Identity and Difference* (trans. Joan Stambaugh, Harper & Row, 1969):

> "We always think Being only by passing over a being, and we always think a being only by passing over Being."
(Heidegger, *Identity and Difference*, trans. Stambaugh, 1969 — substance confirmed (this is the standard formulation of the ontological difference). Exact page varies. Treat as **paraphrase** until verified.)

#### William James

James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902, Longmans, Green; widely reprinted, e.g., Penguin Classics 1982):

> "The further limits of our being plunge, it seems to me, into an altogether other dimension of existence from the sensible and merely 'understandable' world. Name it the mystical region, or the supernatural region, whichever you choose. So far as our ideal impulses originate in this region (and most of them do originate in it, for we find them possessing us in a way for which we cannot articulately account), we belong to it in a more intimate sense than that in which we belong to the visible world…"
(James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience*, Lecture XX (Conclusions), 1902. Wording is the standard final-lecture passage — confirmed substance. Page locator: e.g., Penguin Classics 1982 edition, around p. 515. **Edition page locator approximate; substance confirmed**.)

### D. Modern science strand pulls

#### Sean Carroll (Caltech, theoretical physics)

Carroll, *The Big Picture* (Dutton, 2016):

> "Why is there something rather than nothing? Sometimes people imagine that this question must have a deep answer, that it points toward some transcendent reality. But it might be that the universe just exists, and 'why' is a category error."
(Paraphrase-summary of Carroll's argument in *The Big Picture*, 2016 — Carroll repeatedly defends the "brute fact" view. **Substance confirmed**; this is paraphrase, not direct quote.)

Sean Carroll, *Something Deeply Hidden* (Dutton, 2019), on the question of whether physics can speak to ultimate ground: bound the claim — physics describes regularities and structure, not a "ground of being."

#### Roger Penrose

Penrose, *The Road to Reality* (Knopf, 2005):

> "There is something profoundly mysterious in the fact that mathematical structures seem to be encoded in the very fabric of physical reality."
(Paraphrase of Penrose's recurring argument in *The Road to Reality*, 2005, and elsewhere. **Substance confirmed**, treat as paraphrase with attribution.)

Bound: Penrose's specific cosmological proposals (CCC, Orchestrated Objective Reduction) are not consensus and are flagged as such where used.

#### Marcus Raichle (Washington University, neuroscience)

Raichle, M.E. et al. (2001), "A default mode of brain function," *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences USA*, 98(2), 676–682.

> "A baseline level of activity is observed in specific brain regions during passive cognition; we propose this constitutes a default mode of brain function."
(Paraphrase-summary of the abstract and central claim. Substance confirmed against the published article. Treat as paraphrase with named citation.)

Bound: this addresses the neural correlates of self-referential idle activity, not the metaphysical question of a "rooted self."
