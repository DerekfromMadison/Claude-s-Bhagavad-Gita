## Phase 1 — Schools and named voices to engage

### Interpretive viewpoints planned (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara on 6.5, 6.34–35; meditation as preparatory.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Bhakti** — Ramanuja and the Vaishnava framing of
   meditation as resting on the Lord.
3. **Activist / Gandhian** — Gandhi reads 6.5 as moral self-reliance; the
   real battlefield is interior.
4. **Integral** — Aurobindo on the silent mind as a doorway, not a
   destination.
5. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizer** — Easwaran on practice as
   passage-meditation in the household life.
6. **Comparative / academic philological** — Edgerton or Johnson, set
   against Mascaró literary register.
7. **Gaudiya Vaishnava** — Prabhupada's reading: meditation completed only
   in Krishna-consciousness.
8. **Theosophical** — Annie Besant, with her lens noted carefully.

### Modern counterpoints planned (≥ 2)

- **Simone Weil**, *Waiting for God* and *Gravity and Grace*: attention as
  the substance of love, the substance of prayer.
- **Iris Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good*: "unselfing" through
  attention to the real.
- **Bhikkhu Bodhi** / Buddhist analogue: *samatha* and *vipassana* as the
  twin disciplines that the Gita's *abhyasa-vairagya* parallels.
- (reserve) **Jon Kabat-Zinn** for the secular-mindfulness move — handle
  with care, not as endorsement.

### Modern-science strands planned (≥ 1, target 2)

- **Neuroplasticity / structural changes from meditation** — Sara Lazar
  et al. (2005): MRI evidence of cortical thickness differences in
  long-term meditators.
- **Default Mode Network and meditation** — Judson Brewer et al. (2011):
  experienced meditators show reduced DMN activity at rest.
- **Attention training** — Amishi Jha's work on meditation and attentional
  control under stress (e.g., military and athletic populations).
- **Affective neuroscience** — Richard Davidson's work with long-term
  practitioners (Lutz, Davidson et al.) on gamma synchrony and
  compassion practice.

### Anchor verses (chapter brief) and translations to set side-by-side

- **6.5** ("uddhared atmanatmanam ...") — one must lift oneself by
  oneself; the self is friend or enemy of the self.
- **6.6** — the self conquered is a friend; the self unconquered is its
  own enemy.
- **6.19** — "as a lamp in a windless place does not flicker" — the yogin
  of restrained mind.
- **6.26** — wherever the restless mind wanders, lead it back to the self.
- **6.34** — Arjuna: "the mind is restless, turbulent, strong, obstinate."
- **6.35** — Krishna: "no doubt the mind is hard to restrain — but by

- **6.36** (corollary) — for one of unrestrained self, yoga is hard;
  for the self-restrained, it is attainable by means.
- **6.40–6.45** — what becomes of the one who falls from yoga.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls (verbatim or marked paraphrase)

### Anchor verse 6.5 — "Lift yourself by yourself"

**Source 1 — Eknath Easwaran (1985, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Nilgiri Press
edition; widely reprinted; the 2007 Vintage edition is the modern
standard).**

> "Reshape yourself through the power of your will; never let yourself
> be degraded by self-will. The will is the only friend of the Self,
> and the will is the only enemy of the Self."

(Gita 6.5, trans. Easwaran 1985, page locator approximate p. 138 in
later printings; **confidence: paraphrase-confirmed-substance,
wording-confirmed-against-Easwaran-rendering-style**, edition page
unconfirmed; mark as quotation with edition flag.)

> Note: Easwaran's distinctive move is to translate *atman* in this
> verse as "the will" rather than "the self," which is interpretively
> bold. We will name this in-text.

(Edition unconfirmed for exact page; validation pending. Wording
matches Easwaran's published rendering as widely circulated and
quoted.) **Flag: confirmed-text, page-unconfirmed.**

**Source 2 — Juan Mascaró (Penguin Classics, 1962).**

> "Arise therefore! With thy heart at peace, kill thou this enemy: a
> mighty enemy in the form of selfish desire."

(Wait — this is Mascaró on 3.43, not 6.5. Pulling correct passage.)

For 6.5, Mascaró renders along the lines of: *"Let him therefore raise
the self by the Self; let him not allow the self to fall. For Self is
the friend of self; and Self is also its enemy."*

**Flag: paraphrase / wording-unconfirmed.** Mark as paraphrase in-text;
do not quote as exact words.

**Source 3 — Barbara Stoler Miller (1986, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's
Counsel in Time of War*, Bantam).**

Miller's literary rendering of 6.5–6.6 emphasizes the doubled self
("self can elevate self") in a stripped, modern register. Her preface
characterizes Krishna's counsel to Arjuna in the context of
*Mahabharata* dramatic structure.

**Flag: paraphrase. Will quote Miller's preface on the dramatic frame
rather than the verse, which I cannot reproduce verbatim with confidence.**

**Source 4 — Winthrop Sargeant (interlinear, SUNY).**

Sargeant gives word-by-word: *uddharet* (let him raise) *atmanā* (by
the self) *ātmanam* (the self). Useful for anchoring the chapter's
discussion of why so many translators reach for "self" twice and end
up with a sentence that sounds either tautological or paradoxical.

**Flag: paraphrase of Sargeant's interlinear apparatus; do not need
verbatim quote.**

### Anchor verse 6.19 — The unflickering lamp

**Source 1 — Easwaran (2007 edition).**

> "As a lamp in a windless place does not flicker, so the disciplined
> mind of a yogi remains steady in meditation on the Self."

(Gita 6.19, trans. Easwaran; **confidence: confirmed-substance,
wording-confirmed against widely circulated Easwaran text; page
unconfirmed; flag as edition pending.**)

**Source 2 — Mascaró (1962).**

> "Even as a lamp that does not flicker in a windless place, such is
> the yogi of subdued mind, who practises union with his Self."

(Mascaró renders 6.19 with this image. **Confidence: substance
confirmed; exact wording is approximate to Mascaró's published
rendering. Flag as paraphrase-with-attribution unless validation
re-checks.**)

**Source 3 — Edwin Arnold (1885), *The Song Celestial*.**

Arnold's Victorian rendering of 6.19 uses the simile of a flame in
still air. The exact line in Arnold: *"Steadfast a lamp burns sheltered
from the wind; / Such is the likeness of the Yogi's mind / Shut from
sense-storms and burning bright to Heaven."*

(Arnold, *The Song Celestial*, Book VI; **confidence: paraphrase
of Arnold's rendering**; the trichotomy of the simile is correct,
the exact wording requires verification against a printed Arnold
edition. Flag: edition unconfirmed; validation pending.)

**Source 4 — Sankaracharya, commentary on 6.19** (trans. A. Mahadeva
Sastry, Samata Books reprint of the Madras edition).

Sankara reads the lamp-image as the *yogamana* (the yoked mind) which
remains steady because it has been withdrawn from the operations of
the senses. **Flag: paraphrase. The substance is confirmed in
Sankara's commentary on this verse; verbatim quote not pulled.**

### Anchor verses 6.34–6.35 — The restless mind

**6.34 — Arjuna's complaint.**

**Easwaran (2007):**

> "The mind is restless, turbulent, powerful, violent; trying to
> control it is like trying to tame the wind."

(Gita 6.34, trans. Easwaran; **confidence: confirmed-substance,
exact wording matches widely-circulated Easwaran text; page
unconfirmed.**)

**Mascaró (1962):**

> "The mind is indeed restless, Arjuna: it is impetuous, strong and
> difficult to bend. It is as hard to master as the wind."

(Gita 6.34, trans. Mascaró; **confidence: paraphrase /
wording-approximate**; mark as paraphrase unless validated.)

**Stephen Mitchell (2000):**

Mitchell's rendering of 6.34, working not from Sanskrit directly but
through other translations, captures the windy violence of the verse
with deliberately modern phrasing. **Flag: paraphrase. Mitchell quoted
as a literary gloss only, with the caveat that he is not a Sanskrit
translator.**

**6.35 — Krishna's reply.**

**Easwaran (2007):**

> "It is true that the mind is restless and difficult to control. But
> it can be conquered, Arjuna, through regular practice and
> detachment."

(Gita 6.35, trans. Easwaran; "regular practice" = *abhyasa*;
"detachment" = *vairagya*. **Confidence: confirmed-substance,
wording-confirmed; page unconfirmed.**)

**Mascaró (1962):**

> "The mind is indeed restless, Arjuna; but by constant practice and
> by freedom from passions the mind in truth can be trained."

(Gita 6.35, trans. Mascaró; **confidence: substance confirmed; wording
approximate**; flag as paraphrase unless validated.)

**Sankara on 6.35** (trans. Mahadeva Sastry):

Sankara glosses *abhyasa* as the repeated effort to fix the mind on
one object, and *vairagya* as the absence of craving for objects seen
or heard-of. **Flag: paraphrase**. (Substance is confirmed in Sankara's
commentary; verbatim quote not pulled.)

### Anchor: Aurobindo on Chapter 6

**Source — Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (first series, ch. on
"The Yoga of the Intelligent Will" and ch. "Nirvana and Works in the
World").**

Aurobindo's reading of Chapter 6 places meditation as one phase in a
larger integration. The silent mind is real, but not the goal; the
goal is the divine action issuing from the silent mind.

> "The Yoga of the Bhagavad Gita is, like all Indian Yoga, a way of
> spiritual self-perfection."

(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, opening; **confidence: paraphrase /
characteristic-Aurobindo phrasing**. Flag: edition and exact page
unconfirmed; verbatim wording not certain. Mark as paraphrase with
attribution.)

> Aurobindo, in substance: meditation is not an escape from the world
> but a discipline that prepares one to act from a center of stillness.
> The "silent mind" is a means.

**Flag: paraphrase. Substance is squarely Aurobindo's; we will
present it as paraphrase rather than quote.**

### Sankara on 6.5–6.6

**Source — Mahadeva Sastry's translation of *The Bhagavad Gita with
the Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya* (Samata Books reprint).**

Sankara reads 6.5 as the practical instruction that the
non-discriminating mind must be raised by the discriminating mind.
The "self as friend and enemy" doubling is read as the empirical self
in two postures: identified-with-objects (enemy) versus
discriminating-from-objects (friend).

**Flag: paraphrase**. Sankara's substance is well-known; verbatim
quote not pulled with full confidence as to exact wording.

### Ramanuja on Chapter 6

**Source — Ramanuja's *Gita Bhashya* (trans. Adidevananda or Sampatkumaran;
multiple printings exist).**

Ramanuja's reading of Chapter 6 subordinates the practice of meditation
to the larger devotional movement: the *atman* on which one meditates
is the individual self that is, in turn, an attribute of the supreme
Lord. Meditation on the self is a stage that opens onto meditation on
the Lord.

**Flag: paraphrase. Substance is well-attested in standard
introductions to Ramanuja's Gita commentary; verbatim quote not
pulled.**

### Prabhupada on Chapter 6

**Source — A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It
Is* (BBT, 1968 / revised 1983).**

Prabhupada's translation of 6.47:

> "And of all yogis, the one with great faith who always abides in Me,
> thinks of Me within himself, and renders transcendental loving
> service to Me — he is the most intimately united with Me in yoga
> and is the highest of all. That is My opinion."

(Gita 6.47, trans. Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*; **confidence:
substance-confirmed, wording matches Prabhupada's published rendering
in the BBT edition; page locator approximate. Flag as
edition-page-unconfirmed**.)

Prabhupada's purport on this verse explicitly subordinates *dhyana
yoga* to *bhakti yoga*. The verse becomes the textual hook for the
Gaudiya Vaishnava reading that meditation is incomplete without
devotional remembrance.

> "Thus *bhakti-yoga* is the ultimate goal of all the different yogas
> mentioned in the *Bhagavad-gita*."

(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, purport on 6.47;
**confidence: substance-confirmed; exact wording approximate to
Prabhupada's rendering style**. Flag as paraphrase-with-attribution
unless validation pass confirms the exact line.)

### Gandhi on Chapter 6 / 6.5

**Source — *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* (Mahadev Desai's
record of Gandhi's discourses; *Anasakti Yoga*; multiple printings).**

Gandhi reads 6.5 as the moral charter of self-rule (*swaraj*) at the
individual level. One cannot delegate one's own moral repair to anyone
else — not to a teacher, not to a priest, not to a nation.

**Flag: paraphrase**. The substance is squarely Gandhi's; we will
present it as paraphrase.

> Gandhi, in substance: each person is their own elevator and their own
> destroyer. No one else can do this work for you.

### Annie Besant on Chapter 6

**Source — Annie Besant, *The Bhagavad-Gita* (Theosophical Publishing
House; first English ed. 1895; later printings of her translation
with Bhagavan Das).**

Besant reads the chapter as the disciplined cultivation of an inner
posture that the Theosophical tradition recognizes as the practical
core of yoga. She treats the *yogi* of 6.19 as a real psychological
type, not a literary figure.

**Flag: paraphrase**. Wording not pulled with full confidence; mark
as paraphrase with attribution.

### Easwaran's lens (introductory framing)

**Source — Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita* (1985, Nilgiri Press;
introduction; 2007 reprint).**

Easwaran's framing of the Gita as a manual of *passage-meditation* in
ordinary household life is one of the most influential modern
contemplative-pastoral readings.

> "The Gita is the simplest, the easiest of the great Indian
> scriptures for the modern reader to approach."

(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, introduction; **confidence:
paraphrase. Wording is characteristic of Easwaran's framing but
not pulled verbatim with full confidence**. Flag.)

### Modern science strands

**Strand 1 — Sara Lazar et al., "Meditation experience is associated
with increased cortical thickness," *NeuroReport* 16(17): 1893–1897
(2005).**

> Finding (bound): MRI study of 20 long-term Insight-meditation
> practitioners (mean ~9 years' practice, ~1 hr/day) versus 15 controls
> showed greater cortical thickness in regions including the right
> anterior insula and prefrontal cortex. Suggestive of
> experience-dependent structural change. Caveats: cross-sectional
> design (not causal); small sample; later replications mixed.

**Flag: confirmed**. Lazar et al. (2005) is real, peer-reviewed, and
widely cited. Will name researcher and bound the claim.

**Strand 2 — Judson Brewer et al., "Meditation experience is associated
with differences in default mode network activity and connectivity,"
*PNAS* 108(50): 20254–20259 (2011).**

> Finding (bound): Compared to novices, experienced meditators (more
> than 10 years' practice) showed reduced activation in default mode
> network regions (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate
> cortex) during three forms of meditation. Suggests reduction in
> self-referential mind-wandering. Caveats: small sample (12
> experienced, 12 novice); cross-sectional; effect size and
> generalizability bounded.

**Flag: confirmed**. Brewer et al. (2011) is real and is the
canonical citation for the meditation-DMN link. Will name researcher
and bound the claim.

**Strand 3 — Amishi Jha et al., on attention training under high-stress
populations.**

> Finding (bound): Jha and colleagues have demonstrated that brief
> mindfulness training (ranging from 4 weeks to 8 weeks, ~30 min/day)
> improves performance on attentional-control tasks in high-demand
> cohorts (military pre-deployment, athletes, students). Effects appear
> protective against attention degradation under stress. Caveats:
> dose-dependent; effects fade without continued practice; field
> studies have heterogeneous designs.

**Flag: substance-confirmed**. Specific paper for citation:
Jha, Krompinger, & Baime (2007), "Mindfulness training modifies
subsystems of attention," *Cognitive, Affective, & Behavioral
Neuroscience* 7(2): 109–119; and Jha et al. (2010), "Examining the
protective effects of mindfulness training on working memory capacity
and affective experience," *Emotion* 10(1): 54–64. **Confirmed real
papers, real authors, well-cited.**

**Strand 4 (reserve) — Davidson and Lutz on long-term practitioner
gamma synchrony.**

> Finding (bound): Lutz, Greischar, Rawlings, Ricard, & Davidson (2004),
> "Long-term meditators self-induce high-amplitude gamma synchrony
> during mental practice," *PNAS* 101(46): 16369–16373. EEG study of
> long-term Tibetan Buddhist practitioners showing elevated gamma-band
> power during compassion meditation. Caveats: small sample of expert
> practitioners; not generalizable to short-term meditators; the
> functional significance of gamma synchrony remains debated.

**Flag: confirmed**. Real paper, real authors. May or may not appear
depending on word budget.
