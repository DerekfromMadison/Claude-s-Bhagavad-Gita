## Phase 1 — Plan

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara on Ch. 10. Lens: non-dualism; the *vibhutis* (glories) are pointers to the one *Brahman* underlying all distinction. Bias: reads the personal-theistic surface as preparatory toward non-dual realization.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja. Lens: qualified non-dualism; the world is the body of the divine; *vibhutis* literally are the glories of a personal Lord whose body is the cosmos. Bias: devotional, theistic.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnava / ISKCON** — Prabhupada. Lens: the personal Krishna is the supreme; vibhutis name his direct presence in things. Bias: strong sectarian framing; literalist.
4. **Activist / Gandhian** — Gandhi (*Anasakti Yoga*). Lens: practical-ethical; the listing teaches the seeker to find God in the best of every kind, training reverence in the world. Bias: ethical and political over metaphysical.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*. Lens: every excellence is a partial self-disclosure of the Divine working through evolutionary nature. Bias: synthesizing, evolutionary.
6. **Literary-universalist** — Mascaró (Penguin Classics, 1962); Barbara Stoler Miller. Lens: read for poetic register, structure, and dramatic shape; the *vibhuti* list as a hymn of particularity.
7. **Comparative-academic** — Radhakrishnan (1948); R.C. Zaehner (1969). Lens: comparative philosophy; the chapter as the meeting of the via negativa and the via affirmativa.
8. **Contemplative-pastoral** — Easwaran. Lens: practical daily devotion; *vibhuti* practice as training of attention.

That gives us roughly seven distinct lenses. The chapter §3 will engage at least five with full quotation; the others appear in the synthesis or framing.

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

1. **Gerard Manley Hopkins** on *inscape* and *instress*. Lens: 19th-century English Catholic poet, Jesuit; coined "inscape" (the distinctive inner form of a thing) and "instress" (the energy by which a thing holds together and presses on the perceiver). His "Pied Beauty" and "As kingfishers catch fire" are explicit hymns to particularity-as-theophany. Direct counterpart to *vibhuti*. **Status: quotable from confirmed published poems.**
2. **Iris Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970). Lens: 20th-century moral philosopher; attention as the central moral act; argues that "love is the perception of individuals" and that just attention to a particular thing is itself moral progress. Direct philosophical counterpart to recognition-of-the-divine-in-particulars.
3. **Simone Weil**, "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God" and "Attention and Will." Lens: French mystic-philosopher; attention as prayer; the singular thing as the place where the eternal touches the temporal.
4. **Charles Taylor**, *A Secular Age* (2007). Lens: Catholic philosopher of secular modernity; on "fullness," disenchantment, and re-enchantment. Useful for naming the modern reader's predicament before the *vibhuti* list.
5. **Marilynne Robinson**, essays (e.g., *The Givenness of Things*, *Mother Country*). Lens: novelist-essayist; theology of the particular and the everyday. Use in modern application.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

1. **Dacher Keltner** on the psychology of awe. Lens: UC Berkeley psychologist; *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder* (2023); Keltner & Haidt's foundational 2003 paper "Approaching awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion" (*Cognition & Emotion*, 17(2): 297–314); his lab's work showing awe expands perceived self-size, increases prosociality, decreases self-focus. Direct fit for §5.
2. **Paul Piff & Dacher Keltner** (2015), "Awe, the small self, and prosocial behavior" (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 108(6): 883–899). Six experiments showing awe reduces entitlement and increases ethical choices.
3. **Michelle Shiota** on awe's cognitive effects. Lens: ASU psychologist; co-author with Keltner; her studies on awe's effect on cognitive accommodation.
4. **Yannick Stephan / Joshua Hicks / others on perceived sacredness and meaning** — to be checked. (Hold lightly.)
5. **Adam Anderson** lab on aesthetic chills / frisson — supporting material on the embodiment of recognition. (Hold lightly.)

Primary science strand: Keltner. Secondary: Piff & Keltner study. That gives two named, bounded strands.

### B. Commentary pulls — the *acharyas* and major moderns

#### B.1. Sankara — Advaita

**Sankara, *Bhagavad Gita Bhashya*, ch. 10**, in **A.G. Krishna Warrier (trans.), *Srimad Bhagavad Gita Bhashya of Sri Sankaracharya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math, Madras**:

- The standard Sankara line on the *vibhutis* is that they are *upalakshana* — illustrative pointers, not exhaustive. The list is meant to *ground meditation*, not catalog a literal list of identities. Sankara repeatedly distinguishes the *Lord's own form* (the Self) from the *vibhutis* (effects), and stresses that meditation on any glory leads back to the Self.

- I will paraphrase Sankara rather than quote him verbatim, because I lack a verified page locator for the precise English translation wording. Mark all Sankara content as `paraphrase` with attribution.

  **Paraphrase, attributed:** *Sankara argues, in substance, that the catalogue of vibhutis is not a doctrine of identity between Krishna and each thing listed but a teaching method: the seeker fixes the mind on what is most excellent in any kind, and through that excellence is led toward the source of all excellence, the one Self. The list is upalakshana — illustrative — and its rhetorical force is propaedeutic.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase` — reflects Sankara's well-documented interpretive moves on Ch. 10 and on similar lists (e.g., *Brihadaranyaka* commentary on the heart). Cited as substance, not as quotation.

#### B.2. Ramanuja — Vishishtadvaita

**Ramanuja, *Gita Bhashya*, ch. 10**, accessed via **Swami Adidevananda (trans.), *Sri Ramanuja Gita Bhasya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math**.

- Ramanuja's central move on Ch. 10: the *vibhutis* are not symbols of a hidden non-dual identity but real glories of a real personal Lord *whose body is the universe*. The verse 10.42 — "I support this entire universe with a single fragment of myself" — is, for Ramanuja, the keystone: the cosmos is the *sharira* (body) of *Bhagavan*, and what we call particular excellences are particular limbs of his body.

- **Paraphrase, attributed:** *Ramanuja argues that the verses do not subordinate the world to a higher unmanifest reality. They affirm it. The vibhutis are real, the world is real, the Lord is real, and the relation between them is the relation of body and embodied self — distinct, yet inseparable, the world being constituted as the Lord's body and the Lord as its inner self.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`. This is the standard Vishishtadvaita reading well-documented in Ramanuja's *Gita Bhashya* and Sri Vaishnava expository tradition.

#### B.3. Madhva — Dvaita

**Madhva, *Bhagavad Gita Bhashya*, ch. 10**, accessed via **B.N.K. Sharma's expositions** and standard Dvaita commentaries.

- For Madhva, the *vibhutis* preserve *bheda* (real distinction): each glory is a *real, distinct excellence* through which the supreme Vishnu manifests *power* without any collapse of distinction. There is no "the world is identical with God." There is a real hierarchy.

- **Paraphrase, attributed:** *Madhva's reading preserves real distinction between the Lord and his glories. To say that the lion among beasts is "of him" is not to say that the lion is Vishnu but that the supreme excellence of any kind discloses the supreme excellence of the supreme Lord, who is real, distinct, and hierarchically over all.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### B.4. Aurobindo — Integral

**Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust, Pondicherry. Standard editions: 1922 (first series), 1928 (second series); collected and reprinted by Sri Aurobindo Ashram (e.g., 1995, 1997 editions).**

- Aurobindo on Ch. 10 (*Essays on the Gita*, second series, ch. on "The Divine Manifestation"). The *vibhutis* are not arbitrary illustrations. They are *partial self-revelations* of the Divine working through Nature in her movement of evolutionary self-disclosure.

- **Direct quote candidate (verify before publishing):**
  > "Each Vibhuti is a sign-post on the way to that supreme realisation; each is a partial revelation given to the soul of man as a guidance to the highest secret of his being."
  - Confidence: `unconfirmed` exact wording. Aurobindo expresses this idea repeatedly in *Essays on the Gita*; the wording above is plausible but I will mark unconfirmed and either re-verify or convert to paraphrase. *(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)*

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Aurobindo argues, in substance, that each vibhuti is a partial self-disclosure of the Divine, a sign-post by which the soul is led from the partial excellence to the totality of which it is a fragment.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`. **Use this in chapter unless the direct quote is verified.**

#### B.5. Gandhi — Anasakti Yoga

**Gandhi, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* / *Anasakti Yoga*. Standard edition: Mahadev Desai's English translation, *The Gita According to Gandhi*, 1946; reprints widely available.**

- Gandhi's reading of Ch. 10 is practical: the *vibhutis* are not a metaphysical doctrine but a discipline of devotion. The seeker is taught to recognize God in the best of every kind, *not in order to map God metaphysically* but in order to train the moral imagination.

- **Quote candidate (verify):**

  - Confidence: `unconfirmed`. This wording is consistent with Gandhi's discursive register but I will mark unconfirmed pending check against Mahadev Desai's published translation.

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Gandhi argues that the chapter's purpose is practical: to teach the devotee to recognize divinity in whatever is good, beautiful, and excellent in the world, and through that recognition to grow in reverence and ahimsa.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`. **Use this in chapter.**

#### B.6. Prabhupada — Gaudiya Vaishnava

**Prabhupada (Bhaktivedanta Swami), *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1968 and subsequent editions.**

- Prabhupada's purport on 10.41 reads as a literal expansion of Krishna's personal presence: the brilliance of the sun, the sound of the syllable A, the gambling of the cheats, all are *direct manifestations of Krishna's potency*. There is no hedging into metaphor.

- **Quote candidate (verify):**

  - Confidence: `unconfirmed` exact wording; Prabhupada's purport on 10.41 contains this kind of formulation. Will mark unconfirmed.

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Prabhupada reads each vibhuti as a literal disclosure of Krishna's opulence. The exceptional and excellent in any category is a direct, ontologically real manifestation of the personal Lord.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### B.7. Radhakrishnan — comparative-philosophical

**Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita: With an Introductory Essay, Sanskrit Text, English Translation, and Notes*, George Allen & Unwin, London, 1948 (and reprints; e.g., HarperCollins India 1993).**

- Radhakrishnan in his commentary on Ch. 10 emphasizes that the chapter is the meeting of via affirmativa (the catalog of positive predications) and via negativa (the unmanifest beyond all predication). The chapter, on Radhakrishnan's reading, refuses the choice between immanence and transcendence.

- **Quote candidate (verify):**
  > "The Supreme cannot be defined by any of His specific manifestations, but each of them indicates the Supreme."
  - Confidence: `unconfirmed`. Plausible Radhakrishnan register; pending verification against the 1948 edition. *(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)*

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Radhakrishnan reads the chapter as a teaching that each particular excellence indicates the divine without exhausting it; the catalog is illustrative rather than definitional, and the verse 10.42 — "I support this entire universe with a single fragment of myself" — restores the unmanifest dimension.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### B.8. Mascaró — literary-universalist

**Juan Mascaró, introduction to *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 1962.**

- Mascaró frames the Gita explicitly as world-literature in dialogue with the Upanishads, the Buddha, and the Christian mystics. His introduction emphasizes the *poetic* register of Ch. 10's catalog.

- **Quote candidate (verify):**
  > "The poetry of the Gita is the poetry of the soul."
  - Confidence: `unconfirmed`. Mascaró's introduction contains this kind of universalist claim. Will paraphrase.

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Mascaró reads Ch. 10's catalogue as a hymn — its rhetorical structure is that of poetry, not of philosophy, and its claim on the reader is the claim a great poem makes: to be received whole, in its cumulative force.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### B.9. Easwaran — contemplative-pastoral

**Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, second edition, Nilgiri Press, 2007. Introduction and chapter introductions throughout.**

- Easwaran reads Ch. 10 as practical training of attention and reverence. His chapter introduction stresses that the long catalog is a *meditation aid* — the seeker is to dwell on the best of each kind until the dwelling itself becomes prayer.

- **Quote candidate (verify):**

  - Confidence: `unconfirmed`. Pending verification.

- **Safer paraphrase:** *Easwaran reads Ch. 10 as a practice: train the eye on the best of each kind until reverence becomes habitual. The chapter for him is closer to a discipline than a doctrine.*
  - Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### B.10. Miller — academic-literary

**Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, Bantam/Columbia University Press, 1986.** Her introduction emphasizes the dramatic and dialogic structure of the Gita; her notes on Ch. 10 engage the literary effect of the catalog as enumeration in service of theophany.

- **Lens note:** *Hopkins (1844–1889) was an English Catholic poet and Jesuit priest. His theological commitments — Scotist, Christ-centred — explicitly shape his aesthetic vocabulary; "inscape" and "instress" are techn-terms he coined. They are theologically loaded but they are also the most precise English vocabulary I know for what the vibhuti chapter is doing.*

#### C.2. Iris Murdoch — attention as moral

- (Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, "The Idea of Perfection," in the standard Routledge Classics edition, p. 33.)
- Confidence: `confirmed`. This is one of the most-quoted sentences in 20th c. moral philosophy and is verified across multiple editions.

> "Love is the perception of individuals. Love is the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real."
- (Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, "The Sublime and the Good" / "The Idea of Perfection," widely quoted, page in Routledge Classics edition c. p. 37 or in "The Sublime and the Beautiful Revisited" — confirm specific source.)
- Confidence: `confirmed` substance; **flag**: Murdoch makes both formulations (the second appears in "The Sublime and the Good" essay, 1959, reprinted in *Existentialists and Mystics*, ed. Conradi, 1997). Cite carefully — locator pending verification across editions.

- **Lens note:** *Iris Murdoch (1919–1999) was a British philosopher and novelist. The Sovereignty of Good (1970) is a slim, highly cited book of three essays arguing against the dominant existentialist-utilitarian moral picture and for a Platonic moral realism in which attention to the particular is itself the central moral act.*

### D. Modern science pulls

#### D.1. Dacher Keltner & Jonathan Haidt (2003) — the structure of awe

**Keltner, D., & Haidt, J. (2003). "Approaching awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion." *Cognition and Emotion*, 17(2), 297–314.**

- Keltner and Haidt argue that awe has two appraisals: (a) *vastness* (the perception that the stimulus is much larger than the self, in scale, social significance, beauty, or power), and (b) *need for accommodation* (the perception that one's existing mental schemas cannot assimilate the stimulus).

- **Quote candidate (verify):**
  > "We define awe as an emotional response to perceptually vast stimuli that overwhelm current mental structures, yet facilitate attempts at accommodation."
  - Confidence: `confirmed` substance; this is the standard summary of Keltner & Haidt 2003, frequently quoted in their later work and in Keltner's *Awe* (2023). Direct wording approximate; verify against the published article. **Flag**: I am presenting this as quotation; if verification fails, downgrade to paraphrase.

- **Lens note:** *Dacher Keltner is professor of psychology at UC Berkeley and director of the Greater Good Science Center. His research program on awe (with Haidt, Piff, Shiota, and others) has been the most sustained empirical investigation of awe as a discrete emotion. The work is bounded: it describes proximate psychological mechanisms and observable behavioral effects; it does not adjudicate metaphysical claims.*

#### D.2. Piff, Dietze, Feinberg, Stancato & Keltner (2015) — awe and prosocial behavior

**Piff, P. K., Dietze, P., Feinberg, M., Stancato, D. M., & Keltner, D. (2015). "Awe, the small self, and prosocial behavior." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 108(6), 883–899.**

- Across six studies, Piff and colleagues found that experiences of awe — induced via standing in a grove of tall trees, recalling an awe-eliciting event, or watching nature footage — were associated with reduced sense of self-importance ("the small self"), increased ethical decision-making, increased generosity in resource allocation, and greater willingness to help.

- The mechanism the authors propose: awe reduces the salience of the self, freeing attention and motivation for collective concerns.

- **Bound:** This is a tightly designed program of correlational and experimental studies on US adults, mostly undergraduates, in laboratory or field settings. The effects are robust within the studies but the long-term persistence and cross-cultural generality are open questions. The studies do not show that awe makes people *good*; they show specific local effects on specific decisions.

- Confidence: `confirmed` for citation, journal, year, and substance of findings.
