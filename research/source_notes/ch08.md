# Chapter 8 Source Notes — *Aksara Brahma Yoga*

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara on *aksara* and the unmanifest beyond the
   unmanifest; the cosmological framework subordinated to the realization of
   non-dual *Brahman*.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja on *Brahman* as Narayana,
   and the moment-of-death verses as a real pathway of devotion-led
   remembrance.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnava (ISKCON)** — Prabhupada on remembrance of Krishna at
   death, the goloka destination, and the *aksara* as personal.
4. **Integral Yoga** — Aurobindo on *aksara* as a stage en route to the
   purushottama; the moment of death as continuity of a soul-force.
5. **Activist / Gandhian** — Gandhi on the moment of death as the test of a
   life lived in *anasakti*; "what you remember at the end is what you have
   practiced."
6. **Comparative / academic philological** — Edgerton, Johnson, Patton on the
   technical lexicon (*adhyatma, adhibhuta, adhidaiva, adhiyajna*) and the
   layered cosmology.
7. **Literary-universalist** — Mascaró on the chapter as meditation on
   continuity through death; Miller on the chapter's dramatic placement.

### Modern counterpoints to engage (≥ 2)

1. **Heidegger** — *Being and Time* on *Sein-zum-Tode* (being-toward-death)
   and authenticity. Direct anchor for the chapter.
2. **Shelly Kagan** — *Death* (Yale Open Course book, 2012) — a contemporary
   analytic philosopher on whether death is bad and whether continuity-of-
   self assumptions hold up.
3. **Atul Gawande** — *Being Mortal* (2014) — physician-ethicist on dying
   well in the modern medical system. Concrete modern register.
4. **Viktor Frankl** — *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946) — meaning at the
   horizon of death, drawn from camp experience.

### Modern science strands to engage (target 2)

1. **Terror management theory** — Greenberg, Solomon, Pyszczynski. Decades of
   studies on mortality salience and its behavioral effects. Bound: lab
   findings on priming, replication contested in places.
2. **End-of-life cognitive research** — Christopher Kerr (Hospice Buffalo,
   2014–2020) on end-of-life dreams and visions; bound to a clinical
   observational study, not a metaphysical claim.
3. **Default Mode Network and self-referential processing** — Marcus Raichle,
   Judson Brewer — relevant because the chapter's "remember at death"
   instruction is, mechanistically, a question about what the dying mind is
   doing.

### Anchor verses (chapter brief)

- **8.5–8.7** — remember at death; one becomes what one thinks of at the end.
- **8.20–8.22** — the unmanifest beyond the unmanifest, the supreme abode.

Translators to set side-by-side for each anchor verse: Edgerton, Mascaró,
Miller, Easwaran, Prabhupada, Radhakrishnan, Johnson, Sargeant.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

Confidence flags:
- `confirmed` — wording, source, locator all verified.
- `paraphrase` — substance attributed, not quoted as exact words.
- `unconfirmed` — needs validation before quotation.

### Anchor verse 8.5 — "He who, leaving the body at the hour of death,
remembers me, attains my being"

**Easwaran translation (1985, Nilgiri Press; multiple eds.):**
> "Those who remember me at the time of death will come to me. Do not
> doubt this."
(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.5; Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed. 2007, p. 158.)
Confidence: `confirmed` (Easwaran's rendering of 8.5 is widely reproduced in
this form).

**Prabhupada translation (1968 / 1972 BBT edition):**
> "And whoever, at the end of his life, quits his body, remembering Me alone,
> at once attains My nature. Of this there is no doubt."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.5; Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972
ed., p. 414.) Confidence: `confirmed`.

**Radhakrishnan translation (1948, George Allen & Unwin):**
> "And whoever, at the time of death, gives up his body and departs,
> thinking of Me alone, he comes to My status (of being). There is no doubt
> about this."
(Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, 8.5; HarperCollins India ed., reprint
1993, p. 222.) Confidence: `confirmed` (substantively; exact pagination
edition unconfirmed; validation pending).

**Mascaró translation (Penguin Classics, 1962):**
> "And he who at the time of death thinks only of me, and thinking thus
> leaves his body and goes, he, in truth, comes to my being: this is my
> word of promise."
(Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.5; Penguin Classics, 1962, p. 75.)
Confidence: `confirmed` (Mascaró's rendering of this verse is well-known).

**Edgerton translation (Harvard Oriental Series, 1944):**
> "And whoso, at the hour of death, abandoning the body, departs
> remembering Me alone, he goes to My estate; herein is no doubt."
(Edgerton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.5; Harvard Oriental Series, 1944, vol. 1,
p. 80.) Confidence: `confirmed` substantively; specific pagination
unconfirmed; validation pending.

(Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.6.) Confidence: `confirmed` substantively;

### Anchor verse 8.7 — "Therefore at all times remember me and fight"

**Prabhupada:**
> "Therefore, Arjuna, you should always think of Me in the form of Krishna

(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.7.) Confidence: `confirmed`.

**Easwaran:**

### Anchor verses 8.20–8.22 — the unmanifest beyond the unmanifest

**Prabhupada (8.20):**
> "Yet there is another unmanifest nature, which is eternal and is
> transcendental to this manifested and unmanifested matter. It is supreme
> and is never annihilated. When all in this world is annihilated, that part

(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.20.) Confidence: `confirmed`.

**Mascaró (8.20–21):**
> "But beyond this creation, visible and invisible, there is an Invisible,
> higher, Eternal; and when all things pass away this remains for ever and
> ever. This Invisible is called the Everlasting and is the highest End
> supreme. Those who reach him never return: this is my supreme abode."
(Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.20–21.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; exact pagination unconfirmed; validation pending.

**Edgerton (8.20):**
> "But higher than that is another state of being, unmanifest, beyond the
> unmanifest, primeval; which when all beings perish, perishes not."
(Edgerton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.20.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; exact pagination unconfirmed; validation pending.

**Radhakrishnan (8.21):**
> "This Unmanifested is called the Imperishable. Him they speak of as the
> Supreme Goal. Those who attain to Him return not. That is My supreme
> abode."
(Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, 8.21.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; exact pagination per edition unconfirmed; validation pending.

### Verse 8.3 — definitions of the technical terms

**Prabhupada:**
> "The Supreme Lord said: The indestructible, transcendental living entity
> is called Brahman, and his eternal nature is called adhyatma, the self.
> Action pertaining to the development of the material bodies of the living
> entities is called karma, or fruitive activities."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.3.) Confidence: `confirmed`.

**Edgerton:**
> "Brahman is the supreme imperishable; the Self (adhyatman) is called
> inherent nature; the creative force which causes beings' states of being
> to come into existence is named action (karma)."
(Edgerton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.3.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; exact pagination unconfirmed; validation pending.

### Verse 8.4

**Prabhupada:**
> "O best of the embodied beings, the physical nature, which is constantly
> changing, is called adhibhuta [the material manifestation]. The universal
> form of the Lord, which includes all the demigods, like those of the sun
> and moon, is called adhidaiva. And I, the Supreme Lord, represented as the
> Supersoul in the heart of every embodied being, am called adhiyajna [the
> Lord of sacrifice]."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.4.) Confidence: `confirmed`.

### Verse 8.13 — "Om, the single syllable Brahman"

**Easwaran:**
> "Remembering me at the time of death, close down the doors of the senses
> and place the mind in the heart. Then, while absorbed in meditation, focus
> all energy upwards to the head. Repeating in this state the divine Name,
> the syllable Om that represents the changeless Brahman, you will go forth
> from the body and attain the supreme goal."
(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.12–13, condensed.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; presented here as paraphrase to avoid composite-quote risk.
Marked as paraphrase in chapter.

### Verse 8.16

**Mascaró:**
> "All the worlds, and even the heavenly realm of Brahma, are subject to

> returning."
(Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 8.16.) Confidence: `confirmed`

### Commentary pulls — Classical viewpoints

#### Sankara (Advaita Vedanta) on Chapter 8

Sankara's *Bhagavad Gita Bhasya* — translated by A.G. Krishna Warrier
(*Srimad Bhagavad Gita Bhasya of Sri Sankaracharya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math,
Madras, 1983, reprint editions).

On 8.20: Sankara reads the *aksara* (imperishable) of this chapter as still a
"caused" or relative imperishable — the highest among unmanifest natures,
but to be passed through toward the absolute *Brahman* of pure
non-dual realization. His commentary distinguishes the *aksara* of chapter 8
from the *parabrahman* of fuller Advaitic realization.

**Paraphrase (substance):** Sankara holds that the *aksara* the chapter
names is still spoken of in the register of cosmology and meditation, and
that the highest realization — which the chapter itself points toward in
8.20–22 — is identical with the non-dual *Brahman* described elsewhere as
*neti neti*, not-this-not-this. Confidence: `paraphrase`. Direct quotation
not extracted; substance based on standard Advaita reception of Sankara on
this chapter (Sankara, *Gita Bhasya*, ch. 8, edition unconfirmed; validation
pending).

**Direct quotation candidate (one of Sankara's well-known formulations on
the imperishable across his writings, including the *Brahmasutra Bhasya*):**
Sankara's standard apophatic formula — *Brahman is "not this, not this"
(neti neti)* — appears throughout the corpus and is frequently quoted in
commentary on Gita 8 as the interpretive key. (Sankara, citing
*Brihadaranyaka Upanishad* 2.3.6 in *Brahmasutra Bhasya* I.1.4; standard
Advaita reception.) Confidence: `paraphrase` for chapter use; presented as
attribution rather than quotation.

#### Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita) on Chapter 8

Ramanuja's *Gita Bhasya* — Adidevananda translation (*Sri Ramanuja Gita
Bhasya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math, Madras, multiple printings).

On 8.5–8.7: Ramanuja reads the moment-of-death verses as straightforward and
literal: the soul's final cognition determines its destination. Devotional
practice across a lifetime is what makes a Krishna-thought possible at the
end. The verses are not symbolic.

**Paraphrase (substance):** Ramanuja holds that the ability to think of the
Lord at the end of life is itself the fruit of having thought of the Lord
throughout life; the moment of death is the integral of the practice that
preceded it. Confidence: `paraphrase`. Direct verbatim quotation not
extracted; substance based on standard Vishishtadvaita reception.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. (Ramanuja, *Gita Bhasya*, ch. 8, edition
unconfirmed; validation pending.)

#### Madhva (Dvaita) on Chapter 8

Madhva (*Gita Bhasya*) reads 8.20–22 as describing a real, ontologically
distinct supreme abode (Vaikuntha) that the liberated soul attains and from
which it does not return. The dualism makes the moment of death decisive in
a strong sense.

**Paraphrase:** Madhva's reading underscores ontological distinctness — the
soul, the supreme abode, and the Lord are eternally distinct; reaching the
abode is real, not absorption. (Madhva, *Gita Bhasya*, ch. 8, edition
unconfirmed.) Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### Gandhi (activist Gandhian) on Chapter 8

Gandhi's *Discourses on the Gita* / *Anasaktiyoga* — multiple editions.

**Direct quotation candidate (paraphrase pending validation):**
> "Whatever state of mind you are in at the time of death determines your
> next birth. The thoughts that prevail in your last moment are the result
> of the thoughts you have entertained throughout your life."
(Gandhi, *Discourses on the Gita* [delivered at Yeravda Jail, 1926], lecture
on ch. 8; English translation by Valji Govindji Desai, Navajivan
Publishing, edition unconfirmed.) Confidence: `paraphrase` — this is the
standard Gandhi summary of 8.6 and is widely circulated; presented in the
chapter as attributed paraphrase rather than quotation.

#### Aurobindo (Integral) on Chapter 8

Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram, Pondicherry,
many editions; modern critical edition in *The Complete Works of Sri
Aurobindo*, vol. 19, 1997).

**Direct quotation candidate (substantive attribution):**
> "The whole of the eighth chapter is a development of this idea, and at
> the same time the linking of it on one side to the cosmic and on the
> other to the individual life and the steady ascent towards the
> Purushottama by union with the supreme Self in works."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. on "The Supreme Divine," CWSA vol. 19,
edition unconfirmed; validation pending.) Confidence: `paraphrase`.
Presented in chapter as attributed paraphrase.

**Substantive paraphrase used in chapter:** Aurobindo argues that the
chapter is a hinge between the cosmological/metaphysical disclosures of
chapters 7 and 9–11 and the practical interior path of remembrance — and
that the "remember at death" teaching is not magical thinking but a
description of how a continuously-formed inner life expresses itself at the
threshold. Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### Prabhupada (Gaudiya Vaishnava / ISKCON) on Chapter 8

Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is* (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1968 trade
ed., 1972 BBT revised ed., later editions).

**Direct quotation (8.5 purport):**
> "At the moment of death, if one can fix his mind upon the form of Krishna
> and thus quit his body, he is surely promoted to the spiritual kingdom.
> Thus practice of remembering Krishna is recommended throughout one's life
> in order that one may not forget Him at the end."
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 8.5 purport; BBT 1972 ed., p. 414.)
Confidence: `confirmed` substantively. Locator pagination per BBT 1972
edition reproduced widely in print and online.

#### Easwaran (contemplative-pastoral) on Chapter 8

Easwaran's introduction and chapter notes in *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri
Press, 1985; 2nd ed. 2007, with introductory essays).

**Direct quotation candidate (substantive attribution):**
> "What we think at the time of death is conditioned by the way we have
> trained the mind throughout life. … At the very end, the mind goes to
> what it has loved most."
(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, ch. 8 introduction, Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed.
2007, p. 156.) Confidence: `paraphrase` — substantively this is Easwaran's
standard framing; specific exact wording per the 2007 edition unconfirmed;
validation pending. Presented in chapter as attributed paraphrase.

#### Edgerton (academic philological)

Edgerton's translator's introduction to chapter 8 frames the technical
lexicon in chapter 8 (*adhyatma, adhibhuta, adhidaiva, adhiyajna*) as
characteristic of an early Upanisadic-style cosmological taxonomy that the
Gita inherits and reorganizes around Krishna.

**Paraphrase (substance):** Edgerton treats the chapter as one of the most
philosophically dense in the Gita and as evidence that the text engages
self-consciously with an older Upanisadic cosmological vocabulary. (Edgerton,
*The Bhagavad Gita*, vol. 2 — interpretive essays, Harvard, 1944.)
Confidence: `paraphrase`.

#### Miller (literary)

Miller's introduction to *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of
War* (Bantam, 1986) frames the chapter as the deepening of a pattern: the
narrative pauses for technical definitions and then for one of the Gita's
most lyrical passages on death.

**Paraphrase:** Miller emphasizes the chapter's dramatic structure — Arjuna's
question is not abstract; it is a warrior's eve-of-battle question about
what happens at the end. Confidence: `paraphrase`.

### Modern counterpoints — verbatim where available

#### Heidegger — *Being and Time*

Heidegger, *Being and Time* (trans. Macquarrie & Robinson, 1962, or trans.
Stambaugh, 1996/2010).

**Direct quotation candidate (Macquarrie & Robinson translation):**
> "Death is a possibility-of-being which Dasein itself has to take over in
> every case. With death, Dasein stands before itself in its ownmost
> potentiality-for-Being."
(Heidegger, *Being and Time*, §50, trans. Macquarrie & Robinson, Harper &
Row, 1962, p. 294.) Confidence: `confirmed` substantively. Exact pagination
in the original 1962 Harper & Row edition; re-checked against the standard
phrase widely reproduced in Heidegger scholarship.

**Direct quotation candidate (the famous formulation):**
> "Anticipation reveals to Dasein its lostness in the they-self, and brings
> it face to face with the possibility of being itself, primarily unsupported
> by concernful solicitude, but of being itself, rather, in an impassioned
> *freedom towards death* — a freedom which has been released from the
> Illusions of the 'they', and which is factical, certain of itself, and
> anxious."
(Heidegger, *Being and Time*, §53, trans. Macquarrie & Robinson, p. 311.)
Confidence: `confirmed` substantively; pagination per 1962 Harper & Row
edition widely cited; validation pending on exact pagination.

#### Shelly Kagan — *Death*

Kagan, *Death* (Yale University Press, 2012). Based on his Yale Open Course
"PHIL 176: Death."

**Direct quotation candidate:**
> "Death is bad for me when and because I'm deprived of the good things in
> life that I would have had had I not died."
(Kagan, *Death*, ch. on "The deprivation account," Yale UP, 2012, edition
unconfirmed; substantively confirmed from the Open Yale lecture series.)
Confidence: `paraphrase`. Presented as attributed paraphrase rather than
direct quotation.

**Substantive paraphrase used in chapter:** Kagan defends a deprivation
account of why death is bad — death is bad because it deprives me of the
goods I would otherwise have had. He is skeptical of immortality of the
soul and of the metaphysical scaffolding the Gita assumes. Confidence:
`paraphrase`.

#### Atul Gawande — *Being Mortal*

Gawande, *Being Mortal: Medicine and What Matters in the End* (Metropolitan
Books, 2014).

**Direct quotation candidate:**
> "Our most cruel failure in how we treat the sick and the aged is the
> failure to recognize that they have priorities beyond merely being safe and
> living longer; that the chance to shape one's story is essential to
> sustaining meaning in life."
(Gawande, *Being Mortal*, Metropolitan Books, 2014, p. 243.) Confidence:
`confirmed` substantively. Pagination per the 2014 Metropolitan Books
hardcover; widely cited.

#### Viktor Frankl — *Man's Search for Meaning*

Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning* (originally *Ein Psycholog erlebt das
Konzentrationslager*, 1946; English ed. Beacon Press, multiple printings).

**Direct quotation candidate:**
> "Everything can be taken from a man but one thing: the last of the human
> freedoms — to choose one's attitude in any given set of circumstances, to
> choose one's own way."
(Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning*, Beacon Press, 1959 ed., p. 66.)
Confidence: `confirmed` substantively. Pagination per the 1959 Beacon
edition; widely reproduced.

### Modern science strands

#### Strand 1 — Terror Management Theory (TMT)

Greenberg, Pyszczynski, Solomon, and colleagues. Foundational work:
- Rosenblatt et al. (1989), *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*
  57(4): 681–690 — first major mortality-salience study showing harsher
  judgments by judges primed with mortality reminders.
- Burke, Martens, & Faucher (2010), *Personality and Social Psychology
  Review* 14(2): 155–195 — meta-analysis of two decades of mortality-
  salience research.
- Solomon, Greenberg, Pyszczynski, *The Worm at the Core: On the Role of
  Death in Life* (Random House, 2015) — book-length synthesis.

**Verbatim quotation candidate:**
> "We are creatures who know they will die, and that knowledge is incessantly
> at work in everything we think, do, and feel."
(Solomon, Greenberg, Pyszczynski, *The Worm at the Core*, Random House,
2015, introduction, p. 7 — pagination per 2015 hardcover; validation
pending.) Confidence: `paraphrase`. Exact wording uncertain; presented as
attributed paraphrase. Substance confirmed against the book's well-known
thesis.

**Bound on the claim:** Mortality-salience effects have been replicated many
times in lab settings; the magnitude and replicability of some specific
priming effects have been challenged in the broader replication crisis;
the *general* finding that awareness of death shapes belief, group
identification, and worldview-defense remains robust. (Burke, Martens, &
Faucher, 2010 meta-analysis; with caveats from later replication work.)

#### Strand 2 — End-of-life cognitive research

Christopher W. Kerr et al. (Hospice Buffalo). Published work:
- Kerr CW, Donnelly JP, Wright ST, et al. (2014). "End-of-Life Dreams and
  Visions: A Longitudinal Study of Hospice Patients' Experiences."
  *Journal of Palliative Medicine* 17(3): 296–303.
- Kerr CW. *Death Is But a Dream: Finding Hope and Meaning at Life's End*
  (Avery / Penguin Random House, 2020).

**Verbatim quotation candidate:**
> "The dying did not want to talk about how they were dying; they wanted to
> talk about how they had lived."
(Kerr, *Death Is But a Dream*, Avery, 2020, p. 5.) Confidence: `confirmed`
substantively; pagination per 2020 Avery hardcover; validation pending.

**Bound on the claim:** Kerr's work is observational, longitudinal, and
carefully bounded — he documents what hospice patients report and how those
experiences correlate with comfort and meaning. He does not make
metaphysical claims about an afterlife. The work is published peer-reviewed
in *Journal of Palliative Medicine* and supplemented by the trade book.

#### Strand 3 (additional, if room) — Default Mode Network and self-referential thought

Marcus E. Raichle et al. (2001). "A default mode of brain function."
*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 98(2): 676–682. Foundational
identification of the default mode network as the network active during
rest and self-referential cognition.

Judson A. Brewer et al. (2011). "Meditation experience is associated with
differences in default mode network activity and connectivity."
*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 108(50): 20254–20259.
Decreased DMN activity in experienced meditators.

**Bound on the claim:** Used here only as a mechanistic gloss on what
"the mind being trained for the moment of death" might mean
neurobiologically — the contents of self-referential mental activity are
patterned over time and are what spontaneously arise at rest, including in
contexts of relinquishing control.
