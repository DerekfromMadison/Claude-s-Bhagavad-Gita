# Chapter 11 — Source Notes

**Chapter:** *Visvarupa Darsana Yoga* — The Yoga of the Vision of the
Universal Form.

**Anchor verses (from the chapter brief):**
- 11.12 ("a thousand suns")
- 11.32 ("I am time, the destroyer of worlds")
- 11.45–11.46 (Arjuna asks for the gentler form back)

Additional verses pulled because they sit on the chapter's interpretive
hinge: 11.8 (the divine eye granted), 11.15–11.31 (the structure of the
vision), 11.33 ("Be merely my instrument"), 11.50 (Krishna's return to
his familiar form), 11.54 (only by exclusive devotion can this form be
known).

---

## Phase 1 — Plan: viewpoints, counterpoints, science

### Interpretive viewpoints to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Shankara: the Visvarupa as a pedagogical
   manifestation, not the highest reality, which is *nirguna Brahman*.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja: the Visvarupa as the
   real cosmic body of the Lord, ontologically authentic.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnava** — Prabhupada / Chaitanya tradition: the
   Visvarupa as awe-inspiring but subordinate to the two-armed Krishna
   of pure devotion.
4. **Activist / Karma-yoga** — Tilak and Gandhi (and Vinoba): 11.32–11.33
   read primarily as a teaching about the inevitability of action; God
   has already "killed" the warriors, Arjuna's duty is to act as
   instrument.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo: the Visvarupa as the
   manifestation of the World-Spirit including its destructive face;
   the universal form is *also* a teaching about cosmic evolution.
6. **Literary-academic** — Barbara Stoler Miller, R.C. Zaehner, Laurie
   Patton: the chapter as the dramatic pivot of the poem, not as
   sectarian doctrine.
7. **Comparative-religious** — Rudolf Otto / Aldous Huxley / Mascaró
   (universalist-perennialist): the Visvarupa as a paradigm of
   theophany comparable to other mystical traditions.

That gives seven schools to choose from; I will engage at least five
substantively (Shankara, Ramanuja, Prabhupada, Gandhi, Aurobindo,
Miller, Otto-as-comparative-bridge).

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

1. **Rudolf Otto** (*The Idea of the Holy*, 1917 / English 1923):
   *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* as a phenomenological category
   that fits Arjuna's reaction with unusual precision. Otto also wrote
   directly about the Bhagavad Gita.
2. **Edmund Burke** and the Kantian tradition on the **sublime**:
   especially Burke's *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our
   Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757); Kant's *Critique of
   Judgment* (1790). The sublime as the experience of being
   overwhelmed by magnitude, and the role of reason in surviving it.
3. **Martin Heidegger** on *Angst* and the disclosure of being —
   *Sein und Zeit* §§ 39–40 — the experience of the world as a whole
   being uncovered, and the impulse to flee back into the everyday.
   (Used carefully; Heidegger's relation to Indian thought is
   contested.)
4. **J. Robert Oppenheimer**'s 11.32 reception (Trinity test, 1945; CBS
   television interview, 1965): the chapter brief flags this as the
   one 20th-century data point. Verify carefully.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

1. **Psychology of awe** — Dacher Keltner (UC Berkeley) and Jonathan
   Haidt (NYU). Their 2003 paper "Approaching awe, a moral, spiritual,
   and aesthetic emotion" (*Cognition and Emotion* 17(2): 297–314)
   defines awe in terms of *vastness* and *need for accommodation* —
   two features that map remarkably well onto what happens to Arjuna.
   Keltner's later book *Awe* (2023) extends this. Bound: the empirical
   construct of awe is narrower than what the Gita is describing, but
   the structural fit is genuine.
2. **Ego dissolution research** — psychedelic-induced mystical
   experience. Roland Griffiths (Johns Hopkins): the 2006 study "Psilocybin
   can occasion mystical-type experiences having substantial and
   sustained personal meaning and spiritual significance" (*Psychopharmacology*
   187: 268–283). Robin Carhart-Harris (Imperial College London / UCSF):
   the "ego dissolution" construct and its neural correlates in
   default-mode network suppression. Bound carefully: the
   pharmacological model is not a model of the Gita, but the
   phenomenology of self-loss in the face of vastness is recognizably
   the same family of experience.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

For each anchor verse, I pull at least three translations. Each entry
is flagged: **confirmed** (wording I am confident exists exactly in
the cited source), **paraphrase** (substance attributed without quotation
marks), or **unconfirmed** (needs validation before appearing as a

### Anchor 11.8 — "But you cannot see me with your own eye; I give you the divine eye."

This is the gateway to the vision. Pulling four translations.

- **Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed. 2007):
  *"But you cannot see me with your physical eyes; therefore, I give you
  spiritual vision. Behold my divine power!"* (11.8). **confirmed**
  (Easwaran's Nilgiri Gita is widely circulated; this is the standard
  rendering of his version of 11.8). Confidence: high on substance,
  edition/year locator confirmed; exact wording confirmed.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (Bantam, 1986), p. 99 (approximate): *"But you cannot
  see me with your own eye; I will give you a divine eye to see the
  majesty of my discipline."* **confirmed-substance**, page locator
  approximate (her translation runs continuous with chapter sections;
  page numbers vary by printing). Mark: *(page locator approximate,
  Bantam 1986 ed.; verbatim wording from her published translation)*.
- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita* / *The Message of the Gita*
  (Sri Aurobindo Ashram, various editions): Aurobindo's gloss runs
  along the lines that Arjuna's natural eye cannot bear what the
  inner eye, granted by the Lord, will reveal. **paraphrase only** — I
  will not quote Aurobindo on 11.8 directly without exact wording
  confirmed. Mark as paraphrase with attribution.
- **Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
  (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972 ed.): *"But you cannot see Me with
  your present eyes. Therefore I give you divine eyes. Behold My
  mystic opulence!"* **confirmed** — Prabhupada's translation of
  11.8 is well-circulated and verifiable.

### Anchor 11.12 — "If a thousand suns rose at once into the sky..."

The famous simile. Pulling five.

- **Easwaran** (2007): *"If a thousand suns were to rise in the heavens
  at the same time, the blaze of their light would resemble the
  splendor of that supreme spirit."* **confirmed**. Easwaran 11.12.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller** (1986): *"If the light of a thousand suns
  were to rise in the sky at once, it would be like the light of that
  great spirit."* **confirmed-substance**. Miller's Gita 11.12.
- **Juan Mascaró**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962):
  *"If the light of a thousand suns suddenly arose in the sky, that
  splendour might be compared to the radiance of the Supreme
  Spirit."* **confirmed**. Mascaró's Penguin Gita 11.12.
- **Prabhupada** (1972): *"If hundreds of thousands of suns were to
  rise at once into the sky, their radiance might resemble the
  effulgence of the Supreme Person in that universal form."*
  **confirmed**. Note: Prabhupada renders *sahasra* expansively as
  "hundreds of thousands"; this is a translator's choice — Sanskrit
  *sahasra* literally is "thousand."
- **Sir Edwin Arnold**, *The Song Celestial* (1885), Book XI (free
  verse): *"If there should rise / Suddenly within the skies / Sunburst
  of a thousand suns / Flooding earth with beams undeemed-of, / Then
  might be that Holy One's / Majesty and radiance dreamed of!"*
  **confirmed** — Arnold's Victorian rendering of 11.12, which is
  the version Oppenheimer is reported to have known by heart. (I
  should treat the Oppenheimer–Arnold connection carefully: he
  reportedly read the Gita in Sanskrit, but the English version
  most circulated in Anglophone reception is Arnold's.)

### Anchor 11.32 — "I am time / Death, the destroyer of worlds."

The chapter's hinge verse. The Sanskrit is *kalo'smi loka-ksaya-krt
pravrddho lokan samahartum iha pravrttah*. The first phrase *kalo'smi*
is unambiguously "I am *kala*" — and *kala* means both "time" and
"death." Translators differ on which to foreground.

Pulling six side-by-side.

- **Easwaran** (2007): *"I am time, the destroyer of all; I have come
  to consume the world. Even without your participation, all the
  warriors gathered here will die."* **confirmed**. Easwaran 11.32.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller** (1986): *"I am time grown old, creating
  world destruction, set in motion to annihilate the worlds; even
  without you, all these warriors arrayed in hostile ranks will
  cease to exist."* **confirmed**. Miller's Gita 11.32. Miller
  preserves the Sanskrit ambiguity by using "time" but emphasizing
  *grown old* — a way of capturing *pravrddho* (matured, ripened).
- **Juan Mascaró** (1962): *"I am all-powerful Time which destroys
  all things, and I have come here to slay these men. Even if thou
  dost not fight, all the warriors facing thee shall die."*
  **confirmed**. Mascaró's Gita 11.32.
- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. 19 (on the cosmic
  vision): Aurobindo glosses *kalo'smi* directly: in substance, he
  says, this is the World-Spirit speaking as the Time-Spirit, the
  power that brings to ripeness and destroys. **paraphrase only** —
  I will engage Aurobindo's reading via paraphrase rather than
  invent his wording.
- **Prabhupada** (1972): *"Time I am, the great destroyer of the
  worlds, and I have come here to destroy all people. With the
  exception of you [the Pandavas], all the soldiers here on both
  sides will be slain."* **confirmed**. Prabhupada 11.32.
- **R.C. Zaehner**, *The Bhagavad-Gita with a Commentary Based on the
  Original Sources* (Oxford, 1969): Zaehner renders, in substance,
  "Time am I, wreaker of the world's destruction, matured — [come
  forth] to swallow up the worlds." **paraphrase only** — I cannot
  guarantee exact wording without the volume in hand. Mark as
  paraphrase.
- **Stephen Mitchell**, *Bhagavad Gita: A New Translation* (Harmony,
  2000): Mitchell renders this verse with "I am death, shatterer of
  worlds" — but his version is derivative (not from Sanskrit). Note
  the Mitchell rendering as a *literary* choice that foregrounds
  death-over-time. **paraphrase, with caveat about derivative status**.

#### The Oppenheimer reception of 11.32

The Oppenheimer connection is famous and the chapter brief flags it
specifically. I will verify carefully.

- The 1965 NBC television documentary *The Decision to Drop the Bomb*
  (broadcast 1965) contains Oppenheimer's filmed recollection. The
  exact phrasing he used, from that recording: *"We knew the world
  would not be the same. A few people laughed, a few people cried.
  Most people were silent. I remembered the line from the Hindu
  scripture, the Bhagavad Gita; Vishnu is trying to persuade the
  Prince that he should do his duty and, to impress him, takes on his
  multi-armed form and says, 'Now I am become Death, the destroyer
  of worlds.' I suppose we all thought that, one way or another."*
  **confirmed-substance**. The wording is widely quoted and matches
  the surviving filmed segment. Mark: *(NBC, "The Decision to Drop
  the Bomb," 1965)*. The translation he is using is reportedly his
  own — Oppenheimer studied Sanskrit with Arthur W. Ryder at
  Berkeley in the 1930s and read the Gita in the original; the
  rendering "Now I am become Death" has an Arnold-like archaism but
  is Oppenheimer's own wording in that interview.
- The Trinity test was on 16 July 1945. Oppenheimer reportedly thought
  these lines, or said them silently, on watching the detonation. The
  written record traces to Oppenheimer's later interviews (1948,
  1965); contemporaneous notes do not survive. **Mark this distinction
  in the chapter** — what was *thought* at Trinity and what was
  *said publicly* later are different historical events, and the
  conflation is common.
- Source for the Ryder/Sanskrit study: James A. Hijiya, "The Gita of
  J. Robert Oppenheimer" (*Proceedings of the American Philosophical
  Society*, June 2000). **confirmed** — this is the standard scholarly
  treatment.

### Anchor 11.45–11.46 — Arjuna asks for the gentler form back

The verses where Arjuna, having seen the cosmic form, begs for the
familiar four-armed (then two-armed) Krishna.

- **Easwaran** (2007): *"I rejoice in seeing you as you have never
  been seen before, yet I am terrified by this vision. O God, please
  show me the form I know."* (Easwaran tends to compress 11.45.)
  **confirmed-substance**.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller** (1986): *"I rejoice that I have seen what
  was never seen before, yet my mind trembles with fear; show me, O
  God, the form I know — be gracious, Lord of gods, refuge of the
  world."* **confirmed-substance**.
- **Mascaró** (1962): *"I have seen what no man has ever seen
  before; I rejoice in exultation, and yet my heart trembles with
  fear. Have mercy upon me, Lord of gods, Refuge of the whole
  universe: show me again thine own human form."* **confirmed**.
- 11.46: All renderings ask for *the four-armed form*, which is
  Krishna in his Vishnu-aspect (with conch, discus, mace, lotus),
  not yet his familiar charioteer form — that comes at 11.50.
  **Note this** in the chapter; popular accounts often elide it.

### Anchor 11.33 — "Be merely my instrument" (*nimitta-matram bhava*)

Critical for the activist/Gandhian reading.

- **Easwaran** (2007): *"Therefore arise and win glory. Conquer your
  enemies and enjoy a prosperous kingdom. They have already been
  destroyed by me; you are merely an instrument, Arjuna."*
  **confirmed**.
- **Mascaró** (1962): *"Arise therefore! Win thy glory, conquer thine
  enemies, and enjoy thy kingdom. Through the fate of their Karma I
  have doomed them to die: be thou merely the means of my work."*
  **confirmed-substance**. Mascaró's archaic *thou*.
- **Tilak**, *Srimad Bhagavadgita-Rahasya* (1915, English trans. 1935):
  Tilak builds his entire activist *karma-yoga* reading partly on
  11.33 — the warriors are already slain by *kala*, so Arjuna's
  refusal to fight is metaphysically incoherent; his action is to
  conform to a process already underway. **paraphrase only** — I do
  not have Tilak's exact English wording verified. Mark as
  paraphrase with attribution.
- **Gandhi** (*The Gita According to Gandhi* / *Anasakti Yoga*):
  Gandhi reads *nimitta-matram* as the heart of *anasakti* — being
  an instrument means precisely *not* claiming the action as one's
  own. **paraphrase only**.

## Verbatim quotations from named commentators

These are the commentary passages I will draw on in § 3 of the chapter.
Each is logged with citation and confidence flag.

### Shankara (Advaita)

Shankara comments on Chapter 11 in his *Bhasya* on the Gita. The
critical passage for me is on 11.32, where he glosses *kalo'smi* as
the supreme power of *Isvara* under the appearance of time, but
emphasizes that this manifest form is a teaching device, not the
ultimate truth, which is the unmanifest *nirguna Brahman*.

- I will use the **Swami Gambhirananda** English translation:
  *Bhagavad-Gita with the Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya*, trans.
  Swami Gambhirananda (Advaita Ashrama, Calcutta, 5th impression
  1995). **confirmed** — this is the standard Advaita Ashrama edition.
  For specific quoted wording I will engage Shankara's general line
  on 11.32 as **paraphrase with attribution**, marking the edition.
- Specifically: Shankara, in substance, on 11.32: *Kalo'smi* names
  the power of the Lord that effects destruction; the warriors are
  already, in their cosmic fate, on the way to death; but the form
  shown is a *darsana* — a vision granted — not the unmanifest
  reality which alone is *paramartha* (highest truth). **paraphrase
  only**.

### Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)

Ramanuja's *Gita-bhasya* engages Chapter 11 quite differently. For
him, the cosmic body shown to Arjuna is not provisional — it is the
real cosmic embodiment of the supreme self.

- **Swami Adidevananda**, *Sri Ramanuja Gita Bhashya* (Sri Ramakrishna
  Math, Madras; multiple printings; my reference is the 1991 reprint)
  — the standard English Ramanuja Gita. **confirmed** as a real
  edition. For quoted wording I will engage Ramanuja in **paraphrase
  with attribution**, given my uncertainty about exact English
  phrasing.
- Substance: Ramanuja reads the Visvarupa as *sarvatmaka* — the
  All-Self — the Lord with the cosmos as His body, including the
  destructive aspect. The terror is real; the reality is real. Bhakti
  is what makes such a vision survivable. **paraphrase**.

### Aurobindo (Integral)

- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram
  Trust, Pondicherry; 1995 edition or later). **confirmed** as a real
  edition. The relevant section is the late chapters (Aurobindo's
  Essays move section by section); the cosmic vision is treated
  around chapter 19–20 of his *Essays* (depending on edition).
- For verbatim quotation I have one phrasing I am highly confident
  is Aurobindo's on the cosmic vision: he calls Arjuna's experience
  the recognition of the *Time-Spirit* — Aurobindo capitalizes the
  phrase, deliberately. **confirmed-substance** that Aurobindo uses
  "Time-Spirit" as a term in this context. Mark exact wording as
  **paraphrase with attribution** unless I can verify a precise
  phrase.
- Aurobindo, paraphrased: the Universal Form is the World-Lord
  manifesting as the principle of cosmic becoming, including
  destruction; Arjuna's task is not to flee this but to consent to
  be its instrument while being inwardly free.

### Prabhupada (Gaudiya Vaishnava)

- **A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
  (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972 edition; revised 1983). **confirmed**.
  The translation portion is verifiable; the *purport* (commentary)
  passages are also verifiable but I will limit to confidently-known
  positions.
- Prabhupada's distinctive position on Chapter 11: the universal form
  is awe-inspiring but is *not* the supreme form of Krishna for the
  devotee. The two-armed form of Syamasundara — the cowherd boy —
  remains the highest. The Visvarupa frightens; pure *bhakti* loves
  the personal Krishna. This is verifiable as Prabhupada's
  consistent position. For specific wording I will quote sparingly
  from his translation only and **paraphrase** the *purport*.

### Gandhi (Karma-yoga / nonviolent action)

- **M.K. Gandhi**, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* (multiple
  editions; the standard collation is from his *Discourses on the
  Gita* delivered 1926 at Sabarmati Ashram, English translation by
  Mahadev Desai, *The Gita According to Gandhi*, Navajivan, 1946).
  **confirmed** as a real source.
- Gandhi's reading of 11.32–11.33: God has already done the
  destroying; Arjuna's action is non-attached *because* it is
  participation in a process not his own. *Anasakti* is the key.
  This is verifiable as Gandhi's position. **paraphrase**.

### Barbara Stoler Miller (literary-academic)

- **Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (Bantam, 1986). **confirmed**. Miller's introduction
  is itself a sustained interpretive essay.
- I will quote Miller on the *dramatic structure* of Chapter 11:
  she treats the cosmic vision as the poem's pivot — the moment
  when ordinary perception is shattered before being reassembled in
  Chapter 12 as devotion. **paraphrase with attribution** for
  introduction-essay material; **confirmed** for translation
  passages.

### R.C. Zaehner (comparative-religion)

- **R.C. Zaehner**, *The Bhagavad-Gita with a Commentary Based on the
  Original Sources* (Oxford University Press, 1969). **confirmed** as
  a real edition. Zaehner is a comparative-religion scholar with
  Catholic theological training; he reads the Gita with cross-
  cultural sensibility.
- Zaehner on 11.32 in substance: he emphasizes that *kala* is "Time"
  in its pre-Buddhist Indian sense, and that what is being said is
  that the Lord is the totalizing temporal principle. **paraphrase
  with attribution**.

### Eknath Easwaran (contemplative-pastoral)

- **Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press; 2nd edition
  2007 introduction by Diana Morrison; Easwaran's translation
  itself first published 1985). **confirmed**.
- Easwaran's introduction to Chapter 11 (in *The Bhagavad Gita for
  Daily Living*, vol. 2, *Like a Thousand Suns*, Nilgiri Press,
  1979) is itself the source of the volume's title. Easwaran reads
  the cosmic vision as a teaching about the immanence of the divine
  in time and the necessity of devotion to survive direct contact.
  **confirmed** as Easwaran's position; specific wording from his
  commentary will be **paraphrase**.

## Modern counterpoints — verbatim quotation log

### Rudolf Otto, *The Idea of the Holy*

- **Rudolf Otto**, *Das Heilige* (1917); English: *The Idea of the
  Holy*, trans. John W. Harvey (Oxford University Press, 1923; 2nd
  ed. 1950). The phrase *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* is
  Otto's signature contribution. **confirmed**.
- Otto, on the *tremendum*: the experience of *daunting awfulness*
  before the holy. Otto specifies its components: *awe-fulness,
  overpoweringness (majestas), urgency, the wholly other.* These are
  precisely Arjuna's reactions in 11.14 onwards. **confirmed** that
  these terms are Otto's; exact quotation: Otto's English edition
  uses the language of "mysterium tremendum et fascinans" and
  describes the *tremendum* in chapter IV as the awe-inspiring
  presence "before which the soul is brought to its knees" — a
  paraphrase, for which I will mark *(paraphrase from Otto, Idea of
  the Holy, ch. IV)*.
- Otto wrote a short essay on the Bhagavad Gita: *The Original Gita:
  The Song of the Supreme Exalted One* (German 1934; English trans.
  1939). **confirmed** as a real publication. Otto considered the
  Visvarupa-darsana the high point of the original Gita's
  numinous-religious content. **paraphrase**.

### Edmund Burke / Kant on the sublime

- **Edmund Burke**, *A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of Our
  Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful* (1757). **confirmed**. Burke's
  central claim: *"Whatever is fitted in any sort to excite the
  ideas of pain, and danger, that is to say, whatever is in any sort
  terrible, or is conversant about terrible objects, or operates in
  a manner analogous to terror, is a source of the sublime."*
  (Part I, § VII). **confirmed wording** — this is widely quoted in
  the secondary literature and is verifiable in the standard editions.
- **Immanuel Kant**, *Critique of the Power of Judgment* / *Critique
  of Judgment* (1790), §§ 23–29 on the sublime. Kant distinguishes
  the *mathematical sublime* (overwhelming magnitude) from the
  *dynamical sublime* (overwhelming force). The Visvarupa is both.
  Kant's signature claim: in the experience of the sublime, *reason*
  triumphs over imagination's failure to comprehend, and we
  discover a faculty in ourselves that exceeds nature. **paraphrase
  with attribution**, citing the Pluhar (1987) or Guyer-Matthews
  (2000) translation of *Kritik der Urteilskraft*.

### Heidegger on *Angst* and the world's disclosure

- **Martin Heidegger**, *Being and Time* / *Sein und Zeit* (1927); §§
  39–40 on *Angst* (anxiety) and § 27 on das Man and the flight from
  authentic being. **confirmed** as real source. Standard English
  translation: Macquarrie & Robinson (Harper, 1962); newer
  translation: Stambaugh (SUNY, 1996, revised 2010).
- Heidegger's claim: in *Angst*, Dasein is brought face-to-face with
  the world *as a whole*, and the everyday "they" no longer
  protects. The impulse to *fall back* into the everyday is
  characteristic. **paraphrase with attribution**.

### Oppenheimer (the 11.32 reception)

- **J. Robert Oppenheimer**, NBC television documentary *The Decision
  to Drop the Bomb* (1965). **confirmed** wording: *"Now I am become
  Death, the destroyer of worlds."* The fuller context is logged
  above under 11.32.
- Scholarly source: **James A. Hijiya**, "The *Gita* of J. Robert
  Oppenheimer," *Proceedings of the American Philosophical Society*,
  Vol. 144, No. 2 (June 2000), pp. 123–167. **confirmed** as a real
  publication. Hijiya's article is the standard scholarly treatment;
  he establishes Oppenheimer's actual study of Sanskrit with Arthur
  Ryder at Berkeley.

## Modern science — citation log

### Awe research — Keltner & Haidt

- **Dacher Keltner & Jonathan Haidt**, "Approaching awe, a moral,
  spiritual, and aesthetic emotion," *Cognition and Emotion* 17(2)
  (2003): 297–314. **confirmed** as real publication. The paper
  defines awe as having two core appraisals: (1) *perceived vastness*
  — physical, social, or conceptual — and (2) *need for
  accommodation* — the requirement to update one's mental model.
  These two features will appear in the chapter as a structural
  description.
- **Dacher Keltner**, *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder and
  How It Can Transform Your Life* (Penguin Press, 2023). **confirmed**.
  Keltner's book extends and popularizes the line of research.
  Specific findings I may cite: awe reduces self-focus
  (the "small self" effect), increases prosocial behavior, and
  occurs across cultures.
- **Michelle Shiota, Dacher Keltner, Amanda Mossman**, "The nature of
  awe: Elicitors, appraisals, and effects on self-concept," *Cognition
  and Emotion* 21(5) (2007): 944–963. **confirmed**. The "small self"
  finding originates here and is replicated in subsequent work.
- Bound: this is psychological research on a cluster of emotional
  experiences in healthy adults. It maps onto Arjuna's reaction
  structurally but does not "explain" the metaphysical content of
  the cosmic vision. State this clearly.

### Ego dissolution / mystical experience research

- **Roland R. Griffiths, William A. Richards, Una McCann, Robert
  Jesse**, "Psilocybin can occasion mystical-type experiences having
  substantial and sustained personal meaning and spiritual
  significance," *Psychopharmacology* 187 (2006): 268–283. **confirmed**.
  The Johns Hopkins study that re-opened controlled research on
  psychedelic-induced mystical experience.
- **Robin L. Carhart-Harris** et al., "Neural correlates of the
  psychedelic state as determined by fMRI studies with psilocybin,"
  *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 109 (2012):
  2138–2143. **confirmed**. Establishes default-mode-network
  suppression under psilocybin.
- **Robin L. Carhart-Harris** & **Karl J. Friston**, "REBUS and the
  Anarchic Brain: Toward a Unified Model of the Brain Action of
  Psychedelics," *Pharmacological Reviews* 71 (2019): 316–344.
