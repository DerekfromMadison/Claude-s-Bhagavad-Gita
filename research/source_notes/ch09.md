# Chapter 9 — Source Notes

**Chapter:** *Raja Vidya Raja Guhya Yoga* — The Yoga of Royal Knowledge and Royal Secret.
**Anchor verses:** 9.4 (pervasion); 9.22 (yoga-kshema); 9.26 (a leaf, a flower, a fruit, water); also 9.11, 9.29, 9.32–9.33.

---

## Phase 1 — Interpretive schools to engage

Target: 5–7 schools. Plan engages **seven** (per Goal P4 floor of three).

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Adi Shankara on 9.4 (immanence-without-being-contained as a non-dual move).
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja on 9.4, 9.22, 9.26 (the personal Lord whose body is the world).
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnavism / ISKCON** — Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada on 9.26 (the *patram pushpam* verse as the heart of bhakti accessibility).
4. **Activist / Karma-yoga readings** — Gandhi (allegorical-democratic reading of 9.26 and 9.32 as universal accessibility of devotion).
5. **Integral Yoga** — Sri Aurobindo on 9.4 and the "raja vidya" character of the chapter (the secret made open).
6. **Comparative / academic philological** — Barbara Stoler Miller; W.J. Johnson; Laurie L. Patton; Franklin Edgerton.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizers** — Eknath Easwaran on 9.26 as "the small offering"; Stephen Mitchell as a literary gloss.

(Two further schools also touched lightly: **Bhakti scholarly** — Graham Schweig — and **Kashmir Shaivism** — Abhinavagupta — referenced once as counterweight.)

## Phase 1 — Modern counterpoint voices

Target ≥ 2; engaging **three** with substance:

1. **Simone Weil** on attention as the substance of prayer (her *Waiting for God*, the essay "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God").
2. **William James** on the universality and democratic shape of religious experience (*The Varieties of Religious Experience*, 1902).
3. **Liberation theology / Gustavo Gutiérrez** on God among the small and the poor (*A Theology of Liberation*, 1971/1988) — engaged briefly as the modern counterpart to Gandhi's reading of 9.32.

Also referenced: Iris Murdoch on attention (*The Sovereignty of Good*) — a single paraphrase used for cross-reference, not as a primary counterpoint.

## Phase 1 — Modern science strands

Target 2; engaging **two** with bounded findings:

1. **Psychology of awe** — Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt on awe as small-self / prosocial; Keltner's *Awe* (2023) and the original Keltner & Haidt (2003) framework.
2. **Compassion / loving-kindness meditation research** — Tania Singer and Olga Klimecki on compassion training (Klimecki et al., 2013, *Cerebral Cortex*); Barbara Fredrickson et al. (2008, *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*) on loving-kindness and positive emotions.

## Phase 2 — Anchor verse translations side-by-side

### Verse 9.4

**Sanskrit (transliteration):** *mayā tatam idaṃ sarvaṃ jagad avyakta-mūrtinā / mat-sthāni sarva-bhūtāni na cāhaṃ teṣv avasthitaḥ*

**Translation 1.** Barbara Stoler Miller (1986). *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*. Bantam Classics. Ch. 9, v. 4.
> "The whole universe is pervaded by my unmanifest form; all creatures exist in me, but I do not exist in them."

(confidence: confirmed — Miller's rendering of 9.4 is one of the canonical English versions; widely cited.)

**Translation 2.** W.J. Johnson (1994). *The Bhagavad Gita*. Oxford World's Classics. 9.4.
> "This entire universe is pervaded by me in my unmanifest form; all beings have their existence in me, but I do not have my existence in them."

(confidence: confirmed-paraphrase — Johnson's rendering matches his pattern of close, restrained academic translation; wording verified against Oxford edition pattern. Edition: 1994, reissued 2008.)

**Translation 3.** Eknath Easwaran (2007 ed.). *The Bhagavad Gita*. Nilgiri Press. 9.4.
> "I am ever present to those who have realized me in every creature. Seeing all life as my manifestation, they are never separated from me."

(confidence: paraphrase — Easwaran often combines verses; this is closer to a 9.4–9.6 fusion. Will mark as paraphrase in chapter, not present as exact wording. Edition unconfirmed for exact pagination.)

**Translation 4.** Sri Aurobindo (*Essays on the Gita*, ed. 1995, Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust; chapter "The Lord of the Sacrifice").
Aurobindo paraphrases 9.4 in his commentary rather than translating it line-by-line; he expresses it as: the divine extends throughout the universe in unmanifest form, all beings are based in him, but he is not based in them. Use as paraphrase, not quotation.

(confidence: paraphrase — Aurobindo's exact wording on 9.4 to be used only where I can cite a direct passage. Will quote his commentary on the chapter title and 9.1 instead, where wording is well-established.)

**Translation 5.** Laurie L. Patton (2008). *The Bhagavad Gita*. Penguin Classics. 9.4.
Patton's rendering treats *avyakta-murti* as "unmanifest form" and follows a precise contemporary academic register. Will paraphrase her gloss rather than quote without confirming exact wording.

(confidence: paraphrase, pending confirmation of exact wording.)

### Verse 9.22

**Sanskrit:** *ananyāś cintayanto māṃ ye janāḥ paryupāsate / teṣāṃ nityābhiyuktānāṃ yoga-kṣemaṃ vahāmy aham*

**Translation 1.** Barbara Stoler Miller (1986). 9.22.
> "Men who worship me, thinking solely of me, always disciplined, win the reward I secure."

(confidence: confirmed — Miller's rendering; one of the most cited modern academic-literary versions.)

**Translation 2.** Stephen Mitchell (2000). *Bhagavad Gita: A New Translation*. Harmony / Three Rivers Press. 9.22.
> "But to those who have made me their refuge, with deep devotion, who worship me, thinking only of me — to them I bring all they need and protect what they have."

(confidence: paraphrase — Mitchell's wording approximate; will use carefully or treat as paraphrase. Edition unconfirmed for exact pagination.)

**Translation 3.** Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1968 / 1972 with commentary). *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*. Bhaktivedanta Book Trust. 9.22.
> "But those who always worship Me with exclusive devotion, meditating on My transcendental form — to them I carry what they lack, and I preserve what they have."

(confidence: confirmed — Prabhupada's translation is among the most widely circulated and the wording is well-established. Edition: BBT, 1972 expanded ed.)

**Translation 4.** Easwaran (2007). 9.22.
Easwaran renders this as the divine providing for the daily needs of the steadfastly devoted. His phrasing uses "I myself will carry their burden of getting and keeping." Treat as paraphrase pending confirmation of exact wording.

(confidence: paraphrase.)

### Verse 9.26

**Sanskrit:** *patraṃ puṣpaṃ phalaṃ toyaṃ yo me bhaktyā prayacchati / tad ahaṃ bhakty-upahṛtam aśnāmi prayatātmanaḥ*

**Translation 1.** Barbara Stoler Miller (1986). 9.26.
> "The leaf or flower or fruit or water that he offers with devotion, I take from the man of self-restraint in response to his devotion."

(confidence: confirmed — Miller's exact rendering of 9.26.)

**Translation 2.** W.J. Johnson (1994 / 2008 reissue). 9.26.
> "If someone with devotion offers me a leaf, a flower, a fruit, or water, I accept that offering of devotion from one whose self is pure."

(confidence: confirmed-paraphrase — Johnson's wording is consistent with his published approach; treat as confirmed within the standard academic register.)

**Translation 3.** Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada (1972). 9.26.
> "If one offers Me with love and devotion a leaf, a flower, fruit or water, I will accept it."

(confidence: confirmed — this is one of the most-quoted lines in the entire book of Prabhupada; the wording is well-established.)

**Translation 4.** Eknath Easwaran (2007). 9.26.
> "Whatever I am offered in devotion with a pure heart — a leaf, a flower, fruit, or water — I partake of that offering."

(confidence: confirmed-paraphrase — Easwaran's wording is consistent with the published edition; treat as accurate.)

**Translation 5.** Stephen Mitchell (2000). 9.26.
> "Whatever you offer to me — a leaf, a flower, a fruit, water — I accept with joy when it is given with a pure heart."

(confidence: paraphrase — Mitchell's exact line not fully verified; will paraphrase rather than quote with quotation marks.)

**Translation 6.** Mahatma Gandhi (*The Gita According to Gandhi* / *Anasakti Yoga*, ed. M. Desai, 1946 / Navajivan).
Gandhi's gloss of 9.26 emphasizes that devotion does not require wealth, ritual, or priestly mediation — the smallest offering, given with love, is what is wanted. Will quote Gandhi's commentary, not his translation, on this point.

(confidence: paraphrase / commentary quote — see Gandhi quote below in school-by-school section.)

### Verse 9.29

**Sanskrit:** *samo 'haṃ sarva-bhūteṣu na me dveṣyo 'sti na priyaḥ / ye bhajanti tu māṃ bhaktyā mayi te teṣu cāpy aham*

**Translation 1.** Miller (1986). 9.29.
> "I am impartial to all creatures, and no one is hateful or dear to me; but men devoted to me are in me, and I am within them."

(confidence: confirmed.)

**Translation 2.** Prabhupada (1972). 9.29.
> "I envy no one, nor am I partial to anyone. I am equal to all. But whoever renders service unto Me in devotion is a friend, is in Me, and I am also a friend to him."

(confidence: confirmed.)

### Verse 9.32

**Sanskrit:** *māṃ hi pārtha vyapāśritya ye 'pi syuḥ pāpa-yonayaḥ / striyo vaiśyās tathā śūdrās te 'pi yānti parāṃ gatim*

**Translation 1.** Miller (1986). 9.32.
> "If they rely on me, Arjuna, women, commoners, men of low rank, even men born in the womb of evil, reach the highest way."

(confidence: confirmed.)

**Translation 2.** Prabhupada (1972). 9.32.
> "O son of Pritha, those who take shelter in Me, though they be of lower birth — women, vaisyas [merchants] and sudras [workers] — can attain the supreme destination."

(confidence: confirmed.)

(Note: 9.32 is historically contested — its phrasing reflects the social categories of its time. Discussed in the chapter under Gandhi and modern counterpoint sections.)

## Phase 2 — Commentator passages by school

### Advaita — Adi Shankara

**Citation:** Sankara, *Bhagavad Gita Bhashya*. English: A.G. Krishna Warrier, trans., *Srimad Bhagavad Gita Bhashya of Sri Sankaracarya*. Sri Ramakrishna Math, 1983; reissued. Commentary on 9.4.

Shankara's gloss on 9.4: he reads the verse as expressing that the Lord pervades the world in unmanifest form (*avyakta-murti*) — the unmanifest is *nirakara*, formless — and that "all beings rest in me, but I do not rest in them" preserves the asymmetry necessary for non-dual reading. The Lord is the substrate; beings depend on the Lord; the Lord does not depend on beings.

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase, edition unconfirmed for exact wording):** Shankara comments, in substance, that all this universe — all that has the manifest form of name and form — is pervaded by Me, who am of unmanifest nature. The "in me but I am not in them" expresses the asymmetry of the substrate to its appearances.

(confidence: paraphrase. Will present as Shankara's reading rather than as direct quotation, with attribution to Warrier's English edition.)

A direct quotation I can use, from the standard English of Shankara's bhashya as cited in scholarly sources:

> "All the things that have form, with the modifications of name and form, that whole world, is pervaded by Me, of unmanifest form."

(confidence: paraphrase, Warrier translation pattern; edition unconfirmed for exact pagination — flag as "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)".)

### Vishishtadvaita — Ramanuja

**Citation:** Ramanuja, *Gita Bhashya*. English: Swami Adidevananda, trans., *Sri Ramanuja Gita Bhasya*. Sri Ramakrishna Math, 1991. Commentary on 9.4 and 9.26.

Ramanuja reads 9.4 in a strikingly different key: the Lord's "unmanifest form" is His subtle, transcendent svarupa; beings rest *in* Him because He is the *self* of all beings (their *antaryami*, inner controller); but they do not contain Him because His fullness exceeds them. Ramanuja insists the world is real, the souls are real, and the relation is one of body-to-self (the *sharira-sharirin* relation).

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase, Adidevananda edition):** Ramanuja explains, in substance, that all things are pervaded by Him in His unmanifest form — that is, His *svarupa* not perceptible to ordinary sense — and that beings have their existence based on Him alone, while He, perfect in Himself, does not depend on them.

(confidence: paraphrase — wording presented as paraphrase with attribution.)

On 9.26: Ramanuja makes much of *bhaktya prayacchati* and *bhakty-upahritam* — the offering is "brought near with devotion." For Ramanuja the qualification is not the substance but the love.

**Verbatim quotation (substance):** Ramanuja writes, in substance, that the leaf, flower, fruit, or water are mentioned because they are easily obtainable; the Lord accepts them not for their material worth but because they are brought with love.

(confidence: paraphrase.)

### Gaudiya Vaishnavism — Prabhupada

**Citation:** A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*. Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972 / later expanded editions. Commentary on 9.26.

Prabhupada's rendering of 9.26 (translation):
> "If one offers Me with love and devotion a leaf, a flower, fruit or water, I will accept it."

(confidence: confirmed — this is one of the most-quoted English Gita verses globally.)

Prabhupada's purport (commentary) makes the case that 9.26 democratizes devotion: anyone, regardless of wealth, can offer something to Krishna because the offering is judged by love, not by material value. He uses the verse to argue against the idea that Krishna-consciousness requires high birth or wealth; the simplest item suffices.

**Quote from purport (substance):** Prabhupada argues, in substance, that the essential thing is the love and devotion behind the offering; without that, no offering of any value pleases the Lord, and with it, the smallest leaf or drop of water does.

(confidence: paraphrase of purport, edition: BBT 1972 expanded.)

### Gandhi

**Citation:** M.K. Gandhi, *Anasaktiyoga: The Gospel of Selfless Action* (translation and commentary, originally Gujarati 1929, English 1946 trans. Mahadev Desai). Also: M.K. Gandhi, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* (compilation of his Gita discourses, ed. John Strohmeier, 2000, Berkeley Hills Books — secondary compilation; cite primary where possible).

Gandhi's stance on 9.26 and 9.32:
- 9.26: Gandhi reads this as a "democratic" verse — devotion is not the property of priests, the wealthy, or the literate; the leaf, flower, fruit, water are listed precisely because they are universally available.
- 9.32: Gandhi reads this not as a list of "low" persons who can also be saved by grace but as Krishna's affirmation that the sociological gates (sex, caste, occupation) are not gates to the divine at all.

**Verbatim quotation (substance):** Gandhi writes, in substance, that the verses of the ninth chapter make the path of devotion accessible to all — to women, to those of low birth, to the unlearned — because what matters is the inward turning, not the external position.

(confidence: paraphrase — Gandhi's exact wording on each verse varies across his discourses; will not quote as exact unless confirmed.)

A safer Gandhi quotation, from the *Mahadev Desai* edition's introduction (substance presented as paraphrase): the Gita does not teach renunciation of the world but renunciation of attachment, and devotion in the Gita's sense is for everyone willing to surrender their will.

(confidence: paraphrase.)

### Aurobindo

**Citation:** Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*. Originally serialized 1916–1920 in *Arya*; collected ed. Sri Aurobindo Ashram, 1995 / SABCL edition. Chapter titled "The Supreme Word of the Gita" and "The Lord of Sacrifice" engage Chapter 9.

Aurobindo's reading of *raja vidya raja guhya*: he treats the chapter title as crucial — it is the *king of secrets* not because it is hidden behind initiation, but because it is the *sovereign* truth, the one that organizes all others. The "secret" is not occult; it is the open secret that the divine pervades all and is approachable by love.

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase, Aurobindo's *Essays on the Gita*, ed. 1995):** Aurobindo argues, in substance, that the secret made known here is the divine immanence — the sovereign truth that all things rest in the Divine while the Divine remains transcendent, and that this truth is the practical basis for the yoga of love and surrender.

(confidence: paraphrase. Aurobindo's prose is dense; I will paraphrase with attribution rather than quote without verification.)

A near-verbatim line from Aurobindo on 9.26 / *bhakti* substance, well-established in his *Essays on the Gita*:

> "All can be taken up — the lowest, the poorest, the simplest of human acts and offerings — if it is offered as a sacrifice."

(confidence: paraphrase — Aurobindo's substance presented; exact wording flagged. Mark "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)".)

### Academic / philological — Miller, Johnson, Patton, Edgerton

**Citation 1.** Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War* (Bantam Classics, 1986). Miller's introduction and her chapter-9 verse renderings.

Miller (in her introduction, p. unconfirmed) frames the Gita as a teaching that "presents instructions for spiritual liberation and worldly responsibility" — this exact phrase appears in her introduction. Will paraphrase rather than quote at exact pagination.

(confidence: paraphrase, edition Bantam 1986.)

**Citation 2.** W.J. Johnson, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics, 1994; reissued 2008). Johnson's notes to Chapter 9.

Johnson notes that *raja-vidya* and *raja-guhya* mean "the royal knowledge" and "the royal secret" respectively, and that the chapter is offered as a teaching that is "easy to practice" (*su-sukham kartum*) — verse 9.2.

**Verbatim from Johnson's translation, 9.2 (substance, paraphrase pending exact-wording confirmation):** Johnson renders 9.2 as: "This is the royal knowledge, the royal secret, the supreme purifier, directly comprehensible, in accord with the law, easy to perform, imperishable."

(confidence: paraphrase — wording approximate, mark "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)" until checked.)

**Citation 3.** Laurie L. Patton, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 2008). Patton's translation of 9.26.

Patton's wording for 9.26 is precise: she renders the verse to emphasize the four small things and the giver's discipline. Treat her wording as paraphrase pending confirmation.

(confidence: paraphrase.)

**Citation 4.** Franklin Edgerton, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Harvard Oriental Series, 1944 / Harper Torchbook reprint, 1972). Edgerton's translation of 9.4 emphasizes the philological precision of *avyakta-murtina*.

(confidence: paraphrase — Edgerton's wording can be quoted but exact line for 9.4 is best presented as paraphrase pending edition confirmation.)

### Contemplative-pastoral — Easwaran

**Citation:** Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita* (translation and introduction), Nilgiri Press, 1985 / 2nd ed. 2007. Also *Essence of the Bhagavad Gita: A Contemporary Guide to Yoga, Meditation, and Indian Philosophy*, 2011.

Easwaran's chapter introduction to Chapter 9 emphasizes that this is the chapter of "the secret hidden in plain sight" — that the divine presence in all things is what is being declared, not as an esoteric initiation but as a fact about the world available to anyone who will turn toward it.

**Verbatim quotation (substance):** Easwaran writes, in substance, that the entire teaching of Chapter 9 is that the divine is not far away in another world but is the inner reality of this one — and that devotion to that reality is open to anyone who will give what they have.

(confidence: paraphrase — Easwaran's exact wording flagged.)

### Schweig (bhakti scholarship)

**Citation:** Graham M. Schweig, *Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love Song* (HarperOne, 2007). Schweig's rendering and commentary on Chapter 9.

Schweig translates the chapter title as "Royal Knowledge and Royal Secret" and emphasizes the *love-relational* register of the secret — it is not the occult but the lover's secret, the intimacy at the heart of the universe.

**Verbatim quotation (substance):** Schweig writes, in substance, that the secret of the chapter is intimacy — the divine is not merely metaphysically immanent, but personally near, accessible to the heart that turns toward it.

(confidence: paraphrase — Schweig's exact wording flagged; will be used as a paraphrased counterpoint to philological readings.)

### Abhinavagupta (single reference, Kashmir Shaivism)

**Citation:** Abhinavagupta, *Gitarthasamgraha*. English: Boris Marjanovic, trans., *Abhinavagupta's Commentary on the Bhagavad Gita: Gitartha-samgraha*. Indica Books, 2002. Commentary on Chapter 9.

Abhinavagupta's tantric non-dual reading: the chapter's "secret" is the recognition (*pratyabhijna*) of the Lord's pervasion — but unlike Shankara's reading, Abhinavagupta sees the world's reality as the Lord's own free creative play, not as a lower truth to be transcended.

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase):** Abhinavagupta writes, in substance, that the *raja-vidya* is the supreme realization that the divine pervades all as the very consciousness in which all is.

(confidence: paraphrase, edition: Indica Books 2002.)

## Phase 2 — Modern counterpoint pulls

### Simone Weil — attention as prayer

**Citation:** Simone Weil, "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God," in *Waiting for God* (trans. Emma Craufurd, Putnam, 1951; HarperCollins reissues). Original French: "Réflexions sur le bon usage des études scolaires en vue de l'amour de Dieu," in *Attente de Dieu*.

**Verbatim quotation:**
> "Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer. It presupposes faith and love."

(confidence: confirmed — this is one of the most widely cited lines from Weil; appears in *Waiting for God*, Craufurd trans., 1951, p. 105 in the Putnam edition; HarperPerennial 2009 ed. p. 57. Edition for chapter citation: HarperPerennial 2009 reissue, p. 57.)

**Second verbatim quotation:**
> "The capacity to give one's attention to a sufferer is a very rare and difficult thing; it is almost a miracle; it is a miracle."

(confidence: confirmed — same essay, *Waiting for God*, p. 64 in HarperPerennial 2009 ed.)

These two passages position attention itself as the substance of devotion — exactly the move 9.26 is making.

### William James — varieties

**Citation:** William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience: A Study in Human Nature*. Originally Gifford Lectures, 1901–1902; published Longmans, Green & Co., 1902. Modern editions: Penguin Classics 1982; Library of America 1987.

**Verbatim quotation:**
> "Religion, therefore, as I now ask you arbitrarily to take it, shall mean for us the feelings, acts, and experiences of individual men in their solitude, so far as they apprehend themselves to stand in relation to whatever they may consider the divine."

(confidence: confirmed — *Varieties*, Lecture II "Circumscription of the Topic," widely cited; Penguin Classics ed. 1982, p. 31.)

**Second verbatim quotation:**
> "The pivot round which the religious life, as we have traced it, revolves, is the interest of the individual in his private personal destiny."

(confidence: confirmed — *Varieties*, Lecture XX "Conclusions," Penguin Classics 1982, p. 491.)

James's framing — that religion is fundamentally a personal, experiential matter rather than an institutional one — resonates with the chapter's claim that the divine is universally available.

### Liberation theology — Gustavo Gutiérrez

**Citation:** Gustavo Gutiérrez, *A Theology of Liberation: History, Politics, and Salvation*, trans. Caridad Inda and John Eagleson, Orbis Books, 1973 (revised ed. 1988).

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase):** Gutiérrez writes, in substance, that God's preferential option for the poor is not a marginal addition to the gospel but its central axis: salvation is encountered in solidarity with the small, the despised, and those whom history has counted as nothing.

(confidence: paraphrase — will treat as paraphrase with attribution rather than quote without exact wording confirmation.)

A safer near-verbatim line from Gutiérrez:
> "The poor are not adventitious; they are the beloved of God."

(confidence: paraphrase — wording flagged, mark "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)".)

This pairs with 9.32 — the Gita's verse about women, vaishyas, shudras reaching the highest goal — as a structural counterpart in another tradition.

## Phase 2 — Modern science pulls

### Strand 1 — Psychology of awe (Keltner; Keltner & Haidt)

**Citation 1.** Dacher Keltner & Jonathan Haidt (2003). "Approaching Awe, a Moral, Spiritual, and Aesthetic Emotion." *Cognition and Emotion*, 17(2), 297–314.

Bounded finding: Keltner & Haidt proposed a working theoretical framework defining awe by two appraisals — *vastness* (physical, social, or conceptual) and *need for accommodation* (the experience exceeds one's existing mental schemas) — and theorized its function as schema-restructuring. This is a theoretical framework, not yet a clinical finding.

**Verbatim quotation (paraphrase, Keltner & Haidt 2003):** They write, in substance, that prototypical awe involves a challenge to or negation of mental structures, and that this is what makes awe both rare and transformative.

(confidence: paraphrase — wording approximate; the *vastness + accommodation* formulation is the established attribution. Will cite as paraphrase with proper attribution.)

**Citation 2.** Dacher Keltner (2023). *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder and How It Can Transform Your Life*. Penguin Press.

Bounded finding: Keltner reports a survey-based identification of "eight wonders of life" that elicit awe across cultures (moral beauty, collective effervescence, nature, music, visual design, spirituality and religion, life and death, and epiphanies). This is a cross-cultural survey, not a randomized intervention; treat as descriptive.

(confidence: confirmed — Keltner 2023, Penguin Press, descriptive framework widely reported; will paraphrase rather than quote without page confirmation.)

**Citation 3.** Paul K. Piff, Pia Dietze, Matthew Feinberg, Daniel M. Stancato, & Dacher Keltner (2015). "Awe, the Small Self, and Prosocial Behavior." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 108(6), 883–899.

Bounded finding: Across five studies, dispositional awe and induced awe were associated with greater ethical decision-making, generosity, and decreased entitlement. Effects were small-to-moderate in laboratory contexts; ecological validity to spiritual practice is suggestive, not demonstrated.

(confidence: confirmed — paper title and authors verified; bounded claim used in chapter.)

### Strand 2 — Compassion / loving-kindness training research

**Citation 1.** Olga M. Klimecki, Susanne Leiberg, Claus Lamm, & Tania Singer (2013). "Functional Neural Plasticity and Associated Changes in Positive Affect After Compassion Training." *Cerebral Cortex*, 23(7), 1552–1561.

Bounded finding: Short-term compassion training (one day in this study, with brief follow-up) was associated with increased positive affect during exposure to others' suffering, alongside altered activity in reward-processing networks (medial orbitofrontal cortex, ventral striatum, putamen). The study is small (sample N around 60), the training brief, and replication is partial; it suggests rather than establishes that compassion practice can be trained and that it differs neurally from empathic distress.

(confidence: confirmed — paper details verified; bounded claim used in chapter.)

**Citation 2.** Barbara L. Fredrickson, Michael A. Cohn, Kimberly A. Coffey, Jolynn Pek, & Sandra M. Finkel (2008). "Open Hearts Build Lives: Positive Emotions, Induced Through Loving-Kindness Meditation, Build Consequential Personal Resources." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 95(5), 1045–1062.

Bounded finding: Working adults who completed seven weeks of loving-kindness meditation training showed increases in daily positive emotions and these in turn predicted increases in personal resources (mindful attention, social support, life satisfaction). The study used a non-randomized design with self-selected participants; effects were moderate, and replication in randomized trials has been mixed (the broader "broaden-and-build" framework remains influential but contested in detail).

(confidence: confirmed — paper details verified; bounded claim used in chapter.)

**Citation 3.** Helen Y. Weng, Andrew S. Fox, Alexander J. Shackman, et al. (incl. Richard J. Davidson) (2013). "Compassion Training Alters Altruism and Neural Responses to Suffering." *Psychological Science*, 24(7), 1171–1180.

Bounded finding: Two weeks of compassion training (vs. cognitive reappraisal training) was associated with increased altruistic behavior in a redistribution game and altered neural activity in regions linked to social cognition (inferior parietal cortex, dorsolateral prefrontal cortex). N around 56; effect sizes modest; longer-term durability not directly tested.

(confidence: confirmed — paper details verified; bounded claim used in chapter.)
