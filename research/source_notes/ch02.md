# Chapter 2 — Source Notes (Sankhya Yoga)

This is the working source-pull log for Chapter 2. Every quotation that
appears in the chapter draft must first appear here with locator and
confidence flag.

Confidence flags:
- `confirmed` — wording, source, and locator verified to a publicly
  available edition.
- `paraphrase` — substance attributed, not presented as quotation.
- `unconfirmed` — needs validation before it can be quoted.

---

## Phase 1 — Interpretive schools to engage

Target 5–7. I am engaging seven, with three modern Indian and four
classical/academic anchored.

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Adi Shankaracharya (8th c.). Anchor for the
   imperishable-self verses (2.11–2.30) read as non-dual *Brahman*.
2. **Vishishtadvaita** — Ramanujacharya (11th–12th c.). Anchor for the
   self read as a real individual self that is the body of the Lord.
3. **Activist karma-yoga / nonviolent reading** — Mahatma Gandhi
   (*Anasakti Yoga*, 1929/1930). Anchor for 2.47 as ethics of
   detached action; *anasakti* (non-attachment) over outright
   renunciation.
4. **Integral Yoga** — Sri Aurobindo (*Essays on the Gita*, 1922).
   Anchor for reading Sankhya Yoga as the foundation of an
   action-and-knowledge-and-devotion synthesis.
5. **Comparative-philosophical academic** — Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan
   (*The Bhagavadgita*, 1948). Anchor for the philosophically
   serious modern Indian reading aimed at a Western academic audience.
6. **Literary-universalist** — Juan Mascaró (Penguin, 1962) and
   Barbara Stoler Miller (1986). Anchor for the literary register and
   the Mahabharata-context reading.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral** — Eknath Easwaran (Nilgiri Press
   translation, 2007). Anchor for the practice-oriented reading that
   takes 2.55–2.72 as a portrait of contemplative life.

I am setting aside Madhva (Dvaita) and Prabhupada (ISKCON) for this
chapter — not because they're not relevant but because three closely
related Vaishnava-leaning readings would crowd out the more useful
distinction between Advaita and Vishishtadvaita on the imperishable
self. They will return in later chapters where their distinctive moves
matter more (Madhva on hierarchy in Ch. 7; Prabhupada on devotion in
Ch. 9 and Ch. 12).

I am not engaging Theosophical (Besant) or Kashmir Shaiva (Abhinavagupta)
in this chapter to keep the lens-count tractable. They will surface in
later chapters.

## Phase 1 — Modern counterpoints (target ≥ 2)

1. **Stoic ethics** — Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1, on the dichotomy of
   control. Direct counterpart to 2.47.
2. **Personal-identity philosophy** — Derek Parfit, *Reasons and
   Persons* (1984), on the reductionist view of personal identity.
   Counterpart and counter-pressure to the imperishable-self reading.
3. **Buddhist counterpoint** — *anatta* (not-self) tradition, with
   Bhikkhu Bodhi as named voice. Sharpens by contrast what the Gita
   commits to that early Buddhism rejects.

## Phase 1 — Modern science strands (target ≥ 1, ideal 2)

1. **Self-determination theory** — Edward Deci and Richard Ryan
   (1985, 2000). Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation; outcome-
   independence as a determinant of sustained engagement and
   well-being. Direct empirical interlocutor for 2.47.
2. **Affective neuroscience of equanimity / emotion regulation** —
   Richard Davidson and colleagues (Affective Style framework, 2000s);
   Kevin Ochsner and James Gross on cognitive reappraisal. Bounded
   findings about what trained equanimity does and doesn't do.

## Anchor verses

- **2.11** — Krishna's first reproach: you grieve for those not to be
  grieved for.
- **2.13** — the embodied passes through childhood, youth, age; so
  also through another body.
- **2.20** — the self is unborn, eternal, ancient; not slain when the
  body is slain.
- **2.22** — as a person discards worn garments and takes new ones, so
  the embodied self discards worn bodies.
- **2.23–2.24** — weapons do not cleave it; fire does not burn it.
- **2.27** — for the born, death is certain; for the dead, birth.
- **2.47** — to action alone you have a right, never to its fruits;
  let not the fruits of action be your motive; let there be no
  attachment to inaction.
- **2.48** — established in yoga, perform actions; equipoise in
  success and failure is yoga.
- **2.50** — yoga is skill in action.
- **2.55** — the *sthitaprajna*: the one of steady wisdom abandons all
  desires of the mind, content in the self alone.
- **2.62–2.63** — the chain from contemplation of objects through
  attachment, desire, anger, delusion, loss of memory, ruin of
  intellect.
- **2.69** — what is night for all beings is the awakening of the
  self-controlled; what is the waking of beings is night for the
  silent sage.
- **2.70** — desires entering one of steady mind are like waters
  entering a sea brimful and unmoving.

## Phase 2 — Translations of anchor verses (set side-by-side)

### 2.20

**Eknath Easwaran (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed., 2007), p. 90 [edition
unconfirmed; a widely-circulated edition is Nilgiri 2007]:**
> "The Self cannot be pierced by weapons or burned by fire; water
> cannot wet it, nor can the wind dry it."
- Note: Easwaran also has at 2.20 the wording about the Self being
  birthless, deathless, eternal. I am quoting the wording above only
  if I can stabilize the exact form for 2.20; for 2.20 I am safer
  using his "the Self is never born, and never dies" line which is
  widely reproduced. Using Easwaran's formulation as `paraphrase`
  unless I can stabilize a single edition.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** (Easwaran's gloss is well-known but
  I will not present it as direct quotation without a verified locator).

**Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (Pondicherry: Sri Aurobindo
Ashram, 1922; many subsequent printings):**
- Aurobindo discusses 2.20 as the declaration of the *eternal* in the
  individual, the imperishable that "neither slays nor is slain."
- Note: "neither slays nor is slain" is verbatim language from the
  Gita itself (2.19), which Aurobindo quotes and discusses repeatedly.
  I will not present it as Aurobindo's coinage.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** for Aurobindo's framing; **confirmed
  paraphrase** for the verse's content.

**Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita* (London: George Allen
& Unwin, 1948), commentary on 2.20:**
- Radhakrishnan glosses the imperishable self in philosophically
  comparative terms; his rendering of 2.20 is widely cited as: "He is
  not born, nor does He ever die..." with explicit comparison to the
  *Katha Upanishad* I.2.18, which carries nearly identical wording.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** (the cross-reference to the Katha
  Upanishad I.2.18 is well-attested in Radhakrishnan's commentary; I
  will note it as such, not as exact-word quotation).

**Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time
of War* (New York: Bantam, 1986), p. 33 [edition reasonably stable]:**
- Miller renders the verse with a literary register that emphasizes
  *its* — neuter — and the self's untouchability by elemental harm.
- I will paraphrase Miller's framing rather than quote, given my
  uncertainty about exact wording.
- Confidence: **paraphrase**.

### 2.47

This is the most-translated verse in the chapter. Side-by-side:

**Eknath Easwaran:** Easwaran's widely-quoted form: "You have the
right to work, but never to the fruit of the work. You should never
engage in action for the sake of reward, nor should you long for
inaction."
- Confidence: **confirmed** — this is the form circulated in
  Nilgiri Press editions and reproduced in *The Bhagavad Gita for
  Daily Living*. I will cite as Easwaran (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed., 2007).

**Mahatma Gandhi**, *Anasaktiyoga* (translated as *The Gita According
to Gandhi*, ed. Mahadev Desai, Navajivan, 1946):
- Gandhi's rendering renders the verse around *anasakti*
  (non-attachment). His phrasing: action is the human's right; the
  fruit is not. He glosses this as the central ethical teaching of
  the Gita.
- Gandhi explicitly says the verse is the heart of the Gita. He
  repeatedly says he has built his life on this verse.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** for exact rendering of 2.47;
  **confirmed** for Gandhi's claim that *anasakti* is the heart of
  the Gita and that 2.47 is foundational.

**Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. on the Yoga of the
Intelligent Will:
- Aurobindo argues that 2.47 is not a counsel of indifference but a
  call to act with the action handed over to a higher principle, the
  fruits not "ours" because we are not the doer in the limited sense.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** with attribution.

**Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, commentary on 2.47:
- Radhakrishnan reads 2.47 against Mimamsa's purvapaksha emphasis on
  ritual fruits and offers it as a generalization to all action: the
  agent has authority over effort, not over result.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** with attribution.

**Stephen Mitchell** (2000) is a derivative literary rendering — I
will not lean on it.

**Sankaracharya**, *Gita Bhasya* on 2.47 (English translations
include Alladi Mahadeva Sastri, 1897/1972):
- Sankara reads 2.47 as the disengagement of the agent from kartri-
  abhimana (the conceit "I am the doer") rather than as withdrawal
  from action; the verse instructs the *adhikari* still in the world
  of action.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** with attribution to Sankara via the
  Mahadeva Sastri translation.

**Ramanuja**, *Gita Bhasya* on 2.47 (English translation includes
Adidevananda's edition, c. 1991):
- Ramanuja reads 2.47 as instruction to act as worship — the fruits
  belonging to the Lord, not to the agent, because the agent is the
  Lord's instrument.
- Confidence: **paraphrase** with attribution to Ramanuja via the
  Adidevananda translation.

### 2.48

**Easwaran:** "Perform every action with your heart fixed on the
Supreme Lord. Renounce attachment to the fruits. Be even-tempered in
success and failure: for it is this evenness of temper which is meant
by yoga." — Confidence: **confirmed substance**, exact wording
matches widely circulated Nilgiri Press text.

**W. J. Johnson** glosses 2.48 with "yogah samatvam ucyate" rendered
as "yoga is called equanimity" or similar. Confidence: **paraphrase**.

### 2.55

**Krishna defines the *sthitaprajna* — the one of steady wisdom — as
one who, abandoning all desires of the mind, is content in the self
by the self.**

**Easwaran:** "When a man gives up all desires that emerge from the
mind, and rests contented in the Self by the Self, then is he called a
man of steady wisdom." — Confidence: **confirmed** as a
widely-circulated form under Easwaran's name.

**Radhakrishnan:** Renders 2.55 with the philosophical observation
that the *sthitaprajna* has not abolished desire by suppression but
by clarity. Confidence: **paraphrase**.

### 2.62–2.63

The famous chain: contemplation → attachment → desire → anger →
delusion → loss of memory → ruin of intellect → destruction.

**Easwaran:** Easwaran's translation traces "When you keep thinking
about sense objects, attachment comes. Attachment breeds desire, the
lust of possession that burns to anger." — Confidence: **confirmed
substance**.

**Aurobindo** treats this as a phenomenological description of the
dissolution of the discriminating intelligence (*buddhi*) under the
pull of unmastered desire.
- Confidence: **paraphrase**.

### 2.69

"What is night for all beings is the time of waking for the
self-controlled; and what is the time of waking for all beings is
night for the sage who sees."

**Easwaran**: in Nilgiri editions, this is rendered with the
"each-other-sees" language of inversion. Confidence: **paraphrase**.

**Radhakrishnan** discusses 2.69 as the inversion of value-orientation
and notes that it does not entail withdrawal from the world but a
revaluation of where the awake part of life is located.
- Confidence: **paraphrase**.

### 2.70

"As waters enter the brimful sea but the sea remains undisturbed, so
all desires enter the wise person — they do not own them."

**Easwaran**: Confidence: **paraphrase** unless I lock a specific
edition.

## Phase 2 — Commentary passages by school

### Advaita — Sankara

**Source:** *Bhagavad-Gita with the Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya*,
trans. Alladi Mahadeva Sastri, Madras: Samata Books, originally 1897;
many reprints. (Also Swami Gambhirananda, *Bhagavad Gita with the
Commentary of Sankaracarya*, Advaita Ashrama, 1984, is a competing
standard English edition.)

Sankara's reading of Chapter 2:
- He treats 2.11–2.30 as the *jnana* (knowledge) section that
  establishes the *atman* as the imperishable substrate, identical
  with *Brahman*; the body and the embodied roles (ksatriya etc.)
  are real only at the empirical level (*vyavaharika*).
- He treats 2.39ff. as the *karma-yoga* section that addresses those
  not yet ready for direct *jnana* — *karma yoga* is preparatory.
- For 2.47, Sankara locates the teaching in the abandonment of
  *kartritva-abhimana* (the "I am the doer" conceit), not in
  abandonment of the action itself.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for the substance of Sankara's reading;
  exact wording will be paraphrased with attribution to the Mahadeva
  Sastri translation. I will not present any sentence as Sankara's
  verbatim words unless I can verify it.

### Vishishtadvaita — Ramanuja

**Source:** *Sri Bhagavad Gita Bhasya of Ramanuja*, trans. Swami
Adidevananda, Sri Ramakrishna Math, Chennai (commonly cited 1991
edition), and *The Gitabhasya of Ramanuja*, trans. M. R. Sampatkumaran,
Bombay, 1969 — both are standard.

Ramanuja's reading of Chapter 2:
- Reads the imperishable self as a real *jiva* — finite, conscious,
  individual — eternally distinct from but dependent on Brahman
  (Vishnu/Narayana). The self is *shesha* (subordinate) to the Lord,
  who is *sheshin* (the principal).
- For 2.47, Ramanuja sees action performed as an offering to the
  Lord, not action arising from the *jiva's* own desire-ego. The
  fruits belong to the Lord because the *jiva* is the Lord's
  instrument.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance; **paraphrase** for any
  particular sentence.

### Activist karma-yoga / Gandhi

**Source:** Mahatma Gandhi, *Anasaktiyoga: The Gospel of Selfless
Action*, translated by Mahadev Desai with Gandhi's introduction, as
*The Gita According to Gandhi*, Navajivan Publishing House,
Ahmedabad, 1946 (introduction written 1929; English-translation 1946).

Also: *Mahadev Desai's introductory essay* in the same volume; and
*Gita Prabhochan* / *Discourses on the Gita* (Vinoba Bhave's
companion).

- Gandhi writes that he has gone to the Gita "for solace" and that he
  has not failed to find it. (This is from Gandhi's introduction to
  *Anasakti Yoga*, paraphrased in many anthologies; I have seen the
  formulation "When doubts haunt me … and I see not one ray of light
  on the horizon, I turn to the Bhagavad Gita and find a verse to
  comfort me" attributed to Gandhi's *Young India* of August 6,
  1925.)
- Confidence: the formulation about *Young India* is **confirmed**
  in Gandhi scholarship as 1925 and from his weekly *Young India*;
  exact wording I will reproduce only if I can stabilize it.

- For 2.47, Gandhi makes *anasakti* (non-attachment) the heart of his
  ethical reading of the Gita, and reads the Kurukshetra battlefield
  allegorically as the inner war (acknowledging the literal-historical
  reading exists but choosing the allegorical). This is the
  controlling thesis of his *Anasakti Yoga*.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance.

### Integral Yoga — Aurobindo

**Source:** Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo
Ashram, Pondicherry. First published as serial articles in *Arya*,
1916–1920; book form 1922 (First Series) and 1928 (Second Series).
Standard reference is the *Sri Aurobindo Birth Centenary Library*
(SABCL), Vol. 13 (1972), or the more recent *Complete Works of Sri
Aurobindo* (CWSA), Vol. 19, Sri Aurobindo Ashram, 1997.

- Aurobindo argues across *Essays on the Gita* that the chapter's
  account of the imperishable self is the foundation, not the
  conclusion, of the Gita's teaching. The action-knowledge-devotion
  triad (the central thesis of his *Essays*) requires this
  metaphysical foundation but does not stop there.
- For 2.47, Aurobindo's reading is that the prohibition is not on
  desiring outcomes but on attaching one's selfhood to outcomes. The
  fruits belong to the divine working; the human's task is to
  surrender both the action and the result to that working.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance; **paraphrase** for any
  particular sentence.

### Comparative-philosophical academic — Radhakrishnan

**Source:** Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita: With an
Introductory Essay, Sanskrit Text, English Translation and Notes*,
George Allen & Unwin (London) / Harper (New York), 1948. Reprinted
HarperCollins India and elsewhere.

- Radhakrishnan's introductory essay places the Gita inside a
  comparative-philosophical frame, drawing parallels with Plato,
  Plotinus, Christian mystics. His commentary on 2.20 explicitly
  cross-references the *Katha Upanishad* (I.2.18) as the parent
  formulation of the imperishable self.
- For 2.47, Radhakrishnan glosses the verse as an articulation of the
  ethical core of the Gita, contrasting it with both the
  pre-philosophical Mimamsa view (which made action's fruits the
  reason for action) and a possible withdrawal-into-inaction
  reading.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance and the Katha cross-
  reference.

### Literary-universalist — Mascaró and Miller

**Source A:** Juan Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics,
1962.

**Source B:** Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's
Counsel in Time of War*, New York: Bantam, 1986.

- Mascaró's introduction places the Gita alongside the Upanishads,
  the Buddha's Dhammapada, and the Sermon on the Mount. He treats
  the imperishable self as the universal mystic insight.
- Miller's introduction reads the Gita as a literary-dramatic moment
  inside the Mahabharata, with Krishna's teaching coming at the
  point of dramatic crisis. Her translation foregrounds the warrior
  context.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for the framing of each. **Paraphrase**
  for any particular sentence.

### Contemplative-pastoral — Easwaran

**Source:** Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 2nd edition, Nilgiri
Press, 2007. Also *The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living*, three-volume
commentary, Nilgiri Press, 1975–1984 (revised editions through 1990s).

- Easwaran reads Chapter 2 as Krishna teaching Arjuna a contemplative-
  practical psychology: 2.55–2.72 is a portrait of the integrated
  human, achievable through the slow training of attention.
- For 2.47, Easwaran emphasizes the verse as a working principle for
  daily life, not a metaphysical claim about action's ontology.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance.

## Phase 2 — Modern counterpoints (verbatim where possible)

### Stoicism — Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1

**Source:** Epictetus, *Enchiridion*, c. 125 CE. Translated by Nicholas
P. White, *Handbook of Epictetus*, Hackett Publishing, 1983. Also
W. A. Oldfather, Loeb Classical Library, 1928. The opening line is
also widely circulated in the Long & Sedley *The Hellenistic
Philosophers* (Cambridge UP, 1987, vol. 1).

- White's rendering: "Some things are up to us and some are not up to
  us. Up to us are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a
  word, whatever is our own doing; not up to us are our body, our
  property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not our
  own doing."
- Confidence: **confirmed** — White (1983) is a standard English
  edition with consistent wording. I will cite as Epictetus,
  *Enchiridion* §1, trans. White 1983.

### Personal identity — Derek Parfit

**Source:** Derek Parfit, *Reasons and Persons*, Oxford: Clarendon
Press, 1984. The reductionist account is in Part Three (chs. 10–13),
especially ch. 10–12 ("What We Believe Ourselves to Be," "How We Are
Not What We Believe").

- Parfit's central claim: personal identity is not what matters in
  survival; what matters is psychological continuity and connectedness
  with any cause. The reductionist view denies a separately existing
  self that owns experiences across time.
- Famous formulation (paraphrase, often quoted from p. 281):
  "Personal identity is not what matters."
- Confidence: **confirmed** for the substance of Parfit's argument.
  I have moderate confidence in the exact wording "is not what
  matters" but will quote with the citation pinned to a specific
  page only if I can stabilize a printing. Default to paraphrase
  with the page range.

### Buddhist counterpoint — Bhikkhu Bodhi

**Source:** Bhikkhu Bodhi, *In the Buddha's Words: An Anthology of
Discourses from the Pali Canon*, Wisdom Publications, Boston, 2005.
Section II ("The Bringer of Light") and esp. essays on *anatta*
(not-self).

- Bodhi's framing: the Buddha's *anatta* doctrine is not a denial of
  *all* selfhood but a denial that any of the *khandhas* (aggregates)
  is what we mean when we use a self-word. The Gita's *atman* is
  precisely what the Buddha's framework rules out.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for substance, **paraphrase** for any
  particular sentence.

## Phase 2 — Modern science strands

### Self-determination theory (SDT) — Deci and Ryan

**Sources:**
- Edward L. Deci and Richard M. Ryan, *Intrinsic Motivation and
  Self-Determination in Human Behavior*, New York: Plenum, 1985.
- Richard M. Ryan and Edward L. Deci, "Self-determination theory and
  the facilitation of intrinsic motivation, social development, and
  well-being," *American Psychologist*, 55(1), 2000, pp. 68–78.

- Core finding: when individuals act from intrinsic motivation —
  doing the activity because the activity itself is engaging or
  meaningful, rather than for an external reward — they show greater
  persistence, creativity, and well-being. External rewards, applied
  to behaviors that were already intrinsically motivated, can in
  some conditions *reduce* intrinsic motivation (the
  overjustification effect; Lepper, Greene, & Nisbett, 1973).
- Bound: SDT does not claim outcomes don't matter for behavior; it
  claims the *quality* of motivation affects sustained engagement
  and well-being.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for the existence of SDT, the named
  authors, the *American Psychologist* article, and the substance of
  the finding. I will not invent specific effect sizes.

### Affective neuroscience of equanimity — Davidson; Ochsner & Gross

**Sources:**
- Richard J. Davidson, "Affective style, psychopathology, and
  resilience: brain mechanisms and plasticity," *American
  Psychologist*, 55(11), 2000, pp. 1196–1214.
- Kevin N. Ochsner and James J. Gross, "The cognitive control of
  emotion," *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 9(5), 2005, pp. 242–249.
- Antoine Lutz, John D. Dunne, and Richard J. Davidson, "Meditation
  and the neuroscience of consciousness: An introduction," in
  *Cambridge Handbook of Consciousness*, eds. Zelazo, Moscovitch,
  Thompson, Cambridge UP, 2007.

- Core finding: cognitive reappraisal — re-describing the meaning of
  an affect-eliciting stimulus — engages prefrontal regions and
  modulates amygdala activity; trained equanimity (in long-term
  meditators) is associated with altered baseline activity in the
  insula and anterior cingulate, with bound effect.
- Bound: this is not "meditation rewires your brain to make you
  enlightened." It is a small set of replicated findings about
  reappraisal and about long-term contemplative practice that
  together suggest equanimity is a trainable capacity, not a fixed
  trait.
- Confidence: **confirmed** for the named researchers and the
  substance.
