# Source Notes — Chapter 17 (Sraddhatraya Vibhaga Yoga)

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive viewpoints to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Adi Shankara on 17.2–17.3: *sraddha* as the
   characteristic disposition that follows from prior conditioning, ultimately
   to be transcended through *jnana*. Lens: non-dualist; reads the chapter as
   stage-setting for the renunciation chapter that follows.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Vaishnava-bhakti** — Ramanuja: *sraddha* as the
   formative orientation toward the divine; the typology of practices is read
   through what kind of devotion they enact.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnava (ISKCON)** — Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada:
   *sraddha* read theologically and sectarianly — the *sattvic* faith is
   already turning toward Krishna; *Om Tat Sat* read as the personal name of
   the Supreme.
4. **Activist / Karma-yoga** — Tilak and Gandhi: faith as the deep stance
   that organizes engaged moral life; food/austerity/gift typology read as
   social and ethical, not merely ritual.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Sri Aurobindo: *sraddha* as the soul's
   gravitational pull toward what it really seeks; the *guna* typology as
   developmental stages within an evolving consciousness.
6. **Comparative / academic philological** — W.J. Johnson, Barbara Stoler
   Miller, Franklin Edgerton, Laurie Patton: text-critical readings; what
   the Sanskrit can and cannot bear; the *Om Tat Sat* formula's ritual
   anchor.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizers** — Eknath Easwaran: *sraddha* as
   what one is willing to stake one's life on; the typology read as a
   framework for self-examination.
8. **Literary-universalist** — Juan Mascaró: faith as the inner orientation
   of the whole person, rendered for a 20th-century reader as a humanist
   psychology of sincerity.

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2; targeting 3)

1. **William James** — *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902); "The
   Will to Believe" (1896). Faith as a live, forced, momentous option; the
   pragmatist ground of belief.
2. **Paul Tillich** — *Dynamics of Faith* (1957). Faith as ultimate concern;
   the structure of faith and the demonic distortion of it; idolatrous
   faith as misplaced ultimacy.
3. **Contemporary philosophy of trust/belief** — Annette Baier on trust
   ("Trust and Antitrust", 1986); Karen Jones on rational trust.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1; targeting 2)

1. **Psychology of values clarification** — Steven Hayes (Acceptance and
   Commitment Therapy / ACT); the values-clarification literature in
   contextual behavioral science. Specifically: the distinction between
   stated values and lived values surfaced through behavior.
2. **Psychology of belief formation and identity** — work on "you are what
   you eat" framings (Paul Rozin's research on disgust, food, and moral
   identity); Roy Baumeister and Kathleen Vohs on self-regulation as a
   limited resource; Carol Dweck on implicit theories (mindset) as a kind
   of operative faith.

### Anchor verses for the chapter

- **17.2–17.3** — "Threefold is the faith of the embodied, born of their
  own nature… A person is what their faith is."
- **17.7** — Threefold is the food preferred by each.
- **17.11–17.13** — Threefold sacrifice (*yajna*).
- **17.14–17.16** — Austerity of body, speech, mind.
- **17.17–17.19** — Threefold austerity.
- **17.20–17.22** — Threefold gift (*dana*).
- **17.23–17.27** — *Om Tat Sat*, the threefold designation of *Brahman*.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### Anchor verse 17.3 ("a person is what their faith is")

The Sanskrit half-line is *yo yacchraddhaḥ sa eva saḥ* — "as is one's faith,
so is one." All translators converge on this remarkable assertion, but their
phrasings reveal much.

**Eknath Easwaran (2007)**, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 2nd ed., Nilgiri Press,
17.3, p. 245: "Faith is the very nature of every human being… Indeed, a
person is his faith; what his faith is, that, verily, he is."
- **Confidence: paraphrase** — Easwaran's rendering is essentially "you are
  what you believe in" but I cannot guarantee this exact wording from the
  Nilgiri edition. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Contemplative-pastoral; aimed at daily practice.

**Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan (1948)**, *The Bhagavadgita*, 17.3, HarperCollins
ed., p. 348 (approx.): "The faith of every individual, O Bharata, is in
accordance with his nature. Man is made up of faith; whatever his faith is,
that, verily, he is." (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of the cadence; the half-line "Man is made up
  of faith; whatever his faith is, that, verily, he is" is the standard
  Radhakrishnan rendering and circulates widely; I am confident in its
  substance but treat the exact comma-by-comma wording as paraphrase until
  validated.
- **Lens note:** Scholar-statesman; comparative philosophy; English Gita
  for an inter-cultural philosophical audience.

**Juan Mascaró (1962)**, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 17.3:
- **Confidence: paraphrase only** — Mascaró typically renders this verse
  as something close to "the faith of each is in accordance with his
  nature; man is made of faith: as his faith is, so he is." I will quote
  Radhakrishnan and paraphrase Mascaró rather than risk misquoting the
  Penguin text. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Literary-universalist mid-20th-century; a humanist
  rendering for the lay Western reader.

**W.J. Johnson (1994)**, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Oxford World's Classics, 17.3:
- **Confidence: paraphrase** — Johnson's restrained academic rendering
  preserves the structural claim ("a person is constituted by his faith");
  I will paraphrase him with attribution. (edition unconfirmed; validation
  pending)
- **Lens note:** Precise academic translation.

**Barbara Stoler Miller (1986)**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
Time of War*, Bantam: 17.3 — Miller renders the chapter with attention to
the *Mahabharata* dramatic context.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** — Miller's phrasing for 17.3 emphasizes that
  faith follows from inherent nature and that "a person consists of his
  faith." Treat as paraphrase. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Literary scholar; emphasizes dramatic-narrative context.

**Stephen Mitchell (2000)**, *Bhagavad Gita: A New Translation*, Three
Rivers Press, 17.3:
- **Confidence: paraphrase** — Mitchell is not from Sanskrit. Treat as
  paraphrase only.
- **Lens note:** Literary; flagged as derivative.

### Anchor verses 17.7–17.10 (food)

The three modes of food (*ahara*) are rendered consistently but with
notable interpretive valence-shifts.

**Easwaran** rendering of 17.8: foods that promote life, vitality, strength,
health, happiness, joy — *sattvic*.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of standard rendering; treat as paraphrase
  unless quoted directly from a verified copy of the Nilgiri edition.

**Prabhupada (1968/1972)**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, BBT, 17.7–17.10. The
*sattvic* food list is rendered with theological framing — foods that
"increase the duration of life" become foods conducive to Krishna
consciousness.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of theological framing; the actual rendering
  of the verse is widely circulated and I can describe but not quote
  precisely without verification.
- **Lens note:** Gaudiya Vaishnava; ISKCON's foundational English Gita;
  highly sectarian, globally distributed.

### Anchor verses 17.23–17.27 (Om Tat Sat)

The triadic formula: *Aum* (the primal sound), *Tat* (That), *Sat*
(Being / the Real). The closing of the chapter ties all preceding
sacrifice/austerity/gift practice to this designation of *Brahman*.

**Radhakrishnan, 17.23:** "OM, Tat, Sat: this is considered to be the
threefold symbol of *Brahman*."
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of standard rendering. Treat the wording
  as paraphrase. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)

**Easwaran, 17.23:** Easwaran typically renders the formula simply as
the threefold name of *Brahman* taught from the beginning.
- **Confidence: paraphrase**.

### Commentary passages

**Adi Shankara, *Gita Bhashya* on 17.3.** Shankara's commentary
characterizes *sraddha* as the disposition (*samskara*-shaped) that an
embodied being expresses through worship and conduct. The substance is:
faith follows from one's own nature (*svabhava*), which is itself shaped
by the *gunas*; therefore one's actions and even one's worship reveal
one's prior conditioning.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution. I will not quote Shankara
  verbatim from the *Bhashya* without textual confirmation.
- **Source:** Shankara's *Gita Bhashya*, Sanskrit text with English
  translation. The Alladi Mahadeva Sastri English translation
  (Samata Books, reprinted 1995) is the standard. (edition unconfirmed;
  validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Advaita Vedanta; non-dualist; reads stage-setting toward
  *jnana*.

**Ramanuja, *Gita Bhashya* on 17.3.** Ramanuja reads *sraddha* as the
inclination of a person toward the appropriate object of worship. The
*sattvic* devotee orients toward the Supreme; the *rajasic* and *tamasic*
toward lesser deities and toward dark powers respectively.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution.
- **Source:** Ramanuja's *Gita Bhashya*, English translation by Adidevananda
  (Sri Ramakrishna Math). (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Vishishtadvaita; qualified non-dualism; Vaishnava-devotional
  register.

**Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, on Chapter 17.** Aurobindo treats
*sraddha* as the soul's deeper assent — what it really, beneath surface
opinion, gravitates toward. He reads the *guna* typology as developmental:
*tamasic* faith is what the dark, inert nature reaches for; *rajasic* is
what the kinetic, ego-driven nature reaches for; *sattvic* is the lighted
upward inclination; and beyond all three is the integral surrender.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution. I will not quote a specific
  page from *Essays on the Gita* without verification, but the substance
  of Aurobindo's reading is well established in his published essays.
- **Source:** Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo Ashram
  Trust, multiple editions. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Integral Yoga; evolutionary reading.

**Mahatma Gandhi, *Discourses on the Gita / Anasakti Yoga*, on
Chapter 17.** Gandhi reads the chapter as confirmation that conduct,
diet, and discipline reveal one's true creed — that words about belief
are cheaper than the testimony of how one lives. The *sattvic* austerity
becomes a confirmation of *ahimsa* and self-restraint.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution.
- **Source:** M.K. Gandhi, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi*
  (compiled from his discourses, ed. John Strohmeier; or *Discourses on
  the Gita*, Navajivan Publishing House). (edition unconfirmed;
  validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Gandhian; nonviolent action; allegorical-moral reading.

**Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, on 17.3.**
Prabhupada reads *sraddha* through ISKCON's framework: faith is the
disposition that determines whether one will accept Krishna as the
Supreme; *sattvic* faith already moves toward Krishna; *rajasic* and
*tamasic* faiths worship demigods or ghosts. The framing is
theologically pre-decided.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of the established interpretive framing;
  the *purport* (Prabhupada's commentary) consistently steers verses
  toward Krishna-supremacy.
- **Source:** *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, complete edition, Bhaktivedanta
  Book Trust, 1972 (revised). (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Gaudiya Vaishnava; sectarian; widely circulated.

**Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living* (3 vols.),
Nilgiri Press, on Chapter 17.** Easwaran's commentary on *sraddha*
returns to a contemplative-pastoral reading: faith is what we are willing
to stake our daily life on, expressed through what we put into our
bodies, what we discipline ourselves toward, and what we give away.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution.
- **Source:** Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living*, vol. 3,
  Nilgiri Press. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)
- **Lens note:** Contemplative-pastoral; daily practice register.

**Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time
of War*, 1986, on Chapter 17.** Miller reads the *guna* typology as a
literary device — Krishna as a teacher classifies practices to give
Arjuna a diagnostic apparatus, not a sectarian doctrine.
- **Confidence: paraphrase** with attribution.
- **Lens note:** Literary scholar; emphasizes dramatic-narrative context.

**Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, 1948, commentary on
Chapter 17.** Radhakrishnan's commentary frames *sraddha* as the
"orientation of one's whole being" — a phrase he uses repeatedly. He
approaches it through both the Sanskrit and a Western philosophical
register (citing James, occasionally Kant).
- **Confidence: paraphrase** of substance; the phrase "orientation of
  one's whole being" is the Radhakrishnan motif but I treat it as
  paraphrase pending validation.
- **Lens note:** Scholar-statesman; bridge to Western philosophy.

### Modern counterpoint sources

**William James, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902).** James
on faith as a live, working hypothesis; his pragmatist account of belief.
Specific text: *Varieties*, Lecture III ("The Reality of the Unseen"),
and "The Will to Believe" essay (1896).
- **Quotable passage from "The Will to Believe":** James's argument that
  faith is justified when an option is *live*, *forced*, and *momentous*
  is the famous structural argument. The exact wording I am most
  confident about: "Our passional nature not only lawfully may, but must,
  decide an option between propositions, whenever it is a genuine option
  that cannot by its nature be decided on intellectual grounds." (James,
  "The Will to Believe", in *The Will to Believe and Other Essays in
  Popular Philosophy*, 1897).
- **Confidence: confirmed** — this is one of the most widely quoted
  sentences in 20th-century American philosophy and the wording is
  stable across editions.
- **Lens note:** American pragmatism; faith analyzed as a working
  hypothesis with practical consequences.

**Paul Tillich, *Dynamics of Faith* (Harper Torchbooks, 1957/1958).**
Tillich's central definition: "Faith is the state of being ultimately
concerned." (Tillich, *Dynamics of Faith*, 1957, ch. 1, p. 1).
- **Confidence: confirmed** — this is the opening sentence of *Dynamics
  of Faith* and is one of the most quoted lines in 20th-century
  Protestant theology.
- **Lens note:** Mid-20th-century Protestant existentialist theology;
  faith reframed as ultimate concern, allowing dialogue with secular
  existentialism.

**Annette Baier, "Trust and Antitrust" (*Ethics*, vol. 96, no. 2,
January 1986, pp. 231–260).** Baier reframes trust as the substrate of
moral relation, distinct from contract.
- **Quotable passage:** "Trust is letting other persons (or institutions)
  take care of something the truster cares about, where such caring-for
  involves some exercise of discretionary powers." (Baier, "Trust and
  Antitrust", 1986, p. 240, approximate.)
- **Confidence: paraphrase** — the substance is Baier's argument; treat
  the exact wording as paraphrase. (edition unconfirmed; validation
  pending)
- **Lens note:** Late-20th-century feminist moral philosophy; trust as
  a moral primitive.

### Modern science sources

**Steven C. Hayes (with Kirk Strosahl, Kelly Wilson), Acceptance and
Commitment Therapy (ACT).** Specifically: the "values clarification"
component of ACT. Reference: *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: The
Process and Practice of Mindful Change*, 2nd ed., 2012, Guilford Press
(Hayes, Strosahl, Wilson). The ACT model holds that lived values are
revealed through behavioral commitments under pressure, not through
declarative statements.
- **Confidence: confirmed** — this is the established ACT model in
  contextual behavioral science. I will name the model and bound the
  claim, not quote a specific page without verification.
- **Lens note:** Contextual behavioral science; functional contextualism;
  empirically supported psychotherapy.

**Paul Rozin's research on food, disgust, and moral identity.** Rozin
(University of Pennsylvania) has documented that food choices function
as identity markers, that "you are what you eat" maps to moral self-
concept, and that disgust extends from food contamination to moral
contamination. Reference: Rozin & Fallon, "A Perspective on Disgust",
*Psychological Review*, 1987; Rozin, Markwith, & Stoess, "Moralization
and Becoming a Vegetarian", *Psychological Science*, 1997.
- **Confidence: confirmed in substance** — Rozin's research program on
  disgust, food, and moralization is well-established. I will cite by
  researcher and bound the finding.
- **Lens note:** Cultural psychology; moral psychology; Penn.

**Carol Dweck on mindset / implicit theories.** Dweck's work on fixed
versus growth mindsets functions as a kind of operative faith — what
one believes about ability shapes effort and outcome. Reference: Dweck,
*Mindset: The New Psychology of Success*, 2006, Random House.
- **Confidence: confirmed** — Dweck's body of work is well-established;
  the popular book and underlying empirical research (with appropriate
  caveats from later replication discussions) are widely available.
- **Lens note:** Stanford social psychology; implicit theories of
  intelligence and ability.
