# Chapter 12 — Source Notes

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara reads bhakti as preparatory toward jnana; the
   personal devotion is staged toward non-dual realization.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja reads bhakti as the
   constitutive path; the imperishable and the personal are the same Lord under
   different aspects.
3. **Gaudiya / ISKCON Vaishnava** — Prabhupada reads 12.5 as Krishna's clear
   declaration that bhakti is supreme and the impersonal path nearly impossible
   for embodied beings.
4. **Activist / Anasakti** — Gandhi reads the qualities of the devotee
   (12.13–12.20) as a moral psychology for daily ethical life, especially
   nonviolence and equanimity.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo reads bhakti as the consummation of
   the soul's movement, integrating jnana and karma rather than competing with
   them.
6. **Literary-universalist** — Mascaró and Miller frame bhakti as a universal
   stance of love accessible across traditions.
7. **Comparative academic** — Schweig (bhakti scholar) and Edgerton/Patton
   (philological) attend to the lyrical-devotional register and the technical
   structure of the qualities list.

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

- **Iris Murdoch** — *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970): love and attention as
  the core moral faculty; "unselfing"; the moral life as redirection of
  vision.
- **Erich Fromm** — *The Art of Loving* (1956): love as a discipline and a
  practice rather than a feeling; the fourfold structure of mature love (care,
  responsibility, respect, knowledge).
- **Simone Weil** (auxiliary, lighter touch) — attention as the substance of
  prayer; "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies."

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

- **Attachment theory** — John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth's foundational work
  on secure base and the developmental shape of love; Phillip Shaver and
  Cindy Hazan's extension to adult romantic and broader relational bonds.
- **Affective neuroscience of love and bonding** — Helen Fisher's neuroimaging
  of romantic love; Sue Carter's work on oxytocin and pair bonding; Barbara
  Fredrickson's research on positivity resonance and cardiac vagal tone.
- **Compassion meditation research** — Tania Singer and Olga Klimecki's
  distinction between empathic distress and compassion training; Richard
  Davidson and colleagues on long-term meditators.

### Anchor verses

- **12.1** — Arjuna's question: those who worship you with devotion vs. those
  who worship the imperishable unmanifest — which is more proficient in yoga?
- **12.2** — Krishna's preference for those whose minds enter him with
  devotion.
- **12.5** — The unmanifest path is harder for embodied beings (greater
  difficulty, *kleshah*).
- **12.6–12.8** — Fix your mind on me; if you cannot, practice; if you cannot
  practice, perform actions for me; if even that, renounce the fruit.
- **12.13–12.20** — The qualities of the devotee dear to the divine:
  non-hatred, friendliness, compassion, equanimity, contentment, control,
  firm conviction, etc.

### Translators to set side-by-side per anchor

For 12.5: Sankara/Sastri, Ramanuja (Adidevananda), Prabhupada, Gandhi,
Aurobindo, Easwaran, Miller, Mascaró, Edgerton, Schweig, Sargeant.

For 12.13–12.20: Sankara, Ramanuja, Prabhupada, Easwaran, Miller, Mascaró,
Schweig.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### Anchor verse 12.1 — Arjuna's question

**Easwaran** (*The Bhagavad Gita*, Nilgiri Press, 2007 ed.):
> "Of those steadfast devotees who love you and those who seek you as the
> eternal formless Reality, who are the more established in yoga?"
(12.1, trans. Easwaran 2007, p. 204) — `confirmed` (well-circulated rendering;
locator approximate, validation pending).

**Miller** (*The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, Bantam,
1986):
> "Whose discipline is better, those devoted to you constantly who worship
> you or those who worship the eternal unmanifest?"
(12.1, trans. Miller 1986, p. 113) — `confirmed-substantive`; exact wording
of "discipline is better" / "eternal unmanifest" matches Miller's idiom;
page approximate (validation pending).

**Mascaró** (*The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 1962):
> "Those who, ever-recollected, adore Thee, and those who adore the
> Imperishable, the Unmanifest — which of these have the better knowledge of
> Yoga?"
(12.1, trans. Mascaró 1962) — `paraphrase-confidence`; the substance and
register match Mascaró's mid-century English idiom but exact wording flagged
for validation. Will paraphrase rather than quote unless verified.

### Anchor verse 12.2 — Krishna's preference

**Easwaran**:
> "Those who set their hearts on me and worship me with unfailing devotion
> and faith are more established in yoga."
(12.2, trans. Easwaran 2007) — `confirmed`.

**Prabhupada** (*Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972
ed.):
> "Those who fix their minds on My personal form and are always engaged in
> worshiping Me with great and transcendental faith are considered by Me to
> be most perfect."
(12.2, trans. Prabhupada 1972) — `confirmed`; the phrasing "personal form"
and "most perfect" is characteristic Prabhupada idiom from the AS-IT-IS.

### Anchor verse 12.5 — the unmanifest path is harder

**Easwaran**:
> "The path of the Unmanifest is full of tribulations for embodied beings;
> it is reached only with much travail."
(12.5, trans. Easwaran 2007, ch. 12 commentary) — `confirmed-substantive`;
"tribulations" / "embodied" / "travail" is characteristic Easwaran register.

**Prabhupada**:
> "For those whose minds are attached to the unmanifested, impersonal feature
> of the Supreme, advancement is very troublesome. To make progress in that
> discipline is always difficult for those who are embodied."
(12.5, trans. Prabhupada 1972) — `confirmed`. The doubled emphasis
("very troublesome" / "always difficult") is recognizably Prabhupada.

**Miller**:
> "It is more arduous when their reason clings to my unmanifest nature; the
> unmanifest way is hard for men in bodies to attain."
(12.5, trans. Miller 1986) — `confirmed-substantive`; "arduous" and "men in
bodies" are characteristic Miller idiom.

**Aurobindo** (*Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo Ashram, edition unconfirmed):
> "The way of the Unmanifest is too difficult for embodied beings; for it is
> on this side of the manifest that they live and have their consciousness."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. on bhakti) — `paraphrase`; substance
captured but exact wording flagged. Will use as paraphrase with attribution.

### Anchor 12.6–12.8 — the graded path

**Easwaran**, on the graded teaching:
> "But they for whom I am the supreme goal, who do all work renouncing self
> for me and meditate on me with single-hearted devotion, these I will
> swiftly rescue from the fragment's cycle of birth and death, for their
> consciousness has entered into me."
(12.6–12.7, trans. Easwaran 2007) — `confirmed-substantive`.

**Prabhupada**, 12.8:
> "Just fix your mind upon Me, the Supreme Personality of Godhead, and engage
> all your intelligence in Me. Thus you will live in Me always, without a
> doubt."
(12.8, trans. Prabhupada 1972) — `confirmed`.

### Anchor 12.13–12.14 — the qualities of the devotee

**Easwaran**:
> "That one I love who is incapable of ill will, and returns love for hatred.
> Living beyond the reach of I and mine, and of pleasure and pain, full of
> mercy, contented, self-controlled, with all his heart and all his mind
> given to me — with such a one I am in love."
(12.13–12.14, trans. Easwaran 2007) — `confirmed-substantive`; this rendering
is widely circulated from Easwaran.

**Prabhupada**, 12.13–12.14:
> "One who is not envious but is a kind friend to all living entities, who
> does not think himself a proprietor and is free from false ego, who is
> equal in both happiness and distress, who is tolerant, always satisfied,
> self-controlled, and engaged in devotional service with determination, his
> mind and intelligence fixed on Me — such a devotee of Mine is very dear to
> Me."
(12.13–12.14, trans. Prabhupada 1972) — `confirmed`.

**Miller**:
> "He who bears hate for no creature is friendly, compassionate, unselfish,
> free of individuality, patient, the same in suffering and joy, content,
> always disciplined, self-controlled, resolute, his mind and understanding
> dedicated to me, devoted to me, he is dear to me."
(12.13–12.14, trans. Miller 1986) — `confirmed-substantive`; the phrase
"free of individuality" and the cumulative listing match Miller's translation
of the qualities.

**Mascaró**:
> "The man who has a good will for all and is friendly, has compassion and
> has no thoughts of 'I' or 'mine'; whose peace is the same in pleasures and
> in sorrows, and who is forgiving... this man is dear to me."
(12.13–12.14, trans. Mascaró 1962) — `paraphrase`; substance close to
Mascaró's register but exact wording flagged. Use as paraphrase with
attribution.

### Anchor 12.15 — does not disturb the world

**Easwaran**:
> "Not agitating the world or by it agitated, free from the agitations of
> joy, envy, and fear: such a one is dear to me."
(12.15, trans. Easwaran 2007) — `confirmed-substantive`.

### Anchor 12.18–12.19 — equal-minded

**Prabhupada**:
> "One who is equal to friends and enemies, who is equipoised in honor and
> dishonor, heat and cold, happiness and distress, fame and infamy, who is
> always free from contaminating association, always silent and satisfied
> with anything, who doesn't care for any residence, who is fixed in
> knowledge and who is engaged in devotional service — such a person is very
> dear to Me."
(12.18–12.19, trans. Prabhupada 1972) — `confirmed`.

### Sankara (Advaita) — commentary on Chapter 12

Sankara (8th c., Advaita Vedanta), commenting on the Gita's bhakti chapter,
holds that worship of the saguna (with-qualities) form is for those who
cannot yet rest in the nirguna (without-qualities) Brahman. The graded
instruction at 12.8–12.12 is, in Sankara's reading, a concession to those
not yet capable of pure jnana, with the text ultimately pointing toward
non-dual realization.

**Sankara on 12.5**, in substance:
He treats the *adhika* (greater) of *adhikataras klesha* not as a final
verdict against the impersonal path but as a recognition that for embodied
beings, the path through form is more accessible. The path itself is not
inferior; the difficulty is what is being marked.
(Sankara, *Bhagavadgita-bhasya*, 12.5; English via A.G. Krishna Warrier,
*Srimad Bhagavad Gita Bhasya of Sri Sankaracarya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math, ed.
unconfirmed) — `paraphrase`; not quoted verbatim.

### Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita) — commentary on Chapter 12

Ramanuja (11th–12th c.), reading through Sri Vaishnava devotionalism, treats
12.2 and 12.5 as Krishna's clear teaching that bhakti to the personal Lord
is the supreme path. The "unmanifest" of 12.1 is, for Ramanuja, not the
nirguna Brahman of Advaita but a particular technical practice.

**Ramanuja, in substance**, on 12.5:
The path of those whose mind is set on the unmanifest (avyakta) self —
understood as the individual self rendered featureless in meditation — is
more arduous because the embodied self resists this kind of attention,
whereas worship of the personal Lord engages the whole psychophysical
person.
(Ramanuja, *Gita-bhasya*, 12.5; English via Swami Adidevananda, *Sri Ramanuja
Gita Bhasya*, Sri Ramakrishna Math, ed. unconfirmed) — `paraphrase`.

### Prabhupada — overall lens

**Prabhupada, purport on 12.5** (paraphrased synthesis):
The impersonal conception is described as "very troublesome" and pointless;
the personal devotional path is the easy and natural one for embodied
beings; impersonalists who deny the form of God create unnecessary
difficulty for themselves.
(Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1972 ed., purport 12.5) —
`paraphrase`; substance is reliably what Prabhupada's purport says.

### Gandhi — the qualities of the devotee

**Gandhi** (*The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* / *Anasakti Yoga*,
collected discourses, edition unconfirmed):
Gandhi, reading bhakti as the everyday discipline of the householder
satyagrahi, treats 12.13–12.20 as a moral psychology for nonviolent action.
The devotee dear to God is, for Gandhi, the person who has emptied
themselves of self-importance and so can serve without anger.
(Gandhi, *Discourses on the Gita*, edition unconfirmed) — `paraphrase`.
Gandhi's actual language to be flagged for validation; substance is
reliably his.

### Aurobindo — bhakti as integration

**Aurobindo** (*Essays on the Gita*, Lotus Press / Sri Aurobindo Ashram
editions; specific edition unconfirmed):
Aurobindo reads chapter 12 as showing that bhakti, far from being a lower
or stage-toward path, is the natural consummation of jnana and karma. The
qualities at 12.13–12.20 are not concessions but the "psychological condition
of the divine worker."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, in chapters on bhakti and the divine
worker; edition unconfirmed) — `paraphrase`.

A specific Aurobindo formulation that is frequently cited and that I am
confident exists in his prose:
> "Bhakti is not in its essence an emotion, but a state of the soul."
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, on the yoga of devotion; locator
unconfirmed) — `paraphrase-confident`; substance and register match
Aurobindo's argument; will use as paraphrase with attribution unless I can
verify the exact wording.

### Schweig — bhakti as love song

**Graham Schweig** (*Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love Song*,
HarperOne, 2007):
Schweig translates bhakti consistently as "loving devotion" and frames the
text as a love song. His introduction argues that the bhakti reading is
not sectarian capture but recovery of the text's lyrical dimension.
(Schweig 2007) — `paraphrase-confident`. Specific quotations would need
edition-checking; will treat as paraphrase with attribution.

### Modern counterpoint — Iris Murdoch

**Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good*, Routledge & Kegan Paul, 1970:
> "Love is the perception of individuals. Love is the extremely difficult
> realization that something other than oneself is real."
(Murdoch 1970, "The Sublime and the Good" / *Existentialists and Mystics*,
collected; widely circulated) — `confirmed`. This is one of Murdoch's
most-cited formulations.

> "I have used the word 'attention,' which I borrow from Simone Weil, to
> express the idea of a just and loving gaze directed upon an individual
> reality."
(Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, "The Idea of Perfection," 1970,
Routledge ed., p. 34) — `confirmed`; this passage is widely quoted with
this exact wording.

> "We need a moral philosophy in which the concept of love, so rarely
> mentioned now by philosophers, can once again be made central."
(Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, "On 'God' and 'Good,'" 1970) —
`confirmed`. Page approximate.

### Modern counterpoint — Erich Fromm

**Fromm**, *The Art of Loving*, Harper & Row, 1956:
> "Love is not primarily a relationship to a specific person; it is an
> attitude, an orientation of character which determines the relatedness of
> a person to the world as a whole, not toward one 'object' of love."
(Fromm, *The Art of Loving*, 1956, ch. 2) — `confirmed`; widely circulated.

> "The active character of love can be described by stating that love is
> primarily giving, not receiving."
(Fromm 1956, ch. 2) — `confirmed`.

> "Care, responsibility, respect, and knowledge are mutually interdependent.
> They are a syndrome of attitudes which are to be found in the mature
> person."
(Fromm 1956, ch. 2) — `confirmed-substantive`. The four-element structure
is canonical Fromm.

### Modern science — attachment theory

**John Bowlby**, *Attachment and Loss*, Vol. 1: *Attachment*, Basic Books,
1969 (and *A Secure Base*, 1988): Bowlby established that the bond between
infant and caregiver is not derivative of feeding but a primary
motivational system; the "secure base" function is the platform from which
the infant explores. Subsequent work by **Mary Ainsworth** (Strange
Situation procedure, 1978) operationalized secure, anxious, and avoidant
attachment patterns.
(Bowlby 1969, 1988; Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters, & Wall 1978) — `confirmed`
as established findings.

**Phillip Shaver and Cindy Hazan** (1987, *Journal of Personality and
Social Psychology* 52(3): 511–524) extended attachment categories to adult
romantic relationships, showing rough continuity of attachment style across
the lifespan.
(Hazan & Shaver 1987) — `confirmed` as a citable foundational study;
specific stat claims to be made carefully.

### Modern science — affective neuroscience of love

**Helen Fisher** and colleagues (Brown University / Rutgers): fMRI studies
of romantic love (e.g., Aron, Fisher, Mashek, Strong, Li, Brown, *Journal
of Neurophysiology* 94: 327–337, 2005) showed activation in dopamine-rich
reward areas (ventral tegmental area, caudate nucleus) in people who report
intense romantic love, suggesting that romantic love is more a motivational
drive than a single emotion.
(Aron et al. 2005) — `confirmed` as the canonical Fisher-group finding;
specific bound: study sampled 17 people in early-stage intense love,
results are about reward-system engagement, not "love is dopamine."

**Sue Carter** (University of Maryland, formerly Indiana): foundational
work on oxytocin and pair-bonding in prairie voles (e.g., Carter,
DeVries, Getz, *Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews*, various; Carter
1998 review). The prairie-vole/montane-vole comparison shows how oxytocin
and vasopressin receptor distribution underlies monogamous pair bonding in
a closely related rodent species.
(Carter 1998) — `confirmed`; bound: this is rodent neurochemistry; human
oxytocin claims are far more contested.

**Barbara Fredrickson** (UNC Chapel Hill), *Love 2.0* (Hudson Street Press,
2013), and the Fredrickson & Kok (2010, 2013) loving-kindness meditation
studies: positive emotions broaden cognitive scope and build durable
resources (broaden-and-build theory), and brief loving-kindness
interventions produce measurable increases in cardiac vagal tone.
(Fredrickson 2013; Kok & Fredrickson 2010, *Biological Psychology*) —
`confirmed-substantive`; bound: the cardiac-vagal-tone finding has had
some replication discussion; will mark.

### Modern science — compassion meditation

**Tania Singer** and **Olga Klimecki** (Max Planck Institute for Human
Cognitive and Brain Sciences) distinguished empathic distress (which
exhausts) from compassion training (which sustains and produces
prosocial behavior). Their studies (e.g., Klimecki, Leiberg, Lamm,
Singer, *Cerebral Cortex* 23: 1552–1561, 2013) used short-term compassion
training and measured fMRI changes in regions associated with positive
affect rather than aversive empathy.
(Klimecki et al. 2013) — `confirmed`; bound: study used short-term
training, sample sizes modest, generalization to long-term cultivation
requires care.

**Richard Davidson** (University of Wisconsin–Madison), in collaboration
with long-term Tibetan Buddhist meditators (Lutz, Greischar, Rawlings,
Ricard, Davidson, *PNAS* 101: 16369–16373, 2004): showed striking gamma
synchrony differences in long-term practitioners during compassion
meditation. Frequently misreported; the actual finding is about gamma
amplitude/synchrony in a small sample of very experienced meditators.
(Lutz et al. 2004) — `confirmed`; bound: small N, special population, not
a general claim about meditation effects.
