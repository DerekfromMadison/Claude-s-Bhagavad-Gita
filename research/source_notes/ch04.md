# Source Notes — Chapter 4 (Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga)

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara — the avatara as functional/empirical
   appearance; renunciation as inner non-doership grounded in jnana.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja — the avatara as
   real, ontological descent of the personal Lord; non-attachment lived
   from devotion, not from non-doership.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnavism** — Prabhupada — avatara strongly ontological,
   teaching read as direct address; lineage as parampara.
4. **Activist karma-yoga** — Tilak (and Gandhi) — 4.18 as license for
   engaged action; 4.7–4.8 read as an ethical-political summons.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo — avatara as evolutionary
   intervention; jnana yajna as the offering of the ego-mind.
6. **Comparative academic / literary** — Barbara Stoler Miller, R.C.
   Zaehner, J.A.B. van Buitenen — historical/literary lens; lineage
   passages as text-internal claim about authority.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral** — Easwaran, possibly Mascaró — lineage and
   "approach the teacher" read as contemplative practice for daily life.

### Modern counterpoints (target ≥ 2)

- **Alasdair MacIntyre** (*After Virtue*, 1981; *Whose Justice? Which
  Rationality?*, 1988). On tradition as the carrier of practical
  rationality. Direct counterpoint to 4.1–4.3 and 4.34.
- **Michael Polanyi** (*Personal Knowledge*, 1958; *The Tacit
  Dimension*, 1966). On the master–apprentice transmission of tacit
  knowledge. Direct counterpoint to "approach the teacher."
- **Hans-Georg Gadamer** (*Truth and Method*, 1960). On tradition,
  prejudice, and the hermeneutic circle. Useful as a third voice.
- (Optional) **Hannah Arendt** on tradition's break in modernity
  (*Between Past and Future*, 1961).

### Modern science strands (target 2)

- **Cognitive science of expertise / deliberate practice** — K. Anders
  Ericsson on deliberate practice; specifically the master–coach pair
  in skill acquisition.
- **Cognitive apprenticeship and tacit knowledge transfer** — Collins,
  Brown, and Newman, "Cognitive Apprenticeship" (1989); contemporary
  research on mentorship in medicine and surgery.

### Anchor verses (translations to set side-by-side)

- 4.7–4.8 (whenever dharma declines)
- 4.18 (action in inaction, inaction in action)
- 4.34 (approach the teacher: tad viddhi pranipatena…)

Side-by-side translators planned: Easwaran, Miller, Mascaró,
Radhakrishnan, Prabhupada, Sargeant (interlinear), van Buitenen,
Zaehner, Edgerton, Aurobindo (where his rendering appears in
*Essays on the Gita*).

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

Each entry: quotation, citation, confidence flag.

### Anchor 1: Bhagavad Gita 4.7–4.8 (whenever dharma declines)

- **Easwaran (translation)**: "Whenever dharma declines and the
  purpose of life is forgotten, I manifest myself on earth. I am born
  in every age to protect the good, to destroy evil, and to reestablish
  dharma." (Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 2nd ed., Nilgiri Press,
  2007, ch. 4 vv. 7–8, p. 102.)
  Confidence: confirmed (substance of Easwaran's well-known rendering;
  exact pagination in 2007 Nilgiri Press 2nd edition; mark page locator
  unconfirmed).
  Flag: page locator — "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)".

- **Miller (translation)**: "Whenever sacred duty decays / and chaos
  prevails, / then, I create / myself, Arjuna. // To protect men of
  virtue / and destroy men who do evil, / to set the standard of sacred
  duty, / I appear in age after age." (Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita:
  Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, Bantam Classics, 1986, 4.7–4.8,
  p. 50.)
  Confidence: confirmed in substance and lineation; exact page locator
  for Bantam paperback flagged as "(page locator unconfirmed;
  validation pending)".

- **Radhakrishnan (translation + commentary)**: "Whenever there is a
  decline of righteousness and rise of unrighteousness, O Bharata
  (Arjuna), then I send forth (create incarnate) Myself. For the
  protection of the good, for the destruction of the wicked and for
  the establishment of righteousness, I come into being from age to
  age." (Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, George Allen & Unwin,
  1948, 4.7–4.8.)
  Confidence: confirmed in substance; standard Radhakrishnan rendering;
  page locator unconfirmed.

- **Prabhupada (translation)**: "Whenever and wherever there is a
  decline in religious practice, O descendant of Bharata, and a
  predominant rise of irreligion — at that time I descend Myself. To
  deliver the pious and to annihilate the miscreants, as well as to
  reestablish the principles of religion, I Myself appear, millennium
  after millennium." (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1972
  Macmillan / 1986 BBT abridged, 4.7–4.8.)
  Confidence: confirmed (this is Prabhupada's near-verbatim signature
  rendering, widely reproduced); edition/page locator unconfirmed
  precisely between Macmillan 1972 and BBT editions.

### Anchor 2: Bhagavad Gita 4.18 (action in inaction)

- **Easwaran**: "The wise see that there is action in the midst of
  inaction and inaction in the midst of action. Their consciousness
  is unified, and every act is done with complete awareness."
  (Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Nilgiri Press, 2007, 4.18, p. 104.)
  Confidence: substance confirmed; exact wording per Easwaran's 2007
  rendering; page locator unconfirmed (validation pending).

- **Miller**: "He really sees who sees that all / actions are
  performed by nature, / while the self remains unmoved as / the
  witness." — *NOTE TO SELF: this is closer to her rendering of a
  different verse (3.27 / 13.29). For 4.18 Miller renders something
  like "He who sees inaction in action and action in inaction, he is
  the wise man." VERIFY before quoting; for now treat as paraphrase
  with attribution.* Confidence: paraphrase. Mark in chapter as
  paraphrase, not quotation.

- **Radhakrishnan (translation)**: "He who in action sees inaction
  and action in inaction — he is wise among men, he is a yogin and he
  has accomplished all his work." (Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*,
  1948, 4.18.)
  Confidence: confirmed in substance; standard Radhakrishnan
  rendering. Page locator unconfirmed.

- **Sankara (commentary on 4.18, paraphrased from Gambhirananda
  translation)**: substance — the "inaction in action" is the
  recognition that the self does not act; *atman* is actionless;
  the body and senses act under prakriti.
  Confidence: paraphrase (do not present as quotation unless verbatim
  Gambhirananda or Alladi Mahadeva Sastri text is cited).

- **Aurobindo**: "To see action in inaction and inaction in action is
  the foundation on which the truth of works has to be built. The
  recognition of the actionlessness of the self in the midst of all
  works is the secret which the Gita has come to declare."
  *NOTE TO SELF: this captures the substance of Aurobindo on 4.18
  in Essays on the Gita but the exact wording is uncertain. Treat as
  paraphrase with attribution.* Confidence: paraphrase.

### Anchor 3: Bhagavad Gita 4.34 (approach the teacher)

- **Easwaran**: "Approach a teacher with reverence and devotion; ask
  questions and serve. Then they who have realized the truth will
  instruct you in this knowledge." (Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*,
  Nilgiri Press, 2007, 4.34, p. 106.)
  Confidence: substance confirmed; page locator unconfirmed.

- **Miller**: "Know it by humble submission, by asking questions, and
  by service; / wise men who see reality / will give you knowledge."
  *NOTE TO SELF: substance accurate to Miller's lineated
  rendering; double-check exact words at validation.* Confidence:
  paraphrase pending verbatim check.

- **Radhakrishnan**: "Learn that by humble reverence, by inquiry and
  by service. The men of wisdom who have seen the truth will instruct
  thee in knowledge." (Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, 1948, 4.34.)
  Confidence: confirmed in substance; standard rendering.

- **Sargeant (interlinear)**: word-by-word: *tad viddhi pranipatena
  pariprashnena sevaya / upadekshyanti te jnanam jnaninas
  tattvadarshinah*. (Sargeant, *The Bhagavad Gita*, SUNY, rev. ed.,
  word-by-word interlinear at 4.34.)
  Confidence: Sanskrit text confirmed (this is the standard reading
  of 4.34); Sargeant's English gloss not quoted verbatim — paraphrase.

### Tradition pulls (one verbatim per school where possible)

#### Sankara (Advaita) — on the actionless self

- "The Self is action-less" — paraphrase of Sankara's commentary on
  4.18 and on the body-mind being the locus of all action while atman
  remains akarta. Confidence: paraphrase. Do not present as
  quotation. (Source: Sankara, *Bhagavad Gita Bhasya*, trans.
  Gambhirananda, Advaita Ashrama; or Alladi Mahadeva Sastri trans.,
  Samata Books.)

- Working in-chapter rendering: Sankara, *Gita Bhasya* on 4.18,
  argues in substance that the wise person sees no real agency in the
  self; agency belongs to the gunas, not to atman, which is
  actionless. — paraphrase, attributed.

#### Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)

- "The avatara is real, taken on by the supreme Person of His own
  free will, for the protection of those who take refuge in Him."
  *NOTE TO SELF: substance of Ramanuja's commentary on 4.7–4.8 in his
  Gita Bhasya (trans. Adidevananda or Tapasyananda). Treat as
  paraphrase with attribution; substitute verbatim if a verified
  edition is at hand.* Confidence: paraphrase.

#### Prabhupada (Gaudiya Vaishnava)

- "Krishna is the original Personality of Godhead, and His
  appearances in this material world are not the appearances of an
  ordinary man. Although He appears like a human being, His body is
  not material." (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, purport to
  4.6 / 4.7, BBT.) Confidence: substance confirmed; this is a stock
  Prabhupada formulation, ubiquitous across BBT printings; specific
  page locator unconfirmed.

#### Tilak (activist karma-yoga)

- *Gita Rahasya* reads 4.18 (action in inaction) as the heart of
  the active-engagement reading: it dissolves the apparent
  contradiction between renunciation and action by relocating
  renunciation inside the agent's intention rather than the body's
  activity. (Tilak, *Srimad Bhagavadgita Rahasya*, trans. B.S.
  Sukthankar, Tilak Bros., Poona, 1935 (vol. 1) / 1936 (vol. 2).)
  Confidence: paraphrase with attribution; treat any in-text
  quotation as paraphrase unless a verified Sukthankar passage is
  present.

#### Aurobindo (Integral)

- "The Gita's teaching of avataras is not the teaching of an
  occasional descent of the Divine into a moral wreckage that has to
  be set right; it is the affirmation of an evolutionary working in
  which the Divine takes up the human nature again and again to lead
  the race forward." *NOTE TO SELF: this captures Aurobindo's
  position in Essays on the Gita, ch. on the Divine Teacher and
  Avatarhood, but the exact wording is uncertain — treat as
  paraphrase.* Confidence: paraphrase.

#### Miller (literary-academic)

- From Miller's introduction: *NOTE TO SELF: Miller frames the Gita
  as a dialogic poem within the Mahabharata and emphasizes its
  dramatic structure. Specific quoted line on lineage / avatara —
  treat the chapter's reference to Miller as paraphrase from her
  introduction unless verbatim is available.* Confidence: paraphrase.

#### Zaehner (comparative-religion)

- "The Gita's god is, then, both transcendent and immanent. He acts
  in the world without being entangled in the world." (Zaehner, *The
  Bhagavad-Gita with a Commentary based on the Original Sources*,
  Oxford, 1969.) *NOTE TO SELF: this paraphrases Zaehner's running
  commentary near ch. 4. Treat as paraphrase pending verbatim
  verification.* Confidence: paraphrase.

### Modern counterpoint pulls

#### MacIntyre — on tradition

- "A living tradition then is an historically extended, socially
  embodied argument, and an argument precisely in part about the
  goods which constitute that tradition." (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*,
  3rd ed., University of Notre Dame Press, 2007, ch. 15, p. 222.)
  Confidence: confirmed (this is one of MacIntyre's most-cited
  formulations and appears in the third edition near p. 222; if the
  working edition is the 2nd ed. (1984), the passage is on p. 222 of
  the 1984 edition as well; mark edition explicitly).

- "I can only answer the question 'What am I to do?' if I can answer
  the prior question 'Of what story or stories do I find myself a
  part?'" (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, 3rd ed., p. 216.) Confidence:
  confirmed in substance; very widely quoted MacIntyre line.

#### Polanyi — on tacit knowledge and apprenticeship

- "An art which cannot be specified in detail cannot be transmitted
  by prescription, since no prescription for it exists. It can be
  passed on only by example from master to apprentice." (Polanyi,
  *Personal Knowledge: Towards a Post-Critical Philosophy*, University
  of Chicago Press, 1958/1962, ch. 4 "Skills," p. 53.) Confidence:
  confirmed (this is Polanyi's classic line on master/apprentice; the
  pagination cited is the standard Routledge / Chicago paginations).

- "We can know more than we can tell." (Polanyi, *The Tacit
  Dimension*, Doubleday, 1966, p. 4.) Confidence: confirmed (this is
  the opening line of *The Tacit Dimension*, very widely quoted).

#### Gadamer — on tradition and prejudice

- "It is not so much our judgments as it is our prejudices that
  constitute our being." (Gadamer, *Truth and Method*, 2nd rev. ed.,
  trans. Weinsheimer & Marshall, Continuum, 1989/2004, "Foreword to
  the Second Edition," p. xxx — note locator approximate.)
  Confidence: confirmed in substance, widely cited; exact page locator
  flagged as unconfirmed.

#### Optional: Arendt — on the break with tradition

- Treat as optional third counterpoint if word-count headroom allows.
  Arendt, *Between Past and Future*, ch. on tradition and the modern
  age. Confidence: paraphrase only.

### Modern science pulls

#### Ericsson — deliberate practice and the coach/teacher

- "The development of expert performance is the result of deliberate
  practice — that is, engagement in activities specifically designed
  by a teacher to improve performance." (Ericsson, Krampe, & Tesch-
  Römer, "The Role of Deliberate Practice in the Acquisition of Expert
  Performance," *Psychological Review* 100(3), 1993, pp. 363–406, p.
  368.) Confidence: paraphrase of the central thesis of the 1993
  paper; exact wording above is reconstructive — present as
  paraphrase with attribution to Ericsson, Krampe, Tesch-Römer 1993,
  and bound the claim ("in domains of well-defined expertise such as
  music, chess, and athletics, where the work has been replicated;
  the deliberate-practice account is contested in some recent work,
  e.g., Macnamara, Hambrick, & Oswald, *Psychological Science*,
  2014").

#### Collins, Brown, Newman — cognitive apprenticeship

- "Cognitive apprenticeship methods try to enculturate students into
  authentic practices through activity and social interaction, in a
  way similar to that evident — and evidently successful — in craft
  apprenticeship." (Collins, Brown, & Newman, "Cognitive
  Apprenticeship: Teaching the Crafts of Reading, Writing, and
  Mathematics," in L. Resnick (ed.), *Knowing, Learning, and
  Instruction*, Erlbaum, 1989, p. 453.) Confidence: confirmed in
  substance; the exact phrase "to enculturate students into authentic
  practices" is well attested in this paper. Page locator may be
  approximate.

#### Optional: surgical mentorship research

- Bound paraphrase only: Long-running studies of surgical training
  consistently show that the quality of one-on-one mentorship
  predicts both the speed of skill acquisition and the rate of
  technical errors in independent practice (e.g., Birkmeyer et al.,
  "Surgical Skill and Complication Rates after Bariatric Surgery,"
  *New England Journal of Medicine* 369, 2013, pp. 1434–1442).
  Confidence: paraphrase; named study, bounded claim.
