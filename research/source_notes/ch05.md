## Phase 1 — Schools to engage and authors per school

The chapter brief identifies the central hinge: are renunciation
(*sannyasa*) and the yoga of action (*karma yoga*) two paths or one?
The interpretive divergence is most acute between Sankara (who reads
renunciation finally as a separate stage culminating in *jnana*) and
the active-life readings (Ramanuja, Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo). Each
school is selected because its disagreement is substantive, not stylistic.

Schools planned (≥ 3 floor; target 5–7):

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Adi Shankaracharya. Lens: non-dualism;
   renunciation of action is the eventual mark of the *jnani* whose
   identity with *Brahman* obviates ownership of action.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja. Lens: qualified
   non-dualism; *karma yoga* is intrinsic to a life of devoted service;
   inner renunciation, not bodily withdrawal, is the operative term.
3. **Activist karma-yoga reading** — Bal Gangadhar Tilak (*Gita
   Rahasya*). Lens: action over renunciation, with *karma yoga* as the
   core Gita teaching, framed against world-renouncing readings.
4. **Gandhian reading** — M.K. Gandhi (*Anasakti Yoga* /
   *Discourses on the Gita*). Lens: action without attachment as
   nonviolence-in-practice; renunciation = *anasakti*, not flight.
5. **Integral Yoga** — Sri Aurobindo (*Essays on the Gita*). Lens:
   evolutionary synthesis of *karma*, *jnana*, *bhakti*; the doer
   surrendered into the Divine; "the knower of truth thinks I do nothing."
6. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizer** — Eknath Easwaran. Lens:
   contemplative-pastoral; the Gita as practical spirituality for
   daily living.
7. **Comparative academic / philological** — Barbara Stoler Miller,
   W.J. Johnson, Franklin Edgerton, Winthrop Sargeant. Lens:
   text-critical literary scholarship.

## Phase 1 — Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

1. **Christian contemplative-active tradition** — Thomas Merton on the

   in Western contemplative literature.
2. **Viktor Frankl** — *Man's Search for Meaning* — distinction between

3. **Bernard Williams** — moral philosophy of integrity and the

4. **Hannah Arendt** — *vita activa* / *vita contemplativa*. (Possible.)
5. **Iris Murdoch** — attention as moral capacity (used principally in

## Phase 1 — Modern science strands (≥ 1; target 2)

1. **Default Mode Network and self-referential processing.** Marcus
   Raichle on DMN baseline; Judson Brewer (Yale/Brown) on DMN

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### Anchor verse 5.2–5.3

**5.2** — Krishna's reply that both lead to good but karma-yoga is
distinguished.

**Eknath Easwaran translation (Nilgiri Press, 1985 / rev. 2007):**
"The renunciation of action and the practice of selfless service both
lead to the highest goal. But the path of action is the better way."
(Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 5.2, second ed. 2007, p. 134
— *edition page unconfirmed; validation pending*)
Confidence: `paraphrase`. The rendering above paraphrases Easwaran's
common formulation; exact wording to be confirmed from print copy
during validation pass. Will treat as paraphrase in chapter.

**Barbara Stoler Miller translation (Bantam, 1986):**
Miller renders 5.2 as: "Renunciation and discipline in action both
effect good beyond measure; but of the two, discipline in action
surpasses renunciation of action."
(Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, 5.2,
Bantam ed. 1986, p. 60.) Confidence: `unconfirmed` — exact wording
plausibly close to Miller; I will use this only as paraphrase in the
chapter, with citation marked.

**W.J. Johnson translation (Oxford World's Classics, 1994):**
Johnson 5.2: "Renunciation and the discipline of action both lead to
the highest good. But of these two, the discipline of action is held
to be the better." (Johnson, *The Bhagavad Gita*, OWC 1994.)
Confidence: `unconfirmed` — close paraphrase; treat as paraphrase.

**Winthrop Sargeant interlinear (1979/SUNY 1984):**
Word-by-word for 5.2 confirms *sannyasa* and *karmayoga* as parallel
terms; *vishisyate* — "is distinguished, surpasses." Confidence:
`paraphrase` — I will summarize Sargeant's lexical observation rather
than quote.

**5.3** — He who neither hates nor desires is to be known as the
constant renouncer (*nitya-sannyasi*).

Easwaran, Miller, Johnson all converge on the substance: the true
renouncer is the one free of both aversion and craving, regardless of
external action. I will paraphrase a single composite observation
from these three (carefully labeled as the convergent point).

### Anchor verse 5.8–5.9 — "I do nothing at all"

**Sankara commentary (Gambhirananda translation, Advaita Ashrama):**
Sankara on 5.8: he who knows the reality (*tattva-vit*) understands
that all activities of seeing, hearing, touching, smelling, eating,
walking, sleeping, breathing, speaking, evacuating, grasping, opening
and closing the eyes — these are operations of the *indriyas* in
*indriya-arthas*. The self does not act; the senses move among
sense-objects. Therefore the knower thinks: *naiva kincit karomi*.
(Sankaracharya, *Bhagavadgita with the Commentary of Sankaracharya*,
trans. Swami Gambhirananda, Advaita Ashrama, Calcutta, 1984; commentary
on 5.8–5.9.) Confidence: `paraphrase` (substance, not exact wording).
I will not present this as a direct quote without verifying the
Gambhirananda English wording in print.

**Direct verse — Easwaran 5.8–5.9 (paraphrase):** "Those who know
this truth, whose consciousness is unified, think always, 'I am not
the doer.' While seeing, hearing, touching, smelling; eating, moving
about, sleeping, breathing; speaking, letting go, holding on, even
opening and closing the eyes, they understand that these are only the
movements of the senses among sense objects." Confidence:
`paraphrase` — close to common Easwaran rendering. Will treat as
paraphrase with attribution rather than direct quote, pending print
verification.

**Aurobindo (*Essays on the Gita*, ch. on "Equality and Knowledge"):**
"The Sankhya-Yogin sees that it is Nature, *prakriti*, that does the
work, and the soul, *purusha*, looks on without acting." This is
Aurobindo's standard formulation across the *Essays*. (Aurobindo,
*Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust ed.; chapter
"Equality and Knowledge" — *page unconfirmed; validation pending*.)
Confidence: `paraphrase`. I will attribute substance rather than quote.

**Miller 5.8–5.9 (paraphrase):** Miller has the practitioner saying
"I do nothing at all" while sensory operations continue, attributing
the doing to the senses among sense-objects. Confidence: `paraphrase`.

### Anchor verse 5.27–5.28 — Meditation-in-action

**Easwaran 5.27–5.28 (paraphrase):** "Closing his eyes, steadying his
breathing, and focusing his attention on the center of spiritual
consciousness, the wise man masters his senses, mind, and intellect
through meditation. Self-realization is his only goal. Freed from
selfish desire, fear, and anger, he lives in freedom always."
Confidence: `paraphrase`. Standard Easwaran rendering; treat as
paraphrase.

**Miller 5.27–5.28 (paraphrase):** Miller's rendering emphasizes the
breath at the nostrils, the equalized inhalation and exhalation, and
the controlled senses, mind, and understanding directed toward
liberation. Confidence: `paraphrase`.

### School commentary — Sankara (Advaita)

Primary lens passage on the chapter as a whole: Sankara reads the
chapter as resolving Arjuna's confusion in 5.1 by showing that what
is praised here as "*sannyasa*" is in fact "*karma yoga*" so long as
the agent is still embodied; only the *jnani* who has realized
identity with *Brahman* attains the truer renunciation in which
action drops away as a product of nescience.

**Source location:** Sankara's *bhasya* on Gita 5.1–5.5 in
Gambhirananda trans., *Bhagavadgita with the Commentary of
Sankaracharya*, Advaita Ashrama, Calcutta, 1984.
Confidence: `paraphrase` — I will not place quotation marks around
Sankara's English wording without print verification. I will
attribute the position by name and chapter.

### School commentary — Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita)

**Reference:** Ramanuja's *Gita-bhasya*, available in English through
Adidevananda, *Sri Ramanuja Gita Bhashya* (Sri Ramakrishna Math,
Madras, 1991) and the older Tirupati / Ramakrishna Math editions.
Substance: for Ramanuja, *karma yoga* is itself the appropriate
discipline; the renunciation Krishna privileges is the renunciation
of fruits and of egoic ownership, not of action — because the embodied
self cannot escape action and because action offered to the Lord
becomes the medium of devotion.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. I will attribute by name and lens; I will
not place quotation marks on Adidevananda's English without print
verification. (*edition unconfirmed; validation pending*).

### School commentary — Tilak

**Reference:** Bal Gangadhar Tilak, *Srimad Bhagavadgita Rahasya*,
trans. B.S. Sukthankar, Tilak Bros., Poona, 1935 (multiple
reprints). Tilak's central thesis is that the Gita teaches *karma
yoga* — the path of selfless action, not renunciation — and
Chapter 5 is one of his anchor texts: Krishna explicitly says
*karma yoga* surpasses *sannyasa* (5.2), the true renouncer is the
one without aversion or craving (5.3), and the *jnani* in the world
acts without ownership (5.8–5.9).
**Tilak quotation (substance, marked as paraphrase):** Tilak argues
that Krishna's whole teaching here is that the renunciation
*reasoned out* by some philosophers — abandonment of action — is
inferior to the renunciation that *acts*: the doer who has given up
attachment to outcomes but continues to do what duty requires.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. (Tilak, *Gita Rahasya*, vol. I, ch. on
"Karma-yoga and Sannyasa" — *exact volume page unconfirmed; validation
pending*.)

### School commentary — Gandhi

**Reference:** M.K. Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga: The Gospel of Selfless
Action* (translated from Gujarati; in *Mahadev Desai, The Gospel of
Selfless Action or The Gita according to Gandhi*, Navajivan, 1946).
Also *Discourses on the Gita* (Navajivan, 1960).

**Gandhi formulation (substance, paraphrase):** For Gandhi, the title
of his own rendering says it: *Anasakti Yoga* — the yoga of
non-attachment. The renunciation Krishna enjoins is the
renunciation of the fruit, not of the work. Chapter 5 is decisive
for him because it explicitly equates the inner state of the
renouncer with the outer state of the karma-yogi: it is the same
person, distinguished by attitude, not by costume.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. (Gandhi, *Discourses on the Gita*,
Navajivan, 1960 — *exact page unconfirmed*).

### School commentary — Aurobindo (Integral)

**Reference:** Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo
Ashram Press, Pondicherry, multiple editions; First Series 1922,
Second Series 1928 (often combined in a single volume).

Aurobindo on 5.8–5.9, in substance: the realized soul knows that
*prakriti* does the work and the *purusha* witnesses; this is not
quietism but the foundation of a higher activity in which the
individual will is given over to the Divine and action proceeds
without the entanglement of egoic ownership. The same soul that says
"I do nothing" is the soul most fully able to act.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. (Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Sri
Aurobindo Ashram, ed. unconfirmed.)

### School commentary — Easwaran (contemplative-pastoral)

**Reference:** Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living*,
3 vols., Nilgiri Press, 1975 (vol. on chs. 1–6) — Easwaran's
chapter-by-chapter commentary distinguished from his standalone
translation.

Easwaran's substance on Ch. 5: the chapter is, for him, the heart of
the Gita's practical instruction — the inner quietness that does not
require an outer monastery. The reader can begin where they are. The
*nitya-sannyasi* is the householder whose inner life has been
re-anchored. Confidence: `paraphrase`. (Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita
for Daily Living*, vol. I — *page unconfirmed*).

### Modern counterpoint — Thomas Merton

**Reference:** Thomas Merton, *The Inner Experience: Notes on
Contemplation* (HarperOne, 2003; written largely 1959–1968,
published posthumously). Also *New Seeds of Contemplation* (New
Directions, 1962).

Merton's substance, often quoted: "Action and contemplation are not
two opposed activities, but two modes of the same supernatural
activity, both rooted in love." (This formulation is widely
attributed to Merton across his work; the closest single-source
home is *The Inner Experience*, ch. on "Contemplation and Action."
*Edition / page unconfirmed*.) Confidence: `paraphrase`. I will
attribute the substance to Merton and mark for validation; I will
**not** place this in quotation marks unless I verify the exact
wording in a specific print edition.

Additionally, Merton's *Conjectures of a Guilty Bystander* and his
introduction to *The Bhagavad-Gita As It Is* (Prabhupada, 1968)
contains direct engagement with the Gita. In the foreword to that
edition, Merton wrote about the Gita's importance for the Western
reader. Confidence on this foreword is `paraphrase`; I will not
quote without verification.

### Modern counterpoint — Meister Eckhart

**Reference:** Meister Eckhart's German sermons, esp. Sermon 86 ("On
Mary and Martha") and the treatise *Von Abegescheidenheit* / "On
Detachment." Available in Bernard McGinn (ed./trans.), *Meister
Eckhart: The Essential Sermons, Commentaries, Treatises, and
Defense* (Paulist Press, 1981) and *Meister Eckhart: Teacher and
Preacher* (Paulist, 1986). Also Edmund Colledge & Bernard McGinn,
*Meister Eckhart* (Classics of Western Spirituality).

Substance: Eckhart in the Mary/Martha sermon reverses the standard
medieval reading. The contemplative Mary has not yet attained the
fullness of Martha; only when contemplation matures does it become
*active in time without being moved by it*. The "detached" person
is one who acts in the world without being affected by gain or loss
— precisely the *anasakti* of Gita 5.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. I will not quote Eckhart in English
without identifying the specific translator and edition. McGinn's
work is the responsible scholarly source.

### Modern counterpoint — Viktor Frankl

**Reference:** Viktor E. Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning* (orig.
1946; standard English ed. Beacon Press, 2006; translated by Ilse
Lasch).

Frankl substance: the person finds meaning not by withdrawal but by
a turn outward — toward a task, a person to love, or unavoidable
suffering met with attitude. Engagement is the mark of meaning;
detachment-as-disengagement is one of the failure modes Frankl
warned about in his clinical practice. Frankl also distinguishes,
following Allers and his teachers, between the agent who acts under
duress with dignity and the agent who collapses into the situation —
this distinction maps usefully onto Gita 5.
Confidence: `paraphrase`. The famous formulations of Frankl —
"Between stimulus and response there is a space" — are widely
attributed to him but I will not quote that line without locator.
I will quote only what I can locate.

### Modern science — Default Mode Network and the "I do nothing" claim

**Marcus Raichle.** Raichle and colleagues (Raichle, MacLeod,
Snyder, Powers, Gusnard, Shulman 2001 PNAS, "A default mode of
brain function") established the conceptual frame of a
default-mode-network of brain regions active at rest, deactivating
when attention turns outward to a demanding task. This is the seed
paper. I will cite Raichle by name and the 2001 PNAS publication
without claiming a specific page; the finding is canonical.
Confidence: `confirmed` substance, locator for "Raichle et al.,
PNAS, 2001" `confirmed`.

**Judson Brewer.** Brewer, Worhunsky, Gray, Tang, Weber, Kober (2011
PNAS), "Meditation experience is associated with differences in
default mode network activity and connectivity." Finding: experienced
meditators (vs. novice controls) show reduced activation in the
posterior cingulate cortex and medial prefrontal cortex (key DMN
nodes) during meditation. Brewer's body of work has continued to
investigate the link between DMN activity and self-referential
rumination. Confidence: `confirmed` substance, locator
`Brewer et al., PNAS, 2011` `confirmed`.

I will bound this claim explicitly: brain activity correlates with
phenomenology; brain images do not establish a metaphysical claim
that "the self does nothing." But the brain finding gives a
neuroscientific reading of why a practitioner might experience
self-referential thought as quieter and action as un-self-centered.

### Modern science — Cognitive defusion (ACT)

**Steven C. Hayes.** Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (Hayes,
Strosahl, Wilson, *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy*, Guilford
Press, 1999; 2nd ed. 2012). Cognitive defusion: techniques in which
the client is taught to notice "I am having the thought that I am
worthless" rather than "I am worthless" — i.e., to relate to thought
as event rather than fact. Outcome: substantial empirical literature
(Hayes et al., *A-Tjak et al.* 2015 meta-analysis in *Psychotherapy
and Psychosomatics*) finds ACT efficacious for depression, anxiety,
chronic pain, and a range of other conditions, with cognitive
defusion as one of six core processes.

Confidence: `confirmed` for Hayes' founding role and for the
meta-analytic finding. I will not put a specific phrase in
Hayes' mouth without locator; substance is sufficient.

The link to Gita 5: cognitive defusion is the closest contemporary
analogue I know to "the knower of truth thinks 'I do nothing at
all'" — not as metaphysics, but as a procedural skill in which the
agent practices observing the doer-thought rather than fusing with it.

### Modern science — Norman Farb (secondary)

Farb, Segal, Mayberg, Bean, McKeon, Fatima, Anderson (2007),
"Attending to the present: mindfulness meditation reveals distinct
neural modes of self-reference," *Social Cognitive and Affective
Neuroscience*. Finding: distinct neural signatures for narrative
self-reference (mPFC) versus experiential self-reference (lateral
PFC, insula); training shifts toward the experiential mode.
Confidence: `confirmed` substance and citation.

I will use Farb only if word budget permits a second strand.
