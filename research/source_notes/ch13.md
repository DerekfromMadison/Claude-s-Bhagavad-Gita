# Source Notes — Chapter 13 (Ksetra Ksetrajna Vibhaga Yoga)

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara reads the *ksetra/ksetrajna* distinction as
   penultimate; the apparent dualism dissolves into the non-dual *atman*
   identical with *Brahman*. The "knower of the field" is finally one knower
   in all bodies.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** — Ramanuja preserves a real
   distinction: the individual *jiva* is a real knower, ontologically
   distinct from the supreme Self, even as both are knowers of fields.
3. **Dvaita** — Madhva radicalizes the distinction further; the supreme
   knower (Vishnu) and individual knowers are eternally separate substances.
4. **Gaudiya Vaishnava (ISKCON)** — Prabhupada reads the chapter as
   doctrinal proof of the soul's distinction from body and from God.
5. **Integral / evolutionary** — Aurobindo reads the field as the
   evolutionary medium of consciousness; the knower is not merely the
   witness but the agent of progressive self-disclosure.
6. **Gandhian / activist karma-yoga** — Gandhi reads the qualities of
   *jnana* (13.7–13.11) as a moral psychology — humility, truthfulness,
   non-violence — rather than a metaphysical curriculum.
7. **Comparative academic / philological** — Edgerton, Johnson, Patton
   approach the text without theological commitments; foreground its
   Sankhya substrate and its terminological precision.
8. **Literary-universalist** — Mascaró, Miller render the chapter for
   readers who meet it as a wisdom text; emphasize the contemplative tone.

### Modern counterpoint voices (≥ 2 required, 3 planned)

1. **Thomas Nagel** — "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (*Philosophical
   Review*, 1974). The irreducibility of subjective experience to
   objective description.
2. **David Chalmers** — *The Conscious Mind* (1996), "Facing Up to the
   Problem of Consciousness" (*Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 1995).
   The hard problem.
3. **Maurice Merleau-Ponty** — *Phenomenology of Perception* (1945; English
   1962 / 2012). The body as the subject of perception, not its object.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

1. **Interoception and the embodied self** — A.D. (Bud) Craig, "How do
   you feel? Interoception: the sense of the physiological condition of
   the body" (*Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 2002); Sarah Garfinkel's
   work on heartbeat-detection and self-related processing.
2. **Predictive processing of the self / cognitive neuroscience of self
   reference** — Anil Seth, *Being You* (2021), and the controlled-
   hallucination model; Marcus Raichle on the Default Mode Network and
   self-referential processing.

### Anchor verses

- **13.0/13.1 (Krishna's opening, sometimes counted as Arjuna's question
  in Recensions including the verse 13.0)** — Field and knower of the
  field defined.
- **13.1–13.2 (or 13.2–13.3 by recension)** — "Know me as also the
  *ksetrajna* in all *ksetras*."
- **13.7–13.11** — Twenty qualities constituting *jnana*: humility,
  unpretentiousness, non-violence, patience, uprightness, service to a
  teacher, purity, steadfastness, self-control, dispassion, absence of
  ego, perception of the suffering inherent in birth/death/old-age/
  disease, non-attachment, equanimity in pleasant and unpleasant,
  unswerving devotion, resort to solitude, distaste for crowds,
  constancy in self-knowledge, perception of the goal of true
  knowledge.
- **13.12 (or 13.13 in some numberings)** — "It is said to be neither
  existent nor non-existent" (the description of the knowable, *jneya*).
- **13.13 / 13.14** — "With hands and feet everywhere, with eyes, heads,
  mouths everywhere, with hearing in all directions" — the cosmic
  knowable.
- **13.16 / 13.17** — Indivisible, yet appearing divided in beings.
- **13.22 / 13.23** — *Purusa* in the body as witness, consenter,
  upholder, experiencer.
- **13.27 / 13.28** — Seeing the same Lord standing equally in all
  beings.
- **13.32 / 13.33** — As one sun illumines this whole world, so the
  *ksetrin* illumines the entire field.

(Verse numbering varies by recension. The most common modern Gita texts
follow either the recension Sankara comments on, which begins ch. 13
without Arjuna's question and so has 34 verses, or the Kashmiri/
Ramanuja recension with 35 verses. I will note recension where
relevant.)

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

Confidence flags: `confirmed` = wording, source, and locator verified
from working knowledge of the source; `paraphrase` = substance attributed
without quotation; `unconfirmed` = exact wording or locator not certain,
flagged for validation.

### Translations of Bhagavad Gita 13.1–13.2 (the field/knower distinction)

**A. Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, 2nd edition
2007).
- Easwaran 13.1–2 (on the body as field and the knower):
  > "This body, Arjuna, is called the field. Those who know
  > discriminate between the field and the knower of the field."
  Citation: Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 2nd ed., Nilgiri Press, 2007,
  ch. 13, vv. 1–2.
  Confidence: `confirmed` (this is Easwaran's standard wording for this
  pair; the verse numbering corresponds to the Sankara recension that
  Easwaran follows).

**B. Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
Time of War* (Bantam Classics / Columbia University Press, 1986).
- Miller 13.1: "Arjuna, this body is called the field; one who knows it
  is called knower of the field by men who know the difference."
  Citation: Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita*, Bantam, 1986, ch. 13, v. 1.
  Confidence: `unconfirmed` for exact wording; substance certain.
  Miller's translation is well-known to render this as a tight gnomic
  pair; flagged for validation.

**C. Juan Mascaró**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962).
- Mascaró 13.1–2: "This body, Arjuna, is called Kshetra, the field. He
  who knows this is called Kshetragna, the knower of the field. Know
  that I am the knower in all the fields of my creation; and that the
  vision of the field and of its knower is true vision."
  Citation: Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 1962,
  ch. 13.
  Confidence: `unconfirmed` for verbatim; paraphrase-confidence high.
  Mascaró renders these verses in a unified literary block; flagged for
  validation.

**D. Winthrop Sargeant**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (interlinear ed., SUNY
Press, 1984; revised editions).
- Sargeant 13.1: "This body, son of Kunti, is called the field; him who
  knows this, those who know thus call the field-knower."
  Citation: Sargeant, *The Bhagavad Gita*, SUNY Press, 1984/2009, ch.
  13, v. 1.
  Confidence: `unconfirmed` (exact wording); paraphrase-confidence
  high. Sargeant's interlinear renders the bhavati/abhidhīyate
  carefully; flagged for validation.

**E. W.J. Johnson**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics, 1994).
- Johnson 13.1: "Son of Kuntī, this body is called 'the field';
  those who know it are described by those who see things as they are
  as 'the knower of the field.'"
  Citation: Johnson, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Oxford World's Classics, 1994,
  ch. 13.
  Confidence: `unconfirmed`; paraphrase-confidence high. Flagged.

**F. Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, *The Bhagavadgita* (Allen & Unwin,
1948; numerous reprints).
- Radhakrishnan 13.1–2: "This body, O son of Kuntī, is called the
  field, and him who knows it, those who know thereof, call the knower
  of the field. Know Me also as the knower of the field in all fields,
  O Bhārata. The knowledge of the field and its knower, that I regard
  as true knowledge."
  Citation: Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, Allen & Unwin, 1948,
  ch. 13.
  Confidence: `unconfirmed` exact wording; paraphrase-confidence high.
  Radhakrishnan's prose translation typically merges 1–2 like this.
  Flagged.

### 13.7–13.11 (the qualities of *jnana*)

**G. Easwaran 13.7–11** — The list of twenty qualities. I will paraphrase
the list from Easwaran's translation in the chapter rather than quoting
the long block, to avoid wording uncertainty across reprints. Easwaran's
list emphasizes "humility, modesty, non-violence, forgiveness,
uprightness, service of the teacher, purity, steadfastness,
self-control, detachment from the objects of the senses, freedom from
egotism, perception of the misery of birth, old age, disease, and
death; freedom from attachment, even to one's children, spouse, and
home; constant equanimity in the face of welcome and unwelcome events…"
- Citation: Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita*, 2nd ed., 2007, ch. 13,
  vv. 7–11.
- Confidence: `paraphrase` for the list as I render it; `confirmed`
  that the list of twenty appears here.

### Sankara — Advaita commentary

**H. Adi Shankaracharya**, *Bhagavad-Gita Bhasya*. Standard reference
edition: trans. Alladi Mahadeva Sastri, *The Bhagavad Gita with the
Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya* (Samata Books, reprint of 1897 first
ed.). Also: trans. Swami Gambhirananda, *Bhagavad Gita with the
Commentary of Shankaracharya* (Advaita Ashrama, 1984).
- Sankara on 13.2: paraphrase-confidence high. Sankara's gloss is that
  the Lord declares himself the *ksetrajna* in all bodies; that the
  apparent multiplicity of knowers is owed to the *upadhis* (limiting
  adjuncts) of the bodies; and that the realization of the knower is
  identical with the realization of *Brahman*.
  Citation: Sankara, *Bhagavad-Gita Bhasya*, ch. 13, v. 2; trans.
  Gambhirananda, Advaita Ashrama, 1984.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` (substance certain; exact wording from a
  specific edition not confidently cited).
- Quotable line (Mahadeva Sastri trans., commentary on 13.2),
  approximate: "The Lord and the *kshetrajna* are not different;
  difference is owing to the *upadhi*, the limiting adjunct of the
  *kshetra*."
  Confidence: `unconfirmed` exact wording. Flagged.

### Ramanuja — Vishishtadvaita commentary

**I. Ramanujacharya**, *Gita Bhasya*. Standard English: trans.
Swami Adidevananda, *Sri Ramanuja Gita Bhasya* (Sri Ramakrishna Math,
Madras, various reprints).
- Ramanuja on 13.2: substance — the *jiva* (individual self) is the
  *ksetrajna* in its own *ksetra*; the Lord is the *ksetrajna* of all
  *ksetras* in a different sense — as the antaryamin, the inner ruler
  who sustains both the field and the individual knower. The
  distinction between the *jiva* and the *paramatman* is preserved.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Adidevananda's translation is the standard
  English vehicle for Ramanuja; exact wording flagged.

### Madhva — Dvaita commentary

**J. Madhvacharya**, *Bhagavad Gita Bhasya* (Gītābhāṣya). English: trans.
B.N.K. Sharma, *Madhva's Bhagavadgita Bhasya* (Motilal Banarsidass).
- Madhva on 13.2: substance — Madhva insists that *ksetrajna* in the
  Lord and *ksetrajna* in the soul are not numerically identical;
  the supreme is the eternal master, the soul is the eternal servant.
  The chapter, far from teaching identity, teaches taratamya
  (gradation).
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Sharma's translation is the standard
  vehicle. Flagged.

### Aurobindo — Integral

**K. Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust,
multiple eds., including 1995 reprint of the second series). The two
series of essays were originally serialized in *Arya* (1916–1920).
- Aurobindo on the field and knower: paraphrase — the field is Prakriti
  acting through the *gunas*; the knower is the *Purusha*, but the
  Gita's deeper teaching is that the supreme *Purushottama* is both
  the witness and the active power that operates through Prakriti. The
  field is not illusion but the medium of progressive self-disclosure
  of the divine.
  Citation: Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, Second Series, ch. on
  "The Field and Its Knower," Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust, ed. 1995.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Exact wording flagged. Aurobindo's chapter
  on field/knower in the Second Series is well-attested; specific page
  citation pending.
- Quotable line (paraphrase-confidence high, substance certain):
  Aurobindo argues that the dichotomy of *ksetra* and *ksetrajna* is
  not the final word — the Gita "exceeds" both Sankhya and Advaita by
  positing the *Purushottama* who contains both the alterable and the
  inalterable.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`.

### Gandhi — activist / interior

**L. M.K. Gandhi**, *Discourses on the Gita* (originally given at the
Sabarmati Ashram 1926; English trans. Mahadev Desai and others,
Navajivan Publishing House, multiple eds.) and *The Gita According to
Gandhi* (Anasakti Yoga, ed. Mahadev Desai, Navajivan, 1946).
- Gandhi on the qualities of *jnana* (13.7–13.11): substance — Gandhi
  reads the list as an ethical curriculum, not a metaphysical credential.
  Humility (*amanitvam*) and non-pretension are the gateways. Without
  these, the metaphysical talk of *Brahman* is empty.
  Citation: Gandhi, *The Gita According to Gandhi*, Navajivan, 1946,
  commentary on ch. 13.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Flagged for exact-wording validation.

### Prabhupada — ISKCON Vaishnava

**M. A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
(BBT, second revised and enlarged ed., 1989; first ed. Macmillan 1968).
- Prabhupada 13.2 purport: substance — the body is the field of
  activities; the individual soul is the knower of one body; Krishna
  (the Supersoul) is the knower of all bodies. This is the doctrine of
  *jiva-atman* and *paramatman*. The chapter is read as a refutation of
  monism: the soul is *not* identical with the Supersoul.
  Citation: Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, BBT, 1989, ch. 13,
  text 2 purport.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Specific wording flagged for validation;
  the doctrinal stance is certain.

### Patton — academic philological

**N. Laurie L. Patton**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 2008).
- Patton 13.1: Patton renders these verses with attention to the
  Sankhya substrate. Her introduction notes the chapter's dependence on
  Sankhya-Yoga categories.
  Citation: Patton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 2008, ch. 13.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Exact wording flagged.

### Modern counterpoints — verbatim where confidence permits

**O. Thomas Nagel**, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" *Philosophical
Review*, vol. 83, no. 4 (October 1974), pp. 435–450.
- Nagel: "fundamentally an organism has conscious mental states if and
  only if there is something it is like to be that organism — something
  it is like *for* the organism."
  Citation: Nagel, "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" *Philosophical Review*
  83.4 (1974): 436.
  Confidence: `confirmed` (this is the canonical formulation; widely
  reprinted; the "for the organism" italicization is Nagel's).
- Nagel: "the subjective character of experience"
  Citation: Nagel, ibid., p. 436.
  Confidence: `confirmed` as a phrase from the article.

**P. David Chalmers**, "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness,"
*Journal of Consciousness Studies* 2.3 (1995): 200–219; also in
Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind: In Search of a Fundamental Theory*
(Oxford University Press, 1996).
- Chalmers: "It is widely agreed that experience arises from a physical
  basis, but we have no good explanation of why and how it so arises.
  Why should physical processing give rise to a rich inner life at all?
  It seems objectively unreasonable that it should, and yet it does."
  Citation: Chalmers, "Facing Up to the Problem of Consciousness,"
  *JCS* 2.3 (1995): 201.
  Confidence: `confirmed`. This is one of Chalmers's signature passages.
- Chalmers's distinction between the "easy problems" and the "hard
  problem":
  > "The really hard problem of consciousness is the problem of
  > experience."
  Citation: Chalmers, ibid., p. 201.
  Confidence: `confirmed`.

**Q. Maurice Merleau-Ponty**, *Phenomenology of Perception*. French
original *Phénoménologie de la perception* (Gallimard, 1945). English
trans. Colin Smith, *Phenomenology of Perception* (Routledge & Kegan
Paul, 1962); newer trans. Donald Landes (Routledge, 2012).
- Merleau-Ponty: "The body is our general medium for having a world."
  Citation: Merleau-Ponty, *Phenomenology of Perception*, trans. Colin
  Smith, Routledge & Kegan Paul, 1962, p. 146 (locator approximate;
  edition unconfirmed page number).
  Confidence: `confirmed` for wording (this is among the most-quoted
  Merleau-Ponty lines); `unconfirmed` for exact page in a specific
  edition.
- Merleau-Ponty on the body as subject rather than object: paraphrase
  high — his central thesis is that perception is not the mind's
  reception of stimuli but the body's inhabiting of a world; the body
  is "the vehicle of being in the world."
  Citation: Merleau-Ponty, ibid., trans. Smith, ch. on "The Body,"
  p. 82 area.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`; locator unconfirmed.

### Modern science strands — named researchers, bounded claims

**R. A.D. (Bud) Craig**, "How do you feel? Interoception: the sense of
the physiological condition of the body," *Nature Reviews
Neuroscience* 3 (August 2002): 655–666.
- Craig's central claim: the insular cortex, particularly the right
  anterior insula, integrates afferent signals from the body's
  physiological state (visceral, thermal, nociceptive, hedonic), and
  this integration is the neurobiological substrate of "feelings"
  understood as awareness of bodily condition.
  Citation: Craig, "How do you feel?" *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*
  3 (2002): 655–666.
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the substance and citation; the precise
  wording I use in the chapter will be paraphrased rather than quoted
  to keep within verified-quote discipline.

**S. Sarah Garfinkel et al.**, "Knowing your own heart: Distinguishing
interoceptive accuracy from interoceptive awareness," *Biological
Psychology* 104 (2015): 65–74.
- Garfinkel's central claim: interoceptive accuracy (objective
  performance on heartbeat-detection) and interoceptive awareness
  (metacognition about one's accuracy) are separable dimensions and
  each contributes differently to emotion.
  Citation: Garfinkel, Seth, Barrett, Suzuki, Critchley, "Knowing your
  own heart," *Biological Psychology* 104 (2015): 65–74.
  Confidence: `confirmed`.

**T. Anil Seth**, *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness* (Faber &
Faber / Dutton, 2021); see also Seth, "The real problem,"
*Aeon*, 2 November 2016, and Seth's earlier paper with Karl Friston,
"Active interoceptive inference and the emotional brain,"
*Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* (2016).
- Seth's framing of perception and self as "controlled hallucinations":
  > "We're all hallucinating all the time. When we agree about our
  > hallucinations, we call it 'reality.'"
  Citation: Seth, TED Talk, "Your brain hallucinates your conscious
  reality," 2017; also restated in *Being You* (2021).
  Confidence: `confirmed` (this is Seth's signature framing, repeated
  in multiple venues; the wording is consistent).
- Seth on the bodily self: paraphrase — Seth argues that the felt
  experience of being a particular embodied self is a predictive
  inference the brain makes about its own physiological state.
  Citation: Seth, *Being You*, 2021, esp. ch. 7 on the embodied self.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`.

**U. Marcus Raichle et al.**, "A default mode of brain function,"
*Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 98.2 (2001): 676–682.
- Raichle's claim: a coherent network of midline brain regions is
  consistently active during rest and self-referential thought, and
  deactivates during attention to external tasks.
  Citation: Raichle, MacLeod, Snyder, Powers, Gusnard, Shulman, "A
  default mode of brain function," *PNAS* 98.2 (2001): 676–682.
  Confidence: `confirmed`.
