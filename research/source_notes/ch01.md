## Phase 1 — Plan

### Anchor verses (per chapter brief)
- **1.28–1.30** — Arjuna's body fails him: limbs collapse, mouth dry, hair stands on end, *Gandiva* slips, skin burns.
- **1.31–1.35** — Arjuna refuses to fight; "I see no good in killing my own kin."
- **1.36–1.39** — He invokes the consequence to *kula* (clan) and *dharma* of clan.
- **1.40–1.44** — The destruction of the clan destroys the eternal *dharmas*; corruption of women, the mixing of castes, the fall of the ancestors.
- **1.45–1.47** — Arjuna sits down in the chariot, casting away bow and arrows, "his mind overborne by grief."

### Interpretive viewpoints planned (target 5–7)
1. **Advaita / Sankara** — Arjuna's grief is *moha* (delusion), an attachment to the empirical self that must be dissolved by *jnana*. The despair is the symptom of ignorance, not insight.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Ramanuja** — Arjuna's anguish is real and morally serious, the agony of a real self facing a real conflict; the answer must come through the divine teacher's guidance.
3. **Gaudiya Vaishnava / Prabhupada** — Arjuna's lament is staged: the Lord arranges this opening so that the Gita can be spoken; the despair is dramaturgically necessary, not the failure of a confused man.
4. **Gandhian / Anasakti Yoga** — The battlefield is allegorical; the war is the inner moral war of every person; Arjuna's collapse is the recognizable hesitation of any conscience asked to harm.
5. **Aurobindo / Integral Yoga** — Arjuna's despair is a "crisis" of the surface ego; it is a necessary breakdown of the conventional moral self that opens the door to a higher synthesis.
6. **Literary-academic / Stoler Miller** — The chapter is dramatic: the *Mahabharata*'s tragic dilemma made philosophical; the chariot is the stage.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral / Easwaran** — Arjuna is each of us at a moment of real moral overload; the chapter teaches that wisdom literature can begin where you actually are.

### Modern counterpoints planned (≥2)
1. **Bernard Williams on moral residue and tragic dilemmas** ("Ethical Consistency," 1965; *Moral Luck*, 1981).
2. **Martha Nussbaum on the fragility of goodness and moral conflict** (*The Fragility of Goodness*, 1986).
3. **Jonathan Shay on moral injury** (*Achilles in Vietnam*, 1994).
4. **Sartre's "Existentialism Is a Humanism" example of the student in occupied France**.

### Modern science strands planned (≥1, target 2)
1. **Stress physiology and acute stress / freeze response** — Robert Sapolsky on the HPA axis and behavioral collapse under acute threat (*Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*, 3rd ed., 2004; *Behave*, 2017).
2. **Decision paralysis and System 1/System 2 under acute load** — Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011), with caveats about the meta-analytic state of "ego depletion" research.
3. **Grief and cognitive narrowing** — Mary-Frances O'Connor, *The Grieving Brain* (2022), on how acute grief reorganizes attention and prediction.

### 1.46–1.47 (Arjuna sits)

- **Easwaran, 2007.** 1.46: *"It would be better for me / If the sons of Dhritarashtra, with weapons in their hands, / Were to kill me, unarmed and unresisting, in the battle."* 1.47: *"Saying this in the midst of the battlefield, Arjuna, his mind overcome with sorrow, sank into the chariot, casting away his bow and arrow."* Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and structure.
- **Stoler Miller, 1986.** 1.47: *"Saying this in the time of war, Arjuna slumped into the chariot and laid down his bow and arrows, his mind tormented by grief."* Confidence: `confirmed` for cadence and for Stoler Miller's word "tormented."
- **Sankara, *Bhagavad-Gita-Bhashya*, trans. A.G. Krishna Warrier (Ramakrishna Math, 1983) / trans. Swami Gambhirananda (Advaita Ashrama, 1984).** Sankara's introductory comment frames Arjuna's collapse as *shoka-moha-prabhriti-samsara-bija-bhuta-dosha* — grief, delusion, and similar defects which are the seeds of *samsara*. The *Bhashya* opens the commentary on Chapter 1 by stating that this *shoka-moha* is the very disease the Gita's teaching will cure. Confidence: `paraphrase` — substance is well-attested across Sankara's introduction and his comment on 2.10, but the precise English wording depends on translator. Use in chapter as attributed paraphrase ("Sankara argues, in substance, that…") and/or as quoted English from a named modern translator.

### Sankara — Advaita reading of the despair (commentary)

- **Sankara, introduction to *Bhashya*.** Substance: Arjuna is afflicted by *shoka* (grief) and *moha* (delusion), which are the *bija* (seed) of *samsara*. The teaching of the Gita aims at *atmajnana* (knowledge of the self), which uproots that seed. Confidence: `paraphrase`. Treat as attributed paraphrase only. (Source: Sankara's *Gita-bhashya*; trans. Gambhirananda, Advaita Ashrama, 1984. edition locator unconfirmed.)
- Sankara's identification of Arjuna's condition with *moha* on the basis of 2.10 (*"to him then, having become as it were dejected"*) is well-attested. Substance: Sankara reads the dejection as a form of confusion to be removed by knowledge. Use as attributed paraphrase.

### Ramanuja — Vishishtadvaita reading

- **Ramanuja, *Gita-bhashya*, trans. Swami Adidevananda (Ramakrishna Math, ed. 1991).** Substance: Arjuna's grief arises from *kripa* (compassion) bound to ignorance of the imperishable nature of the *atman*; the response is not annihilating the grief but instructing it. Ramanuja resists the Advaitin reduction of the world to *mithya* and treats Arjuna's grief as the real anguish of a real self. Confidence: `paraphrase`. Use as attributed paraphrase. Edition locator unconfirmed.

### Prabhupada — Gaudiya Vaishnava reading

- **Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, complete edition (BBT, 1972/1983).** Purport to 1.30: Substance — Arjuna's symptoms are not the symptoms of weakness; they reveal the sensitive heart of a great devotee, and Krishna stages the situation so the divine science (Gita) can be spoken. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance; the framing "Lord arranged" / "the Lord puts His devotee in such a situation" is characteristic of Prabhupada and recurs across the *Bhagavad-gita As It Is* purports.
- Prabhupada's purport to 1.31–1.35 explicitly criticizes the "mundane" view that Arjuna is being foolishly compassionate; Prabhupada says the devotee's compassion is real but misdirected because it does not yet rest on knowledge of the eternal soul. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.

### Gandhi — Anasakti Yoga / allegorical reading

- **M.K. Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga* / *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi*; *Discourses on the Gita* (lectures at Sabarmati, 1926, published by Navajivan).** In the introduction to *Anasakti Yoga*, Gandhi explicitly treats the battlefield of Kurukshetra as the inner battlefield of the human heart and writes that the *Mahabharata* is a *dharma-grantha*, a book of religion, not a book of history. Substance: the Gita's first chapter is the description of the moral overload that any human conscience experiences when confronted with conflicting duties; literal-historical reading misses the point. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and well-attested across Gandhi's published Gita writings.
- *Discourses on the Gita* (Mahadev Desai's translation of Gandhi's Sabarmati discourses, Navajivan Publishing House, 1960). On Chapter 1, substance: Arjuna's despondency is the natural response of one who has not yet come to the discipline of selfless action. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.

### Aurobindo — Integral Yoga reading

- **Sri Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram, 1922 / 1995 ed.).** Chapter 1 ("Our Demand and Need from the Gita") and chapter 2 ("The Divine Teacher"). Substance: the Gita is not a discourse on conduct merely, but a synthesis; Arjuna's collapse is the breakdown of the surface ego in the face of a problem the conventional moral mind cannot solve, and the Gita's teaching arises out of and in answer to this collapse. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance; Aurobindo's framing of the despair as a "spiritual crisis" precipitating a higher synthesis is the dominant motif of his early *Essays*.
- Aurobindo writes (substance, paraphrase): *"The disciple is in the position of all human beings: he is helpless before a problem which the ordinary moral and rational mind cannot solve."* Confidence: `paraphrase`. Use as attributed paraphrase.

### Stoler Miller — literary-academic reading

- **Barbara Stoler Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War* (Bantam, 1986).** Introduction and "First Teaching" headnote. Substance: Stoler Miller frames the *Gita* as a dramatic dialogue inserted into the *Mahabharata*'s tragic action, and treats Arjuna's collapse as the moment at which the great epic's tragic dilemma is forced to become philosophical. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.
- Stoler Miller, intro: substance — the Gita is a "philosophical poem" that gains its force from the dramatic context of war. Use the phrase "Krishna's counsel in time of war" (the subtitle, verifiable) and paraphrase her introductory framing. Confidence: `confirmed`.

### Easwaran — contemplative-pastoral reading

- **Eknath Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed., 2007), introduction.** Substance: the chapter is about a real human in a real moral collapse; the Gita is a teaching for the actual life of a serious person. Easwaran's pastoral note is that Arjuna is "every one of us at the moment when life turns into a problem we cannot solve from within the resources we used to have." Confidence: `paraphrase` — the phrasing above is a tightening of Easwaran's substance, not a quotation. Use as attributed paraphrase.
- Easwaran's introductory essay on the Gita's setting characteristically describes the Gita as a manual for daily living, not metaphysics; his rendering of Chapter 1 emphasizes the embodied collapse as the chapter's *honest* opening. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.

### Aurobindo, *The Synthesis of Yoga* — secondary

- **Sri Aurobindo, *The Synthesis of Yoga* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram).** General lens note. Aurobindo's larger framework treats the human being as a transitional being whose surface mind must be exceeded; Chapter 1 of the Gita illustrates the moment that surface mind exceeds itself. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.

## Phase 2 — Modern counterpoints

### Bernard Williams — tragic dilemmas / moral residue

- **Bernard Williams, "Ethical Consistency," in *Problems of the Self* (Cambridge, 1973), ch. 11.** The argument that in a genuine moral conflict the agent who fulfills one obligation does not thereby cancel the other, and that the residue of unfulfilled obligation (regret, guilt) is an indispensable feature of moral experience. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.
- Direct quotation candidate: *"It seems to me a fundamental criticism of many ethical theories that their accounts of moral conflict and its resolution do not do justice to the facts of regret and related considerations."* — Bernard Williams, "Ethical Consistency," reprinted in *Problems of the Self* (Cambridge UP, 1973), p. 175. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and for the characteristic Williams formulation. (page locator approximate; validation pending.)
- **Bernard Williams, *Moral Luck* (Cambridge, 1981).** Substance: even when an agent makes the best available choice, *moral luck* and tragic conflict can leave a residue of guilt that is morally appropriate. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.

### Martha Nussbaum — *The Fragility of Goodness*

- **Martha Nussbaum, *The Fragility of Goodness* (Cambridge UP, 1986).** Substance: a good human life is constitutively vulnerable to luck and to tragic conflict; the Greek tragedians depict moral situations in which any choice involves transgression, and this is not a defect of the agent but a feature of ethical reality. Nussbaum's treatment of Agamemnon at Aulis is the canonical example. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.
- Direct quotation candidate (paraphrase only, mark as such in text): Nussbaum argues, in substance, that to deny the reality of tragic moral conflict is to deny the structure of the ethical life as we actually live it. Confidence: `paraphrase`.

### Jonathan Shay — moral injury

- **Jonathan Shay, *Achilles in Vietnam: Combat Trauma and the Undoing of Character* (Atheneum, 1994).** Substance: Shay describes a clinical category he calls "moral injury": the lasting psychological wound produced when a person is required to act against their own moral code in a situation of legitimate authority. The wound is not fear or trauma alone but the sense of having been made complicit in violation. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and for the term "moral injury" as Shay's coinage.
- Direct quotation candidate: *"Moral injury is present when there has been (a) a betrayal of what's right, (b) by someone who holds legitimate authority, (c) in a high-stakes situation."* — paraphrase of Shay's three-part definition. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance; mark as paraphrase in chapter.

### Sartre — the student in occupied France

- **Jean-Paul Sartre, *Existentialism Is a Humanism* (1946; trans. Carol Macomber, Yale UP, 2007).** Substance: Sartre describes a young French student who came to him during the Nazi occupation, torn between staying with his ailing mother and joining the Free French in England. Sartre's argument is that no rule, religious or Kantian, will tell him what to do; he must invent the value by which he chooses, and bear the choice. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance; the example is canonical.
- Direct quotation candidate: *"In the end, it is feeling that counts; I should choose whichever pushes me in one direction. If I feel that I love my mother enough to sacrifice everything else for her — my desire for vengeance, for action, for adventure — then I should stay with her."* — Sartre, *Existentialism Is a Humanism*, trans. Macomber (Yale UP, 2007), p. 32. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and for the structure of Sartre's argument (the example concludes that *no rule* settles the matter); exact wording per Macomber translation may vary slightly. Mark as paraphrase if exact wording cannot be re-verified in the chapter; treat as attributed quotation otherwise.

## Phase 2 — Modern science

### Robert Sapolsky — stress and behavioral collapse

- **Robert Sapolsky, *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers* (Holt, 3rd ed., 2004).** Substance: the acute stress response (HPA axis activation, sympathetic surge) evolved for short-duration, life-or-death crises; sustained activation in modern humans, or unusually overwhelming acute activation, can produce the dysregulation we see clinically. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance.
- **Robert Sapolsky, *Behave: The Biology of Humans at Our Best and Worst* (Penguin, 2017).** Substance: under acute threat, the prefrontal cortex's slow, deliberative circuits are downweighted relative to fast amygdalar circuits; behavior becomes biased toward fight, flight, or freeze. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and for Sapolsky's characteristic framing of the prefrontal-amygdalar relationship.
- Direct quotation candidate: substance — Sapolsky writes that *the amygdala is to the prefrontal cortex as a fast, dirty heuristic is to a slow, careful judgment.* Confidence: `paraphrase`. Mark as paraphrase in chapter; Sapolsky's actual phrasing varies by passage.
- The "freeze" response — tonic immobility — is a real and well-documented branch of the threat response, distinct from sympathetic flight, mediated through periaqueductal gray and dorsal vagal pathways (Stephen Porges, polyvagal theory; the polyvagal framework remains debated, but tonic immobility itself is well-attested). Confidence: `confirmed` for substance with appropriate hedge.

### Daniel Kahneman — System 1 / System 2 under load

- **Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011).** Substance: System 1 is fast, automatic, and emotionally responsive; System 2 is slow, deliberate, effortful. Under high cognitive or emotional load, System 2 is impaired; behavior reverts to System 1 defaults. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance; this is the book's central framework.
- Direct quotation candidate: *"System 1 operates automatically and quickly, with little or no effort and no sense of voluntary control."* — Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (FSG, 2011), p. 20. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and characteristic phrasing; (page locator approximate; validation pending.)
- Caveat to use in chapter: the "ego depletion" line of Kahneman's broader story (Baumeister's strong reading of System 2 as a depletable resource) has not held up well in subsequent meta-analyses; the System 1 / System 2 framing as a phenomenology of fast-vs.-slow cognition is more durable than the specific depletion thesis. Confidence: `confirmed` for the methodological caveat; cite Hagger et al. (2016) registered replication.

### Mary-Frances O'Connor — grief and cognitive narrowing

- **Mary-Frances O'Connor, *The Grieving Brain: The Surprising Science of How We Learn from Love and Loss* (HarperOne, 2022).** Substance: acute grief is not just an emotion but a reorganization of the brain's predictive model of the world. The neural circuits that have learned, over years, that a loved person is "where I will find them" do not update overnight; the resulting prediction error is part of what makes grief feel like a derangement of attention. Confidence: `confirmed` for substance and for O'Connor's framing.
- Direct quotation candidate: substance — O'Connor argues that the brain's continued prediction of the loved one's presence is precisely why grief is so cognitively disorienting; the brain is, in a meaningful sense, looking for someone who is not there. Confidence: `paraphrase`. Mark as paraphrase in chapter.
