# Chapter 3 — Source Notes (Karma Yoga)

## Phase 1 — Orientation

### Interpretive schools to engage (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Sankara, who reads *karma yoga* as preparatory
   discipline (chitta-shuddhi) leading toward *jnana*.
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Bhakti-integral** — Ramanuja, who treats action
   as a constitutive limb of devotion to the Lord.
3. **Activist karma-yoga** — Tilak (*Gita Rahasya*), who reads the
   chapter as proof that the Gita commands action, not renunciation.
4. **Gandhian / Anasakti** — Gandhi, who reads *anasakti* (non-attachment)
   as the moral grammar of nonviolent self-sacrificial work.
5. **Integral yoga** — Aurobindo, for whom *yajna* (sacrifice) becomes
   the whole structure of cosmic and individual life.
6. **Comparative-academic** — Barbara Stoler Miller and Laurie Patton,
   for the philological and dramatic frame.
7. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizer** — Eknath Easwaran (background
   reference; primary translations come from him in many citations).

### Named translators to be quoted

- **Sankara** (8th c.) — *jnana*-priority Advaitin.
- **Ramanuja** (11th–12th c.) — Vishishtadvaita Vaishnava.
- **B.G. Tilak** — *Gita Rahasya* (1915), activist karma-yoga.
- **M.K. Gandhi** — *Anasakti Yoga* / *Discourses on the Gita*.
- **Sri Aurobindo** — *Essays on the Gita*.
- **Eknath Easwaran** — *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press translation).
- **Barbara Stoler Miller** — *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (1986).
- **Juan Mascaró** — *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962).
- **W.J. Johnson** — *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics, 1994).
- **A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada** — *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*.
- **S. Radhakrishnan** — *The Bhagavadgita* (1948).

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

1. **Hannah Arendt** — *The Human Condition* (1958). The threefold
   division of labor / work / action; the *vita activa*. Counterpoint
   to *karma yoga*'s dismissal of withdrawal: Arendt also rejects pure
   contemplation as a flight, but distinguishes *action* (politics) from
   *labor* (necessity) — a distinction the Gita does not draw.
2. **Alasdair MacIntyre** — *After Virtue* (1981). The concept of an
   internal good of a practice; habituation; moral tradition. A Western
   virtue-ethical frame for what *svadharma* could mean now.
3. **Bernard Williams** — *Moral Luck* (1981) and "Persons, Character,
   and Morality." The "ground project" critique of impartialist ethics
   resonates with *svadharma*; but Williams worries about the very kind
   of moral self-loss the Gita seems to recommend.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

1. **Self-Determination Theory** — Edward Deci and Richard Ryan
   (Rochester). Intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation; autonomy,
   competence, relatedness as basic psychological needs. Speaks to
   "act without attachment to fruits."
2. **Flow research** — Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi. Optimal experience as
   absorption in the act, autotelic activity. Speaks to action where
   the doer-doing-done collapses.
3. **Habit / basal ganglia neuroscience** — Ann Graybiel (MIT) on
   habit chunking; speaks to "no one can remain inactive even for an
   instant."

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### Anchor verse 3.5 — "no one can remain actionless"

**Sanskrit (Devanagari, common transliteration):**
*na hi kashchit kshanam api jatu tishthaty akarmakrit /*
*karyate hy avashah karma sarvah prakriti-jair gunaih //*

**Translations pulled:**

- Easwaran (Nilgiri Press, 2007 ed., p. 99 region):
  > "No one can remain without action even for a moment. Indeed, all
  > beings are compelled to act by their *gunas*."
  Confidence: **paraphrase** of standard Easwaran rendering. Wording
  closely tracks his idiom but should be marked unconfirmed at exact
  punctuation. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)

- Mascaró (Penguin, 1962, p. 56 region):
  > "And no one can ever rest from his work. We are all driven to action
  > by the forces of nature."
  Confidence: **paraphrase / closely tracking**. Mascaró's voice is
  highly identifiable. (edition unconfirmed; validation pending)

- W.J. Johnson (Oxford, 1994):
  > "Indeed, no one ever, even for a moment, exists without performing
  > action; everyone, regardless of their will, is made to perform
  > action by the constituents which originate from material nature."
  Confidence: **closely tracking** Johnson's careful philological
  phrasing. (validation pending)

- Miller (Bantam/Columbia, 1986):
  > "No one exists for even an instant without performing action;
  > however unwilling, every being is forced to act by the qualities of
  > nature."
  Confidence: **closely tracking** Miller's restrained literary
  voice. (validation pending)

- Radhakrishnan (1948):
  > "For no one can remain even for a moment without doing work; every
  > one is made to act helplessly by the impulses born of nature."
  Confidence: **closely tracking** Radhakrishnan's idiom.
  (validation pending)

### Anchor verse 3.8 — "do your prescribed action"

**Sanskrit:**
*niyatam kuru karma tvam karma jyayo hy akarmanah /*
*sharira-yatrapi cha te na prasidhyed akarmanah //*

**Translations pulled:**

- Mascaró: "Action is greater than inaction: perform therefore thy
  task in life. Even the life of the body could not be if there were
  no action." (paraphrase / closely tracking; validation pending)

- Easwaran: "Perform all work carefully, guided by compassion. Action
  is better than inaction." (paraphrase only; the second clause is
  reliable, the first is interpretive Easwaran flavor)

- Miller: "Perform necessary action; it is more powerful than inaction;
  without action you even fail to sustain your own body." (paraphrase
  closely tracking; validation pending)

### Anchor verse 3.9 — "yajna" (sacrifice as the structure of action)

*yajnarthat karmano 'nyatra loko 'yam karma-bandhanah /*

- Easwaran: "Selfish action imprisons the world. Act selflessly, without
  any thought of personal profit." (paraphrase; Easwaran's pastoral
  voice; validation pending)
- Miller: "Action imprisons the world unless it is done as sacrifice;
  free from attachment, Arjuna, perform action as sacrifice!"
  (paraphrase closely tracking; validation pending)
- Sankara, in commentary: action that is not done for the sake of
  *yajna* (i.e. for the divine, or in the spirit of offering) binds
  the agent to its results.

### Anchor verse 3.21 — "what the great do, others follow"

*yad yad acharati shreshthas tat tad evetaro janah /*

- Mascaró: "In the actions of the best men others find their rule of
  action. The path that a great man follows becomes a guide to the
  world." (paraphrase / closely tracking; validation pending)
- This is a key verse for Gandhi's reading.

### Anchor verse 3.35 — "svadharma"

*shreyan sva-dharmo vigunah para-dharmat svanushthitat /*
*sva-dharme nidhanam shreyah para-dharmo bhayavahah //*

**Translations pulled:**

- Mascaró: "And do thy duty, even if it be humble, rather than
  another's, even if it be great. To die in one's duty is life: to
  live in another's is death." (paraphrase very close to Mascaró's
  signature rendering; validation pending)

- Easwaran: "It is better to perform one's own duties imperfectly than
  to master the duties of another. By fulfilling the obligations he is
  born with, a person never comes to grief." (paraphrase; validation
  pending)

- Miller: "Better to do one's own duty imperfectly than to do another
  man's well; it is better to die in one's own duty; another man's
  duty is perilous." (paraphrase closely tracking; validation pending)

- Johnson (Oxford): "It is better to practice your own inherent duty
  deficiently than another's duty well. It is better to die conducting
  your own duty; the duty of others is perilous." (paraphrase closely
  tracking Johnson's restrained register; validation pending)

- Prabhupada: "It is far better to discharge one's prescribed duties,
  even though faultily, than another's duties perfectly. Destruction
  in the course of performing one's own duty is better than engaging
  in another's duties, for to follow another's path is dangerous."
  (paraphrase tracking Prabhupada's edition; validation pending)

### Commentator pulls (one verbatim or paraphrased substantive
passage per school)

#### Sankara on 3.5

In his *bhashya* on 3.5, Sankara stresses that the verse describes
embodied life under the *gunas* — even the wish to abstain is itself
an act, and the body must continue to be sustained. He uses this to
ground his larger argument that *karma yoga* is the discipline
appropriate to those not yet capable of pure *jnana-nishtha* (steady
abidance in knowledge). The full *jnani* has transcended the need for
prescribed action, but *until then*, action performed without
attachment purifies the mind (*chitta-shuddhi*) and prepares it for
realization.

Confidence: **paraphrase of standard Sankara reading**. Direct
verbatim from Sankara's bhashya in English translation typically uses
the Alladi Mahadeva Sastri translation (1897) — I do not have a
verified verbatim sentence to quote. **Mark as paraphrase** in
chapter and attribute to Sankara's *Gita Bhashya* on 3.5 (edition
unconfirmed; validation pending).

#### Ramanuja on 3.5

Ramanuja, in his *Gita Bhashya*, integrates *karma yoga* into the
larger *bhakti* path: action performed as offering to the supreme
self (*paramatman*) is never bondage; the same action performed for
self-aggrandizement is. For him, the natural compulsion to act is not
a curse but the very medium through which the embodied jiva relates
to the Lord.

Confidence: **paraphrase** of standard Ramanuja position; cite as
Ramanuja, *Gita Bhashya* on 3.5 (Adidevananda or Sampatkumaran
translation, edition unconfirmed; validation pending).

#### Tilak — *Gita Rahasya*

Tilak's central claim, repeated throughout *Gita Rahasya*, is that the
Gita is fundamentally a treatise on *karma yoga* — the philosophy of
energetic action — and that all renunciatory readings impose a
*sannyasi* gloss the text does not warrant. He marshals Chapter 3 as
his strongest evidence.

Sample paraphrase / position (from *Gita Rahasya*, vol. I, edition
unconfirmed): Tilak argues that the entire trajectory of the Gita
moves from Arjuna's despondent wish *not* to act, to Krishna's
articulation of the discipline that would let him act *rightly*, and
that to read this as a recommendation of withdrawal is to invert the
text. Confidence: **paraphrase**, attributed to Tilak's argument in
*Gita Rahasya*; cite as Tilak, *Gita Rahasya* (1915), ch. on karma
yoga (edition / page unconfirmed; validation pending).

#### Gandhi — *Anasakti Yoga* / *Discourses*

Gandhi's translation of the Gita's title for his own rendering is
*Anasakti Yoga* — "the yoga of non-attachment" — which itself is his
core interpretive move. He reads 3.35 as a defense of moral
self-reliance: a person should follow the path that grows out of
their own being, however clumsily, rather than imitate another path
they admire.

Substantive paraphrase (Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga* introduction / *Discourses on
the Gita*): Action without attachment is the essence; renunciation is
not in the act but in the desire for fruit. Confidence: **paraphrase**;
cite Gandhi, *Anasakti Yoga* (introduction), Navajivan Press; or
*Discourses on the Gita* (delivered in Yeravda Prison, 1926, published
posthumously). Edition unconfirmed.

#### Aurobindo — *Essays on the Gita*

Aurobindo treats *yajna* as the metaphysical key of Chapter 3.
Sacrifice is not a ritual subset of action but the inner form of all
right action: a turning of one's energies toward something larger than
the ego.

Closest paraphrase (Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, ch. on the
"Possibility and Purpose of Avatarhood" / chs. on karma yoga, ed.
unconfirmed): action becomes sacrament when it is offered up — when
the doer no longer claims it as his own. Confidence: **paraphrase**,
attributed; cite *Essays on the Gita*, Sri Aurobindo Ashram /
Lotus Press editions, edition unconfirmed; validation pending.

#### Easwaran — pastoral introduction to Ch. 3

Easwaran, in his commentary, repeatedly frames Chapter 3 as the
practical dismantling of the romantic idea of withdrawal. He reads
*karma yoga* as the discipline of working "for the welfare of all"
(*lokasangraha*) without making the work a vehicle for the small
self. Confidence: **paraphrase** of Easwaran's well-known editorial
voice; cite Easwaran, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, 2007 ed.,
introduction to Ch. 3 or *Essence of the Bhagavad Gita*); edition
unconfirmed.

#### Miller / Patton — academic-literary

Miller frames the chapter dramatically: Krishna meets Arjuna's longing
for a way out by closing every exit. Patton (Penguin, 2008)
foregrounds the dialogic structure — that Arjuna *asks* the question
that opens the chapter, and the chapter is genuinely an answer, not a
monologue.

Confidence: **paraphrase of standard scholarly framing**; cite
Miller's introduction to her *Bhagavad-Gita* (Bantam, 1986) and
Patton's introduction (Penguin, 2008). Edition / page unconfirmed.

### Modern counterpoint pulls

#### Hannah Arendt — *The Human Condition* (1958)

Arendt's threefold of *labor / work / action* gives a vocabulary the
Gita does not have. *Labor* is the cyclic activity that sustains
biological life (and is "imprisoning" in the very sense the Gita
gives *yajnarthat karmano 'nyatra*). *Work* is the fabrication of
durable objects. *Action* in Arendt's strict sense is appearing in
public among others, beginning something new.

Direct quotation, *The Human Condition*, "Prologue" / Ch. 1:

> "With word and deed we insert ourselves into the human world, and
> this insertion is like a second birth, in which we confirm and take
> upon ourselves the naked fact of our original physical appearance."
> (Arendt, *The Human Condition*, 2nd ed., University of Chicago
> Press, 1998, p. 176–177 region; locator unconfirmed; validation
> pending)

Confidence: **closely tracking Arendt's exact phrasing of the
"second birth" line** — this is one of the most-quoted sentences in
*The Human Condition* and is reasonably stable. Mark as
**paraphrase / closely tracking** until validation.

Substantive paraphrase to use: Arendt argues that purely contemplative
withdrawal is itself a stance — a refusal to begin — and that to be
human is to act among others, with all the unpredictability that
entails.

#### Alasdair MacIntyre — *After Virtue* (1981)

MacIntyre's notion of a *practice* — an activity with internal goods
not reducible to external rewards — gives a clean modern frame for
*svadharma* + *karma yoga*. The internal good of the practice is the
yajna; the external reward is the *phala*.

Direct quotation candidate, *After Virtue*, ch. 14, "The Nature of the
Virtues":

> "By a 'practice' I am going to mean any coherent and complex form of
> socially established cooperative human activity through which goods
> internal to that form of activity are realized in the course of trying
> to achieve those standards of excellence which are appropriate to,
> and partially definitive of, that form of activity..."
> (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, 3rd ed., University of Notre Dame
> Press, 2007, p. 187; locator approximate; validation pending)

Confidence: **closely tracking MacIntyre's well-known definition** of
a practice. Mark as **paraphrase / closely tracking** until
validation; or trim to a shorter, safer quote: "any coherent and
complex form of socially established cooperative human activity
through which goods internal to that form of activity are
realized..." This phrase, virtually verbatim, is widely circulated.

#### Bernard Williams — "Persons, Character, and Morality" (1976/1981)

Williams's concept of a "ground project" — the projects that give a
person a reason to go on living — is in productive tension with
*karma yoga*. Williams worries that impartialist ethics asks people
to cancel the very projects that make them themselves. The Gita
seems, on first reading, to ask exactly that. The synthesis must hold
the tension.

Quotation candidate, "Persons, Character, and Morality" (in *Moral
Luck*, Cambridge UP, 1981):

> "Unless such things [ground projects] exist, there will be no
> categorical desire for him to go on at all..."
> (Williams, *Moral Luck*, Cambridge UP, 1981, p. 13; locator
> approximate; validation pending)

Confidence: **paraphrase / closely tracking**; mark as such in chapter.

### Modern science pulls

#### Self-Determination Theory — Deci & Ryan

Edward Deci and Richard Ryan have spent five decades developing
Self-Determination Theory (SDT). Foundational paper: Deci, E. L., &
Ryan, R. M. (1985). *Intrinsic motivation and self-determination in
human behavior*. New York: Plenum.

Bounded claim: across hundreds of studies, SDT finds that activities
performed for their intrinsic value (engagement, mastery,
relatedness) tend to produce more sustained effort, better performance
on complex tasks, and higher subjective well-being than activities
performed for external rewards. The classic experimental finding (Deci,
1971): introducing extrinsic monetary rewards for an already
intrinsically motivating puzzle task *reduced* subsequent free-choice
engagement.

Paper to cite: Deci, E. L. (1971). "Effects of externally mediated
rewards on intrinsic motivation." *Journal of Personality and Social
Psychology*, 18(1), 105–115.

Bound on the claim: the "undermining effect" is robust but
context-sensitive — it appears most clearly with tangible, contingent
rewards on already-engaging tasks; for tedious tasks, extrinsic
rewards do not undermine intrinsic motivation (because there isn't
much to undermine). I will state the bound.

Confidence: **confirmed substance**; phrasing of the claim is mine.

#### Flow research — Csikszentmihalyi

Csikszentmihalyi's body of work on optimal experience (*Flow: The
Psychology of Optimal Experience*, Harper & Row, 1990) defines flow as
absorption in an activity such that self-consciousness recedes and the
activity becomes "autotelic" — done for its own sake.

Bounded claim: in interview and ESM (experience sampling method)
studies across rock climbers, surgeons, chess players, factory
workers, and others, Csikszentmihalyi consistently finds that flow
arises when challenge and skill are both high and roughly matched, and
that flow states correlate with reported well-being.

Direct quotation candidate, *Flow* (1990):

> "The best moments usually occur when a person's body or mind is
> stretched to its limits in a voluntary effort to accomplish
> something difficult and worthwhile."
> (Csikszentmihalyi, *Flow*, Harper Perennial Modern Classics, 2008
> ed., p. 3; locator approximate; validation pending)

Confidence: **closely tracking** — this is one of Csikszentmihalyi's
most-quoted lines and appears in his preface region. Mark
**paraphrase / closely tracking** until validation.

#### Habit / basal ganglia — Ann Graybiel

Graybiel, A. M. (2008). "Habits, rituals, and the evaluative brain."
*Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 31, 359–387.

Bounded claim: Graybiel and colleagues at MIT have shown that habits —
sequences of behavior — become "chunked" in basal ganglia circuits,
so that once initiated, they run as units with reduced cortical
involvement. This gives empirical structure to the Gita's claim that
"no one can remain inactive": much of human life is run by chunked
sequences that proceed without conscious initiation.

Confidence: **confirmed researcher and confirmed approximate finding**;
phrasing of the claim is mine.
