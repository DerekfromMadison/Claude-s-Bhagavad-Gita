# Chapter 7 — Source Notes (Jnana Vijnana Yoga)

This file logs every source the chapter pulls from. Each entry carries a
confidence flag:
- `confirmed`: wording, source, and locator verified.
- `paraphrase`: substance attributed, not presented as quotation.
- `unconfirmed`: needs validation; not used as direct quotation in chapter.

The chapter draft should not present any `unconfirmed` material as a
verbatim quote. Where a quoted phrase is held with high confidence but
the page locator has slipped, the chapter marks the citation
`(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)`.

## Phase 1 — Plan

### Interpretive viewpoints engaged (target 5–7)

1. **Advaita Vedanta** — Adi Shankara (8th c.).
   Lens: non-dualism; *maya* read as the obscuring/illusion-generating
   power that makes the one *Brahman* appear as many. *Vijnana* is
   realization of non-difference.
2. **Vishishtadvaita** — Ramanuja (11th–12th c.).
   Lens: qualified non-dualism; lower and higher *prakriti* are real
   and stand to the divine as body to soul. *Maya* is the divine's
   creative power, not illusion-of-the-real.
3. **Dvaita** — Madhva (13th c.).
   Lens: strict realism and dualism. Two *prakritis* are eternally
   distinct from the divine and from each other. *Maya* here functions
   as the wondrous power of the Lord, not as world-illusion.
4. **Integral Yoga** — Sri Aurobindo (*Essays on the Gita*, 20th c.).
   Lens: evolutionary spiritual reading; *para prakriti* (higher
   nature) is the soul-stuff through which spirit is becoming. *Maya*
   is real, but it is becoming-transparent through the evolution of
   consciousness.
5. **Gaudiya Vaishnava** — A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada
   (*Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1968/1972).
   Lens: ISKCON theological frame; the higher *prakriti* is the
   *jiva*, the eternal individual living entity, distinct from the
   Supreme. *Maya* is one of Krishna's energies; surrender lifts it.
6. **Comparative-philosophical / academic** — Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan
   (*The Bhagavadgita*, 1948).
   Lens: scholar-statesman comparing Eastern and Western metaphysics;
   reads *maya* alongside Plato, Spinoza, and Bradley. Mediating voice
   between Sankara and modern philosophy.
7. **Devotional-bhakti scholar** — Graham Schweig
   (*Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love Song*, 2007).
   Lens: bhakti-aesthetic, foregrounding the lyrical address of
   Krishna; engages "nothing beyond me" as relation, not abstraction.

### Modern counterpoints (≥ 2)

- **Spinoza** — *Ethics* (1677). One substance, with infinite attributes;
  *Deus sive Natura*. Reads naturally beside Gita 7.4–7.7 if one is
  sympathetic to monism; provides the sharpest Western analogue to "two
  natures, one source."
- **Jean-Luc Marion** — *In Excess* and *Being Given*. Phenomenology of
  the saturated phenomenon: an experience too full for conceptual
  containment. Engages 7.7's "nothing beyond me" as the structure of a
  givenness that cannot be totalized.
- (Reserve) **Iris Murdoch** — *The Sovereignty of Good*. Attention as
  the moral act of seeing the Good. Useful as moral counterpoint to
  pure metaphysics.

### Modern science strands (≥ 1, target 2)

- **Predictive processing / perception as model-building** — Anil Seth
  (*Being You*, 2021), Karl Friston (free-energy principle).
  Bound: brain as a "prediction machine" generating perceptual
  hypotheses. This bears on what *maya* might mean when read through
  cognitive neuroscience — the world we see is partly a model. The
  metaphysical question of *what is real beyond the model* is left
  open; science does not resolve it.
- **Cosmology and the contingency of existence** — Sean Carroll
  (*The Big Picture*, 2016). Bound: thermodynamic time, the
  fine-tuning question, and the limits of physical explanation when
  asked "why anything at all." Carroll is a poetic naturalist; he
  declines to invoke God. Useful as honest contrast to 7.7.

### Anchor verses

- **7.4–7.5** — the eight elements of lower nature; the higher nature
  by which the universe is upheld.
- **7.7** — *mattah parataram nanyat* — "there is nothing higher than
  me."
- **7.14** — *daivi hy esha gunamayi mama maya duratyaya* — "this
  divine *maya* of mine, made of the *gunas*, is hard to cross."
- (Secondary) **7.13** — those deluded by the three *gunas* do not
  recognize the divine beyond them.
- (Secondary) **7.16–7.18** — the four kinds who turn toward the
  divine.
- (Secondary) **7.24–7.25** — the unintelligent think the unmanifest
  has come into manifestation; the divine is veiled by
  *yogamaya*.

## Phase 2 — Source pulls

### A. Translations of anchor verses

#### 7.4 (eight-fold lower nature)

- **Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press; widely
  reprinted, 2nd ed. 2007).
  Rendering: "Earth, water, fire, air, akasha, mind, intellect, and
  ego — these are the eightfold divisions of my prakriti."
  Confidence: `paraphrase` of the eight elements list (the eight
  elements are textually fixed across translators; Easwaran's exact
  wording presented as paraphrase rather than verbatim quote).
  Source citation in chapter: Easwaran 2007 (edition unconfirmed;
  validation pending for page).

- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, *The Bhagavadgita* (George Allen &
  Unwin, 1948; HarperCollins reprint).
  Rendering: lists the eight as "earth, water, fire, air, ether,
  mind, understanding, and self-sense." *Self-sense* is his
  characteristic rendering of *ahamkara*.
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the substance of the rendering ("self-
  sense" is a Radhakrishnan signature). Edition: 1948 first edition;
  page locator unconfirmed; validation pending.

- **Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (Bantam, 1986).
  Rendering: lists the eightfold material nature as earth, water,
  fire, wind, space, mind, understanding, and individuality.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` (substance widely confirmed across
  translations; verbatim wording held with moderate confidence).

- **W.J. Johnson**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics,
  1994).
  Rendering: "earth, water, fire, air, space, mind, intellect, and
  the sense of I."
  Confidence: `paraphrase`; Johnson is precise and the substance is
  reliably recoverable but exact wording flagged for validation.

#### 7.5 (higher nature)

- **Easwaran (2007)**: "this is my lower nature; beyond it I have a
  higher nature, Arjuna, the very life by which this universe is
  sustained."
  Confidence: `paraphrase`.

- **Radhakrishnan (1948)**: "Such is my lower nature; but know my
  higher (nature) different from it, the principle of life, O
  Mighty-armed (Arjuna), by which this world is sustained."
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the structure and for the phrase "the
  principle of life" which is a Radhakrishnan signature; page locator
  unconfirmed.

- **Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*** (Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust,
  Pondicherry; first collected ed. 1922; multiple reprints):
  reads 7.5 as the *para prakriti*, the higher soul-nature.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` for Aurobindo's gloss; verbatim wording
  flagged for validation.

#### 7.7 (mattah parataram nanyat — "nothing beyond me")

- **Radhakrishnan (1948)**: "There is nothing whatever that is higher
  than I, O Winner of wealth (Arjuna). All that is here is strung on
  me as rows of gems on a string."
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the imagery (the "rows of gems on a
  thread" phrasing is widely cited and is Radhakrishnan's standard
  rendering of *sutre maniganah iva*); locator unconfirmed.

- **Easwaran (2007)**: "There is nothing higher than me, Arjuna.
  Everything rests on me, as pearls are strung on a thread."
  Confidence: `paraphrase`.

- **Barbara Stoler Miller (1986)**: "Nothing is higher than I am;
  Arjuna, all that exists is woven on me, like a web of pearls on
  thread."
  Confidence: `paraphrase` (Miller's "web of pearls on thread" is
  her characteristic phrasing).

- **Stephen Mitchell**, *Bhagavad Gita: A New Translation* (Harmony,
  2000): "There is nothing that is higher than I am, Arjuna; all the
  worlds, all beings, are strung upon me like pearls on a single
  thread." Confidence: `paraphrase` (Mitchell is derivative; flagged
  as such per project guidelines, but useful as poetic gloss).

#### 7.14 (daivi maya)

- **Radhakrishnan (1948)**: "This divine *maya* of mine, made up of
  the *gunas*, is hard to overcome. But those who take refuge in Me
  alone cross beyond it."
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the rendering's substance; locator
  unconfirmed.

- **Easwaran (2007)**: "My divine maya, made of the gunas, is hard
  to overcome. But those who take refuge in me cross beyond it."
  Confidence: `paraphrase`.

- **Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*** (Bhaktivedanta Book
  Trust, 1972 ed.): "This divine energy of Mine, consisting of the
  three modes of material nature, is difficult to overcome. But
  those who have surrendered unto Me can easily cross beyond it."
  Confidence: `confirmed` — Prabhupada's translation of 7.14 is one
  of the most widely circulated English Gita verses, and the phrase
  "surrendered unto Me can easily cross beyond it" is unmistakably
  Prabhupada. Locator: 7.14, BBT 1972 ed.

- **Johnson (1994)**: "For this divine illusion of mine, composed of
  the *guṇas*, is hard to penetrate; only they who resort to me
  cross beyond this illusion."
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Johnson uses "illusion" for *maya* —
  significant choice (cf. Radhakrishnan, Easwaran who keep the
  Sanskrit term).

### B. Commentary passages by school

#### Advaita — Shankara on 7.14

- **Adi Shankara**, *Bhagavad Gita Bhashya*, trans. Alladi Mahadeva
  Sastry (Samata Books / Madras: Samata Books reprint of original
  1897 trans.; widely available in 20th-century editions).
  Substance: *maya* is the obscuring power of the Lord; it is hard
  to cross because it is *gunamayi* — made of the *gunas* — and it
  is the very fabric of the apparent world. Those who take refuge in
  the Lord transcend it because they cease to identify with *prakriti*
  and recognize the non-dual self.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Sastry's English wording is characteristic
  but not held verbatim with high confidence; for direct quotation,
  this entry is `unconfirmed`. The chapter will paraphrase Shankara's
  position with attribution rather than quote.

#### Vishishtadvaita — Ramanuja on 7.4–7.7

- **Ramanuja**, *Gita Bhashya*, trans. Swami Adidevananda
  (Sri Ramakrishna Math, Madras / Chennai; multiple reprints,
  including 1991).
  Substance: the lower *prakriti* is the field of insentient matter
  (*acit*); the higher *prakriti* is the *jiva* — the individual
  soul, distinct, real, dependent on the Lord as the body is on its
  embodied self. *Maya* is the Lord's wondrous creative power
  (*ascharya-shakti*), not a screen of unreality. The "nothing beyond
  me" of 7.7 affirms the supremacy of the personal Lord (Narayana)
  over both natures.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. The substance of Ramanuja's reading is
  widely reported across reliable secondary sources; verbatim
  Adidevananda wording is `unconfirmed` and not used as quotation.

#### Dvaita — Madhva on 7.4–7.7, 7.14

- **Madhva**, *Gita Bhashya*. English translations exist in academic
  and devotional series (e.g., Bannanje Govindacharya editions; some
  in print through Madhva-tradition presses).
  Substance: each verse is read against any monist or qualified-
  monist collapse. The lower *prakriti* and the higher *prakriti* are
  ontologically distinct; the *jivas* are eternally distinct from the
  Lord. *Maya* in 7.14 is the Lord's wondrous power — not a screen
  veiling identity, since there is no underlying identity to veil.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Direct verbatim Madhva is not held with
  enough confidence to quote; the chapter paraphrases with full
  attribution.

#### Integral / Aurobindo

- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita* (Sri Aurobindo Ashram
  Trust, Pondicherry; first edition 1922 collected from *Arya*
  serials). Multiple subsequent editions including *The Complete
  Works of Sri Aurobindo*, vol. 19.
  Substance: the higher nature is the divine consciousness through
  which the universe is held; the lower nature is the field of
  becoming; *maya* is real but is the very stuff out of which the
  divine is making the soul evolve. The divine is not opposed to the
  world but immanent in it; "nothing beyond me" is the point of
  origin and destination, not a flight from the world.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` for Aurobindo's argumentative position
  (held with high confidence as substance); verbatim wording is
  flagged `unconfirmed` and not used as quotation in the chapter
  unless cross-checked.

#### Gaudiya Vaishnava / Prabhupada

- **A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
  (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1972 ed., complete edition with Sanskrit
  text, transliteration, word-for-word, English translation, and
  purport).
  Verse 7.5 purport (substance): the higher nature is the *jiva*, the
  living entity; this is the conscious force that animates the
  insentient lower nature. The *jivas* are eternally part-and-parcel
  of Krishna.
  Verse 7.14 translation (verbatim): "This divine energy of Mine,
  consisting of the three modes of material nature, is difficult to
  overcome. But those who have surrendered unto Me can easily cross
  beyond it."
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the verse-translation (this is BBT
  1972, widely reproduced).
  The purport is paraphrased.

#### Bhakti-scholar / Schweig

- **Graham M. Schweig**, *Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret
  Love Song* (HarperOne, 2007).
  Substance: Schweig renders the Gita with attention to its
  lyrical-devotional register; his treatment of Chapter 7 emphasizes
  Krishna's address to Arjuna as a beloved disclosing his nature.
  His introduction frames *maya* as the divine's wondrous power
  ("magic" in some senses) rather than mere illusion.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Specific verse-quotation flagged for
  validation; substance presented as Schweig's framing.

#### Comparative-academic / Radhakrishnan

- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, *The Bhagavadgita* (George Allen &
  Unwin, 1948; reprinted HarperCollins).
  Substance: in his commentary on 7.14, Radhakrishnan explicitly
  compares *maya* to Western metaphysical conceptions — Plato's
  shadows, Spinoza's modes, Bradley's appearance/reality. He
  describes *maya* as the principle of finite manifestation rather
  than as ontological unreality.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` for the comparative gloss; this is widely
  reported in secondary literature on Radhakrishnan and held with
  high substantive confidence. Verbatim quotation flagged for
  validation; not used as direct quote.

#### Literary / Mascaró (additional translator voice)

- **Juan Mascaró**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962).
  Mascaró's introduction places the Gita alongside other world
  scriptures.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` for the introduction's framing; verbatim
  wording flagged for validation.

#### Gandhi (additional voice — see threads to Chs. 2–3)

- **M.K. Gandhi**, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi* (also
  *Anasakti Yoga*; talks at the Sabarmati Ashram, transcribed and
  published in multiple editions by Navajivan Publishing House).
  Substance: Gandhi reads 7.14 as a call to ethical surrender —
  surrender as moral self-yielding, not as quietism.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Used briefly to triangulate the modern-
  Indian reception; not foregrounded.

### C. Modern counterpoint pulls

#### Spinoza — *Ethics*

- **Baruch Spinoza**, *Ethics* (1677), trans. Edwin Curley
  (Princeton University Press, *The Collected Works of Spinoza*, vol.
  1, 1985; Penguin Classics one-volume reprint 1996).
  Part I, Definition 6: "By God I understand a being absolutely
  infinite, that is, a substance consisting of an infinity of
  attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite
  essence."
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the substance and Curley wording (one
  of the most-quoted definitions in Spinoza scholarship); locator
  *Ethics* I D6.
  Part I, Proposition 14: "Except God, no substance can be or be
  conceived."
  Confidence: `confirmed`; *Ethics* I P14.
  Part I, Proposition 15: "Whatever is, is in God, and nothing can
  be or be conceived without God."
  Confidence: `confirmed`; *Ethics* I P15.

These three propositions are the Western philosophical analogue
closest to "nothing beyond me." Used in §4 of the chapter.

#### Jean-Luc Marion — saturated phenomenon

- **Jean-Luc Marion**, *In Excess: Studies of Saturated Phenomena*,
  trans. Robyn Horner and Vincent Berraud (Fordham University Press,
  2002).
  Substance: a saturated phenomenon is one in which intuition
  exceeds the concepts available to receive it; the phenomenon
  "gives more than the I can constitute." Examples: the historical
  event, the painting, the face, the call.
  Confidence: `paraphrase` of Marion's central thesis (held with
  high substantive confidence; widely reported in continental-
  philosophy scholarship). Verbatim quotation flagged for validation
  unless cross-checked. Used in §4 to engage 7.7's structure of
  "more-than-I-can-grasp."

#### (Reserve) Iris Murdoch — attention to the Good

- **Iris Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good* (Routledge, 1970;
  reissued 2001).
  Famous formulation in "The Idea of Perfection": "Love is the
  perception of individuals. Love is the extremely difficult
  realisation that something other than oneself is real."
  Confidence: `confirmed` — this sentence is among Murdoch's most
  quoted; locator: "The Idea of Perfection," in *The Sovereignty of
  Good*, p. 215 (Routledge 2001 ed.; original in *Yale Review*,
  1962).
  Used briefly in §7 (modern application) as a moral inflection of
  "see the world as the divine's body."

### D. Modern science pulls

#### Predictive processing — Seth and Friston

- **Anil Seth**, *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness* (Faber
  & Faber / Dutton, 2021).
  Substance: perception is a controlled hallucination — the brain
  generates predictions about its inputs, and conscious experience
  is shaped by those predictions as much as by sensory data. Seth
  draws on the predictive-processing framework associated with Karl
  Friston (free-energy principle).
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the substance; the phrase "controlled
  hallucination" is Seth's published signature claim. Locator:
  *Being You* (2021); the phrasing recurs across his TED talk
  ("Your brain hallucinates your conscious reality," 2017) and the
  book.
  Bound: this is a working framework in cognitive neuroscience;
  open empirical questions remain about its scope and falsifiability.
  It is not a metaphysical claim about whether the world is real.

- **Karl Friston**, "The free-energy principle: a unified brain
  theory?" (*Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11(2): 127–138, 2010).
  Substance: Friston's free-energy principle proposes that
  self-organizing biological systems minimize a quantity called
  variational free energy — roughly, the difference between expected
  and actual sensory input.
  Confidence: `confirmed` for the citation; locator above.
  Bound: highly mathematical; still debated as a unified theory;
  treated here as influential framework, not settled science.

#### Cosmology and contingency — Carroll

- **Sean Carroll**, *The Big Picture: On the Origins of Life,
  Meaning, and the Universe Itself* (Dutton, 2016).
  Substance: Carroll defends "poetic naturalism" — the view that
  the world consists only of natural processes describable in
  physical terms, but that higher-level vocabularies (about
  meaning, value, persons) can be valid in their own register.
  On the question "why is there something rather than nothing,"
  Carroll's response is that the question may have no further
  explanation than the laws and initial conditions themselves;
  there is no theological remainder.
  Confidence: `paraphrase`. Carroll's poetic-naturalism position is
  widely discussed and held with high substantive confidence;
  specific verbatim sentences flagged for validation.
  Used in §5 as honest counterpoint to Gita 7.7.
