# Synthesis Notes — Chapter 17 (Sraddhatraya Vibhaga Yoga)

These are the working notes that became the spine of § 6 of the chapter.
They are reasoning-out-loud for myself, expanded beyond what the chapter
shows.

---

## The interpretive question that organizes the chapter

Chapter 17 makes a strange and powerful claim in 17.3: *yo yacchraddhaḥ
sa eva saḥ* — "as is one's faith, so is one." A person *is* what they
trust at the level beneath words. The rest of the chapter then tries to
make that faith legible by classifying its expressions: food, sacrifice,
austerity, gift — each rendered threefold by the *gunas* — closing with
the formula *Om Tat Sat*.

The interpretive question is: **what kind of work is the *guna* typology
doing here?** The schools answer differently:

- **Ritualist / textual reading:** the typology is a literal taxonomy of
  ritual practices, anchored in late-Vedic ritual culture. *Sattvic* food
  is what an *agnihotrin* eats; *sattvic* sacrifice is the prescribed
  one; *Om Tat Sat* are ritual utterances. The chapter is read as a
  ritual handbook.
- **Theological reading (Vaishnava, including Prabhupada):** the typology
  ranks practices by their orientation to the personal divine. *Sattvic*
  faith is the one that already turns toward the Supreme; the lower
  modes are dispersed or perverted. *Om Tat Sat* names the Lord.
- **Advaita reading (Shankara):** the typology classifies the practices
  of those still embedded in the *gunas*, en route to a *jnana* that
  transcends the typology entirely. The chapter is preparatory.
- **Activist / Gandhian reading:** the typology is a moral diagnostic.
  Conduct, diet, austerity, gift — not declaration — reveal the actual
  creed. Faith is read out of practice.
- **Integral reading (Aurobindo):** the typology is developmental. Each
  *guna* names a stage of the soul's gravitational pull; the work is
  to move from *tamas* through *rajas* into *sattva* and then to a
  surrender beyond all three.
- **Psychological reading (Easwaran, contemplative-pastoral):** the
  typology is self-examination. What you actually trust shows in what
  you eat, what you discipline, what you give. Faith is what your life
  argues, not what your mouth claims.
- **Literary-academic reading (Miller, Patton, Johnson):** the typology
  is a teaching device — Krishna gives Arjuna a diagnostic apparatus
  appropriate to a moment of decision; the *Om Tat Sat* coda anchors
  it to ritual *but* the reach of the chapter is anthropological more
  than ritualistic.

## Why I am choosing the psychological-developmental reading

The reading I weave forward in § 6 sits closest to the activist /
Gandhian, the Aurobindonian, and the contemplative-pastoral readings —
with the academic literary readings as the ground-level constraint
against over-allegorizing.

The reasons:

1. **Textual fidelity.** The verse 17.3 explicitly says a person is
   *constituted* by their *sraddha*. This is anthropological. The
   chapter then immediately moves into examples drawn from ordinary
   life (food! gifts! austerity!) — not from temple ritual exclusively.
   The chapter's own structure resists the pure-ritualist reduction.
2. **Coherence with the rest of the Gita.** *Sraddha* is named earlier
   (4.39, 9.3, 12.20) as what enables the seeker to move on the path
   at all. Chapter 17 develops what was already there: faith is the
   substrate of practice. To read the chapter as a ritual handbook is
   to amputate it from the prior 16 chapters.
3. **Lived usefulness.** The reading "you are what you trust, and what
   you trust shows in your conduct" is doing real work for a present-
   day reader. The reading "the *agnihotrin* should eat such-and-such"
   is doing very little.
4. **Honesty under modern psychology.** The values-clarification
   literature (ACT, Hayes) and Rozin's work on food, disgust, and
   moral identity converge with the psychological-developmental reading
   in a non-trivial way. Lived values *do* show up in committed action
   under pressure; food choices *do* function as identity markers. The
   modern science is not mere decoration here — it is corroborative.
5. **Generosity to the rejected readings.** The ritualist reading is
   right that the chapter *is* anchored to ritual — *Om Tat Sat* is
   liturgical language and the chapter's coda explicitly says so. I
   absorb this: any reading that strips the ritual anchor entirely is
   missing something. But I read the ritual anchor as *one expression*
   of the underlying anthropological claim, not as the claim itself.
   The Vaishnava theological reading is right that *sraddha* is
   directed; faith has an object and the choice of object matters. I
   absorb this: I do not flatten *sraddha* into "any commitment will
   do." Some objects of trust are better than others, and the *guna*
   typology is one way of saying so.

## What I set aside

I set aside the *strong* sectarian reading that says the *sattvic*
faith is already and only the faith that turns to a particular named
deity. The chapter does not say that. It says the *sattvic* faith
turns toward the *devas* (the gods, in 17.4) — not toward a sectarian
named-supreme. To read 17.4 as already an ISKCON-style theological
declaration is to import later sectarian commitments into the verse.
I name this in the chapter and let Prabhupada's reading speak in his
own voice; I do not adopt it.

I also set aside the *strong* allegorical reading that empties the
chapter of any reference to actual food, actual gifts, actual
austerities. Gandhi is right that the chapter is a moral diagnostic;
he is wrong if his reading collapses the literal layer. The literal
*ahara* matters; what you eat matters. The chapter does not stop being
about food when it becomes about more than food.

## What the modern science layer changes

The science layer is unusually well-fitted to this chapter, because the
chapter is explicitly making a claim that is *psychological* — that
inner orientation manifests outwardly. Two strands constrain and
support the chosen reading:

1. **ACT values clarification (Hayes).** This is the closest modern
   analogue to what 17.3 is saying. Lived values surface under
   commitment-pressure, not in self-report. This both supports and
   sharpens the chosen reading: the chapter is right that faith is
   constitutive, *and* the chapter underspecifies how to clarify one's
   own faith — modern values work fills in a method.
2. **Rozin on food, disgust, moralization.** Food choices function as
   identity-markers; disgust crosses from food into morality. This is
   strong empirical support for the chapter's claim that what you eat
   reveals who you are — but it also limits the claim. The chapter's
   *guna* taxonomy of food does not survive translation into 21st-
   century nutritional science untouched. I name this. I do not pretend
   *sattvic* food maps onto modern nutrition; I do say that food *as
   identity-marker* is real and well-documented.

A third strand worth surfacing — Carol Dweck on mindset — shows that
implicit theories function operationally as a kind of faith: what you
believe about your own capacity shapes what you can do. This isn't
exactly *sraddha*, but it rhymes with it. I surface it in § 5 with
appropriate caveats (and noting the replication conversation in
mindset research).

## How § 6 will be structured

1. State the choice plainly: I read the chapter as a psychological-
   developmental anthropology of faith with a ritual anchor preserved.
2. Show why textual fidelity, coherence, lived usefulness, and modern
   psychology converge on this reading.
3. Name what I set aside (sectarian reading, pure-ritualist reading,
   over-allegorizing reading) and what I take from each.
4. Say what *Om Tat Sat* does in the chosen reading: not a magic
   formula, not a sectarian name, but the chapter's reminder that any
   honest practice points beyond itself toward the Real.
5. Say what 17.3's claim demands of a reader: that they look at their
   life — not their stated beliefs — to see what they actually trust.

## How § 7 (modern application) will land

The application is unusually concrete because the chapter is unusually
concrete. The four diagnostic axes — food, sacrifice, austerity, gift
— translate cleanly into present-day life:

- **Food** → diet, but also media diet, information diet, social-
  feed diet.
- **Sacrifice** → what you offer your time and attention to; the
  unpaid labor you give without resentment.
- **Austerity** → the disciplines you undertake; what you say no to;
  how you talk to yourself.
- **Gift** → what you give without strings; the receiver who can
  never repay.

For each axis I will name a concrete present-day situation — not
"modern life" in the abstract — and show how the *guna* diagnostic
illuminates a specific choice. I will close with the sharp
implication of 17.3: your stated beliefs are not your faith. Your
calendar is your faith. Your bank statement is your faith. Your
browser history is your faith. The Gita is willing to say this. So
am I.
