# Chapter 6 — Synthesis Notes (Dhyana Yoga)

These notes are the reasoning behind the chapter's § 6 (Synthesis). They
are written for myself, not for the reader — the chapter compresses them.

---

## What the chapter has to do

Chapter 6 is the most directly *practical* chapter in the Gita. It is also
the one that lends itself most easily to two modern misreadings:

1. **The proto-mindfulness misreading.** The Gita's *dhyana* gets flattened
   into secular attention training. Krishna becomes a yoga app.
2. **The escapist misreading.** The withdrawn lone yogi of 6.10–14, in a
   clean place with the right cushion, gets read as the goal — and the
   householder reader closes the book.

Both readings have the verses to point at. Both readings ignore other
verses that contradict them. The chapter has to refuse both without
becoming a third reductive thing.

## The interpretive divergence

The classical readings divide on what *dhyana* is for.

- **Sankara (Advaita).** Meditation is preparation. The point is *jnana* —
  knowing the non-dual self. *Dhyana* steadies the mind so it can hold
  the recognition that has to come from elsewhere (sruti, the teacher,
  the discriminating intellect). Meditation is necessary, but meditation
  is not the goal. The goal is *moksha*, achieved through knowing.
- **Ramanuja (Vishishtadvaita).** Meditation rests on the divine. The
  *atman* you reach in *dhyana* is your real individual self, but that
  self is the body of the Lord. Meditation passes naturally into
  remembrance of the Lord. The transition from chapter 6 to chapter 7,
  on Ramanuja's reading, is exactly this opening from inwardness to
  devotion.
- **Prabhupada (Gaudiya).** *Dhyana yoga* is not the highest yoga. The
  highest is bhakti. Krishna's own statement in 6.47 — that the yogi of
  faith who thinks of him is the highest of all — settles the
  interpretive question for Prabhupada. The whole sixth chapter becomes,
  on this reading, a long approach to its own self-supersession.
- **Aurobindo (Integral).** The silent mind is real, but it is a
  doorway. The actual prize is divine action — action issuing from a
  center that is no longer rajas-driven and no longer tamasic. The lone
  yogi on the cushion is not the picture; the integrated worker in the
  field is.
- **Gandhi (Activist / Anasakti).** 6.5 is the moral charter of
  self-rule. No one else can do this work for you. The battlefield is
  interior. The discipline is concrete: diet, sleep, speech, action.
  Meditation is one of the disciplines, not the discipline.
- **Easwaran (Contemplative-pastoral).** *Dhyana* is daily, slow,
  household-compatible. The repetition of a passage one has chosen and
  made one's own. Practice is what changes you over years, not a
  single technique deployed for relief.
- **Theosophical (Besant).** Meditation is a real psychological
  discipline that produces real psychological change. The "yogi" is a
  genuine human type, not a literary figure.

## What I'm choosing and why

I'm picking the synthesis Aurobindo gestures at — meditation as
cultivation of a center of stillness from which action proceeds — and
folding into it Easwaran's householder pragmatism, Sankara's caution
that meditation is not itself liberation, and Murdoch/Weil's modern
recovery of attention as a moral capacity.

I'm refusing Prabhupada's verticalization (that *dhyana* is hierarchy-
subordinated to *bhakti*) as the *organizing* frame for this chapter,
while preserving his correct observation: 6.47 does point ahead. Krishna
*does* end the chapter by saying the yogi who thinks of him is highest.
The chapter does, in fact, hand off to the next several chapters which
are bhakti-saturated. That handoff is real. But to read all of chapter 6
through 6.47 as Prabhupada does is to read the destination back into
the journey. Sankara's caution — that meditation is preparation — and
Aurobindo's reframing — that the silent mind is a doorway — both warn
us off that move.

I'm refusing the secular-mindfulness flattening for a different reason.
The Gita is plainly making a metaphysical claim about what one finds
when one steadies the mind: the *atman*, the self that is friend or
enemy of the self, the lamp that does not flicker because there is
something to be steady toward. Modern mindfulness is technologically
impressive at quieting the wind. It is mostly silent on what the
unflickering lamp is for. That silence is the difference. I will say so.

## What the rejected readings get right that the chosen reading must absorb

- **Prabhupada is right** that the chapter does end with 6.47 and
  that 6.47 is not decorative. The yogi who is integrated with the
  divine in love is not a footnote; Krishna calls him "highest" with
  what reads as genuine ranking. My synthesis must hand off to chapter
  12's territory cleanly, not by treating 6.47 as a typographical
  surprise.
- **Sankara is right** that meditation cannot be self-grounding. If
  the yogi of 6.19 is steady, *something* is steadying him. The
  question of what that something is — the *atman*, the Lord, simply
  the practiced habit of return — is the real question. Sankara
  presses it; my synthesis cannot let it slide.
- **The secular-mindfulness reading is right** that the *technique*
  of returning the mind to its object is the technique the Gita
  describes. The mechanics are the mechanics. What changes is the
  framing of what the mechanics are *for*.
- **Gandhi is right** that 6.5 is the most uncompromising line in the
  chapter, and that no spiritual program is honest if it lets you
  outsource the work.

## How the modern science layer constrains and supports the chosen reading

The neuroplasticity and DMN findings (Lazar 2005; Brewer 2011) support
two specific claims the Gita makes:

1. **Practice changes the practitioner.** The Gita's *abhyasa* is not
   wishful — repeated mental return appears to have measurable
   structural and functional correlates in long-term practitioners.
2. **Self-referential thinking quiets.** Brewer's DMN finding is the
   single most striking modern correlate of the Gita's claim that the
   yogi's mind no longer wanders into restless self-referential
   imagination.

These findings *bound* what the Gita can claim, too:

1. The effects are dose-dependent. A weekend retreat does not deliver
   what 9 years of practice delivers.
2. The studies are mostly cross-sectional, with small samples, and
   replication has been mixed. The basic phenomenon — that long
   practice changes the brain — is well-supported. The specifics are
   not.
3. None of the studies tells us what the unflickering lamp is *for*.
   That is a question modern science is not equipped to answer, and
   the chapter says so directly.

Amishi Jha's attention-training work supports a related but distinct
Gita claim: that meditation training has protective effects on
attentional capacity under stress. This matters for chapter 6
because the Gita locates meditation in the context of a war — Arjuna
is being asked to do this work in conditions Arjuna himself cannot
control. Jha's military and athletic samples are not Arjunas, but
they are at least closer to him than the lab volunteer with 8 weeks
of MBSR.

## The synthesis position, stated precisely

Meditation, in chapter 6, is the discipline by which the self learns
to stop being its own enemy. Two practices are named together —
*abhyasa* (repeated return) and *vairagya* (loosened grip on
sense-objects) — and neither alone suffices. The mechanics work: the
mind, returned often enough to its object, eventually returns by
default. This is a real psychological fact. The metaphysical claim
the chapter makes on top of the mechanics is that what one returns
*to* is the self that is friend rather than enemy: a steady center,
not a void. Whether that center is the non-dual *atman* (Sankara),
the body of the Lord (Ramanuja), the doorway to divine action
(Aurobindo), the moral charter of self-rule (Gandhi), or the seat of
loving remembrance (Prabhupada) — the chapter does not finally
decide. It says, with Krishna in 6.47, that the yogi who carries this
center toward something larger than the yogi is the yogi most
realized. That is enough for chapter 6 to do. The metaphysical
question is held for the chapters that follow.

## What this means for the present-day reader

Three concrete commitments come out of the chosen reading:

1. **Practice is the work.** Episodic technique-deployment is not.
   *Abhyasa* is structural — daily, repeated, unglamorous, with the
   sit-down often happening on bad days as well as good ones.
2. **Vairagya is the other half.** Without loosening the grip on what
   the senses promise, *abhyasa* hardens into yet another grasping.
   The Gita is precise on this: practice and dispassion together, not
   one without the other.
3. **The center is for something.** A steady mind that is not aimed at
   any larger reality eventually rediscovers that it is just a more
   poised version of the same restless self. The yogi of 6.47 is
   highest because the steadiness is in service of remembrance, of
   love, of action issuing from somewhere clearer than appetite.

The modern application section will name specific situations where
this matters: the day that flies apart at 11 a.m.; the relationship
where one's own reactive pattern is the recurring problem; the work
that rewards either focus or anxiety, and which one delivers on a given
day; the loss that has nowhere to put itself; the phone, which is
6.34's wind given a designer.
