# Chapter 1 — Arjuna's Despair

The chariot has stopped. It is a Sunday night, and the open laptop
glows on the kitchen table where the warrior has been sitting for
three hours. Sanjaya, who sees, watches him pull the chair back from
the screen and rest his face in his hands.

---

I asked to be driven out where I could see them.

I told myself I needed to look at it directly. The spreadsheet is
open. The list is here. Twenty-three names. I know each one. I
hired most of them. I sat across from them in coffee shops and
small conference rooms and told them what we were going to build
together. I asked about their kids. I told them this was a place
they could stay.

I see Maya, who took the offer instead of the other one because I
told her what kind of manager I would be. I see Daniel, six months
from closing on the house. I see Priya, whose husband just started
chemotherapy and who came in last Tuesday and thanked me for the
flexibility, eyes wet, and I nodded and I knew already. I see the
two new hires I closed last quarter — I shook their hands at their
desks on their first day and I knew.

These are not abstractions. These are the people I told a story to
about the kind of company we were. I was the story. They believed
me because I believed me. Now I am the one with the spreadsheet,
the one cutting the names, the one who will say the words on
Tuesday morning that end something for them. The one who will
become, in their telling, the man who sat across from them in a
coffee shop and lied.

I look across the table and I see my first manager — the woman who
hired me out of school, who taught me how to run a one-on-one,
who told me on a hard day fifteen years ago that the work of a
manager was to protect the people doing the work. I see my father,
who came to this country and held the same job for thirty-one
years and was proud of it. I see the friend who started this
company with me and walked away two years in and told me I had
gone soft, that I would not do what was needed when it was needed,
and I said I would, I said I would not become that, and now look.

Every face that taught me what kind of person I wanted to be is
arrayed across the table from the act I am being asked to do.

I have run the numbers. I have run them again. I know the company
does not survive the next year without these cuts. I know that if
I do not do this on Tuesday, the cuts will be larger in three
months, and worse, and they will fall on people who do not deserve
them either. I know that the alternative I keep reaching for —
some version of we'll figure it out, some version of let me try
one more thing — is a story I am telling myself so I do not have
to be the one holding the knife. I know there is no good move.

And still.

If I do this, I lose the people. Not just the twenty-three. The
others, too — the ones who stay. They will see what I am willing
to do. They will know. The trust I have spent six years building
will not survive Tuesday. I will keep my job and I will be alone
in it. I will be the manager they describe to the next person they
work for as the warning. *Watch out for the smile. Watch out for
the speeches about culture. Watch what they actually do when the
quarter goes bad.* I will become the thing I have spent my whole
working life saying I would not become.

If I do this, I lose myself. The version of me that I was trying
to be — the one my first manager would recognize, the one my
father would understand, the one who told the story honestly to
the people in the coffee shops — that person does not survive
Tuesday morning either. I will walk out of the conference room as
someone else. Someone I have spent years refusing to be. I do not
know how to live as that person. I do not know who I am on the
other side of the room.

And if I do not do this — if I refuse — the company fails in six
months and four hundred people lose their jobs instead of
twenty-three, and I will have done that, too, because I could not
bear to be the one who chose. My refusal will not be clean. My
refusal will just be a worse version of the same thing, dressed
up as conscience.

There is no clean exit. I have looked. I have looked for weeks. I
have looked at every angle of this and the only paths out are
paths I do not get to take. There is the path where I am the
manager who does it cleanly and lives with what I become. There is
the path where I refuse and let the larger collapse happen and
live with that. There is the path where I quit tonight and hand
the spreadsheet to someone else and lie to myself that I kept my
hands clean. I see what each one costs. I see that I have to pay
one of them.

And what I notice, sitting here at this table at a quarter past
eleven on a Sunday night, is that I cannot.

My hands will not type. I have been trying for an hour to draft
the email that goes out Monday morning and I cannot write the
sentences. I open the document and I sit and the cursor blinks and
my chest tightens and nothing comes. My breathing is wrong. My
hands feel cold. There is a sound in my ears that has been there
for two days that I think is just a sound my body makes now.

I push the laptop away. I do not close it. I just push it across
the table and lean back and I find I am shaking, very slightly,
the way you shake when you have been holding something heavy for
longer than your body could hold it and you have set it down at
last and the muscles do not know yet that they are allowed to
stop. The screen still glows. The list is still there. Maya,
Daniel, Priya. Twenty-three names. The cursor still blinks where I
left it, in the middle of a sentence I could not finish.

I cannot do this. I do not mean *I should not.* I mean my body
will not. The instrument I have been using to live this life — the
focus, the resolve, the practiced ability to do hard things and
keep moving — has stopped working. It is not available to me
right now. It has gone wherever the body sends things when the
body has decided enough.

I am sitting at the kitchen table. The house is quiet. My wife is
asleep upstairs. The dog has come over and put her head on my foot
and I have not moved my foot. I do not know what I am supposed to
do now. I do not know what comes next. I know I am supposed to
close the laptop and go to bed and wake up tomorrow and do this,
and I am telling you I cannot, I am telling you the part of me
that does the doing is not at the desk.

I am at a threshold and I have run out.

I would rather not be here. I would rather have been wrong about
the numbers. I would rather have a way out I have not found yet.
I would rather be someone who does not flinch. I am none of those
things. I am a man at a kitchen table with a list of names and
hands that will not work and I do not know who is supposed to
help me now.

The laptop hums. The dog breathes against my foot. I sit.

Somewhere in the room, or somewhere closer than the room, a voice
I have not yet heard is about to begin.
