# Chapter 17 — Three Kinds of Faith

**Arjuna:** Krishna, what about the people who act with faith but never read the scriptures, never sat at the feet of a teacher, never learned the proper forms? They give, they keep disciplines, they make their offerings. Are they standing in clarity, in restlessness, or in dullness?

**Krishna:** Faith is born of the substrate. Each person's faith takes the shape of what their nature is made of. A person is what their faith is.

Do not look for faith in the sentences a person speaks about themselves, in what they post, in what they would say if asked. Faith hides under the words and shows in the life. What a person actually trusts shows in what they take in, in what they offer, in what they submit to as practice, in what they give. Four registers. Each one sorts threefold.

The first register is what you take in. The food on the plate, yes — but also what you watch, what you read, what you let through the small screen in your pocket from waking to sleep. Intake of clarity steadies the body, lengthens attention, leaves you settled. Intake of restlessness is sharp, agitating, oversalted, overheated — food and feeds that excite and exhaust in the same motion. Intake of dullness is stale, deadened, what numbs you, what leaves residue without nourishment. The body and mind you have today were built by it.

The second register is what you offer. The unpaid act done because the act itself is the point. Offering of clarity is given as duty, with no eye on the return. Offering of restlessness is made for show — the mentorship taken on for the resume, the board seat for the network, the careful generosity that has a ledger running quietly underneath. Offering of dullness is made without sincerity, or skipped entirely though it was your turn. Most of you, most of the time, do restless offering with a coat of clear language painted over it. You are asked to notice it.

The third register is what you submit to as practice. Discipline of the body: how you sleep, how you eat, whether you walk, whether you sit in a chair for ten hours and call that a life. Discipline of speech: speech that does not agitate, that is true, that is pleasant, that is beneficial — and the steady study of saying it. Discipline of mind: serenity, gentleness, silence, the ungrasping of the small self.

Each submits threefold. Clear discipline is steady, kept without performance, done because the work asks. Restless discipline is the discipline you post about, the practice oriented toward image, the austerity that announces itself. Dull discipline is self-punishing, distorted, cover for control or for the desire to harm someone — including yourself. Read your last twenty sent messages. How many were true? How many were pleasant? How many were beneficial? How many did not agitate? That is your faith.

The fourth register is what you give to others. The clear gift is given as duty, in the right place, at the right time, to a worthy recipient who cannot repay you, with no thought of return. The restless gift comes with reluctance, with expectation, with a hope of advantage hiding behind the smile of the giving. The dull gift lands at the wrong place, to the unworthy, with contempt. The donation made publicly so the room sees it is a different gift from the one made when no one will know. The time given to a child, to an aging parent, to a friend whose grief will not resolve into a story you can tell — that gift cannot be measured by the room.

Now hear the audit. Look at what you took in this week. Look at what you offered without payment. Look at what discipline you kept when no one was watching. Look at what you gave, and to whom, and what you wanted back. Underneath these four, the substrate of your faith shows. You do not have to ask yourself what you believe. You only have to look at what your week did.

The unkind sentence you spoke yesterday — what was it oriented toward? Recognition you were not getting? A wound that has not closed? The weight of a day in which you stopped paying attention to who you were speaking to? That sentence was your faith showing itself in five seconds. What it shows is correctable. Not by a vow. By the next sentence.

Faith pointed at less than the largest cannot bear the weight of a life. When something less than ultimate is treated as ultimate, the person warps around it. You only need to ask, of your last hour and your last meal and your last gift and your last unkind sentence: what was this oriented toward?

The calendar is your faith. The bank statement is your faith. The browser history is your faith. Open them. Read what your faith has been doing.
