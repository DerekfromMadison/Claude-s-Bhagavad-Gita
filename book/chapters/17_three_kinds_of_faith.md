# Chapter 17 — Your Faith Is What Your Life Does

Your faith is what your life does. Not what you say. Not what you tell
yourself in the shower at six in the morning when you are
reconstructing yourself for the day ahead. What your life *does* — in
the registers where action is small, repeated, and visible to anyone
willing to look. What you eat. What you offer when no one is paying.
What disciplines you keep when no one is watching. What you give to
people who cannot repay you. That is your *sraddha*, and that is who
you are.

This is the seventeenth chapter's claim, and the chapter is willing to
make it diagnosable. If you want to know your *sraddha*, look at your
calendar last week. Look at your bank statement last month. Look at
your browser history yesterday. The chapter is unsentimental about
this: faith is not declared, it is enacted, and the enactment is
legible.

The Sanskrit word *sraddha* is usually translated *faith*, and the
English word drags theological freight the Sanskrit does not. *Sraddha*
is closer to *deep trust* — the orientation of the whole person toward
what they take to be most real. The verb root *srat-dha* means *to
place* (*dha*) the *heart* (*srat*, cognate with Latin *cor*). Faith
is heart-placement. Where you have laid down your heart — that is your
*sraddha*. Not what you have signed up to believe. What your heart has
already chosen, often without your noticing.

The Gita's seventeenth chapter takes a question that sounds technical
— what about people who set aside prescribed rules but worship with
sincerity? — and turns it into an anthropology. Krishna's answer at
17.3 is one of the sharpest single moves in the text: *sraddhamayo'yam
puruso yo yacchraddhah sa eva sah*. *This person is faith-made;
whatever a person's faith is, that is what the person is.* A person is
not what they say they believe. A person is what their faith is. There
is no hidden self underneath, holding different views in private. There
is just the operative trust, beneath words, that organizes the day.

Take that claim straight. The chapter is right.

You probably already half-believe it, because you have learned to be
skeptical of declared belief — to suspect that what people say about
their values is mostly performance or self-soothing. The Gita got there
two thousand years earlier and with more force. The skepticism is not
the move. The move is: *if declared belief is so often wrong, what is
right?* The lived pattern. The conduct. The places your time, money,
and attention have actually gone.

The chapter does not leave *sraddha* abstract. It works four registers
— *ahara* (food, intake), *yajna* (sacrifice, offering), *tapas*
(austerity, discipline), *dana* (gift, giving) — and shows how each
sorts threefold by the *guna* it expresses: *sattvic* (luminous,
settled), *rajasic* (kinetic, restless), *tamasic* (heavy, dull). The
three modes were described abstractly in Chapter 14. Here they land
on the surface of your life. *Sattva* does not mean *good* in a
moralistic sense; it means *settled*, transparent to its own nature.
*Rajas* does not mean *evil*; it means *kinetic*, the heat of motion
and ambition. *Tamas* does not mean *stupid*; it means *heavy*, the
dulling weight that makes a person unreachable, including to
themselves. These are weathers. You move among them in the course of
a single afternoon. Read the four registers as four audits of your
week.

The first register is *ahara* — what you take in. The Sanskrit word
literally means *what is taken in*, and the chapter is willing to go
all the way down to your plate. *Sattvic* foods promote vitality and
clarity; *rajasic* foods are sharp, salty, hot, the kind that excite
and inflame; *tamasic* foods are stale, decomposed, leftover-and-
forgotten. The catalog at 17.7–17.10 leans on a humoral theory of
bodily heat that does not map onto contemporary biochemistry, and the
specific sorting of foods is not what you should take from the chapter.
What you should take is the wider claim: that what you take in is a
field of identity-practice. Paul Rozin and his collaborators have
documented across decades that food preferences function as
identity-markers in a way ordinary preferences do not, and that when
food preference becomes a value, disgust gets recruited so the
preference becomes quasi-sacred (Rozin and Fallon, *Psychological
Review*, 1987; Rozin, Markwith, and Stoess, *Psychological Science*,
1997). The science underwrites that food is morally loaded. It does
not vindicate the chapter's specific *guna* taxonomy. The deeper move
— that ordinary registers are where *sraddha* becomes legible — does
the work.

And the chapter authorizes a wider reading. What you eat is one
register of intake. What you take in mentally is another. A morning
that begins with two hours of news outrage and ends with a doomscroll
into bed has *eaten* in a way the chapter calls *tamasic* — heavy,
dulling, decomposed. A morning that begins with thirty minutes of
attention to a difficult book and ends with one phone call to a person
you love has *eaten* differently. Eating in front of a screen, alone,
while answering email, out of a plastic container — that is a lived
statement of your *sraddha*. So is sitting with food, knowing where it
came from, eating with attention, eating with people. Neither is
morally neutral. Both are sentences your life is saying about what it
trusts. The phone in your pocket is the modern register where intake
is most legible. What you let in through that screen, hour after hour,
year after year, becomes the substrate from which your *sraddha* gets
built. This is not metaphor. The thing in your pocket is shaping the
inner climate of the person you are becoming.

So your first audit. Look at your last twenty-four hours of intake —
food and digital. That intake is your faith made visible.

The second register is *yajna* — offering. Classically (17.11–17.13),
*yajna* is structured ritual sacrifice. In your present-day life, it
is recognizable as something close: the unpaid offering you make where
the act itself is the point. The hour you give without counting it.
The work no one is measuring. *Sattvic* offering is given as duty,
without expectation of fruit; the work is given because it is the
right work to give, and you are not waiting to be thanked. *Rajasic*
offering is given for show or gain; the work is given but you are
tracking the thanks, and there is a ledger running in the back of your
head. *Tamasic* offering is given without faith, form, or sincerity,
or not given at all though it is your turn.

Most of us, much of the time, are doing *rajasic* offering with a
thin coat of *sattvic* language over it. The mentee mentored because it
will look good on the performance review. The board served on because
it builds the network. The cause championed because it is the right
cause to be associated with this year. The colleague covered for
because — somewhere — you are quietly counting the moments when you
will be the one needing cover. The chapter is not asking you to feel
terrible about this. It is asking you to notice it.

Your second audit. Look at your last week of unpaid hours. Where did
they go? To whom? Tracked or untracked? The hours you gave with no
ledger are one kind of *sraddha*. The hours you gave with a ledger
running in the back of your head are another. Both are real. Only one
is honest about itself.

The third register is *tapas* — austerity, discipline. Almost no
translation needed, because the chapter breaks *tapas* into three
layers: body, speech, mind (17.14–17.16). Bodily *tapas* in 2026 is
how you treat the body you inhabit — sleep, exercise, what you put in,
how you sit at a desk for nine hours, whether you go outside, whether
you walk. *Sattvic*: discipline undertaken with steady patience,
without showing it off, without punishment. *Rajasic*: the discipline
you post about — the gym selfie, the macros, the tracker — real
discipline, but oriented toward image rather than integration.
*Tamasic*: self-punishing, absent, or used as cover for disordered
control.

Speech *tapas* (17.15) translates almost without remainder. How you
write email when you are angry. Whether you say the careful true thing
in a meeting or the careless aggrandizing thing. Whether you read aloud
to a child. Whether you stay silent in the group chat when silence is
most honest. In 2026, speech *tapas* includes what you type, what you
post, and what you do not post. The audit is sharper than it sounds.
Read your last twenty sent messages. How many were said because they
would land? How many were said because they would *look good* landing?

Mental *tapas* (17.16) — serenity of mind, gentleness, silence,
self-restraint, purity of intent. The list is striking because it is
not heroic. It is small. The small repeated turn-back-to-attention
during a long meeting. The choice not to rehearse the resentment one
more time in the shower. Not staring at the phone at the table with
someone you love. Not narrating, internally, the version of the
incident that makes you the protagonist.

So your third audit. Body, speech, mind. In each layer, what discipline
have you been keeping, and for whom? For yourself in private? Or for
yourself as imagined being seen? Most of us, in most domains, are at
*rajasic* — discipline performed for image, ego in the room. The
chapter is not condemning. It is asking you to see clearly enough to
choose to do the same discipline differently next Tuesday.

The fourth register is *dana* — gift, giving. The chapter's
classification (17.20–17.22) is among the Gita's most morally pointed,
and it is the easiest to test honestly because the receipts are the
most legible. *Sattvic* gift: given as duty, right place, right time,
worthy recipient who cannot return the favor, no thought of repayment.
*Rajasic*: with reluctance, expectation of return, or for advantage.
*Tamasic*: at the wrong place and time, to the unworthy, with contempt.

The donation made publicly to be seen making it is a different gift
from the one made anonymously. Not necessarily less useful — the
recipient may not care which it is — but a different gift from the
giver's side, doing different work in the giver's *sraddha*. So is how
you tip — every time, even when no one is looking. So is the time you
give to those who cannot do anything for you in return: children,
elderly parents, strangers in pain, the friend whose grief will not
resolve into a story you can tell at a dinner party.

Your fourth audit. Look at the last month's giving. Of money. Of time.
Of attention. To people who cannot repay, what did you give? The
answer is one place where your *sraddha* is unusually legible.

William James gives this anthropology its modern philosophical pedigree.
For James, faith is the practical, life-shaping orientation toward a
question that is *live*, *forced*, and *momentous* — and faith is what
operates beneath proposition, in the *passional* layer of the person
that decides before declared belief gets its turn. James's *passional*
corresponds to the Gita's *svabhava* — the inner nature shaped by what
a person has been. The Gita says: that whole *is* your *sraddha*.

Paul Tillich completes the move: faith is the state of being
ultimately concerned. *A person is what their faith is* (Gita 17.3)
and Tillich's formulation say the same thing from two ends of one
thought — and when something less than ultimate is treated as
ultimate, the person warps around it. *Rajasic* faith places ultimate
weight on objects of restless ego-striving — recognition, advantage,
achievement. *Tamasic* faith places it in distortions, or in nothing
in particular. Either way, an under-ultimate object bears an ultimate
weight, and you warp. That warping is the structure of most modern
unease. The career given the weight of a soul. The relationship made
to do the work of God. The phone treated as the place the meaning is.
The under-ultimate object cannot bear it. The chapter saw this two
thousand years ago and named it.

Steven Hayes and the Acceptance and Commitment Therapy tradition have
documented across thousands of patients that what a person says they
value and what their behavior over time commits them to routinely
diverge — and that a great deal of suffering comes from that
divergence (Hayes, Strosahl, and Wilson, *Acceptance and Commitment
Therapy*, 2nd ed., Guilford, 2012). The therapeutic move is *values
clarification*: surface what action under pressure actually points at,
and re-align behavior or revise stated values accordingly. The Gita
says: a person is what their faith is. ACT says: what you actually
value shows in what you do, not in what you say. The metaphysical
horizons differ; the anthropological claim is the same. A clinician
working with a depressed twenty-six-year-old in Chicago in 2026
already believes the Gita's anthropology, even when she has never
opened the book. The science does not prove the chapter. It shows the
chapter is talking about something the human animal actually does.

The chapter closes with a passage often read as a ritual seal — the
formula *Om Tat Sat* invoked at the beginning, middle, and end of any
honest *yajna*, *tapas*, or *dana*. *Om* — the primordial sound. *Tat*
— *That*, the Real beyond naming. *Sat* — *Being*, the Real as
truth-and-presence. The threefold name is the chapter's reminder that
any honest practice is implicitly oriented toward what it cannot
itself name. The *sattvic* gift is *sattvic* not because it has been
hygienically performed but because it has been placed within an
orientation toward what is larger than itself. Strip the orientation
and the gift becomes *rajasic* virtue-signaling at best, *tamasic*
indifference at worst.

You do not have to know what the Real is, exactly, to live with this.
You only have to be willing to ask, of your last hour, your last meal,
your last gift, your last unkind sentence: *what was this oriented
toward?* The chapter's stern coda at 17.28 — *whatever is offered
without faith is unreal; it does not stand* — is not a threat. It is a
description. An act with no implicit orientation beyond itself does
not, in the end, hold.

So here is what survives the chapter, for you. Your *sraddha* is
constitutive. You are what you trust beneath words, and there is no
hidden self underneath holding different views in private; there is
just the operative orientation, made of the ordinary patterns. That
trust is diagnosable through the four registers, extended into their
2026 equivalents: intake (literal and informational), unpaid offering,
the disciplines of body and speech and mind, gift to those who cannot
repay. The diagnostic is for clarity, not condemnation — knowing
today's offering is *rajasic* is the first step out of *rajasic*
blindness about it. *Sattva* is the direction, not the achieved state;
the discipline is to act toward it, repeatedly, in small things. Faith
pointed at less than the largest cannot finally bear the weight of a
life. And every honest practice has an implicit horizon, named or
unnamed.

The simplest test of what you trust is to look at where your time,
your money, and your attention have gone over the last week, the last
month, the last year. Not what you tell yourself you value. What the
receipts say.

The calendar is the *sraddha*. The bank statement is the *sraddha*.
The browser history is the *sraddha*.

There is one more audit the chapter authorizes that the chapter itself
does not name, because the chapter did not have to: the unkind-sentence
audit. The last unkind thing you said — to a partner, a colleague, a
stranger online, yourself in the bathroom mirror — what was it
oriented toward? Recognition you were not getting? A wound that has
not closed? The dulling weight of a long day in which you stopped
paying attention to whom you were speaking to? The unkind sentence is
your *sraddha* showing itself in five seconds. What it shows is
correctable, but not by a vow. By the next sentence, spoken with the
orientation the chapter has been describing.

Open the calendar. Open the bank statement. Open the browser history.
Read what your faith has been doing.
