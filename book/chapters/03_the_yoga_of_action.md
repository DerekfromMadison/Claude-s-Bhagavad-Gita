# Chapter 3 — *Karma Yoga* (The Yoga of Action)

*You cannot withdraw.*

That is the chapter's claim and its operating premise. The fantasy of
clean retreat — the one Arjuna proposed when he sat down in the
chariot, the one you propose every time you imagine the sabbatical
that will fix the wrong life — is impossible. Not forbidden.
Impossible. What can be trained is not whether you act but the *form*
your acting takes. Chapter 3 gives you four pillars to stand on:
*anasakti* (no grasping at outcomes), *yajna* (action as offering),
*svadharma* (your own particular work), and *lokasangraha* (the
holding-together of the world). The four are not a menu. They are a
structure. You stand inside the whole structure or none of it.

Arjuna's question opens the chapter, and it is not a stupid question.
*If knowledge is so superior, why are you sending me into this
slaughter?* You have asked a version of it. Anyone who has heard a
contemplative teaching and concluded that the honest response to its
grandeur is to step back has asked it. *If the inner life matters
more, why should I keep acting in this outer one?* The question
contains a real intuition. Action does seem coarser than knowledge.
The contemplative does seem to have stepped onto a quieter stair.

Krishna's reply at 3.5 does not tell Arjuna that withdrawal is wrong.
It tells him it is impossible. *Na hi kascit ksanam api jatu tisthaty
akarma-krt* — no one, even for a moment, ever rests without
performing action. To be embodied is to be moved by the *gunas*, the
modes of nature Chapter 14 will name. The wish to abstain is itself
a form of acting. Sitting down in the chariot is an act. Announcing
your renunciation is an act. Choosing not to choose is a choice. The
fantasy of a position outside action is a confusion about what kind
of being you are.

Arendt makes the same point phenomenologically. To withdraw from
public action is not to take a stance but to refuse the stance-
taking that being human requires. *With word and deed we insert
ourselves into the human world.* The insertion is what she calls a
second birth — the human gesture that makes a life out of the naked
physical fact. Refuse the gesture and you have not stepped outside
the field; you have only taken a quieter, less honest position
inside it. Her argument and Krishna's converge: there is no neutral
place outside the human field. The contemplative who imagines they
have stepped out has stepped, and the step is itself an entry.

The most lucrative industry of your present moment is selling
withdrawal as a product. Retreat center. Sabbatical. Digital detox.
Silent week in Tulum. Morning routine that promises the rest of the
day will arrange itself around it. Meditation app sold as exit from
the day rather than preparation for it. The wellness-industrial
complex offers the fantasy you came to the Gita to verify, and the
Gita refuses to verify it.

The chapter does not condemn rest. Rest is not the target. The
target is rest used as alibi. *To what are you stepping back
forward?* If the answer is *nothing*, the step back is itself a
form of unconscious acting. The sabbatical not in service of
returning to better engagement is a slower form of the same flight
that prompted it.

## *Yajna*: action as offering

Once the exit is closed, the chapter turns to form. Krishna's hinge
is *yajna* — usually translated *sacrifice*, though the English word
carries the wrong connotations. *Yajna* is not about giving up. It
is about *offering*. The cosmos Chapter 3 describes is metabolic — a
system sustained by exchange, in which no participant is exempt from
giving back. Action performed for its own ends binds. Action
performed as offering does not. *Action imprisons the world unless
it is done as sacrifice; free from attachment, perform action as
sacrifice.*

The verses sound archaic. The structural point is not. Aurobindo
lifts the chapter's *yajna* register to its widest scope: all life
is sacrifice; the original condition of being is a giving-out of
energies that returns transformed; action becomes sacrament when you
no longer claim it as private possession but offer it back to the
rhythm from which it came. Aurobindo's register is the most
metaphysically committed of the chapter's readings, and it has the
cost of altitude. It is hard to operate from without long practice.
But it gives the chapter its widest vocabulary.

The trouble is that *offering* sounds religious, and if you are not
religious you will reasonably ask whether the chapter has anything
to say to you. Here MacIntyre does the work of translation. A
*practice*, in his technical sense, is not a workout or a hobby; it
is a coherent cooperative activity — medicine, teaching, parenting,
chess, farming — through which goods *internal* to that activity
get realized in the course of trying to achieve the standards of
excellence appropriate to it. The crucial distinction is between
*internal goods* — what is good about doing the practice well, on
its own terms — and *external goods* — money, status, prestige,
reputation. A surgeon's externals: income, referral network. Her
internals: the diagnosis well-made, the decision tree well-walked,
the patient stitched together. A parent's externals: the child's
admissions letter. Her internals: years of presence, the intuition
for this child's mood, the conversation at the kitchen table on a
Tuesday at 9 p.m.

This is the cleanest modern translation of *yajna* I know. To act
for the internal goods of a practice is to act in the spirit of
offering — the action given to its proper form rather than
extracted for personal gain. To release your grip on external goods
is the practical face of *anasakti*. MacIntyre is a moral
traditionalist, not a metaphysician of sacrifice. But his
distinction survives translation in both directions. Medicine has
internal goods irreducible to fees. Teaching has internal goods
irreducible to test scores. Parenting has internal goods irreducible
to bragging rights. If you recognize that, you have already
understood what *yajna* is naming.

The chapter's *yajna* language is hospitable to several horizons.
For a theist, the offering is to the divine. For a non-theist, the
offering may be to the practice itself, to the people the practice
serves, to the longer arc of care or justice the practice
participates in. The chapter does not require a particular
metaphysics. It requires that there be *some* horizon larger than
your own small self. Pure ego-economy — work as instrument of
personal brand — fails the chapter, and the chapter is unusually
direct about it: *one who cooks only for himself eats only sin.*
Strong words for what is now called optimizing your personal funnel.

## *Anasakti*: the inner condition

If *yajna* is the structural form of action, *anasakti* is the inner
condition that makes the form recoverable from the inside. Gandhi
titled his own translation of the Gita *Anasakti Yoga*, which is
already an interpretive position: the heart of the Gita's teaching,
on his reading, is action without attachment to fruits. *Anasakti*
is not affective indifference. The surgeon who is *anasakta* still
cares whether her patient lives. The writer still cares whether the
sentence works. The parent still cares whether the child is at peace.
What *anasakti* refuses is the collapse of the self into the outcome
— the move that makes your continued capacity to function contingent
on victory.

The Gita's claim, as I read it, is that *anasakti* does not cancel
the project. It changes your relation to its fruits. The labor
remains. The caring remains. What is released is the bargain — the
implicit deal in which your right to be at peace, or to keep showing
up, is contingent on the project succeeding. Tilak, writing his
*Gita Rahasya* as a political prisoner against a tradition he saw
as having anaesthetized India into quietism, made *anasakti* the
inner discipline that made sustained engaged action survivable.
Without it, the activist eventually compromises the means to secure
the victory, or breaks. With it, the activist can keep showing up.

You will not achieve *anasakti* as a finished state. You will recover
it as a discipline, occasionally, on better days, and lose it again
on worse ones. The chapter recommends the discipline. It does not
promise the state.

Modern science has its own version of the same finding. Deci's 1971
Soma puzzle study: pay people to do something they already enjoy,
and they do less of it in their free time afterward. Fifty years of
subsequent work in Self-Determination Theory has named this the
*undermining effect* of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation.
Intrinsically pursued activities produce more sustained effort,
better creative performance on complex tasks, and higher well-being
than activities pursued for external contingencies. SDT names three
psychological needs whose satisfaction predicts well-being across
cultures — autonomy, competence, relatedness — and the convergence
with Chapter 3 is striking. The chapter's recommendation, in SDT
vocabulary, is motivational hygiene: orient toward the intrinsic
structure of the practice rather than toward extrinsic
contingencies. The chapter goes further; it adds a metaphysical
register the science does not have. But where they overlap, they
overlap well.

## *Svadharma*: the wrong life expertly performed

The third pillar is *svadharma* — your own *dharma*, the form of
right action specific to *this* person, *this* situation, *this*
history, *these* capacities. The locus is 3.35: *better one's own
duty done imperfectly than another's done well; in one's own duty
death is welcome, in another's there is danger.* Read broadly, with
Gandhi and most modern commentators, *svadharma* names the
structural fit between you and your particular life — the work that
is yours to do, the relations yours to honor, the contribution you
are positioned for. The narrow reading — duties prescribed by caste
— has done historical damage and continues to do it. I am taking
the broad reading as the chapter's living force.

*Svadharma*'s sharpest application is to the person who has
succeeded at someone else's life. The verse speaks to them with
unusual directness. The danger is not failure. They are succeeding.
The danger is that the success is hollowing them out.

The doctor who entered medicine for the practice and now spends her
days on billing-code optimization, prior authorizations, RVU
targets, and the dashboard scaffolding that has been laid over the
work — she has not failed at medicine. She has succeeded at
something else, and the something else has displaced the practice
she entered. The teacher whose year is structured around test-score
deltas has not failed at teaching. She has succeeded at the score
regime, which is not the same thing. The mid-career professional who
has crested the ladder she chose at twenty-two and is discovering
that the work belongs to a self she has outgrown has not failed at
her career. She has succeeded at the wrong one. The chapter's verse
is a warning not against bad work but against *expertly performed
wrong work*.

How do you find your *svadharma*? Not by introspection alone, and
not by following passion alone. *Svadharma* is structural fit: the
people you are already responsible to, the capacities you already
have, the position you are already in. Gandhi's reading is honest in
a way the wellness register sometimes is not. *Svadharma* often means
accepting a more modest version of your work than your ego imagined,
doing it where you are rather than where you fantasize about being,
discovering across decades that the *own*-ness is what makes it
bearable. The chapter is not romantic. *To die in one's own duty is
welcome* — not pleasant, *welcome* — because the alternative, a
successful life lived inside someone else's shape, has a worse death.

A handle for major changes — career pivot, relationship at a
threshold, a move: *is this change a movement toward what is more
fundamentally mine, or toward someone else's path I have started to
admire?* The answers are not always clean. The line between *mine*
and *imitated* runs through territory the imitating self does not
always have access to. Ask anyway. Ask aloud. Ask of someone who
knows you well enough to push back on the answer you reach.

## *Lokasangraha*: the holding-together of the world

The fourth pillar is the chapter's social-cosmic horizon.
*Lokasangraha* — holding-together of the world — is the reason the
wise person is told to keep acting. Not for personal gain, not for
self-perfection, but to keep the social and cosmic fabric from
unraveling. Krishna invokes Janaka, the philosopher-king who
attained perfection while ruling a kingdom — the Gita's standing
rebuke to the idea that liberation requires the forest. He invokes
himself: *I have nothing left to do in the three worlds, and yet I
act, lest the worlds be lost.* The wise must act as the unwise act,
he says, but without attachment, *for the welfare of the world*.

What *lokasangraha* gives you is the public horizon of ordinary work.
The plumber who does her work well sustains an invisible piece of
the city's livability. The software engineer who refuses to ship
code that exploits the user sustains a piece of public trust the
larger economy depends on. The parent who shows up at the kitchen
table on a Tuesday evening sustains a piece of the next generation's
emotional vocabulary. The teacher whose tenth year is no different
from her first sustains a piece of an institution that is the
substrate of every other institution. None of these workers are
doing anything heroic. They are *holding the world together*, in
the small sector of it they are responsible for, and the chapter
says that is what the wise do.

The *yajna* register does not require seeing yourself as
participating in cosmic ritual. It requires not seeing yourself as a
private transaction-unit. The work is *for* something larger —
perhaps small in ambition (a household, a neighborhood, a craft, a
ward, a code review queue) but not just *you*. The doctor in a
system under strain, with fifteen patients waiting and a clock
engineered to convert her attention into throughput, faces a moment
in which the chapter is unusually concrete. She does not have an
exit; the chapter has already foreclosed that. She also does not
have the luxury of metric-detachment in the abstract; the metrics
are real, the incentives are real, the consequences for ignoring
them are real. What the chapter offers is a way to be inside the
constraint without being deformed by it. *Do this visit, in the
time available, for the welfare of this person and the practice
this practice is part of.* Release of the *phala* — productivity
score, throughput target, RVUs per session — is not indifference to
the metrics. It is refusal to let them be the inner organization of
your attention. Inside the fifteen minutes, this is a person, this
is a practice, this is a small *yajna*. The metrics will be what
they are.

## The four together

The four pillars are not separable. *Anasakti* without *yajna*
becomes private emotional hygiene — a self-soothing technique
masquerading as a discipline. *Yajna* without *svadharma* becomes
generalized spirituality with no particular shoulder for any
particular load — the desire to serve everyone in general and no
one in particular, which is one of the more comfortable forms of
not actually serving anyone. *Svadharma* without *lokasangraha*
becomes a private vocation no one is served by — the artisan
perfecting her craft in a vacuum, the professional pursuing her
calling without reference to the people the calling is for.
*Lokasangraha* without *anasakti* becomes moralism: heroic effort
with the ego attached to outcomes, the activist whose psychological
survival depends on the reform succeeding, the reformer who breaks.
The chapter has ruled all four singletons out. It recommends the
structure.

So how do you know, on a Tuesday in middle life in 2026, whether
you are inside the structure or only performing it? The chapter
offers one diagnostic, and it is sharp.

*Does the rewards focus produce focus, or anxiety?*

If you cannot stop checking the metric, the metric has become the
practice. If you can let the metric be a metric — visible, recorded,
occasionally consulted, not the inner organization of your attention
— the practice is still the practice. The writer trained by the
platform economy to watch engagement numbers has slipped from
writing into the meta-practice of watching how the writing performs.
The teacher whose lesson plans are reverse-engineered from the
standardized test has slipped from teaching into score-management.
The doctor whose first thought entering each room is the billing code
has slipped from medicine into coding. None of these workers is
wicked. Each is inside a system that has hijacked the *phala* and
made it stand in for the practice itself. The recommendation is not
to leave the system — that exit does not exist. The recommendation
is to refuse the substitution. Hold to what is internal. Let the
metric be a metric.

You cannot withdraw. The chapter has been clear about that from its
first verses. What you can do is choose, again, today, the form
your unavoidable acting takes. Whether the work you cannot get out
of is a *yajna* or a transaction. Whether the practice is yours or
someone else's. Whether you are inside the world or pretending you
have stepped outside it. Whether the metrics are tools or owners.
The chapter is not asking for heroism. It is asking for the
particular form of attention that lets a working life remain a
working life rather than collapsing into its own scoreboard.

Ask it of one task today: *does the rewards focus produce focus,
or anxiety?* The answer will tell you which structure you are
inside — the one Chapter 3 recommends, or the one it spent
twenty-five hundred years warning you about.

Arjuna will pick up the bow at the end of the book. Chapter 3 is
where it becomes clear he was always going to.
