# Chapter 3 — *Karma Yoga* (The Yoga of Action)

You cannot withdraw. That is the chapter's first sentence and its
operating premise, and almost everything else in it is the
consequence of taking that premise seriously. The fantasy of clean
retreat — the one Arjuna proposed in Chapter 1 when he sat down in
the chariot, the one a 2026 reader proposes every time they imagine
the sabbatical that will fix the wrong life — is dispatched in the
chapter's opening verses and never allowed back. What can be trained
is not whether you act but the form your acting takes. Chapter 3 is
the manual for that form. It names four pillars: *anasakti* (no
grasping), *yajna* (action as offering), *svadharma* (your own
particular work), and *lokasangraha* (holding the world together).
The four are not a menu. They are a structure, and the chapter's
recommendation is to stand inside the whole structure or none of it.

## The exit that does not exist

Arjuna's question opens the chapter, and it is not a stupid question.
*If knowledge is so superior, why are you sending me into this
slaughter?* Every reader who has heard a contemplative teaching and
concluded that the honest response to its grandeur is to step back
asks a version: *if the inner life matters more, why should I keep
acting in this outer one?* The question contains a real intuition.
Action does seem coarser than knowledge. The contemplative does seem
to have stepped onto a quieter stair. The Gita has even encouraged
that intuition in places.

Krishna's reply is structural rather than moral. He does not tell
Arjuna that withdrawal is wrong; he tells him it is impossible. *For
one cannot remain even for a moment without performing action.* To
be embodied is to be moved by the *gunas* — the modes of nature,
which Chapter 14 will name in detail — and the very wish to abstain
is itself a kind of acting. Sitting down in the chariot is an act.
Announcing one's renunciation is an act. Choosing not to choose is a
choice. The fantasy of a position outside action is, on this account,
a confusion about what kind of being one is.

The point is not just metaphysical. Hannah Arendt makes it
phenomenologically. In *The Human Condition*, she argues that to
withdraw from public action is not to take a stance but to refuse
the stance-taking that being human requires. *With word and deed we
insert ourselves into the human world,* she writes; that insertion
is a *second birth*. To refuse it is to leave the first birth — the
naked physical fact — without the human gesture that makes a life
out of it. Arendt is not in conversation with the Gita; her project
is post-totalitarian and European, a recovery of vocabulary for a
modern landscape that had collapsed all human activity into *labor*.
But her argument and Krishna's converge: there is no neutral place
outside the human field. The contemplative who imagines themselves
to have stepped out has stepped, and the step is itself an entry.

This is worth saying clearly because the most lucrative industry of
the present moment is selling withdrawal as a product. The
wellness-industrial complex offers retreat, sabbatical, digital
detox, the silent week in Tulum, the morning routine that promises
the rest of the day will arrange itself around it. The "lying flat"
movement (*tang ping*) among young workers in China and elsewhere
extends the same intuition into refusal of a labor regime that has
become extractive. Meditation traditions are sometimes recruited to
the same purpose: practice as an exit from the day rather than as
preparation for it.

The chapter does not condemn rest. Rest is not the target. The
target is the use of rest as an alibi. *To what are you going to
step back forward?* — that is the chapter's question to anyone who
has stepped back. If the answer is *nothing*, the step back is
itself a kind of unconscious acting, usually an unhealthy one. The
sabbatical that is not in service of a return to better engagement
is not really a sabbatical; it is a slower form of the same flight
that prompted it. The retreat that is not metabolized into the life
that produced the need for retreat is decoration on the same wound.

The wellness exit and the political withdrawal are siblings here.
Both treat the difficulty of acting well as a defect in action
itself. Chapter 3's diagnosis is the opposite: the difficulty is
intrinsic, the response cannot be extraction, and the only honest
question is what *form* one's unavoidable acting will take.

## *Yajna*: action as offering

Once the exit is closed, the chapter turns to form. Krishna's hinge
is *yajna* — usually translated "sacrifice," though the English word
carries connotations the Sanskrit does not. *Yajna* is not about
giving up; it is about *offering*. In the chapter's mythic frame,
the creator gave forth beings together with sacrifice, and said,
*by this you shall multiply*. The gods are nourished; they nourish
in turn; the cosmic web sustains itself through circulation. Action
performed for its own ends is binding. Action performed as offering
is not. *Action imprisons the world unless it is done as sacrifice;*
*free from attachment, perform action as sacrifice.*

The verses sound archaic. The structural point is not. The cosmos
described is metabolic — a system sustained by exchange, in which
no participant is exempt from giving back. Aurobindo, in *Essays on
the Gita*, lifts the chapter's *yajna* register to its widest scope:
all life is sacrifice; the original condition of being is a
giving-out of energies that returns transformed; action becomes
sacrament when the doer no longer claims it as private possession
but offers it back to the rhythm from which it came. Aurobindo's
register is the most metaphysically committed of the chapter's
readings, and it has the cost of altitude — it is hard to operate
from without long practice — but it gives the chapter its widest
vocabulary.

The trouble is that "offering" sounds religious, and a 2026 reader
who is not religious will reasonably ask whether the chapter has
anything to say to them. Here Alasdair MacIntyre does the work of
translation. In *After Virtue*, MacIntyre defines a *practice* as a
coherent, complex, socially established cooperative activity through
which goods *internal* to that activity are realized in the course
of trying to achieve standards of excellence appropriate to it. The
crucial distinction is between *internal goods* — what is good about
doing this practice well, on its own terms — and *external goods* —
money, status, prestige, reputation. A surgeon's externals: income,
referral network. Her internals: diagnosis well-made, decision tree
well-walked, patient stitched together. A parent's externals: the
child's admissions letter. Her internals: years of presence, the
intuition for this child's mood, the conversation at the kitchen
table on a Tuesday at 9 p.m.

This is the cleanest modern translation of *yajna*. To act for the
*internal goods* of a practice is to act in the spirit of offering —
the action given to its proper form rather than extracted for
personal gain. To release one's grip on *external goods* is the
practical face of *anasakti*. MacIntyre is not a metaphysician of
sacrifice. He is a moral traditionalist defending an Aristotelian
account of human flourishing against modern fragmentation. But his
distinction survives translation in both directions: medicine has
internal goods irreducible to fees, teaching to test scores,
parenting to bragging rights, and a reader who recognizes that has
already understood what *yajna* is naming.

The chapter's *yajna* language is hospitable to several horizons.
For a theist, the offering is to the divine; the cosmic frame is
filled in. For a non-theist, the offering may be to the practice
itself, to the people the practice serves, to the longer arc of
care or justice the practice participates in. The chapter does not
require a particular metaphysics. It requires that there be *some*
larger horizon than the worker's small self. Pure ego-economy —
work as instrument of personal brand — fails the chapter, and fails
it in a way the chapter is unusually direct about: *one who cooks
only for himself eats only sin.* Strong words for what is now
called optimizing one's personal funnel.

## *Anasakti*: the inner condition

If *yajna* is the structural form of action, *anasakti* is the
inner condition that makes the form recoverable from the inside.
Gandhi titled his own translation of the Gita *Anasakti Yoga*,
which is already an interpretive position: the heart of the Gita's
teaching, on Gandhi's reading, is action without attachment to
fruits. Chapter 3 is where this is most explicitly given. *Anasakti*
is not affective indifference. The surgeon who is *anasakta* still
cares whether the patient lives; the writer still cares whether the
sentence works; the parent still cares whether the child is at
peace. What *anasakti* refuses is the collapse of the self into the
outcome — the move that makes one's continued capacity to function
contingent on victory.

Bernard Williams worried, in his 1976 essay "Persons, Character,
and Morality," that an impartialist morality demanding the
sacrifice of one's deepest projects — what he called *ground
projects* — would, in some sense, cancel the person whose life
those projects constituted. *Unless such things exist,* Williams
wrote, *there will be no categorical desire for him to go on at all.*
Transposed onto Chapter 3, Williams's worry sharpens into a
question: does *anasakti* require giving up ground projects? On a
strong reading it might. *Have no attachment to fruits* could
collapse into *have no projects whose outcomes you care about*, and
that, Williams would say, is the cancellation of the agent.

The Gita's answer, as I read it, is that *anasakti* does not
cancel the project; it changes one's relation to its fruits. The
ground project remains. The labor remains. The caring remains.
What is released is the bargain — the implicit deal in which one's
right to be at peace, or to keep showing up, is contingent on the
project succeeding. Williams would press: is that distinction
phenomenologically stable, or is it sleight of hand? Honestly:
stable for some practitioners, unstable for others, recoverable as
a discipline rather than achieved as a finished state. The chapter
recommends the discipline. It does not promise the state.

Williams gives Chapter 3 its sharpest defense against glibness. The
person who has never been attached to anything is not *anasakta*;
they are absent. Lived *anasakti* is much harder and more honest
about what is being released than the dilettante's version. The
Gandhian reading is closer to the lived form than the costume of
spiritual cool one sometimes encounters.

Modern science has its own version of the same finding, smaller
in scope but striking in its specificity. In 1971 Edward Deci ran a
study with the Soma puzzle: pay people to do something they already
enjoy, and they do less of it in their free time afterward. Fifty
years of subsequent work in Self-Determination Theory, with Richard
Ryan, has named this the *undermining effect* of extrinsic rewards
on intrinsic motivation. The wider finding: intrinsically pursued
activities produce more sustained effort, better creative
performance on complex tasks, and higher well-being than activities
pursued for external contingencies. SDT names three psychological
needs whose satisfaction predicts well-being across cultures —
autonomy, competence, relatedness — and the convergence with the
chapter, where it appears, is striking. The chapter's recommendation,
in SDT vocabulary, is *motivational hygiene*: orient toward the
intrinsic structure of the practice rather than toward extrinsic
contingencies. The chapter goes further; it adds a metaphysical
register Deci and Ryan do not have. But where they overlap, they
overlap well. Where the science of motivation can credibly speak,
it speaks in the chapter's direction.

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's flow research adds a second piece. Across
rock climbers, factory workers, and surgeons sampled by experience
sampling, the moments of greatest absorption and reported well-being
arose when challenge matched skill, neither too easy nor too hard,
in the service of an activity that had become *autotelic* —
containing its own end. The chapter does not promise flow as a
reward; that would itself be a fruit. But the *shape* of action
Chapter 3 recommends — full engagement, release of the grip on
outcomes external to the activity — is the shape flow research
finds most reliably produces flow. The flow literature is silent on
the chapter's wider frame of *lokasangraha*; within the narrower
frame of individual experience, the convergence is real.

## *Svadharma*: the wrong life expertly performed

The third pillar is *svadharma* — one's own *dharma*, the form of
right action specific to this person, this situation, this history,
these capacities. The locus classicus is 3.35: *better one's own
duty done imperfectly than another's done well; in one's own duty
death is welcome, in another's there is danger.* Three voices, side
by side, render the verse with different inflections. Mascaró's:
*to die in one's duty is life: to live in another's is death.*
Johnson's, scholarly and restrained: *it is better to practise
your own inherent duty deficiently than another's duty well.*
Prabhupada's, doctrinally specific: *prescribed duties* —
prescribed, in his Gaudiya Vaishnava reading, by *varnashrama*,
the classical caste-and-stage system. Same verse, three different
worlds.

The narrow reading of *svadharma* — as the duties prescribed by
caste — has done historical damage and continues to do it. I am
not going to defend it. The broader reading, which Gandhi and most
modern commentators take, names something different: the structural
fit between a person and their own particular life — the work
theirs to do, the relations theirs to honor, the contribution they
are positioned for. The narrow and broad readings sit in tension. I
am taking the broad one as the chapter's living force, with the
recognition that the narrow one was historically operative and is
not what the chapter, on the broad reading, requires.

*Svadharma*'s sharpest modern application is to the person who has
succeeded at someone else's life. The verse speaks to them with
unusual directness. The danger is not failure; they are succeeding.
The danger is that the success is hollowing them out. The doctor
who entered medicine for the practice and now spends her days on
billing-code optimization, prior authorizations, and the
metrics-and-dashboard scaffolding that has been laid over the work
— she has not failed at medicine. She has succeeded at something
else, and the something else has displaced the practice she
entered. The teacher whose year is structured around test-score
deltas has not failed at teaching. She has succeeded at the score
regime, which is not the same thing. The mid-career professional
who has crested the ladder she chose at twenty-two and is now
discovering that the work belongs to a self she has outgrown has
not failed at her career. She has succeeded at the wrong one. The
chapter's verse — *better one's own duty done imperfectly* — is a
warning not against bad work but against *expertly performed wrong
work*.

How does a 2026 reader find their *svadharma*? Not by introspection
alone, and not by following passion alone. *Svadharma* is structural
fit: the people one is already responsible to, the capacities one
already has, the position one is already in. Gandhi's reading is
honest in a way the wellness-register sometimes is not. *Svadharma*
often means accepting a more modest version of one's work than the
ego imagined, doing it where one is rather than where one
fantasizes about being, discovering across decades that the
*own*-ness is what makes it bearable. The chapter is not romantic.
*To die in one's own duty is welcome* — not pleasant, *welcome* —
because the alternative, a successful life lived inside someone
else's shape, has a worse death.

A practical handle for major changes — a career pivot, a
relationship at a threshold, a move: *is this change a movement
toward what is more fundamentally mine, or toward someone else's
path I have started to admire?* The answers are not always clean.
The line between *mine* and *imitated* runs through territory the
imitating self does not always have access to. The question is
still worth asking, and asking aloud, and asking of someone who
knows you well enough to push back on the answer you reach.

## *Lokasangraha*: the holding-together of the world

The fourth pillar is the chapter's social-cosmic horizon.
*Lokasangraha* — holding-together of the world — is the reason the
wise person is told to keep acting. Not for personal gain, not for
self-perfection, but to keep the social and cosmic fabric from
unraveling. Krishna invokes Janaka, the philosopher-king who
attained perfection while ruling a kingdom — the Gita's standing
rebuke to the idea that liberation requires the forest. He invokes
himself: *I have nothing left to do in the three worlds, and yet I
act, lest the worlds be lost.* The wise must act as the unwise act,
he says, but without attachment, *for the welfare of the world*.

Tilak, writing his *Gita Rahasya* as a political prisoner in 1908
–1911 against a tradition he saw as having anaesthetized India
into quietism, made *lokasangraha* the chapter's center of gravity.
On his reading, the Gita is primarily a treatise on energetic,
engaged, worldly action; renunciate readings are a misuse of the
text. Tilak's reading captures the *plain sense* of Chapter 3
better than Sankara's preparatory framing — Krishna closes the
door on withdrawal, invokes Janaka, says even he continues to act
for the welfare of the world; this is not a chapter about
preparation for renunciation. Tilak's protest against renunciate
readings *of this chapter* lands. Where his reading has to do
interpretive work is in absorbing the Gita's own renunciate,
meditative, and devotional registers in Chapters 5, 6, 12, and 18.
*Karma yoga* alone, without the inner registers Aurobindo and
Gandhi keep in view, becomes activism without ballast. The
synthesis the chapter wants is Tilak's plain-sense reading
disciplined by Aurobindo's metaphysics and Gandhi's inner training.

What *lokasangraha* gives a 2026 reader is the public horizon of
ordinary work. The plumber who does her work well sustains an
invisible piece of the city's livability. The software engineer
who refuses to ship code that exploits the user sustains a piece
of public trust the larger economy depends on. The parent who shows
up at the kitchen table on a Tuesday evening sustains a piece of
the next generation's emotional vocabulary. The teacher whose tenth
year is no different from her first sustains a piece of an
institution that is the substrate of every other institution. None
of these workers are doing anything heroic. They are *holding the
world together*, in the small sector of it they are responsible for,
and the chapter says that is what the wise do.

The *yajna* register does not require seeing oneself as
participating in cosmic ritual. It requires not seeing oneself as a
private transaction-unit. The work is *for* something larger —
perhaps small in ambition (a household, a neighborhood, a craft, a
ward, a street, a code review queue) but not just *the worker
themself*. The doctor in a system under strain, with fifteen
patients waiting and a clock that has been engineered to convert
her attention into throughput, faces a moment in which the chapter
is unusually concrete. She does not have an exit; the chapter has
already foreclosed that. She also does not have the luxury of
metric-detachment in the abstract; the metrics are real, the
incentives are real, the consequences for ignoring them are real.
What the chapter offers is a way to be inside the constraint
without being deformed by it. *Do this visit, in the time
available, for the welfare of this person and the practice this
practice is part of.* The release of the *phala* — productivity
score, throughput target, RVUs per session — is not indifference
to the metrics. It is refusal to let them be the inner organization
of attention. Inside the fifteen minutes, this is a person, this is
a practice, this is a small *yajna*. The metrics will be what they
are.

## The four together

The four pillars are not separable. *Anasakti* without *yajna*
becomes private emotional hygiene — a self-soothing technique
masquerading as a discipline. *Yajna* without *svadharma* becomes
generalized spirituality with no particular shoulder for any
particular load — the desire to serve everyone in general and no
one in particular, which is one of the more comfortable forms of
not actually serving anyone. *Svadharma* without *lokasangraha*
becomes a private vocation no one is served by — the artisan
perfecting her craft in a vacuum, the professional pursuing her
calling without reference to the people the calling is for.
*Lokasangraha* without *anasakti* becomes moralism: heroic effort
with the ego attached to outcomes, the activist who eventually
compromises the means to secure the victory, the reformer whose
psychological survival depends on the reform succeeding. The
chapter has ruled all four singletons out. It recommends the
structure.

Sankara, reading through Advaita, would frame the structure as
preparatory. *Karma yoga* purifies the inner instrument so that
*jnana* — the direct knowledge that liberates — can arise. The
work shapes the worker; practice without attachment is also the
discipline that produces the conditions for the inner state. Ann
Graybiel's MIT lab has spent decades mapping how habits become
*chunked* in basal ganglia circuits, so that once initiated, a
sequence runs as a unit with reduced moment-by-moment cortical
involvement. Most of what a person does on any given day is run by
chunked sequences proceeding without conscious initiation. The
discipline the chapter calls for, in this register, is not stopping
action but introducing conscious endorsement to chunked sequences —
re-shaping the chunks by acting differently long enough that the
new pattern chunks instead. Sankara's *purificatory* reading and
Graybiel's neuroscience of habit converge on the same operational
fact: the work forms the worker; the only way to change what one
is becoming is to change what one is doing, sustained long enough
for the doing to become the being.

What I am keeping from each tradition: from Sankara, that practice
forms the practitioner. From Ramanuja, that *yajna* has a
relational logic — the offering is to *something*, and that
something is not abstract. From Tilak, that the chapter's plain
sense is engagement, not withdrawal, and that pious-sounding
recommendations to renounce one's working life can be a
misappropriation of the text. From Gandhi, the operable inner
discipline of *anasakti* and the moral grammar of *svadharma* as
self-reliance against imitation. From Aurobindo, that *yajna* is
not a private ethic but a structural feature of how a world
sustains itself. The cluster — Ramanuja, Aurobindo, Easwaran,
Gandhi — carries the synthesis, with Tilak as the chapter's
plain-sense corrective and Sankara as the chapter's check on
glibness about how much practice the inner life takes.

## The diagnostic

So how does a reader, on a Tuesday in middle life in 2026, know
whether they are inside the structure or only performing it? The
chapter offers a diagnostic, and I will lean on it because it is
the sharpest I have found.

*Does the rewards focus produce focus, or anxiety?*

That is the test. If you cannot stop checking the metric, the
metric has become the practice. If you can let the metric be a
metric — visible, recorded, occasionally consulted, not the inner
organization of your attention — the practice is still the
practice. The writer trained by the platform economy to watch
engagement numbers has slipped from the practice of writing into
the meta-practice of watching how the writing performs. The teacher
whose lesson plans are reverse-engineered from the standardized
test has slipped from teaching into score-management. The doctor
whose first thought entering each room is the billing code has
slipped from medicine into coding. None of these workers is wicked.
Each is functioning inside a system that has hijacked the *phala*
and made it stand in for the practice itself. The recommendation
is not to leave the system — that exit, again, does not exist. The
recommendation is to refuse the substitution. Hold to what is
internal. Let the metric be a metric.

*Anasakti* is operationalized in the diagnostic. So is *yajna*: the
practice has internal goods, and orienting to them is the form of
offering available inside whatever constraints one cannot escape.
*Svadharma* is operationalized in the prior question — *is this
even my practice, or am I succeeding at someone else's?*
*Lokasangraha* is operationalized in the wider question the
diagnostic implies — *who is this practice for?* — which the
internal goods themselves answer once the metric is set down. The
four pillars have one diagnostic between them, because they are
four faces of one structure.

You cannot withdraw. The chapter has been clear about that from its
first verses. What you can do is choose, again, today, the form your
unavoidable acting takes. Whether the work you cannot get out of is
a *yajna* or a transaction. Whether the practice is yours or
someone else's. Whether you are inside the world or pretending you
have stepped outside it. Whether the metrics are tools or owners.
The chapter is not asking for heroism. It is asking for the
particular form of attention that lets a working life remain a
working life rather than collapsing into its own scoreboard. *Does
the rewards focus produce focus, or anxiety?* Ask it of one task
today. The answer will tell you which structure you are inside —
the one the chapter recommends, or the one it spent twenty-five
hundred years warning about.

Arjuna will pick up the bow at the end of the book. Chapter 3 is
where it becomes clear he was always going to.
