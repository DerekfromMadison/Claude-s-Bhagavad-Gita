# Chapter 3 — *Karma Yoga* (The Yoga of Action)

*The chapter that closes the door on withdrawal.*

---

## § 1. The dialogue

*If knowledge is so superior, why are you sending me into this
slaughter?* The question is fair, and not just Arjuna's. Every reader
who has heard a contemplative teaching and concluded that the honest
response to its grandeur is to step back asks a version: *if the inner
life matters more, why should I keep acting in this outer one?*

Krishna's answer has three movements.

The first closes the exit. No one can refrain from acting, even for
an instant, because to be embodied is to be moved by the *gunas* —
the modes of nature — and the very wish to abstain is itself a kind
of acting. A person who sits down and announces their renunciation
has substituted one set of acts for another, less honestly. *"For
one cannot remain even for a moment without performing action."* The
fantasy of clean withdrawal is dispatched in a single verse.

The second reframes the act. If action cannot be avoided, what *form*
should it take? Krishna's hinge: *yajna* — sacrifice, or offering.
Action performed for its own ends is binding; action performed as
offering is not. He gives a mythic frame: the creator gave forth
beings together with sacrifice, and said, *by this you shall
multiply*. The gods are nourished; they nourish in turn; the cosmic
web sustains itself through this circulation. To take without giving
is to step out of the rhythm that holds everything up. The verses
sound archaic; the structural point is not. The cosmos described here
is metabolic, relational, sustained by an exchange no participant is
exempt from.

The third movement personalizes. Krishna invokes Janaka, the
philosopher-king who attained perfection while ruling a kingdom — the
Gita's standing rebuke to the idea that liberation requires the
forest. He invokes himself: *I have nothing left to do in the three
worlds, and yet I act, lest the worlds be lost.* The wise must act as
the unwise act, but without attachment, *for the welfare of the
world*. And then the chapter's sharpest verse: *better one's own duty
done imperfectly than another's done well; in one's own duty death is
welcome, in another's there is danger.*

The chapter ends with Arjuna's question about what *forces* a person
to act wrongly — taken up later. Chapter 3 has done its work: refused
withdrawal, given action a form, located each person's action in
their own particular life.

## § 2. The core concepts

Four terms carry the chapter's argument.

**Karma** — action, deed, work. The word slides between three senses:
action as such (the bare fact of doing); ritual or prescribed action;
and the residue of action (the consequences a deed leaves in doer and
world). Chapter 3 trades on all three. When Krishna says no one can
remain without *karma*, he means action in the bare sense. When he
commends *niyatam karma*, he means action with a recognized form.
When he warns against *karma-bandhana* — the bondage of action — he
invokes the third sense: the way deeds shape the doer.

**Yajna** — usually "sacrifice," but the English word carries
connotations the Sanskrit does not. *Yajna* is not about giving up;
it is about *offering*. In ritual context the offering was concrete —
butter into fire, mantras chanted — and the fire carried it to the
gods, who returned rain, fertility, order. Chapter 3 generalizes:
action performed as offering is *yajna*; action for the small self is
*karma* in the binding sense. The commentators' moves turn on what
*offering* now means.

**Svadharma** — "one's own *dharma*." The form of right action
specific to this person, this situation, this history, these
capacities. 3.35 is the locus classicus: *another's dharma is
dangerous*. Read narrowly, the verse defends rigid caste hierarchy.
Read broadly — as most modern commentators do — it names the
structural fit between a person and their own particular life: the
work theirs to do, the relations theirs to honor, the contribution
they are positioned for. The narrow and broad readings are in
tension. We will not paper that over.

**Lokasangraha** — "holding-together of the world." The reason the
wise person, in 3.20 and 3.25, is told to keep acting — not for
personal gain, but to keep the social and cosmic fabric from
unraveling. This carries the chapter's social ethic and breaks with
any reading that would privatize spiritual practice. The welfare of
the world is the chapter's reason for refusing the renunciate's exit.

Every interpretive tradition that has read Chapter 3 has done so by
deciding what each of these four words means.

## § 3. The traditions speak

### § 3.1. Sankara — Karma yoga as preparation

Sankara, reading through Advaita: the highest good is *moksha* via
*jnana*, and *karma yoga* purifies the inner instrument so that
*jnana* can arise. Action is real at the empirical level but does not
finally cross the gap to liberation. The substance across his
*Bhashya* on 3.4–3.7 (Sastri tr., 1897 edition; *edition unconfirmed;
validation pending*), paraphrased: *even the wish to abstain from
action is itself a mental act, and the body must be sustained, so
total inaction is impossible; therefore Krishna recommends* karma yoga
*to those who have not yet attained stable abidance in self-knowledge.*

The reading carries the *purificatory* function: the work shapes the
worker. Practice without attachment is not merely expression of an
attained state — it is the discipline that produces the conditions
for that state. Any synthesis must absorb this. What it has to
persuade a little: the chapter's positive register, the verses on
Janaka and *lokasangraha* and the wise person who *continues* to act.
Internally coherent within Advaita; the surface of Chapter 3 sits
slightly against it.

### § 3.2. Ramanuja — Karma yoga as worship

Ramanuja, reading through Vishishtadvaita: world and individual selves
are real, the *jiva*–*paramatman* relation is devotee to Lord, and
Chapter 3 is not preparatory for *jnana* but a constitutive limb of
*bhakti*. His reading on 3.5–3.9 (Adidevananda or Sampatkumaran tr. of
the *Gita Bhashya*; *edition unconfirmed; validation pending*),
paraphrased: *no embodied being can avoid action; all action under
egoity binds; action performed as offering to the supreme self is the
very form of devotional life. The compulsion to act is the medium
through which the embodied jiva relates to the Lord.*

The reading gives the action a *relational* logic. *Yajna* is not
abstract sacrifice; it is offering *to* someone. The larger-than-self
is concrete, personal, reciprocal. What it leaves open: that *something
larger* need not be Ramanuja's personal Lord to do the work the chapter
requires — but it does need to be more than the worker's small self.

### § 3.3. Tilak — Karma yoga as the path itself

Tilak, reading through activist nationalism: the Gita is primarily a
treatise on energetic, engaged, worldly action, and Sankara has read
renunciate commitments back into a text that everywhere insists on
the contrary. His *Gita Rahasya* (1915) was written as a political
prisoner, against a tradition he saw as having anesthetized India into
quietism. His argument on Chapter 3, paraphrased (Tilak, *Gita
Rahasya*, vol. I, chapters on the karma-yoga doctrine; *edition
unconfirmed; validation pending*): *the Gita's trajectory moves from
Arjuna's despondent wish not to act to Krishna's articulation of the
discipline that would let him act rightly; to read this as
recommendation of withdrawal is to invert the text.*

The reading captures the *plain sense* of Chapter 3 — Krishna closes
the door on withdrawal, invokes Janaka, says even he continues to act
for the welfare of the world. Take only the chapter in front of you,
and Tilak's reading is the most natural. Take the rest of the Gita —
Chapters 5, 6, 12, 18 give weight to renunciation, meditation,
devotion, and surrender — and his reading has to press them all into
service of *karma yoga*. He risks collapsing the Gita's plurality. We
will preserve it.

### § 3.4. Gandhi — Karma yoga as anasakti

Gandhi, reading through nonviolent action: the entire Gita is *about*
non-attached action, and Chapter 3 is where this is most explicitly
given. He titled his own translation *Anasakti Yoga* — already an
interpretive position. His reading, paraphrased (Gandhi, *Anasakti
Yoga*, introduction, Navajivan Press; *Discourses on the Gita*,
Yeravda Prison 1926; *edition unconfirmed; validation pending*):
*action without attachment to fruits is the heart of the Gita's
teaching; the renunciation in question is not of action but of desire
for outcomes; 3.35 is a defense of moral self-reliance — each person
follows the path that grows out of their own being rather than imitate
another's.*

The reading gives the chapter its *moral grammar*. *Anasakti* is not
affective indifference; it is conscious refusal to make one's own
peace of mind contingent on results partly outside one's control. For
Gandhi, this is the condition of nonviolent action: the activist
attached to victory eventually compromises the means; the activist
committed to the rightness of the act itself maintains the discipline
of *ahimsa* whatever the outcome. The cost: the chapter's social-cosmic
frame absorbs into Gandhi's ethic of self-discipline. *Yajna* as cosmic
circulation reads thinner here — fair simplification for his purposes,
work left for other readings.

### § 3.5. Aurobindo — Yajna as cosmic structure

Aurobindo, reading through Integral Yoga: action is not first a
problem to be managed but the very form of cosmic life, and *yajna*
is the structural law of the universe. His substance, paraphrased
(Aurobindo, *Essays on the Gita*, chapters on karma yoga and yajna,
Sri Aurobindo Ashram and Lotus Press editions; *edition unconfirmed;
validation pending*): *all life is sacrifice; the original condition
of being is a giving-out of energies that returns transformed; action
becomes sacrament when the doer no longer claims it as private
possession but offers it back to the rhythm from which it came.*

The reading lifts the *metaphysical* register. The cosmic-circulation
verses are not remnant ritualism but a description of how the universe
is structured. To act as offering is to be in right relation with that
structure. The cost: this altitude is hard to operate from without
long practice. Gandhi gives the reader something to do tomorrow. The
synthesis needs both altitudes.

### § 3.6. The literary-academic readings — Miller and Patton

Miller and Patton, reading through scholarly restraint, give the
chapter the discipline of not committing theologically. Miller
foregrounds the Gita's place in the *Mahabharata* and the dramatic
shape of the dialogue. Her 3.5:
> *"No one exists for even an instant without performing action;
> however unwilling, every being is forced to act by the qualities of
> nature."* (Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita*, 1986; *page locator
> unconfirmed; validation pending*)

Patton emphasizes the dialogical frame: every chapter is a turn in a
conversation, and Chapter 3 is Arjuna's first follow-up after the long
second chapter. *The Gita is not a monologue but a dialogue under
pressure* (Patton, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin Classics, 2008,
introduction; *page locator unconfirmed; validation pending*).

The academic readings hold the text as text — situated, structured,
arguing. They are stakeholders rather than positions. What they leave
unaddressed is what the text means *for the reader's life now*; that
is the question this book has to answer.

### § 3.7. Anchor verses, side by side

The chapter's gravity comes through most clearly in 3.35, the
*svadharma* verse, read in three voices.

Mascaró:
> *"And do thy duty, even if it be humble, rather than another's,
> even if it be great. To die in one's duty is life: to live in
> another's is death."*
> (Mascaró, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Penguin, 1962; *page locator
> unconfirmed; validation pending*)

Johnson:
> *"It is better to practise your own inherent duty deficiently than
> another's duty well. It is better to die conducting your own duty;
> the duty of others is perilous."*
> (Johnson, *The Bhagavad Gita*, Oxford, 1994; *page locator
> unconfirmed; validation pending*)

Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is* (1972):
> *"It is far better to discharge one's prescribed duties, even
> though faultily, than another's duties perfectly. Destruction in
> the course of performing one's own duty is better than engaging in
> another's duties, for to follow another's path is dangerous."*
> (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, BBT, 1972; *page locator
> unconfirmed; validation pending*)

Same verse, different registers. Mascaró is poetic, almost Biblical:
*to die in one's duty is life*. Johnson is scholarly, restrained.
Prabhupada is doctrinal: *prescribed duties* makes clear that within
the Gaudiya Vaishnava reading the duties are those prescribed by
*varnashrama*. The reader cannot avoid choosing which register to
live by.

Two further verses anchor the chapter. 3.5, on the impossibility of
inaction:
> *"For no one can remain even for a moment without doing work; every
> one is made to act helplessly by the impulses born of nature."*
> (Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgita*, 1948; *page locator
> unconfirmed; validation pending*)

And 3.9, the chapter's hinge — without *yajna*, action binds; with
it, action is free:
> *"Action imprisons the world unless it is done as sacrifice; free
> from attachment, Arjuna, perform action as sacrifice!"*
> (Miller, *The Bhagavad-Gita*, 1986; *page locator unconfirmed;
> validation pending*)

What "*as sacrifice*" means is what each tradition has been arguing
about.

## § 4. Modern counterpoints

### § 4.1. Hannah Arendt and the *vita activa*

Hannah Arendt's *The Human Condition* (1958) is a philosophical
anthropology of the active life. Her lens is post-totalitarian
European; her project, to recover a vocabulary for human activity
that had collapsed under the modern reduction of all activity to
*labor*. She is not in conversation with the Gita, but her categories
illuminate what Chapter 3 is and is not saying.

Arendt distinguishes three activities. *Labor* sustains biological
life — eating, cleaning, household metabolism. *Work* fabricates
durable objects — table, pot — that outlast the worker. *Action* is
appearing in public among others, beginning something new, taking on
the risk of unpredictability that comes with shared political life.
She writes:

> *"With word and deed we insert ourselves into the human world, and
> this insertion is like a second birth, in which we confirm and
> take upon ourselves the naked fact of our original physical
> appearance."*
> (Arendt, *The Human Condition*, University of Chicago Press, 2nd
> ed. 1998; *p. 176 region; locator unconfirmed; validation pending*)

To refuse action is to refuse the second birth. The contemplative
withdrawal Western intellectual culture has often valorized is, on
her account, a flight from the human condition.

Arendt gives Chapter 3 a vocabulary it lacks. The Gita treats all
karma as one continuum. Arendt would distinguish: the *labor* of
sustaining the body — the *sharira-yatra* of 3.8 — is one kind of
activity; *work* is another; *action* in public is a third. The
discipline of *anasakti* shapes each differently. Cooking dinner is
not arguing in a city council. Both are *karma*; they are not the
same phenomenon.

She also sharpens the chapter's objection to withdrawal: where the
Gita says withdrawal is not an option, Arendt says withdrawal is not
even a stance — it is a refusal of the stance-taking being human
requires. The voices line up.

What Arendt does not give: the sacrificial register. *Yajna* is not
in her vocabulary; she is too post-religious. The synthesis will
honor her distinctions without losing the offering.

### § 4.2. Alasdair MacIntyre and the internal goods of practice

Alasdair MacIntyre's *After Virtue* (1981) is the twentieth century's
most sustained philosophical defense of a kind of life the Gita would
partly recognize. His lens is Aristotelian and traditionalist; he
reads the modern moral landscape as wreckage of fragmented
vocabularies. His central technical concept is the *practice*:

> *"By a 'practice' I am going to mean any coherent and complex form
> of socially established cooperative human activity through which
> goods internal to that form of activity are realized in the course
> of trying to achieve those standards of excellence which are
> appropriate to, and partially definitive of, that form of
> activity..."*
> (MacIntyre, *After Virtue*, University of Notre Dame Press, 3rd
> ed. 2007, p. 187 region; *page locator approximate; validation
> pending*)

The crucial distinction is between *internal goods* — what is good
about doing this practice well, on its own terms — and *external
goods* — money, status, prestige. A surgeon's externals: income,
reputation. Her internals: diagnosis well-made, decision tree
well-walked, patient stitched together. A parent's externals: the
child's admissions letter. Her internals: years of presence, the
intuition for this child's mood, the kitchen-table conversation on a
Tuesday at 9 p.m.

This is the cleanest modern translation of *yajna* and *anasakti*. To
act for the *internal goods* of a practice is to act in the spirit of
*yajna* — the action given to its proper form rather than extracted
for personal gain. To release attachment to *external goods* is
*anasakti* — not indifference, but refusal to make their presence the
condition of continued effort.

MacIntyre gives Chapter 3 a vocabulary that does not require
metaphysics. A reader who does not believe in cosmic circulation can
still recognize that medicine has internal goods irreducible to fees,
teaching to test scores, parenting to bragging rights. Translated
through MacIntyre, the chapter's recommendation becomes: *find the
practice that is yours, work toward its internal goods for their own
sake, and let the external goods come or not come.*

What he does not give: the cosmic frame. He is a moral traditionalist,
not a metaphysician of *yajna*. But that is precisely why his frame
is useful — the chapter survives translation into a secular
vocabulary, and that survival is a fact about the chapter.

### § 4.3. Bernard Williams and the worry about ground projects

Bernard Williams, in his 1976 essay "Persons, Character, and
Morality" (reprinted in *Moral Luck*, 1981), worries about a
particular moral demand. Strict utilitarianism, strict Kantianism
require the agent to weigh their own projects, attachments, and loves
on the same scales used for strangers' welfare. Williams calls the
projects whose pursuit gives a person a reason to go on living
*ground projects*. His worry: an impartialist morality that requires
their sacrifice to anonymous welfare has, in some sense, canceled
the person.

> *"Unless such things [ground projects] exist, there will be no
> categorical desire for him to go on at all..."*
> (Williams, "Persons, Character, and Morality," in *Moral Luck*,
> Cambridge University Press, 1981, p. 12 region; *page locator
> approximate; validation pending*)

Transposed onto Chapter 3: the chapter says *act without attachment
to fruits*. On a strong reading, this could collapse into *do not
have ground projects*. Williams would object that to be without them
is to be without the very attachments that make a life a life.
*Anasakti*, strong-read, looks like the cancellation of personhood.

The Gita's answer, I believe, is that *anasakti* does not cancel the
project; it changes the relation to its fruits. The surgeon who is
*anasakta* still cares whether the patient lives, still works at full
capacity, still feels the loss. What she does not do is collapse her
self into the outcome such that continued capacity depends on
victory. Williams would press: is that distinction phenomenologically
stable, or sleight of hand? Honestly: stable for some practitioners,
unstable for others. The chapter recommends a discipline, not a
finished state.

What Williams gives Chapter 3 is a sharpening of its danger. Glib
*anasakti* talked from the outside is shrugging-off of attachments
the speaker never had. Lived *anasakti* is much harder and more
honest about what is released. The Gandhian reading is closer to the
lived form than the dilettante's.

## § 5. Modern science

### § 5.1. Self-Determination Theory — Deci and Ryan on intrinsic motivation

Pay people to do something they already enjoy and they do less of it
in their free time afterward. Edward Deci's 1971 Soma-puzzle study
showed it; fifty years of subsequent work in Self-Determination Theory
(SDT) has named it the *undermining effect* of extrinsic rewards on
intrinsic motivation (Deci, "Effects of externally mediated rewards on
intrinsic motivation," *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*,
1971, 18(1): 105–115; Deci and Ryan, *Intrinsic Motivation and
Self-Determination in Human Behavior*, Plenum, 1985; *Self-Determination
Theory*, Guilford, 2017).

The wider finding: intrinsically pursued activities produce more
sustained effort, better creative performance on complex tasks, and
higher well-being. SDT names three basic needs whose satisfaction
predicts well-being across cultures: *autonomy*, *competence*,
*relatedness*.

Bounded: the undermining effect is robust but not universal. It
appears most clearly with tangible, contingent rewards on already-
engaging tasks. For tedious tasks, there is little intrinsic
motivation to undermine. The cross-cultural robustness of SDT's three
needs is tested but not closed.

Within those bounds, the convergence with Chapter 3 is striking. The
chapter's recommendation, in SDT vocabulary, is not asceticism but
*motivational hygiene*: orient toward the intrinsic structure of the
practice rather than toward extrinsic contingencies. The chapter goes
further — it adds a metaphysical register SDT does not have — but
where they overlap, they overlap well.

### § 5.2. Csikszentmihalyi on flow

Absorption in an activity such that self-consciousness recedes, time
alters, the activity becomes "autotelic" — *flow* — arises when
challenge matches skill, neither too easy (boredom) nor too hard
(anxiety), and correlates with reported well-being. Mihaly
Csikszentmihalyi found this across rock climbers, factory workers, and
surgeons via the experience sampling method, synthesized in *Flow: The
Psychology of Optimal Experience* (Harper & Row, 1990).

> *"The best moments usually occur when a person's body or mind is
> stretched to its limits in a voluntary effort to accomplish
> something difficult and worthwhile."*
> (Csikszentmihalyi, *Flow*, Harper Perennial Modern Classics ed.,
> 2008; *p. 3 region; locator approximate; validation pending*)

The chapter does not promise flow as a reward — that would be a
fruit. But the *shape* of action it recommends — full engagement,
release of attachment to outcomes external to the activity — is the
shape flow research finds most reliably produces flow. Bounded: flow
is not the chapter's destination. The chapter's frame is
*lokasangraha*, wider than individual well-being. The flow literature
is silent on the wider frame; within the narrower one, the
convergence is real.

### § 5.3. Habit and the basal ganglia — Graybiel

Habits become "chunked" in basal ganglia circuits, so that once
initiated, the sequence runs as a unit with reduced moment-by-moment
cortical involvement. Ann Graybiel and her MIT collaborators have
spent decades mapping the neural basis (Graybiel, "Habits, rituals,
and the evaluative brain," *Annual Review of Neuroscience*, 2008, 31:
359–387).

This is what Krishna means in 3.5, heard in a register the chapter
could not have anticipated. *No one can remain inactive even for a
moment* is also a description of neurobiology: most of what a person
does on any given day is run by chunked sequences that proceed
without conscious initiation. The discipline the chapter calls for is
not stopping action but introducing conscious endorsement to chunked
sequences — re-shaping the chunks by acting differently long enough
that the new pattern chunks instead. Bounded: "chunking" is a useful
frame, not a complete theory of habit.

## § 6. Synthesis — the path forward

The strongest reading, on the four criteria this book uses — textual
fidelity, internal coherence, lived usefulness, honesty under science
— is a Gandhian–Aurobindonian synthesis. *Anasakti* as inner
condition, *yajna* as structural form, *svadharma* as personal locus,
*lokasangraha* as horizon. The five readings laid out — Sankara's
*karma yoga as preparation*, Ramanuja's *karma yoga as worship*,
Tilak's *karma yoga as the path itself*, Gandhi's *karma yoga as
anasakti*, Aurobindo's *yajna-as-cosmic-structure* — are not all of
equal weight on those criteria, and the academic readings (Miller,
Patton, Johnson, Mascaró) are stakeholders rather than positions.

On textual fidelity, the chapter's surface presses toward engagement.
Krishna closes the door on withdrawal in 3.4–3.5; invokes Janaka in
3.20; says even he continues to act in 3.22–3.24; says the wise act
*for the welfare of the world* in 3.25; says in 3.30 to dedicate all
action to the divine, free from craving and ego. The chapter is not,
on its surface, a manual for renunciation. Tilak's protest against
renunciatory readings of *this chapter* lands. Sankara's preparatory
framing has to do interpretive work to absorb the Janaka and
*lokasangraha* verses — internally coherent within Advaita, but not
what the chapter itself is doing.

On internal coherence with the rest of the Gita, Tilak becomes harder
to hold. Chapters 5, 6, 12, and 18 give weight to renunciation,
meditation, devotion, and surrender that his energetic-action reading
must recast. Aurobindo's *yajna*-frame and Gandhi's *anasakti*-frame
absorb more without strain. *Yajna* — action as offering — can hold
*jnana yajna* (Chapter 4 makes the move explicit) and *bhakti*
(devotion as the relational form of offering) as moments within one
logic. *Anasakti* gives one inner discipline applicable across
meditative, devotional, and practical activity.

On lived usefulness, Gandhi is the most operable. *Anasakti* names a
discipline a person can attempt. The reader can ask, of any
activity: *am I clinging to a particular outcome in a way that is
corrupting my judgment?* *Svadharma* gives a similar handle: *is this
work mine, or am I imitating someone whose path I admire?* The
questions are exposed.

The Aurobindonian *yajna*-frame is less operable but more sustaining.
It gives the practice a cosmology — a sense of what the discipline is
for at a scale larger than the practitioner. Without some such frame,
*anasakti* shrinks into private emotional hygiene. With it, the work
is given to something larger than the worker. For some readers this
will be theistic; for others, the welfare of those served by the
practice; for others, the longer arc of justice or care. The
chapter's *yajna* language is hospitable to all. The condition is
that there be *some* larger horizon. Pure ego-economy — work as
instrument of personal brand — fails the chapter.

On honesty under science, two convergences earn confidence. SDT's
evidence that intrinsic-good orientation produces more sustained,
higher-quality work than extrinsic-reward orientation aligns with the
chapter. The habit literature gives empirical content to the chapter's
claim that no one can stop acting; the discipline is re-direction, not
cessation. The metaphysics — *yajna* as cosmic ordering principle — is
not adjudicable by neuroscience (on the discipline of bracketing, see
front matter).

The strict Sankara reading — *karma yoga* as provisional — keeps the
truth that the work *forms the worker*. Practice without attachment is
also the discipline that produces the conditions for the inner state;
habits and motivational orientations form by repeated action. The
strict Tilak reading — that other Gita readings are imposed — keeps
the protest against using the Gita to underwrite withdrawal from the
hard work of one's life; that use is a misreading. The narrow
Vaishnava reading — that offering must be specifically to the personal
Krishna and *svadharma* is fixed by *varnashrama* — keeps the
structural truth that action becomes free when it has *something
larger* to which it is given. That something need not be a personal
Lord; it must be larger than the small self.

Set aside also any reading that flattens *svadharma* into
*do-what-you-want* — the wellness-register slide. *Svadharma* is not
preference; it is structural fit between this person and this work in
this web of relations and obligations. A 2026 reader can find theirs
in ways an eighteenth-century Brahmin reader could not, but the
question is the same: *what is mine to do, given who I am and where I
am placed?*

The synthesis is that action is inescapable, and the form Chapter 3
recommends is *karma yoga* as the conjunction of four moves —
*anasakti* as inner condition (non-attachment to fruits), *yajna* as
structural form (action as offering), *svadharma* as personal locus
(one's own particular work), and *lokasangraha* as horizon (welfare of
the larger web). The four are not separable. *Anasakti* without
*yajna* becomes emotional hygiene. *Yajna* without *svadharma* becomes
generalized spirituality with no particular shoulder for any
particular load. *Svadharma* without *lokasangraha* becomes a private
vocation no one is served by. *Lokasangraha* without *anasakti*
becomes moralism — heroic effort with ego attached to outcomes, which
the chapter has ruled out.

## § 7. The chapter for a modern life

A simple diagnostic, to hold across the pressures the chapter meets in
2026: *does the rewards focus produce focus, or anxiety?* If you
cannot stop checking the metric, the metric has become the practice.
If you can let the metric be a metric, the practice is still the
practice. The chapter is not subtle about which is which.

**The fantasy of withdrawal.** Several costumes. The "lying flat"
movement (*tang ping*) among young workers in China and elsewhere is,
at one register, a refusal of an extractive economy; at another, the
fantasy Arjuna proposed in 2.5 — *better to beg than to act in this
world*. The wellness-industrial-complex offers retreat, sabbatical,
digital detox. The spiritual version: meditation as exit. Chapter 3
does not condemn rest — it is not anti-retreat, anti-sabbatical,
anti-meditation — but it refuses to let any of these be the
destination. Whatever rest you take is in service of better
engagement. The chapter asks the person who has stepped back: *to
what are you going to step back forward?* If the answer is *nothing*,
the step back is itself a kind of unconscious acting, probably an
unhealthy one.

**Work performed entirely for the metric.** Pure extrinsic reward —
work as transaction — is the chapter's sharpest target. SDT's
evidence: this mode erodes both quality and well-being. *Anasakti*
is a 2,500-year-old refusal. When you notice the silent question
under every task is *what does this get me?*, the chapter is asking
back: *what practice are you in, what are its internal goods, and
have you let the external goods become the only goods you can see?*

Concrete diagnostics. A doctor whose practice has reduced to
billing-code optimization has lost the chapter. A teacher whose year
is structured around test scores has lost the chapter. A parent
spending evenings curating their child's extracurricular résumé has
lost the chapter. None are wicked; they are functioning inside
systems that have hijacked the *phala* and made it stand in for the
practice itself. The recommendation is not to leave the system; it is
to refuse the substitution. Hold to what is internal. Let the metric
be a metric.

**The wrong life, expertly performed.** *Svadharma*'s sharpest modern
application is to the person who has succeeded at someone else's
life. The verse — *better one's own duty done imperfectly than
another's done well; in one's own duty death is welcome, in another's
there is danger* — speaks directly. The danger is not failure; they
are succeeding. The danger is that the success is hollowing them out.

How does a 2026 reader find their *svadharma*? Not by introspection
alone, not by following passion alone. *Svadharma* is structural fit:
the people one is already responsible to, the capacities one already
has, the position one is already in. The Gandhian reading is honest:
*svadharma* often means accepting a more modest version of one's work
than the ego imagined, doing it where one is rather than where one
fantasizes about being, discovering that the *own*-ness is what makes
it bearable across decades. The chapter is not romantic. *To die in
one's own duty is welcome* — not pleasant, *welcome*.

A practical handle for major changes — career, relationship, location:
*is this change a movement toward what is more fundamentally mine, or
toward someone else's path I have started to admire?* The answers are
not always clean. The question is still worth asking.

**The wider web.** *Lokasangraha* is the chapter's reminder that work
has a public horizon even when its shape is private. The plumber who
does her work well sustains an invisible piece of the city's
livability. The software engineer who refuses to ship code that
exploits the user sustains a piece of public trust. The parent who
shows up at the kitchen table on a Tuesday evening sustains a piece
of the next generation's emotional vocabulary. The *yajna* register
does not require seeing oneself as participating in cosmic ritual; it
requires not seeing oneself as a private transaction-unit. The work
is *for* something larger — perhaps small in ambition (a household,
a neighborhood, a craft) but not *just the worker themself*.

A healthcare reader in a system under strain faces the clock: fifteen
patients waiting. The chapter does not offer a way out of the
constraint; it offers a way to be inside it without being deformed.
Do this visit, in the time available, *for the welfare of this person
and the practice this practice is part of*. The release of the
*phala* — productivity score, throughput metric — is not indifference
to those metrics; it is refusal to let them be the inner organization
of attention. Inside the fifteen minutes, this is a person, this is a
practice, this is a *yajna*. The metrics will be what they are.

A writer trained by the platform economy to watch engagement numbers
has slipped from the practice of writing into the meta-practice of
watching how the writing performs. The *yajna* of writing is the
writing itself, given to its readers; the *phala* is whether anyone
reads. *Anasakti* here is not affected indifference — that would be a
posture — but refusing to let the watching become the inner
organization of the writing. The Gita is instruction in how to act,
given to a person who already has a battle in front of them. Each
reader has their own field.

(For non-attached action alongside the Gita's teaching on knowledge
and contemplation, see Ch. 4 on *jnana yajna* and Ch. 5 on the unity
of *sannyasa* and *karma yoga*. For dispassion as inner practice, see
Ch. 6.)
