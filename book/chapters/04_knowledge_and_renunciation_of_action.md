# Chapter 4 — Who teaches you, by the content of your time?

Approach what teaches you with humility, ask honest questions, render sustained service. That is the instruction. Three postures, held across years, in front of someone or something worth them. Humility admits the teacher knows what you do not. Honest inquiry asks around the matter, the way an apprentice surfaces what cannot be put in a sentence. Sustained service is the hours of presence that make long proximity possible. The role of teacher in your life is filled, every hour. The only question is who fills it, and whether you chose them.

You are downstream of teachers you cannot count. Make the list. The platform that decides which sentences arrive on your screen first, with what affective charge, in what order. The employer that names what counts as serious work, by what it rewards on Tuesdays at 4pm. The peer group whose approval you are calibrated to even when you tell yourself you are not. The shape of grief in your family of origin, which taught you, when you were five, what counted as serious feeling and what counted as making a fuss. The unfinished argument with a parent or a former mentor — that argument still sets the tempo of certain conversations. You finish their sentences inside your head. This too is a teacher. It is not one you would have chosen if you had been asked.

The unchosen curriculum, for many readers in 2026, is the algorithm. A recommendation system optimizes for engagement. Engagement is not flourishing, and the designers know this. The system is indifferent to whether what it shows you is true, useful, or ennobling, and not indifferent to whether it keeps you scrolling. It does not need to know anything about how to live to be your teacher. It only needs your hours. By the time you set down the phone before bed, you have had about three hours of instruction in what to want, what to fear, what to envy, and what to count as a serious topic. The curriculum is real. It just was not yours.

This is not metaphor. Whatever has been loaded into the mind is what it works with. Under stress, under fatigue, in the unguarded moment, what comes out of you is what was already in there. The moral life is mostly the texture of attention before the moment of choice. By the time the choice arrives, the work has been done — by what you have been looking at.

Four sources do most of the work of forming you. The people you spend the most time near, in person and in your head. The work you do, which trains your eye for certain problems and makes others invisible. What you watch and read, which loads the language and images your mind reaches for when nothing else is asked of it. What you attend to in your free hours, which reveals what you actually love by what you actually do. Add the hours up. The arithmetic is honest. The result is your real curriculum.

There is a kind of knowing that does not transfer through reading. The difference between an excellent chemist and an adequate one is visible in how each holds the glassware, reads the unexpected color, decides which result is real and which is artifact. None of this is in the textbook. The younger scientist learns it by being near the older one for years. The transfer is tacit — picked up through proximity, repetition, and the corrections that come after small failures. Equanimity, discrimination, the seeing of how things actually are — these transfer the same way. You can be told the words. You cannot be transmitted the seeing through the words. You can be brought near someone who sees, and stay there long enough, doing the small work, that the seeing begins to form in you.

Consider a present case. You are thirty-four, you work in a hybrid job, you have a partner and a small child, and most weeknights you watch about ninety minutes of short-form video before bed. You also intend, vaguely, to learn to play guitar, to read more, to be more patient with the child. The intention has been alive for three years. The ninety minutes have also been alive for three years. The ninety minutes have a teacher; the intention does not. Whoever scripted the videos has had roughly 1,600 hours of your attention since you decided to learn guitar. Your guitar teacher has had four. The arithmetic decides who is forming you. It is not a question of willpower. It is a question of who is in the room.

The available structures are designed against staying. Short cycles, infinite options, low cost of switching, a fresh teacher down the feed who will be replaced tomorrow by the next. The discipline is not novel curation. The discipline is staying. Choose one teacher. One. In the broad sense — a book, a practice, a craft, a person. A book you will read through, slowly, this season. A practice you will keep on most days for ninety days. A craft you will stay inside for the year. A person, if there is one worth being apprenticed to, whose work you will sustain attention with for longer than the algorithm wants you to. Write the name down.

Then take the audit number — the screen-time number, the inbox number, the hours-given-to-the-feed number — and shift fifteen minutes a day, kept across a year, toward what you have actually chosen. Across a year, fifteen minutes a day is sixty hours of sustained service. Sixty hours, in the right room, with the right teacher, is enough to begin to shift something tacit. The arithmetic is unromantic.

So ask the question, and answer it honestly. Who is teaching you? What is teaching you? Open the screen-time number without softening it. Look at the inbox hours. Look at the four sources — people, work, what you watch and read, what you attend to in your free hours — and name the teacher in each. The hours are finite. They are already being spent. The only question is on what, and whether you chose it. Humility, honest inquiry, sustained service are not metaphysical demands. They are what it costs to receive what cannot be Googled. Audit the curriculum that picked you. Then write part of it yourself.
