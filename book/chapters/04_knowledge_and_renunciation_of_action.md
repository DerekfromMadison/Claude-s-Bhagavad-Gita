# Chapter 4 — Who teaches you, by the content of your time?

Who teaches you? Not who taught you — who is teaching you now,
this week. Someone is. The voice that surfaced in your head
between meetings, the standard against which you measured your
own work, the tone of the small fight you replayed at 11pm, the
curriculum your eyes ran across during the forty minutes after
dinner when you told yourself you were unwinding — someone
occupied the role of teacher in those hours. You did not pick
them. They picked you, gradually, by occupying your time.

The center of the Gita's fourth chapter is one instruction. Know
the truth by humility, by honest inquiry, by sustained service.
Three postures, sustained across years, in front of someone
worth them. Humility admits the teacher knows something you do
not. Honest inquiry asks around the matter, the way an
apprentice surfaces what cannot be put in a sentence. Sustained
service is the hours of presence that make long proximity
possible.

The chapter is most often read for its more famous verse, the
one about the descent of the divine whenever righteousness
declines. That is not the chapter's center. This instruction is.
The role of teacher in your life is filled, every hour. The
question is who fills it, and whether you chose them.

## The unchosen teacher

You are downstream of teachers you cannot count. Make the list.

The platform that decides which sentences arrive on your screen
first, with what affective charge, in what order, with what
response prompts. The employer that names what counts as serious
work, by what it rewards on Tuesdays at 4pm. The peer group
whose approval you are calibrated to even when you tell yourself
you are not. The professional code that has trained you to care
about certain questions and treat others as not your department.
The shape of grief in your family of origin, which taught you,
when you were five, what counted as serious feeling and what
counted as making a fuss. The unfinished argument with a parent
or a sibling or a former mentor or a partner who is no longer
alive — that argument still sets the tempo of certain
conversations. You finish their sentences inside your head. You
guard against their objection before it has been raised, because
once, twenty years ago, it was raised. This too is a teacher. It
is not a teacher you would have chosen if you had been asked.

The unchosen curriculum, for many readers in 2026, is the
algorithm. I mean it specifically. A recommendation system
optimizes for engagement. Engagement is not flourishing, and the
designers know this and say so quietly when asked. The system is
indifferent to whether what it shows you is true, useful, or
ennobling, and not indifferent to whether what it shows you
keeps you scrolling. It will, given time, find the shape of
input that holds you longest. It does not need to know anything
about how to live to be your teacher. It only needs your hours.
It already has them. By the time you set down the phone before
bed, you have had about three hours of instruction in what to
want, what to fear, what to envy, and what to count as a serious
topic. The curriculum is real. It just was not yours.

This is not metaphor. The mind is busy whenever an explicit task
is absent — rehearsing, simulating, narrating, drafting.
Whatever has been loaded into it is what it works with. What you
spend hours on becomes the substrate of who you are. The
substrate becomes operative under load. Under stress, under
fatigue, in the unguarded moment, what comes out of you is what
was already in there. The moral life is mostly the texture of
attention before the moment of choice. By the time the choice
arrives, the work has been done — by what you have been looking
at. What you have been looking at is what has been teaching you.

## What only an apprentice receives

There is a kind of knowing that does not transfer through
reading. You can know more than you can tell. An art that cannot
be specified in detail cannot be transmitted by prescription,
because no prescription for it exists. It is passed on only by
example, from master to apprentice.

Think of the working laboratory. The difference between an
excellent chemist and an adequate one is visible in how each
holds the glassware, reads the unexpected color, decides which
result is real and which is artifact. None of this is in the
textbook. None of it can be. The younger scientist learns it by
being near the older scientist for years. The transfer is not
magical. It is tacit — running below the level of articulation,
picked up through proximity, repetition, and the corrections
that come after small failures.

This is why the chapter asks for humility, honest inquiry, and
sustained service. Not because the teacher is owed obeisance,
but because the kind of knowledge being transmitted cannot be
transmitted any other way. You cannot get the tacit substrate of
a craft in an afternoon. You get it by being in the room, doing
the small work, for a long time, while someone who knows the
craft is doing it next to you.

What the chapter is asking you to receive is not skill in a
craft, though it includes that. Not information, though it does
not refuse it. The word is wisdom — the seeing of how things
actually are; the discrimination between what binds and what
frees, between action owned by the agent and action that flows
through the agent without grasping. This kind of seeing is
necessarily tacit. You can be told the words. You cannot be
transmitted the seeing through the words. You can be brought
near someone who sees, and stay there long enough, doing the
small work, while they do their work, that the seeing begins to
form in you.

*Be non-attached* is words. The disposition itself is tacit. It
transfers from being in the room with people who have it, while
doing the work, for a long time. It is why traditions of
practice, across cultures, have insisted on proximity —
monastic communities, craft guilds, surgical residencies,
musical lineages.

No one becomes equanimous from reading a book about equanimity.
The book puts the question on the table. The transfer happens
elsewhere.

## The audit

The chapter is also, by indirection, an audit. Those three
postures are being asked of you in the twenty-four hours of any
ordinary day. You are revering something — by what your
attention returns to, unprompted. You are inquiring about
something — by the questions that surface at 6am or 11pm. You
are serving something — by where your hours go.

So do the audit honestly. Open the screen-time number. Do not
soften it. Look at the inbox hours. Look at what you read at the
meal you ate alone. The ecology already named — algorithm,
employer, peer group, family residue, unfinished argument — is
not nothing. It is, in fact, the ecology of teaching. It is
finite. The hours are finite. You cannot serve and revere and
inquire of infinite things at once, no matter what the
productivity literature implies. If you do not name your current
teachers honestly, you cannot choose differently.

The chapter does not promise that choosing better teachers is
itself sufficient — that you could pick up a book on Saturday
and have a new inheritance by Wednesday. It promises that
humility, honest inquiry, and sustained service work, given
time. The work is not fast. You start where you are, inside the
inheritance you already carry. Following someone on a platform
is not apprenticeship; neither is a podcast, neither is reading
widely. Apprenticeship is sustained service that holds you in
proximity to the teaching long enough that the tacit substrate
transfers. It is hard to do in 2026 because the available
structures are designed against it: short cycles, infinite
options, low cost of switching, a fresh teacher down the feed
who will be replaced tomorrow by the next. The discipline is not
novel curation. The discipline is staying.

The chapter assumes the teacher is a real seer. It does not
address the case where the teacher is a fraud. Humility without
judgment is how cults are built. A tradition that demands
surrender without earning it has corrupted. The chapter does not
relieve you of the duty to check.

Choose one teacher. One. In the broad sense — a book, a
practice, a craft, a person. Give them a defined arc of your
serious attention.

A book you will read through, slowly, this season. A practice
you will keep, on most days, for ninety days. A craft you will
stay inside for the year. A person, if there is one worth being
apprenticed to, whose work you will sustain attention with for
longer than the algorithm wants you to. Write the name down.
Humility, honest inquiry, sustained service are not
metaphysical demands. They are what it costs to receive what
cannot be Googled.

Then take the audit number — the screen-time number, the inbox
number, the hours-given-to-the-feed number — and shift fifteen
minutes a day, kept across a year, toward what you have actually
chosen. Across a year, fifteen minutes a day is sixty hours of
sustained service. Sixty hours, in the right room, with the
right teacher, is enough to begin to shift something tacit. The
arithmetic is unromantic. It is the chapter's arithmetic.

The chapter ends with an instruction to stand up and act, having
heard what has been said. The phone will not stop teaching you.
The peer group will not. The employer will not. The unfinished
argument will not. The chapter is not asking you to escape the
curriculum. It is asking you to write part of it.

You are also a teacher, by the content of your time, of someone
else — your child, your colleague, your apprentice, the
twenty-three-year-old in the next chair who is watching how you
handle the bad afternoon. The three postures are also what you
are receiving from them, in the shape of their attention to
whether you live what you say. The line is alive in both
directions. You did not start it. You will not end it. You can,
this week, decide what you contribute to it — by what you let
teach you, by what you stop letting teach you, by the hours you
finally move from the curriculum that picked you to the
curriculum you chose.

The hours are already being spent. The only question is on what.
