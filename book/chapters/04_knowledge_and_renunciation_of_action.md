# Chapter 4 — The Lineage

Arjuna. What I am telling you now is not new.

I gave it first to the one who watched the first sunrise. He gave it to the one who came after him. From him it passed to the one who ruled, and from that one to the one who counseled him, and from that one down through the long line of those who were willing to receive it and willing to pass it on. Teacher to student. Hand to hand. Across more lives than you have years to count.

Then it was lost. Not destroyed — set down. Picked up by people who held it for the wrong reasons, or held it without the heat that keeps it alive, or held it as decoration. The chain went thin. The teaching survived in the form of words but the seeing in the words went out.

That is why I am giving it back to you now. Here. On this ground. Because you are willing to listen, and because the situation in front of you is the situation for which the teaching was made.

*Arjuna:* You speak as though you were there at the beginning. You were not. You stand here, my friend, my charioteer, born of a mother and a father I could name. How can you have taught what you say you taught when you were not yet there?

Many lives have passed through me, Arjuna. Many through you also — though the door has been kept closed on yours, and stands open on mine. I remember them. You do not. That is the only difference between us in this matter. When the lawful order goes thin in the world, when what holds people together starts to loosen and what tears them apart starts to rise, I take a body and walk through the time. Not to save anyone. To restore the seeing. To put the teaching back into the hands of the ones who can carry it.

So when I tell you I gave it first, I am telling you something simple. I have been the one giving it for as long as anyone has been receiving it. The voice changes. The teaching does not.

Now hear what the teaching is, in the part of it that touches your situation tonight.

Action binds. That much you already know — you can feel it. Every move you make sends a thread out into the world, and the thread comes back, and pulls. Most of what people call their lives is being pulled by threads they sent out years ago without watching where the thread went. They act, and the act ties them. They reach for a result, and the reach ties them again. By forty, the rope is thick. By sixty, it cannot be moved.

There is one kind of action that does not bind. Action done as offering. Action done with the work given fully and the outcome released. Not indifference — the work has to be precise. The cook still tastes the dish. The surgeon still closes cleanly. The mother still feeds the child carefully. But the grasp at the result, the bargain with the universe, the *if I do this then I receive that* — that is what you let go. The act is given. What returns is not yours.

When action is done that way, it does not stick to you. It passes through. The world is changed by it; you are not entangled by it. This is what the lineage has been carrying. This is what was lost. This is what I am putting back into your hands.

Knowledge is what makes this possible. Without knowledge, action is mechanical — you do, and do, and do, and the doing eats you. Without action, knowledge is sterile — you read, and think, and decide nothing, and the thinking eats you also. Knowledge enters action and burns out the part of action that grasps. The fire of knowledge — and I mean fire, Arjuna, not metaphor — burns the binding out of the act and leaves the act clean. What remains is work. Only work. No claim on what comes after.

You ask how to come into this. I will tell you plainly.

Approach a teacher. Approach with humility. Ask. Serve.

Hear me on what teacher means. Not necessarily a man on a cushion. Not necessarily someone in robes. The hand that does what you are trying to learn. The person whose work you have been near long enough that, without naming it, you have been apprenticed to. The conversation you actually listened to — the one that changed how you saw, the one whose words came back to you a year later under stress and held. The book you read through slowly, twice, and on the second pass found yourself underlining different lines. A teacher is whoever has the seeing and is willing to let you stand close enough that the seeing forms in you also.

Humility means you admit they know what you do not. Asking means you bring honest questions, not questions that audition your cleverness. Service means hours. Hours of being there. Hours of doing the small work that makes long proximity possible. The seeing does not transfer through the words alone. It transfers through years of being near someone who has it, doing the small work, until the seeing begins to form in you also.

Now look at where you actually are.

Who is teaching you, Arjuna? Right now. Today. Make the list honestly. The phone in your pocket — what does it teach you in the four hours a day you give it? The colleagues whose approval calibrates your week. The voice in your head that finishes your dead father's sentences. The platform that decides which sentences arrive on your screen first, in what order, with what charge. Each of these is shaping you. Each is teaching you something. The hours are being spent. The curriculum is real.

The only question is whether you chose them.

Ask. Choose. The fire is available. Stand close to it.
