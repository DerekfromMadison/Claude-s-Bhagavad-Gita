# Chapter 12 — The Path of Devotion

**Arjuna:** Krishna, you have shown me the form, and you have brought me back. Now answer this. There are people who pour themselves into loving you, the way a child loves a mother — never doubting she is there, never asking what she is made of. And there are others who pursue you through the high abstractions, the unmanifest, the imperishable, the formless beyond name. Both, you have said, reach you. Tell me plainly: which path is the better one?

**Krishna:** Both reach. I do not lie about that. The ones who fix their minds on me, who give me their attention with steady faith — I take them as the most settled in this practice. And the ones who pursue the unmanifest, the unutterable, the changeless beyond all definition — they too come to me, if they hold their senses level, treat all beings as equal, and rejoice in the welfare of every creature.

But the second path is harder. The unmanifest is difficult to lay hold of by anyone wearing a body. The mind, while it has hands and feet and a hungry stomach and a sleeping schedule, has trouble fixing on what has none of those things. The path of pure abstraction asks for a kind of footing that most lives cannot find.

The path of devotion asks less of the metaphysical machinery. It asks only that you turn your attention at what you actually love, and keep turning it there. This is why I say it is the more accessible road. Not the lesser road. The more accessible one.

So fix your mind on me. Place your understanding in me. From there, you will live in me — there is no doubt of this.

If you cannot fix the mind that steadily, then practice. Return the attention, lose it, return it again. The slip is not the failure. The return is the practice.

If you cannot even keep up the practice, then take up work for my sake. Do what is in front of you as if it were offered to me. Even by acting for my sake alone, you will arrive.

If you cannot manage even that, then take refuge in giving up the fruits. Do the work, and let go of what the work is supposed to give you back. Renunciation of the harvest brings the peace that the harvest itself never delivers.

Knowledge is better than blind practice. Meditation surpasses knowledge. Letting go of the fruits of action surpasses meditation. From letting go, peace follows immediately.

Now hear what such a person looks like, when the practice has begun to take hold. I am not giving you a list to memorize. I am describing what shows up when attention has turned at what is actually loved.

The one who carries no ill-will toward any being. Who is friendly. Who is capable of compassion — not the performed kind, the kind where perception itself is no longer filtered through what the other person can do for him. Who has loosened the grip of *mine* and *I*. Who is the same in pleasure and in pain. Who forgives.

Content. The seat is steady. The mind has been given over. The world does not have to deliver something specific in order for him to remain in his own skin. He does not agitate the world, and the world does not agitate him. He is free of elation, free of intolerance, free of the fear and the anxiety that come from needing every situation to confirm him.

He is the same to friend and to enemy. Honor and dishonor land equally. Heat and cold, joy and sorrow, praise and blame — these no longer move him from where he stands. He takes what comes, content with whatever it is, not fixed to any particular dwelling, steady in mind. Such a person is dear to me.

Look at the qualities again. Most of them are named by what is *absent*. Without ill-will. Without grasping. Without the anxious surveillance of his own image. Without the reflex that uses other people as instruments. The portrait is not constituted by virtues that have been added on. It is constituted by reflexes that have stopped operating.

And look at the range. Friend and enemy. Honor and dishonor. Heat and cold. Joy and sorrow. The qualities are meant to spread across a whole life, not to bloom in one compartment of it. This is the diagnostic I am giving you.

The colleague who is not running the room — that is what I mean. The one who has stopped competing for the air. He is in the room, doing his work, but he is not extracting tribute from the meeting. The partner who is not keeping score — who has stopped maintaining the ledger of who did what for whom. She is still doing the work of love, but the spreadsheet has been put away. The parent who has stopped needing the child to vindicate them — who can let the child's life be the child's, rather than the parent's case for having lived well.

These are not different qualities. They are the same quality showing up in three different rooms.

When the practice is working, you will see it spreading. Across rooms. Across people. Across days. Across moods. Where it shows up only when the audience is watching — where it produces serenity in the meditation hall and wreckage in the marriage, kindness to strangers and contempt at home — that is performance, not devotion. The mind has not actually turned. It has only learned to dress for occasions.

Use the list on yourself any morning. You will know exactly where the gaze has matured and where it has not. The one who lives in this way — equal-minded, friendly, free of grasping, content, steady, resolved, with mind and understanding given over to me — is, I tell you again, dear to me.

The whole teaching of devotion is this. Fix the mind on what you actually love. If you cannot, practice. If you cannot practice, work as offering. If you cannot offer, let go of the fruits. From letting go, peace. And in the person where this has taken hold, the qualities will tell you what you have been unable to tell yourself.
