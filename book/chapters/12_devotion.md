# Chapter 12 — The qualities of a life given to love

The qualities Krishna names at the end of the dialogue on devotion are not virtues you add one by one. They are what shows up in a person whose attention has been turned at what they actually love. Devotion — love-with-attention given to what you take to be ultimately worth your life — is structurally diagnosable. The list is the diagnostic. Non-grasping is what happens to a self that has stopped using the world to modulate its own pleasure and anxiety. Friendliness is what is left when you stop needing to dominate every interaction. Compassion is what perception becomes when it is no longer filtered through what the other person can do for you. One capacity in many guises. Train the gaze. The qualities take care of themselves.

You do not have to take anyone's word, including your own, for whether your devotion is real. Look at how you treated the person who insulted you yesterday. At how you held the project that failed last quarter. At how you sat beside the parent who no longer recognized you. If the qualities showed up, the practice has taken hold. If they did not, no amount of devotional intensity, ritual frequency, or doctrinal correctness substitutes.

The portrait runs from one end of the inner life to the other. One without ill-will, friendly to all beings, compassionate. Without "mine"-ness, without "I"-ness, equal in pleasure and pain, forgiving. Content, self-controlled, mind given over. One who does not agitate the world and is not agitated by it. Free from elation, intolerance, fear, anxiety. Pure, skilled, indifferent to ordinary cares. Same to friend and enemy, in honor and dishonor, heat and cold, joy and sorrow. Taking praise and blame equally, content with whatever comes, steady. *Such ones are extremely dear to me.*

Two structural features decide how to read the list. First, most of the qualities are named by what is *absent*. Without ill-will. Without "mine." Without "I." Without expectation. Uninvolved with petty cares. The profile is constituted by what has been removed, not by what has been added. The person is not someone in whom new virtues have been installed. The person is someone in whom certain habitual self-protective reflexes have stopped operating. The grasping that sees other people as instruments has loosened. The anxious surveillance of one's own image has subsided. What is left is a person more capable of perceiving what is actually in front of them.

Second, the qualities cover the full range of experience. Friend and enemy. Honor and dishonor. Heat and cold. Joy and sorrow. This kind of love either shows up everywhere or has not shown up at all. There is no domain it can selectively skip. The person gracious to colleagues and contemptuous of family does not yet have it. The person whose meditation produces serenity and whose marriage produces wreckage does not yet have it. Equal to enemy and friend is one capacity manifesting in two situations. The list refuses the modern accommodation by which spiritual practice is one compartment of life and ordinary cruelty another.

The standard misreading is to treat the list as a to-do. Become non-envious. Cultivate friendliness. Practice compassion. Devotion then becomes a self-improvement manual with twenty items. This misreads the grammar. The qualities are not commands. They are descriptions. The closing of nearly every verse is *that one is dear to me*. The list is a portrait, and the question is what produces the portrait.

Love is not an emotion but a quality of perception, and the moral act underneath it is the difficult realization that something other than oneself is real. The qualities are the inner shape of a person who has learned to do that. The qualities are not the work. The work is the training of the gaze. A person who really sees others as real does not hate them. A person whose attention is not bent on protecting an anxious self does not need to be honored or fear being dishonored. A person who can perceive a situation as what it is rather than as what it threatens about them is content with what comes. The list reads, on this account, exactly as Krishna delivers it — as a description, not a recipe.

Where the diagnostic does its hardest work is on the parent who no longer recognizes you. This is devotion stripped of recognition. A parent with advanced dementia cannot give back the relationship you used to have. The face does not light up at your arrival. The conversations do not pick up where they left off, because they cannot pick up at all. What is left is the offering. Feeding, bathing, sitting beside someone whose body is present and whose person is mostly gone, done as offering. The qualities, here, are a survival manual. Not agitating the patient. Not agitated by them. Equal in good days and bad. Content with whatever is given. Free of the self-protective reflex that demands recognition as the price of caring.

Without that discipline the caregiver collapses. The reflex of needing to be recognized in order to keep loving runs into the wall of someone who cannot recognize, and the love either stops, transmutes into resentment, or transmutes into the portrait. Many adult children of dementia patients discover, in the years of care, what the verses are pointing at. They do not call it devotion. They call it learning to show up. The structure is the same. Love that has stopped depending on the beloved's response — love that no longer requires the other to be capable of being loved well — is what was being described all along.

Use the list on yourself any morning before the day begins. You will know exactly where the practice has matured and exactly where it has not. You do not need to believe anything in particular. You need to look honestly at how you treated the last person who insulted you, the last project that failed, the last person who could not give back what you gave. Love is the moral faculty; the qualities are its consequences. The test is whether, by the time the day is over, the person who needed you most got what you actually had to give.
