# Chapter 7 — The World Is Real

The world is real. The chair holding you. The coffee going
cold. The face across the room you have lived beside for fifteen
years and stopped seeing. The grief that woke you at four. None
of it is a screen you are projecting on. None of it is rendering
at the speed of your attention. It is where being is happening,
and you are inside it.

Real world. Real divine. Real relation. The divine intimate with
the world rather than behind it. The world is the divine's body
— not in a metaphor that dissolves on inspection, but in the
sense that a body is real, an embodied self is real, the
embodied self is not collapsed into the body, and the body is
wholly the embodied self's. The small self is real but not
final. Real enough that grief does not reach to its bottom; not
so final that identification with it is the wisest stance.

---

Krishna stops describing how Arjuna should act and starts
disclosing what he is. *I am the taste in water. The radiance of
moon and sun. The fragrance of the earth.* He is naming himself
as the inner quality of every kind of thing. The water is real
water. And in it, something else is also being shown — not in
addition to the thing, but as its inwardness. *All this,* he
says, *is strung on me as jewels on a thread.*

Then there is the difficulty. An obscuring power, hard to cross.
You may have heard the word translated as *illusion*. Hold that
translation lightly. It is closer to the veiling that comes with
appearance — the way the desire-driven flicker of the perceiving
mind keeps delivering the world to you in a form that is not the
world. Not the declaration that the world is unreal. The veil
that makes what is real hard to see.

The cost of holding the world as illusion is not metaphysical.
It is what holding it costs you, in a life.

Consider the simulation hypothesis. Respectable theoretical
pedigree. Corrosive when held operationally. If the world is a
simulation, the people in it are non-player characters. The
grief is rendered. The face across the room is a textured
polygon. The work is a quest item. You can hold this position
intellectually and continue to behave well. The simulation
hypothesis is philosophically interesting and not refutable. But
operationally, the day's grief, the hand on a shoulder, the work
in front of you do not change shape under it — they ask the same
things of you whether or not the substrate is what you think it
is.

The same move appears in older language. Treat your daughter's
wedding as a projection on the screen of consciousness. Treat
your father's death as a play of the modes. Most readers, when
they try to hold the world as illusion, end up holding it as
half-real. Half-real is worse than either alternative. Half-real
is the world treated as not-quite-mattering, while the chest
still tightens when the call comes from the hospital. The
mismatch is the suffering.

A real world does something different. The face across the room
is real, and is one of the faces through which what is most
worth your attention is being given. The grief is real, and is
the form in which a real loss is being lived. There is no second
screen. There is no truer reality you are missing while you are
responding to this one.

---

A concrete situation, because the abstraction will not land
otherwise.

You have been having the same disagreement with someone you love
for years. A partner, a parent, a sibling, a grown child. Pick
the one. You keep winning. Your arguments are sharper, your
memory of past conversations more curated, your model of them
more confident. The other person is becoming smaller in the
model — flatter, more predictable, less real. You have stopped
hearing the actual person across from you. You are arguing with
a simulation of them that lives inside your own head. Every win
reinforces the model and makes the actual person less visible.

If you held the position that the world is illusion, this would
be no scandal. Of course it is a model. There is nothing else.
But the world is not illusion. The person across the table is
realer than your model of them. The practice of the relationship
is the practice of staying with the person instead of the model.

This is the operational difference. Under *the world is
illusion,* there is no person to return to — only models within
models. Under *the world is real but I am not finally identical
with the small self running this model,* there is a person to
return to, and a discipline by which the return is made.

Perception is mediated. What you experience as seeing is your
brain's best current hypothesis about what is in front of you,
kept honest by feedback. The model is always there. The reading
of your daughter is mediated by a model of her last fully
updated when she was twelve. The models are real. What is behind
them is also real. The practice is seeing through the models to
what is actually there.

The thought arises. The model is generated. You do not have to
be carried by it. The practice is repeatable, learnable, slow.
What changes, when you have kept it long enough, is not that the
models stop being generated. The brain continues to do what the
brain does. What changes is the small, durable distance between
the model and your action. You see the model. You also see what
is in front of you. You respond to what is in front of you. The
face becomes visible again.

---

You can take the world as a screen you are projecting on. It
corrodes most lives that try to hold it in earnest.

Or you can take the world as a face you are standing inside.
Real face. Real standing. Real inside. The face is not your
projection. It is what is given. The work is to receive the
giving without being carried away by your model of it.

The one who has known the divine, knows the divine at the moment
of death. What you have actually seen, in the sustained practice
of seeing, is what you carry through the moments when you have
nothing else.

The practice is to see. The world is real. There is something
here to be seen.
