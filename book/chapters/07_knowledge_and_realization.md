# Chapter 7 — The World Is Real

The world is real. The chair holding you. The coffee going
cold. The face across the room you have lived beside for fifteen
years and stopped seeing. The grief that woke you at four. None
of it is a screen you are projecting on. None of it is rendering
at the speed of your attention. It is where being is happening,
and you are inside it.

This is the chapter where the book commits to that. The previous
six have taught a posture — act fully without grafting your
selfhood onto the result, attention as the practice that trains
the posture, renunciation as interior. None of those
instructions makes sense if the world is finally an illusion.
You cannot give yourself to what is not there. You cannot love a
face on a screen the way you can love a face. The practice the
book is teaching needs a real ground.

In Chapter 7 of the Gita, Krishna stops describing how Arjuna
should act and starts disclosing what he is. *I am the taste in
water. The radiance of moon and sun. The fragrance of the
earth.* The list is a structure, not a catalog. He is naming
himself as the inner quality of every kind of thing. The water
is real water. And in it, something else is also being shown —
not in addition to the thing, but as its inwardness. *All this,*
he says, *is strung on me as jewels on a thread.*

Then he names the difficulty. There is an obscuring power, made
of the modes, hard to cross. You may have heard the word
translated as *illusion*. Hold that translation lightly. What
the chapter means is closer to the veiling that comes with
appearance — the way the desire-driven flicker of the perceiving
mind keeps delivering the world to you in a form that is not the
world. Not the declaration that the world is unreal. The veil
that makes what is real hard to see.

The position I am committing to is plain. Real world. Real
divine. Real relation. The divine intimate with the world rather
than behind it. The world is the divine's body — not in a
metaphor that dissolves on inspection, but in the sense that a
body is real, an embodied self is real, the embodied self is not
collapsed into the body, and the body is wholly the embodied
self's. The divine is the inner self of the cosmos. The cosmos
is the divine's outward form. They are distinct, and they are
not separate. The small self is real but not final. Real enough
that grief does not reach to its bottom; not so final that
identification with it is the wisest stance.

There is a long-running disagreement in the tradition with which
this book parts company. The strong version of non-dualism reads
the world's plurality as finally not real. The cost is borne by
the world. The water is finally not water. The face across the
room is finally not the face. That reading is serious. Its
contemplative depths are real. But the Gita tells Arjuna to
fight a real battle for real reasons. It asks him to give up the
fruits of his action, not the action. It teaches attention as
the discipline by which a real face becomes more visible, not
less. A finally-unreal world cannot bear that weight. The depths
the contemplative practice opens are depths within the world's
reality, not exits from it.

So: real world, real divine, real relation. This is the
metaphysical floor the rest of the book stands on. Everything
the practice trains — attention, action without grasping,
devotion through what is at hand — is being trained on something
that is genuinely there to be trained on, and what is genuinely
there is, in its inwardness, what the practice is for.

---

The reason this matters is not metaphysical hygiene. It is what
holding the world as illusion costs you, in a life.

Consider the simulation hypothesis. Respectable theoretical
pedigree. Corrosive when held operationally. If the world is a
simulation, the people in it are non-player characters. The
grief is rendered. The face across the room is a textured
polygon. The work is a quest item. You can hold this position
intellectually and continue to behave well. But the position is
heavy, and it bleeds. The longer you hold it, the more the
question *does this actually matter?* recurs. The only honest
answer the position permits is *probably not*.

The same move appears in older language. The world as illusion
in the strong sense — dream, veil over a truer reality. Treat
your daughter's wedding as a projection on the screen of
consciousness. Treat your father's death as a play of the
modes. Treat the project you are pouring yourself into as a
passing form. There is a contemplative tradition that says this
and means it, and I am not denying it has produced practitioners
of striking equanimity. I am saying: you are probably not one of
those practitioners. Most readers, when they try to hold the
world as illusion, end up holding it as half-real. Half-real is
worse than either alternative. Half-real is the world treated as
not-quite-mattering, while the chest still tightens when the
call comes from the hospital. The mismatch is the suffering.

The book's position does something different. The world is real,
and is the place where the divine is being shown. The face
across the room is real, and is one of the faces through which
what is most worth your attention is being given. The grief is
real, and is the form in which a real loss is being lived. There
is no second screen. There is no truer reality you are missing
while you are responding to this one.

The chapter pairs two registers. There is the structural
knowledge — what you know with your mind to be the case, that
the world is real and is the divine's body. Call that wisdom.
And there is the lived recognition of that knowledge in a
particular hour, in this room, with this person across the
table. The practice is the slow conversion of one into the
other. Knowing that the partner is real is one thing.
Recognizing, in the heat of the argument, that they are in fact
real and that what they are saying is in fact what they are
saying — that is the second.

---

A concrete situation, because the abstraction will not land
otherwise.

You have been having the same disagreement with someone you love
for years. A partner, a parent, a sibling, a grown child. Pick
the one. You keep winning. Every conversation, you win. Your
arguments are sharper, your memory of past conversations more
curated, your model of them more confident. The other person,
meanwhile, is becoming smaller in the model — flatter, more
predictable, less real. You have not noticed that you have
stopped hearing the actual person across from you. You are
arguing with a simulation of them that lives inside your own
head. Every win is a loss. Every win reinforces the model and
makes the actual person less visible.

This is what the obscuring power looks like at the
conversational scale. You have been carried by the modes-driven
flicker — the desire to be right, the aversion to being wrong,
the habit of self-defense — and have been delivered to yourself
a person who is not the person. You are responding to the model.
The model is a real production of your cognition. The person is
realer than the model. The practice of the relationship is the
practice of staying with the person instead of the model.

Perception is mediated. What you experience as seeing is your
brain's best current hypothesis about what is in front of you,
kept honest by feedback. The model is always there. The
conversation with your partner is mediated by a model of your
partner. The reading of your daughter is mediated by a model of
her last fully updated when she was twelve. The screens are
real. What is behind them is also real. The practice is seeing
through the screens to what is actually there.

---

Two things follow from the floor.

First: the practice of attention is not private mental hygiene.
It is the practice of meeting what is actually there. The face
you are practicing on is real. The work you are practicing on is
real. The grief, the joy, the small ordinary day, the stranger
you walk past, the bird at the window, the child who cannot
sleep — all real, all the medium through which the divine is
being shown to you, and your attention is the discipline by
which you receive the showing.

Second: the long-running disagreement is not winnable. Not the
way you have been trying to win it. You have been winning
against your model of the other person. The practice is to set
down the model and listen to what is being said. Not in some
abstract therapeutic sense — in the specific, repeatable,
uncomfortable sense of catching yourself rehearsing your
response while they are still talking, and stopping. And then
again the next time. And the time after that.

The thought arises. The model is generated. You do not have to
be carried by it. The practice is repeatable, learnable, and
slow. There is no shortcut. What changes, when you have kept it
long enough, is not that the models stop being generated. The
brain continues to do what the brain does. What changes is the
small, durable distance between the model and your action. You
see the model. You also see what is in front of you. You respond
to what is in front of you. The face becomes visible again. The
partner becomes the partner again. The world stops being a
screen onto which you are projecting and resumes being the face
you are standing inside.

---

You can take the world as a screen you are projecting on. It
corrodes most lives that try to hold it in earnest.

Or you can take the world as a face you are standing inside.
Real face. Real standing. Real inside. The face is not your
projection. It is what is given. The work is to receive the
giving without being carried away by your model of it.

The chapter's last verse says the one who has known the divine,
knows the divine at the moment of death. What you have actually
seen, in the sustained practice of seeing, is what you carry
through the moments when you have nothing else.

The practice is to see. The world is real. There is something
here to be seen.
