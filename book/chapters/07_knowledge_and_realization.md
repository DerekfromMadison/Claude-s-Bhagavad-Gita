## Chapter 7 — *Jñāna Vijñāna Yoga*: The Yoga of Knowledge and Realization

*Krishna stops describing how to act and starts disclosing what he is. The world becomes a face.*

---

### § 1. The dialogue

Krishna stops describing how to act and starts disclosing what he is. *I will declare to you,* he says, *both knowledge and realization in full, knowing which there shall remain nothing more here to be known.* The pairing — *jñāna* (knowledge) and *vijñāna* (realization) — is the chapter's signature. The first is the intellectual grasp. The second is the lived recognition. The chapter's argument is that one without the other is not enough.

He says that among thousands, scarcely one strives for perfection; among those who strive, scarcely one knows him in essence. In two breaths, both the difficulty of the teaching and the seriousness of the listener. Arjuna is not being flattered. He is being told the teaching now opening is reserved.

Then Krishna shifts ground. He distinguishes two natures of his being. A lower nature — eight-fold, the matter and mind out of which the cosmos is composed. A higher nature — the very life by which the cosmos is upheld. He is the source of both. Beyond him there is nothing. All that is, he says, is strung on him as jewels on a thread.

The image is precise. The thread is not the jewels. The jewels are not each other. But every jewel hangs by the thread; without the thread there are no jewels in this configuration at all.

He is the taste in water, the radiance of moon and sun, the syllable *Om* in all the Vedas, the sound in space, the manhood in men, the pure fragrance of the earth, the brilliance of fire, the life in all that lives, the austerity of the austere, the seed of all that is. The list is suggestive, not exhaustive. He is naming himself as the inner principle of every quality that makes a thing what it is.

Then he names the obstacle. *This divine māyā of mine, made of the gunas, is hard to cross.* Those who take refuge in him cross beyond it. Those who do not — deluded by the gunas, by the desire-driven flicker of nature — do not see what stands behind nature.

He distinguishes four kinds of human being who turn to the divine: the suffering, the seeker after knowledge, the seeker after worldly good, and the *jñāni* — the one who knows. All are dear. But the one who knows is dearest, in a sense no longer separate from the knowing.

He closes on those who do not know. They mistake him for one more god among gods. They imagine that he, being unmanifest, has come into manifestation — as if a face had emerged from facelessness. They do not see that he is veiled by his own *yogamāyā*, by the mystery of his own appearing. The one who knows him at the moment of death, knows him.

There is, for the first time, no question from Arjuna. Krishna teaches into a listening silence. The chapter does not resolve into action; it opens a vista. Chapters eight through eleven walk into the vista. Chapter 7 is the door.

### § 2. The core concepts

The chapter's structural work moves through four foregrounded concepts and a fifth that threads quietly through.

**Two natures: *aparā prakṛti* and *parā prakṛti*.** *Prakṛti*, across the Gita and the broader Sankhya inheritance, is the term for nature — the principle of becoming, the field of all that arises and changes. The Gita divides it in two. *Aparā* — lower, near — is the eightfold material nature: earth, water, fire, air, space, mind, intellect, and *ahaṅkāra* (literally "I-maker," the principle of self-reference). *Parā* — higher, ultimate — is the conscious life-principle by which the material world is sustained as a living cosmos. Whether *parā prakriti* is the divine itself, or the soul-stuff (*jīva*-set) through which the divine animates the world, or something subtler — that is where the traditions diverge.

**The world as theophany.** *Theophany* is Greek — *theos*, god; *phainein*, to show — and names the moment in which the divine appears through the world rather than from a sky beyond it. The Gita does not use this vocabulary, but the structure of 7.8–7.11 is clearly theophanic. Krishna names himself as the inner quality of each kind of thing. The water is real water. The fire is real fire. The strength of the strong is real strength. And in each, something else is also showing — not in addition to the thing, but as its inwardness.

**Māyā, and its obscuring power.** *Māyā* is one of the most contested words in classical Indian thought, and its reading in 7.14 is among the sharpest hinges in Gita commentary. The Gita uses it with a possessive and an adjective: *daivī hy eṣā guṇamayī mama māyā* — "this divine *māyā* of mine, made of the *guṇas*." Three things are notable. The *māyā* is the divine's; not an independent principle of unreality. It is made of the *gunas* — the three modes of nature (*sattva*, *rajas*, *tamas*) Chapter 14 will treat in detail. And it is *daivī* — divine — not demonic, not accidental. Its function: it makes the divine *hard to cross over to*. Whether this hardness is because the world is finally unreal (Shankara), real but veiling its source (Ramanuja), incomparable in real distinction (Madhva), or in evolutionary movement toward transparent disclosure (Aurobindo) — that is the divergence the next section lays out.

**The four kinds of seeker, and the *jñāni*.** *Jñāna* is knowledge; *jñāni*, the one who has it. *Vijñāna* is realization; the prefix *vi-* intensifies, makes particular, brings into fullness. Some commentators read *vijñāna* as discriminative knowledge — the kind that distinguishes the real, the apparent, and what falls between; others as the lived saturation of abstract knowledge with felt recognition. Both readings are present. At 7.16–7.18, four kinds of person turn toward the divine: the *ārtaḥ* (suffering), the *jijñāsuḥ* (the one who wants to know), the *arthārthī* (the one who seeks worldly good), and the *jñānī* (the one who has come into knowledge). All are virtuous. All are received. But the *jñānī* is, Krishna says, his very self — *jñāny ātmaiva me matam*. Whatever that "very self" means in the metaphysics — and the schools fight about it — the practical force is that the highest motivation toward the divine is not need, not curiosity, not transactional benefit, but simple recognition. The one who has begun to *see* what the divine is, is closest. The other three are not denigrated; they sit in a developmental sequence that begins with whatever motive the human happens to have and matures into the bare seeing of what is.

A fifth concept threads through and earns naming because it becomes structural later. *Yogamāyā*, used at 7.25, is related to but distinct from the *māyā* of 7.14. Where *māyā* names the obscuring power that holds the perceiver in the gunas-driven flicker, *yogamāyā* names the divine's own active veiling — the way the divine, in disclosing itself, withholds itself in measure. 7.25 says the divine is veiled by *yogamāyā* and not seen by the unintelligent — by those who imagine that what is unmanifest has merely come into manifestation, as if the divine were a face emerging from a prior facelessness. The two veils are reciprocal: one covers the seer's eyes; the other covers the seen's face. To see the divine, both must thin — the seer's by discipline, the seen's by grace, or by recognition, or by whatever mystery the traditions name differently and all point at.

These five concepts — two natures, world as theophany, *māyā*, the *jñānī*, and *yogamāyā* — shape what follows. A thousand years of schools have read them very differently, and as a connected family rather than as five separate puzzles. Hearing each school in its own voice is the next section's work.

### § 3. The traditions speak

Five interpretive traditions hold most of the gravitational weight on Chapter 7, with two more framing the wider conversation.

#### § 3.1. Advaita Vedanta — Adi Shankaracharya

Shankara, reading through Advaita non-dualism, treats Chapter 7 as an articulation of *Brahman* in two registers: as the supreme reality from which all comes and to which all returns, and as the ineffable non-dual ground that the two natures of 7.4–7.5 ultimately point toward. On 7.14, he reads *māyā* as the obscuring power that makes the one appear as many. The *gunas* are the colors *māyā* mixes; the apparent world is the painting that results. Liberation is not the destruction of the painting but the realization that the painting is paint, and the canvas — *Brahman* — was never anywhere else. He tends to read passages on devotion and embodied action as preparatory to *jñāna*: *jñāna* is the highest path; *karma yoga* and *bhakti yoga* are the soil in which it grows.

This reading carries the experiential claim that the small, separate self is not the deepest truth about who anyone is — beneath it there is the awareness in which it arises, and that awareness is not separate from the awareness in which all selves arise. The cost is to the realism of the world: Shankara has to do careful work to read the Gita's affirmations of the divine's distinct, supreme, personal nature as not finally meant in their straightforward sense. A reader who comes to the Gita without an Advaitic prior reads 7.4–7.7 more directly than Shankara needs to.

#### § 3.2. Vishishtadvaita — Ramanujacharya

Ramanuja, reading through Vishishtadvaita (qualified non-dualism), takes 7.4–7.5 directly. The lower *prakṛti* is the field of insentient matter, *acit*. The higher *prakṛti* is the *jīva* — the conscious individual soul, real, distinct, eternal. Both stand to the Lord as a body stands to its embodied self: dependent, enlivened, and not collapsed into. On 7.7's "nothing higher than me," the Lord is the inner controller of all selves and matter, the substantial source of all that is — and this beyondness is one of supremacy and inwardness, not of identity-collapse.

On 7.14, *māyā* is not the metaphysical screen of unreality but the wondrous creative power of the Lord — the divine's *āścarya-śakti*, his "power of wonder." The world the *māyā* presents is fully real; what is hard is recognizing the Lord *through* the world. *Māyā* is the difficulty of seeing the embodying self when one is fixated on the body.

This reading holds the world's reality, the soul's reality, the Lord's distinct supremacy, and the personal, devotional register of the chapter. The contemplative claim that the small self disappears in realization is what gets less air: in Ramanuja, the self does not disappear; it is purified into right relation. For a reader who has tasted the Advaitic experience of self-disappearance, this can feel like a partial reading; for one for whom self-disappearance feels like a doctrinal abstraction, it can feel like a corrective.

#### § 3.3. Dvaita — Madhvacharya

Madhva, reading through Dvaita realism, holds the two natures ontologically distinct from each other and from the Lord. The eight elements of the lower *prakṛti* are real, insentient, and never collapse into mind or spirit. The higher *prakṛti* is the *jīva*-set, real, sentient, and never collapses into matter or into the Lord. On 7.7, the Lord is supreme over all this without becoming any of it.

On 7.14, *māyā* is the Lord's wondrous power, which can delude. There is no thesis of world-illusion. The appearance of unreality is itself a real feature of a fully real world, traceable to the soul's own karmic situation and to the Lord's discriminating disposition of his power.

This reading carries the reality of the world and of the self, the supremacy of the Lord without any collapse, and the moral seriousness of a real cosmos in which choices have real consequences. What it gives less of is the experiential dimension contemplative traditions have so reliably reported. Madhva does not offer self-dissolution as a horizon; he offers a different horizon — the eternally distinct soul drawing closer in love and service to a Lord it can never become.

#### § 3.4. Integral Yoga — Sri Aurobindo

Aurobindo, reading through the Integral Yoga, emphasizes the dynamic, world-affirming character of the Gita's metaphysics. The two natures of 7.4–7.5 are not a static distinction; they are the structure of a becoming. The lower *prakṛti* is the manifest field of evolutionary unfolding; the higher *prakṛti* is the conscious soul-stuff that animates the unfolding. The divine is neither identified with the world (against Shankara's strongest reading) nor severed from it (against the dualist forms of Vaishnava commentary); the divine is source, support, and goal of an unfolding through which spirit comes increasingly into manifestation.

On *māyā*, Aurobindo treats it as real but not final. The world's veiling of the divine is a phase of a process; yoga is participation in spirit's self-disclosure through that world. The opposition "real versus illusion" is the wrong frame; the right frame is *involved versus emergent*, *obscured versus disclosed*, *becoming versus realized*.

This reading puts forward the world's directional becoming, the compatibility of metaphysical seriousness with engagement in history, and a bridge between the Indian tradition and modern thought. What it gives less of is the more austere contemplative reading that takes the world's value to lie in being seen through, not in being transformed.

#### § 3.5. Gaudiya Vaishnava — A.C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada

Prabhupada, reading through the Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition, gives one of the more distinctive English renderings of 7.14:

> "This divine energy of Mine, consisting of the three modes of material nature, is difficult to overcome. But those who have surrendered unto Me can easily cross beyond it." (Prabhupada, *Bhagavad-gītā As It Is*, 7.14, BBT 1972 ed.)

"Those who have surrendered unto Me can easily cross beyond it" is unmistakably Prabhupada. The "easily" is doing real work; many other translations render the crossing as *possible* through refuge in the Lord, not *easy*. Prabhupada makes surrender — *prapatti* in the broader Vaishnava vocabulary — the operational solvent of *māyā*.

In his purport on 7.5, the higher nature is the *jīva*, the eternal living entity, eternally part-and-parcel of Krishna and distinct from the inert lower nature; the *jīva* is conscious, the lower nature is not, and confusing the two is the cognitive mistake at the root of bondage.

The framing is Gaudiya Vaishnava throughout: every verse read through the prior conviction that Krishna is the Supreme Personality of Godhead, with surrender through the nine processes of *bhakti yoga* as the proper response. What this reading carries is surrender as a practical operation, the personal and lovable Lord, and the primacy of the devotional response. What it does not give is any reading in which the Gita is *not* finally a manual of devotion to Krishna under the Gaudiya frame.

#### § 3.6. Comparative-philosophical — Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan

Radhakrishnan, reading through comparative philosophy, gives one of the most quoted English renderings of 7.7:

> "There is nothing whatever that is higher than I, O Winner of wealth (Arjuna). All that is here is strung on me as rows of gems on a string." (Radhakrishnan, *The Bhagavadgītā*, 7.7, 1948 ed.; edition unconfirmed; validation pending)

The simile is ancient (Sanskrit: *sūtre maṇi-gaṇāḥ iva*), and Radhakrishnan's English wears it well. The thread is in every gem; it is not the gem.

In his commentary on 7.14, Radhakrishnan compares *māyā* to Spinoza's modes, Plato's distinction between becoming and being, and Bradley's appearance and reality. He takes *māyā* as the principle of finite manifestation rather than as ontological unreality. *Māyā* names the difficulty of seeing through finite manifestation to its infinite source. The world is not a lie; it is a finite expression. The reader misled by the gunas mistakes the finite expression for the infinite source.

The mediating philosopher's voice carries the Gita into a global philosophical conversation and cools the prose for a Western audience without requiring conversion. The cost is the heat of the devotional address: a reader who comes to Radhakrishnan looking for the Lord's intimate voice may find the philosopher's voice present where the lover's voice is in the Sanskrit.

#### § 3.7. Bhakti-literary — Graham Schweig

Schweig, reading through the bhakti-lyric register, brings the second-person address of the Sanskrit into the foreground. The Gita is not a treatise; it is a song addressed by one to another. The doctrine of two natures, the disclosure of "nothing beyond me," the warning about *māyā* — all are the words of a beloved who is also the source of all love, telling the listener what he is, and what stands between the listener and seeing it.

The reading carries the personal and relational register, the lyric quality of the Sanskrit, and the dialogue as event. The philosophical-systematic readings (Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva) sit at one remove. Schweig does not deny them; he places the address first.

---

What stands out across these seven readings is not that they disagree; it is *where* they disagree.

All affirm that the chapter teaches two natures of the divine, that the world is in some sense the divine's, that *māyā* is a power belonging to the divine and hard to cross, and that recognition of the divine is the chapter's character ideal. The disagreement is how literally to take "the world is the divine's." Shankara: in the deepest sense the world *is* the divine and apart from it is nothing. Ramanuja: the world is real and is the divine's body. Madhva: the world is real and is owned by the divine, never collapsed into the divine. Aurobindo: the world is the divine's becoming. Prabhupada: the world is the divine's energy, surrendered through. Radhakrishnan: the world is finite manifestation of an infinite source. Schweig: the world is the medium of the beloved's address.

The difference matters for what the chapter is asking the reader to *do*.

### § 4. Modern counterpoints

#### § 4.1. Spinoza — substance and the world's inwardness in the divine

Spinoza's *Ethics* (1677) offers the closest sustained Western analogue to the Gita's "two natures, one source." Rationalist metaphysics in geometrical form, anti-anthropomorphic, suspicious of personal-God language, strict necessitarian.

Three propositions from *Ethics* Part I do most of the relevant work:

> "By God I understand a being absolutely infinite, that is, a substance consisting of an infinity of attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite essence." (Spinoza, *Ethics* I D6, trans. Curley 1985)

> "Except God, no substance can be or be conceived." (Spinoza, *Ethics* I P14, trans. Curley 1985)

> "Whatever is, is in God, and nothing can be or be conceived without God." (Spinoza, *Ethics* I P15, trans. Curley 1985)

The structural resonance with Gita 7.7 is exact. *Mattaḥ parataraṁ nānyat* — there is nothing higher than me, beyond me; *idaṁ sarvaṁ sūtre maṇi-gaṇāḥ iva* — all this is strung on me as rows of jewels on a thread. Whatever is, is in God. Nothing can be conceived without God.

But the resonance is not identity. Spinoza's monism is *substance* monism: one substance, the world its modes, expressed under the attributes of thought and extension. Krishna's "nothing beyond me" is more loaded. He is named — Krishna; he is addressed — Arjuna; the relation is personal in a way Spinoza's geometric prose never permits. The Gita's metaphysical claim is closer to Spinoza than to most of theistic Christian theology of Spinoza's day; the Gita's *register* is closer to Christian theology than to Spinoza.

What Spinoza brings is the discipline of taking "nothing beyond" seriously without softening it. Once "nothing higher" is affirmed, one has cut off the possibility that the world is something *other* than the divine, or *separate*, or *abandoned*. That is the surface meaning of 7.7, and Spinoza pushes the reader to feel its full weight. What the Gita adds, and Spinoza's framework lacks, is the second-person address: the divine that is *spoken*, that *speaks*. The two illuminate each other; neither is reducible to the other.

#### § 4.2. Jean-Luc Marion — the saturated phenomenon and the divine that exceeds the I

Marion, working in the phenomenological tradition (Husserl through Heidegger and Levinas) with a Roman Catholic theological inflection, developed the concept of the *saturated phenomenon* across *Being Given* (1997) and *In Excess* (2001). A saturated phenomenon is one in which intuition exceeds the concepts available to receive it. Most phenomena are *given* in a way that fits within the concepts the receiving consciousness brings to bear; the phenomenon is constituted, intelligibly, by the receiving I. Some phenomena give *more* than the I can constitute. The historical event gives more than any narrative can encompass. The painting gives more than any description can exhaust. The face of another person gives more than any account of the other can reach. The call — the sense of being addressed — gives more than any account of who is calling can settle. In each case, intuition saturates the available concepts; the I receives without being able to master.

This is structurally what 7.7 names. *Mattaḥ parataraṁ nānyat*: nothing beyond me. The "me" of that verse is given to Arjuna more fully than Arjuna can conceptually receive. The listener cannot circumscribe the speaker. The listener can only be addressed, and respond, and remain in the asymmetry.

Marion's framework gives the modern reader a vocabulary for the second half of Chapter 7 (and for what will be more vivid still in Chapters 9, 10, and 11). The divine, in the Gita's disclosure, is not one more entity in the world's catalog. It is the structure of an excess no catalog could exhaust. *Yogamāyā* — the veil of mystery in which the divine appears unmanifest — is the phenomenological condition of this excess: the I sees what it can; it does not see all that is given.

The reader does not need to commit to Marion's Catholic-theological frame. They need only register that the Gita's "nothing beyond me" is a saturated address, not a metaphysical inventory. That reading is true to the chapter's voice and independently defensible in modern philosophical vocabulary.

#### § 4.3. Iris Murdoch — attention as the moral act of seeing the real

Murdoch's *The Sovereignty of Good* (1970) argues that attention is the central moral act. Post-Wittgensteinian moral philosophy, marked by Plato and Weil, committed to a moral realism grounded in the concrete particular and pushed against the existentialist account of value as projection.

Her most quoted sentence, from "The Idea of Perfection":

> "Love is the perception of individuals. Love is the extremely difficult realisation that something other than oneself is real." (Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, "The Idea of Perfection," p. 215, Routledge 2001 ed.)

The sentence belongs in the counterpoint to Chapter 7 because of what it says about *māyā*. The Gita teaches that the gunas-driven flicker delivers the world to the perceiver in a form that is not the world. The perceiver, in *māyā*, is seeing the projection of their own desire and aversion onto what is given. Murdoch names the obverse practice: the slow, "extremely difficult" act of letting the other be real, not as the perceiver wants them, not as the perceiver fears them, but as they are. For Murdoch, this is the central moral fact.

The convergence is sharp. The way through *māyā*, in 7.14, is to take refuge in the divine. Murdoch's vocabulary is non-theistic, but the structure is the same: the way through the screen of one's projections is to allow what is other to be real. The Gita supplies the metaphysical horizon; Murdoch supplies the moral practice.

What Murdoch resists, and what the chapter must hold against her, is a fully secular reading. Murdoch uses "Good" as an analogue of the divine without metaphysical commitment; the Gita is committing. A reader who finds the Gita's commitment hard to take literally can read with Murdoch's "Good" in mind without misreading the chapter; a reader comfortable with the metaphysical claim can use Murdoch's account of attention without compromising it.

### § 5. Modern science

Two scientific strands speak directly to Chapter 7. Neither resolves the chapter's metaphysical claims; both bear on what those claims, in cognitive and cosmological terms, can mean now.

#### § 5.1. Predictive processing — perception as model

The brain does not passively receive a clean signal. It generates top-down predictions about what should be sensed and uses incoming sensory data largely to update or correct them. What we experience is the brain's best current hypothesis, kept honest (most of the time) by sensory feedback. When predictions are right, perception feels seamless. When wrong, there are illusions, hallucinations, surprises. This is the predictive-processing framework, articulated by Karl Friston's free-energy principle and brought into accessible English by Anil Seth (Friston, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* 2010; Seth, *Being You*, 2021).

Seth's signature formulation:

> "Perception, in this view, is a controlled hallucination, in which the brain's hallucinations are kept in check by sensory information." (Seth, *Being You*, Faber & Faber 2021, edition unconfirmed; validation pending)

The bound. This is a working framework, not a settled science. It has competitors and open empirical questions about scope and falsifiability. It is not a metaphysical claim about the world beyond perception; it is a claim about the structure of perceptual processing.

What it offers Chapter 7. *Māyā* is the obscuring power that makes the divine hard to see *through* the world. Predictive processing offers a precise picture of how perception is shaped by what one expects, fears, wants. The screen between the perceiver and the perceived is partly the perceiver's own model.

This is not the whole of what the Gita names. The chapter's *māyā* concerns not only how one sees a tree but how one sees one's own life, one's death, the source of one's existence. But at the layer the science speaks to, the veil is real. The Gita's claim that one is seeing in part one's own model is, at the perceptual layer, just true.

What the framework does *not* deliver is a verdict on whether what lies behind the model is the divine, or just more matter, or nothing in particular. The Gita answers that. Predictive processing leaves the answer open and tells the reader there is, indeed, a screen on which the question can be asked.

#### § 5.2. Cosmology — the contingency of existence

On the question 7.7 implicitly answers — *why is there something at all?* — the honest scientific reply is that the question may have no further answer than the laws and initial conditions of the universe themselves. There is no scientific demand for a theological remainder. The universe is, in a sense, a brute fact about which one can ask many *how* questions and very few coherent *why* questions. This is Sean Carroll's "poetic naturalism" — the world consists only of natural processes describable in physical terms, while higher-level vocabularies (about meaning, value, persons) remain valid in their own register (Carroll, *The Big Picture*, Dutton 2016; specific verbatim sentences flagged for validation pending).

The bound. Carroll's position is one philosophical reading of contemporary physics, not the consensus. Other physicists and philosophers — some not given to theism — argue the question "why anything at all" is not exhausted by physics. The fine-tuning question, the puzzle of why mathematics describes the world as well as it does, the open question of what (if anything) "caused" the initial conditions: these remain live discussions.

What it offers Chapter 7. The Gita's "nothing higher than me" is not a scientific claim. Carroll's poetic naturalism is the honest scientific counter-position: on the basis of physics alone, one does not need to say "nothing higher." One can stop at the physical, and the physical does not in itself cry out for a beyond.

The chapter neither pretends that science requires the Gita's metaphysics nor that science forbids it. The Gita's claim and Carroll's claim live at different layers of the same human conversation. They contradict each other only if one demands they speak at the same layer. They do not.

### § 6. Synthesis — the path forward

Chapter 7 changes the subject. It is no longer about what Arjuna should do or how he should sit; it is about what is. The reading I am working with takes the surface text seriously, takes the divergent traditional readings seriously, and refuses to flatten the difference between them.

The world is real, and it is the divine's. The lower nature of 7.4 — the eight elements — is not an illusion. Earth is earth, water is water, mind is mind. The higher nature of 7.5 — the conscious life-principle — is not the divine collapsed into the world; it is the soul-stuff through which the divine animates the world. Ramanuja's ancient image of body and embodied self captures the relation: the body is real, the embodied self is real, the embodied self is not collapsed into the body, and yet the body is wholly the embodied self's. On the metaphysical question of two natures, the chapter is closer to qualified non-dualism than to strict non-dualism. The textual ground is at 7.5: *jagad dhāryate* — "the universe is sustained" by the higher *prakriti* — and *jīva-bhūtām* — "having become the *jīva*." "Having become the *jīva*" reads more naturally as ownership-of-soul-stuff than as identity-with-Brahman. Shankara has to do real interpretive work to read it Advaitically; Ramanuja and Madhva are reading the surface; Aurobindo is reading the surface with a directional inflection.

*Māyā*, then, is the obscuring power of the divine, not the unreality of the world. The grammar of 7.14 — *daivī hy eṣā guṇamayī mama māyā* — gives the structure away. The *māyā* is *daivī*, divine; *guṇamayī*, made of the modes; *mama*, mine. It is owned, located, structured. Not an autonomous principle of unreality. The *power* by which the divine appears as the world while remaining beyond it — what one has to cross over not because the world is false but because the gunas-driven mind keeps mistaking the appearing for the source. This coheres with the rest of the Gita. The Gita is about engaged action in a real world; a finally-unreal world cannot bear the weight the Gita places on action, on duty, on devotion, on the moral seriousness of the choices Arjuna is being asked to make. The chapters that follow keep treating the world as something the divine is engaged with, in, and through. They do not read as walking back a doctrine of world-illusion affirmed here.

"Nothing higher than me" is a claim about source, not about identity. Jewels on a thread. The thread is in every jewel. The thread is not the jewel. Without the thread, the jewels are scattered; with it, they are arrayed. This is Ramanuja's reading — the inner controller, the *antaryāmin*, of all selves and matter — and not Shankara's. Shankara reads the imagery as suggestive of identity; Ramanuja reads it as relation. The relational reading is closer to the surface and, in my judgment, more useful to a reader who has to keep living in the world the chapter is naming.

Realization is recognizing the divine through the world. The chapter's character ideal, the *jñānī* of 7.16–7.18, is not the one who has dissolved out of the world; it is the one who has come to see what the world is showing. *Vijñāna* — realization — is precisely this seeing; *jñāna* without *vijñāna* is intellectual grasp without lived recognition; *vijñāna* without *jñāna* is felt recognition without articulate clarity. The chapter wants both.

The contemplative claim — that the small self is not the deepest truth about who one is — has to be absorbed into this synthesis, not declined. Shankara and his successors are reporting from experience: beneath the surface self there is an awareness in which the surface self arises, and that awareness is not exhausted by the contents passing through it. I do not read this report as evidence for Advaitic monism in the strong metaphysical sense, but as evidence that human experience contains depths the ordinary self-model does not encompass. Predictive processing is, in its way, the cognitive-science layer's confirmation: the self the brain models is one model among several, not the final word about what perception is. The Gita's metaphysics is enabling rather than identifying. The world is real, the divine is real, the relation is intimate, and within that relation there are depths of selfhood the ordinary surface does not reach. Recognizing those depths is not the dissolution of the world; it is the world becoming more transparent without ceasing to be the world.

The strong Advaitic claim that the world is *finally* unreal does not survive this reading — on textual fidelity, on internal coherence with the rest of the Gita, on the moral weight the Gita places on action, and on what predictive processing actually shows (the mediation of perception, not the unreality of the perceived). Shankara as a teacher stays. The contemplative practice his metaphysics underwrites — patient seeing through the surface self, recognition of awareness as the medium in which experience arises — is one of the great inheritances of human contemplation, and any responsible reading of the Gita has to be informed by it. Madhva's realism preserves the dignity of the world but loses the chapter's mystical pull; the Gita is calling Arjuna into a relation in which the divine is *within* and not only *above*, and Madhva sees the *within* less. Prabhupada's claim that the Gita is finally a manual of devotion to Krishna in the Gaudiya frame I do not take, but his operational point on surrender — that it is the practical solvent of the gunas-driven mind's grip — is right and is not specifically sectarian. Aurobindo's evolutionary frame I read metaphorically rather than ontologically: the world is the field within which spirit is increasingly disclosed, which is different from saying spirit is, in some final sense, in process. But the major contribution holds — the world is in motion, and the practice is participation in the motion's direction.

A real world held in real relation to a real divine; a divine that is the source of the world without being the world; a *māyā* that is the cognitive-existential screen between the small self and what stands behind it; a *jñānī* whose practice is to see through the screen without leaving the world the screen filters. That is the floor underneath the practice the next section opens. A reader can argue against it precisely — that 7.5 should be read as identity rather than ownership, that "jewels on a thread" is closer to identity than to relation, that *māyā* should be read as the autonomous principle of unreality after all. The citations are above; the seven readings are above in their own voices.

### § 7. The chapter for a modern life

Chapter 7's metaphysics is steep. The application is not. None of what follows requires religious commitment; all of it is sharper if the Gita's metaphysics is held open as a possibility rather than as a foreign claim.

**Reading the world as theophany without leaving science.** The chapter teaches that the world is the divine's body. The reader does not have to accept the metaphysical claim to take seriously what the experiential discipline trains. Stand in front of a tree. Notice that the tree is there. Notice that you are taking it for granted. Notice the small, surprising astonishment if you let yourself feel that there is a tree at all — that the world is the kind of place where there is a tree at all. Cosmology, for what it is worth, makes the astonishment more precise rather than less. There is no scientific obligation to fill the astonishment with the divine. There is no scientific obligation to refuse to. The astonishment is the practice. The Gita names it *vijñāna* — realization — not the conclusion of an argument but the felt registration of a fact.

I do not see most days. I scroll past most things. I do this knowing better. The chapter calls a practice in the opposite direction — not strenuously, not constantly, but enough times in a day that the world stays available as something I am responding to rather than pushing through. A meal eaten while present to its taste. A conversation in which the other person's face is actually being looked at. An hour at work being done because the work is what is in front of me, not because I need it to prove me out. Small. The practical content of "the world is the divine's body" stripped of religious branding.

**The discipline of seeing through one's own model.** Predictive processing tells me that what I am calling "perception" is in part construction. The Gita tells me *māyā* is real and hard to cross. The two layers map. The discipline is to know I am partly making up what I am seeing — and to keep checking.

A specific case. I am in a long-running disagreement with someone close to me. We have had this conversation so many times that I no longer hear them; I hear my model of them. I rehearse my response to my model before they speak. When they say something that does not match the model, the model wins — I bend their words back toward what I expected. This is *māyā* at the conversational scale. The way through is procedural, not mystical: notice the rehearsal, set it down, listen to what is actually said, respond to that.

Steven Hayes's Acceptance and Commitment Therapy — well-attested in the clinical literature, with the technique called "cognitive defusion" — is the closest contemporary therapeutic analogue. It trains a small, repeatable distance from one's own thoughts: not suppression, not indulgence, but the recognition that they are productions, not all of reality. This is operational *māyā*-crossing, in a vocabulary the Gita would not have used and would not, I think, have refused.

Epictetus, twenty centuries earlier, named the same practice as the withholding and granting of assent: the impression arises, and one need not be carried by it; one may, with the trained part of oneself, give or withhold agreement. The Stoic technique is procedurally identical to ACT defusion and structurally cognate to the Gita's *māyā*-discipline. Different vocabularies, the same operation.

**Surrender as a practical operation.** "Take refuge in me" is the Gita's own language at 7.14. It can sound religious. At its operational core it is not. The operation: stop demanding that reality match my model. Stop trying to win the argument with what is.

A specific case. The work I do is not yielding the recognition I had counted on. I had arranged my self-image around a particular outcome. The outcome is not coming. The internal pressure to argue with reality, to insist on the outcome, to grow bitter, is high. The operation: drop the demand. Not drop the work — drop the demand. Continue doing the work because the work is what is in front of me; release the outcome because the outcome was always partly out of my hands and the demand is what is making the situation a wound.

This is not passivity. Krishna's whole teaching in Chapters 2 through 6 has been that one acts, and acts vigorously, while releasing attachment to results. Chapter 7 deepens this: one releases by recognizing that the divine — or, in a less religious vocabulary, the deeper field of what-is — is not under one's control and was not promising to be. *Sharanam* — refuge — is a stance toward what is, not a metaphysical assertion about who is in charge.

The Twelve-Step traditions, in the clinical-recovery literature, name a version of this stance — without the Gita's metaphysics — and report striking benefits when the stance is maintained. A reader who finds the religious language of "refuge" hard to take can substitute: *I am not in charge of the outcome. I am responsible for the action. The action is mine to do; the outcome is not mine to extort.* That sentence, recited honestly enough times, is the operational core of *sharanam*.

**The *jñānī* as character ideal.** Chapter 7 ends with the four kinds of seeker. Krishna calls all of them dear. He calls the *jñānī* dearest. The *jñānī* is not the one who has memorized the verses. The *jñānī* is the one who has seen what the chapter is naming and now lives differently because of it.

What does that life look like, in 2026, in a city, with a job, with bills, with the people who depend on the seer? Like this: someone no longer at war with reality. Someone who has stopped insisting the world be other than it is in order to do what is in front of them to do. Someone for whom the world has become more transparent, not less real — more obviously the kind of place where being itself is happening, not less obviously the kind of place where work and love and grief and ordinary supper are happening. Someone for whom the small daily practices of attention have become so habitual the practices are no longer visible as practices.

Iris Murdoch's sentence belongs at the close: love is the extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is real. The chapter's whole argument folds into that sentence. The world is real. The divine is real. The other person across the table is real. *Māyā* is the great difficulty in seeing this and continuing to see it. The *jñānī* is the one who has, at least in part, seen — and is being changed by what they have seen.

The chapter does not end with an exhortation. It ends with a description: the one who has known me, knows me at the moment of death. The reader can take this metaphysically, as the Gita means it. The reader can also take it as a structural claim about a life: what one has actually seen, in the sustained discipline of seeing, is what one carries through the moments when one has nothing else.
