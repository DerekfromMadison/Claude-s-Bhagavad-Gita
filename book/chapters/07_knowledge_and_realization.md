# Chapter 7 — The World Is Real

The world is real. The chair you are sitting in. The coffee cooling
in front of you. The face of the person across the room you have lived
beside for fifteen years and stopped seeing. The grief that woke you
at four. The light coming through the window now. None of it is a
screen you are projecting on. None of it is a simulation rendering at
the speed of your attention. It is the place where being is happening,
and you are inside it.

This is the chapter where the book commits to that. The previous six
chapters have taught a posture — act fully without grafting your
selfhood onto the result, attention as the practice that trains the
posture, renunciation as interior. None of those instructions makes
sense if the world is finally an illusion. You cannot give yourself to
something that is not there. You cannot love a face on a screen the
way you can love a face. The practice the book has been teaching
needs a real ground, and Chapter 7 of the Gita is where the ground
becomes explicit.

The Gita's seventh chapter is brief and dense. Krishna stops describing
how Arjuna should act and starts disclosing what he is. *I am the
taste in water,* he says, *the radiance of moon and sun, the syllable
Om in all the Vedas, the sound in space, the manhood in men, the pure
fragrance of the earth.* The list is not a catalogue of his
possessions. It is a structure. He is naming himself as the inner
quality of every kind of thing. The water is real water. The fire is
real fire. And in each, he is saying, something else is also being
shown — not in addition to the thing, but as its inwardness. *All
this,* he says, *is strung on me as jewels on a thread.* The thread is
in every jewel. The thread is not the jewel. Without the thread, the
jewels are scattered; with it, they are arrayed.

Then he names the difficulty. *This divine māyā of mine, made of the
gunas, is hard to cross.* The world is the divine's, and the world is
also where the divine is hardest to see — because the modes of nature,
the desire-driven flicker of the perceiving mind, keep delivering the
world to the perceiver in a form that is not the world.

That is the chapter. Two natures of the divine. The world strung on
the divine like jewels on a thread. *Māyā* as the obscuring power that
makes the divine hard to see *through* the world. And four kinds of
seeker — the suffering, the curious, the ambitious, and the one who
has come to see — with the seer named as the dearest.

The position I am committing to is the qualified non-dualism that
reads this chapter most directly: real world, real divine, real
relation, the divine intimate with the world rather than behind it.
Ramanuja's reading. *Vishishtadvaita.* The world is the divine's body
— not in a metaphor that dissolves on inspection, but in the literal
sense that a body is real, an embodied self is real, the embodied self
is not collapsed into the body, and the body is wholly the embodied
self's. The divine is the inner self of the cosmos. The cosmos is the
divine's outward form. They are distinct, and they are not separate.

Shankara reads the chapter differently. He is the great non-dualist of
the Indian tradition, and the experiential force of his reading is
real. Beneath the surface self, he says, there is the awareness in
which the surface self arises, and that awareness is not finally
distinct from the awareness in which all selves arise. The world's
plurality is real to the perceiver who is still in the gunas-driven
flicker, and not finally real to the awareness that has come through.
The cost of his reading is borne by the world. Shankara has to read
the Gita's affirmations of the divine's distinct, supreme, personal
nature as not finally meant in their straightforward sense. The water
is finally not water. The face across the room is finally not the face.

I read against him here. I want to do it carefully, because his
reading is a serious one and his contemplative practice has shaped a
millennium of seers. But on textual fidelity, on internal coherence
with the rest of the Gita, on the moral weight the Gita places on
action — and on what the practice the book is teaching needs in order
to be a real practice — the strong claim that the world is finally
unreal does not survive. The Gita is telling Arjuna to fight a real
battle for real reasons. It is asking him to give up the fruits of his
action, not to give up the action. It is teaching attention as the
discipline by which a real face becomes more visible, not less. A
finally-unreal world cannot bear that weight.

Shankara's diagnosis stays. The contemplative practice his metaphysics
underwrites — the patient seeing through the surface self, the
recognition that awareness is the medium in which experience arises —
is one of the great inheritances of human contemplation, and any
honest reading of the Gita has to be informed by it. The point I am
making against him is narrow: that the depths the contemplative
practice opens are depths *within* the world's reality, not evidence
that the world is finally unreal.

The chapter's grammar at 7.14 makes the case. *Daivī hy eṣā guṇamayī
mama māyā.* This *māyā* is *daivī* — divine, not demonic, not
accidental. It is *guṇamayī* — made of the modes, structured. It is
*mama* — mine, possessed by Krishna, located in him. Not an autonomous
principle of unreality. The power by which the divine appears as the
world while remaining beyond the world, hard to cross because the
gunas-driven mind keeps mistaking the appearing for the source. The
word the Gita uses is the same word Shankara reads as illusion. The
grammar gives a different weight. It is the divine's own veiling
power, not the world's unreality.

Madhva, the third great classical commentator, takes the realism
further. The divine, individual selves, and matter are eternally
distinct. There is no thesis of world-illusion in his reading at all.
The world is fully real and is owned by the divine without being
collapsed into the divine. I read with him on the world's reality and
against him on the strictness of the distinction. The chapter's
intimacy — the divine as the taste in water, the radiance of moon and
sun, the inner quality of every kind of thing — is a closer relation
than Madhva's strict-dualist frame can carry. Ramanuja's image — the
body and the embodied self — holds both the distinctness and the
intimacy. That is the floor I am working from.

So: the world is real. The divine is real. The relation is intimate
without being identity. The depths that contemplative practice opens
are depths within that real relation, not exits from it.

---

The reason this matters is not metaphysical hygiene. It is what
holding the world as an illusion costs you, in a life.

I have been thinking lately about the simulation argument — the
philosophical position, surprisingly popular among people who work in
technology, that there is some non-trivial probability we are inside a
computer simulation run by a more advanced civilization. The argument
is interesting on its own terms. It is also corrosive when held
operationally. If the world is a simulation, then the people in it are
non-player characters. The grief is rendered. The face across the room
is a textured polygon. The work is a quest item. You can hold this
position intellectually and continue to behave well; some people do.
But the position is heavy, and it bleeds. The longer it is held, the
more the question *does this actually matter?* recurs — and the
question is the wrong question, because the only honest answer the
position permits is *probably not*.

A reader of this book may be more familiar with the same move in older
language. The world is *maya* in the strong sense — illusion, dream,
veil over a truer reality. Treat your daughter's wedding as a
projection on the screen of consciousness. Treat your father's death
as a play of the modes. Treat the project you are pouring yourself
into as a passing form. There is a contemplative tradition that says
this and means it, and I am not denying that it has produced
practitioners of striking equanimity. I am saying: most readers of
this book are not those practitioners. Most readers, when they try to
hold the world as illusion, end up holding it as half-real — and
half-real is worse than either of the alternatives. Half-real is the
world treated as not-quite-mattering, while the chest still tightens
when the call comes from the hospital. The mismatch is the suffering.

The simulation argument and the strong reading of *maya* converge in
operational effect even if their philosophies differ. Both teach the
practitioner to hold the immediate world at a slight distance, in case
the immediate world is not finally what is. Both are difficult to hold
without weakening one's responsiveness to what is actually in front of
one. Both have a respectable theoretical pedigree and a difficult
practical track record.

The qualified-non-dualist position does something different. It says:
the world is real, and is the place where the divine is being shown.
The face across the room is real, and is one of the faces through
which what is most worth your attention is being given. The grief is
real, and is the form in which a real loss is being lived. There is
no second screen. There is no truer reality you are missing while you
are responding to this one. The chapter's *vijñāna* — realization, the
felt registration of what the chapter's *jñāna* names intellectually —
is the lived recognition of this. Not that the world is more than it
seems in some hidden way, but that it is exactly what it is, and that
what it is is the divine's own form.

The cost of getting this wrong, in 2026, is concrete. A reader I have
in mind has been treating a long-running disagreement with a partner
the way one might treat a problem in an abstract simulation. They keep
winning. Every conversation, they win. Their arguments are sharper,
their memory of past conversations more curated, their model of the
partner more confident. The partner, meanwhile, is becoming smaller
and smaller in the model — flatter, more predictable, less real. The
reader has not noticed that they have stopped hearing the actual
person across from them. They are arguing with a simulation of the
partner that lives inside their own head, and they are winning every
round.

This is *maya* at the conversational scale. It is exactly what 7.14
names. The reader has been carried by the gunas-driven flicker — the
desire to be right, the aversion to being wrong, the habit of
self-defense — and has been delivered to themselves a partner who is
not the partner. They are responding to the model. The model is real
in a sense; it is a real production of their cognition. But the
partner is realer than the model, and the practice of relationship is
the practice of staying with the partner instead of the model.

Iris Murdoch is the modern voice that names this best. Her *Sovereignty
of Good* is the closest thing in twentieth-century moral philosophy to
the operational ethics the Gita is teaching. She writes that love is
the perception of individuals — that love is, as she puts it, the
extremely difficult realization that something other than oneself is
real. The sentence belongs at the floor of this chapter. It is the
practice of attention turned at a real other. It is what the Gita
means when it says that *jñāna* without *vijñāna* — knowledge without
lived recognition — is incomplete. To know intellectually that the
partner is real is one thing. To recognize, in the heat of the
argument, that they are in fact real and that what they are saying is
in fact what they are saying — that is *vijñāna*. That is the
realization the chapter is teaching.

Murdoch did not believe in the divine in the Gita's sense. Her
theology, such as it was, was Platonic — the Good as the orienting
horizon of the moral life, sovereign in the sense that nothing is more
real, but not personal in the way Krishna is. I do not need her to
have been a believer. I need her account of attention, which is
correct, and her vocabulary, which is sharp. The chapter's
metaphysical floor and her moral practice converge. The world is real.
The other is real. Love is the difficult act of letting that be true.

---

There is a contemporary scientific picture that, properly held, is the
most useful modern analog to *maya* I have found. It is not, as some
popular treatments suggest, a confirmation of the strong illusion-
reading. It is the more modest claim that perception is, in significant
part, construction.

The framework is called predictive processing. Karl Friston's
free-energy principle is the technical version; Anil Seth's *Being You*
is the most accessible English presentation. The picture: the brain
does not passively receive a clean signal from the world. It generates
top-down predictions about what should be sensed, and uses incoming
sensory data largely to update or correct those predictions. What you
experience as "seeing" is your brain's best current hypothesis about
what is in front of you, kept honest by sensory feedback. When the
hypothesis is right, perception feels seamless and obvious. When the
hypothesis is wrong, you get illusions, hallucinations, and the small
surprises of ordinary cognition. Seth's signature line — perception is
a controlled hallucination, kept in check by sensory information — is a
useful provocation as long as the second half is not dropped.

This is a working framework, not a settled science. It has competitors
and open empirical questions. It does not show, and Friston and Seth
do not claim it shows, that the world beyond perception is unreal.
What it shows is that perception is mediated. There is a model. The
model is shaped by what one expects, fears, wants. The screen between
the perceiver and the perceived is partly the perceiver's own
construction.

The Gita names this. *Māyā* is the obscuring power that makes the
divine hard to see through the world. The chapter is not, primarily,
making a claim about the perceptual layer — it is making a claim about
the existential layer, about how the gunas-driven mind delivers the
world to the seer in a form that is not the world. But at the layer
the science speaks to, the veil is real. The Gita's claim that one is
seeing in part one's own model is, at the perceptual layer, just true.
The conversation with the partner is mediated by a model of the
partner. The reading of the daughter is mediated by a model of the
daughter that was last fully updated when she was twelve. The
self-image you carry into a meeting is mediated by a model of yourself
that was assembled in graduate school under pressures you no longer
remember. Predictive processing tells you the screens are real. It
does not tell you what is behind them. The chapter does.

What the framework will not deliver is the chapter's metaphysical
floor. It will not tell you that what lies behind the model is the
divine. Sean Carroll's poetic naturalism is the honest scientific
counter-position: the world consists of natural processes describable
in physical terms, with higher-level vocabularies — about meaning,
value, persons — valid in their own register but not requiring a
theological remainder. On the basis of physics alone, you do not need
to say "nothing higher than me." You can stop at the physical, and the
physical does not in itself cry out for a beyond. Carroll holds this
position without bitterness. He thinks it is more honest than its
alternatives.

The chapter's claim is not a scientific claim. Carroll's position is
the honest scientific edge. They live at different layers of the same
human conversation, and they only contradict each other if one demands
they speak at the same layer. They do not. The reader can take
predictive processing as a precise description of how perception is
shaped by the perceiver's model, and Carroll's naturalism as the
honest baseline against which the Gita's metaphysical claim is being
made, and the Gita's claim as the further commitment about what the
perceiver — having seen the screen for what it is — is then in
relation to.

Spinoza, four centuries earlier, made a structurally similar move
without the predictive-processing apparatus. His *Ethics* — that
strange book in geometrical form, suspicious of personal-God language,
strict in its determinism — argued that there is one substance, that
this substance is what we mean by God or by Nature, and that
everything that is, is in God. *Whatever is, is in God, and nothing
can be or be conceived without God.* The structural resonance with
Gita 7.7 is exact. *Mattaḥ parataraṁ nānyat* — there is nothing higher
than me. *All this is strung on me as jewels on a thread.*

Spinoza is not Krishna. Spinoza's monism is *substance* monism — one
substance, the world its modes, expressed under the attributes of
thought and extension — and the relation is impersonal. Krishna's
"nothing beyond me" is more loaded. He is named — Krishna; he is
addressed — Arjuna; the relation is personal in a way Spinoza's
geometry never permits. But Spinoza is useful here precisely because
he forces the reader to take the *no beyond* seriously without
softening it. Once you have affirmed that there is nothing higher,
you have cut off the possibility that the world is something *other*
than the divine, or *separate*, or *abandoned*. That is the surface
meaning of 7.7, and Spinoza pushes the reader to feel its full weight.

What the Gita adds, and Spinoza's framework lacks, is the second-
person address. The divine is *spoken*, and *speaks*. The world is not
just the divine's modes; it is the medium of the divine's address.
This is closer to what Jean-Luc Marion calls the saturated phenomenon
— the kind of phenomenon in which intuition exceeds the concepts the
receiving consciousness brings to bear. Most of what we encounter is
given in a way that fits within our concepts; the phenomenon is
constituted, intelligibly, by the receiving I. Some phenomena give
*more* than the I can constitute. The historical event gives more than
any narrative can encompass. The face of another person gives more
than any account of the other can reach. The call — the sense of being
addressed — gives more than any account of who is calling can settle.
In each case, intuition saturates the available concepts. The I
receives without being able to master.

This is the structure of "nothing beyond me." The "me" of 7.7 is given
to Arjuna more fully than Arjuna can conceptually receive. Arjuna
cannot circumscribe the speaker. Arjuna can only be addressed, and
respond, and remain in the asymmetry. Marion's vocabulary gives the
modern reader a way of holding the chapter's voice without flattening
it into either a metaphysical inventory or a poetic flourish. The
divine, in the Gita's disclosure, is not one more entity in the
catalog of things. It is the structure of an excess no catalog could
exhaust. *Yogamāyā* — the veil of mystery in which the divine appears
unmanifest — is the phenomenological condition of this excess. The I
sees what it can. The I does not see all that is given.

You do not need to take Marion's Catholic theology. You need only
register that the Gita's "nothing beyond me" is a saturated address,
not a metaphysical inventory. The world is real because there is
something there to be addressed by. The divine is real because the
address is happening. *Maya* is the difficulty of receiving the
address through the conditions of finite perception. Murdoch's
attention is the practice of clearing what can be cleared so that what
is given can be given.

---

If the world is real and is the divine's body, two things follow for a
life in 2026.

First: the practice of attention is not a private mental hygiene. It
is the practice of meeting what is actually there. Every chapter of
this book has been moving toward this. The work of *anasakti* — acting
without grafting selfhood onto the result — only makes sense if the
work is real and the result is real and the not-grafting is a way of
staying in honest relation with both. The work of *dhyana* — meditation
as trained attention — only matters if there is something there for
attention to be turned at. The work of *karma yoga* — engaged action
in the world without being owned by it — only has weight if the world
has weight.

The chapter is the floor underneath all of that. It is what makes the
practice not a solipsistic exercise. The face you are practicing on is
real. The work you are practicing on is real. The grief, the joy, the
small ordinary day, the stranger you walk past, the bird at the
window, the child who cannot sleep — all real, all the medium through
which the divine is being shown to you, and your attention is the
discipline by which you are receiving the showing. *Vijñāna* is what
happens when the attention has been practiced long enough that the
showing comes through.

Second: the long-running disagreement is not winnable. Not in the way
you have been trying to win it. You have been winning against your
model of the other person. Every win has been a loss, because every
win has reinforced the model and made the actual other person less
visible. The qualified-non-dualist floor says the actual other person
is real, and is one of the faces through which what is most worth your
life is being given. The practice is to set down the model and listen
to what is being said. Not in some abstract therapeutic sense — in the
specific, repeatable, uncomfortable sense of catching yourself
rehearsing your response while they are still talking, and stopping.
And then again the next time. And the time after that.

Steven Hayes's clinical work on cognitive defusion gives the
contemporary technique a name. The Stoics gave the same operation a
different name — the withholding and granting of assent. The Gita's
*maya*-discipline is the same operation with a different metaphysical
horizon. Different vocabularies, the same practice. The thought
arises. The model is generated. You do not have to be carried by it.
You may, with the trained part of yourself, give or withhold
agreement. The practice is repeatable, learnable, and slow. There is
no shortcut.

What changes, when the practice has been kept long enough, is not that
the models stop being generated. The brain continues to do what the
brain does. What changes is the small, durable distance between the
model and the action. You see the model. You also see what is in
front of you. You respond to what is in front of you. The face becomes
visible again. The partner becomes the partner again. The work becomes
the work again. The world stops being a screen onto which you are
projecting and resumes being the face you are standing inside.

That last image is the one I want to leave you with. It is the
question the chapter is asking, and the position it is committing to,
and the floor underneath the rest of the book.

You can take the world as a screen you are projecting on. Many readers
of philosophy and contemplative literature have. Some technologists do.
Some serious meditators do. The position has a respectable theoretical
pedigree. It corrodes most lives that try to hold it in earnest.

Or you can take the world as a face you are standing inside. Real
face. Real standing. Real inside. The face is not your projection. It
is what is given. The work is to receive the giving without being
carried away by your model of what is being given. *Maya* is the
difficulty of doing this. *Vijñāna* is the lived recognition that, at
this moment, in this room, with this person across the table and this
work in your hands and this hour of your day, what is given is given.
Not later. Not behind. Here.

The chapter's last verse says the one who has known me, knows me at
the moment of death. You can take this metaphysically, as the Gita
means it. You can also take it as a structural claim about a life:
what you have actually seen, in the sustained practice of seeing, is
what you carry through the moments when you have nothing else.

The practice is to see. The world is real. There is something here to
be seen.
