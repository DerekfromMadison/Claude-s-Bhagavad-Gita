# Chapter 15 — The Supreme Person

Your life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. There
is no third option. If you cannot say what your days are pointed at,
your days are being pointed for you, by whatever is closest at hand —
the promotion, the phone, the mood your spouse came home in, the
disappointment that arrived in this morning's email. The strongest
local pull will, given enough Tuesdays, make the life. The question
is not whether you are being shaped. The question is by what.

That is the chapter's claim, and everything in it — the strange
upside-down tree, the axe you are told to swing, the final word
*purusottama* (the Supreme Person, *uttama* meaning highest, *purusha*
meaning person) — is in service of getting that claim into your
bloodstream so it changes how you spend Wednesday.

## The tree growing the wrong way

Krishna opens the chapter with an image that has been circulating in
Indian thought since the *Katha Upanishad*. There is a tree. Its
roots are above. Its branches grow downward. Its leaves are the sacred
hymns. The tree is the *ashvattha* — the peepal, the sacred fig, the
banyan with its aerial roots that drop down and become trunks of their
own — but inverted, as if you were standing on the sky looking down
at a world that grows toward you.

The tree is *avyaya*, imperishable. Krishna says so plainly. And then,
in the same breath, undermines its solidity: its real form is not
seen, neither its end nor its beginning nor its foundation. It looks
rooted to those caught in the leaves. From inside the canopy, the
canopy is the world.

This is the picture of your life, on a normal day. You are inside the
leaves. The rustle around you — your career, your relationships, the
project that ended hollow, the one underway, the one not started yet
— feels like the floor. You stand on it, plan from it, defend it
against threats. You experience it as solid because you experience it
from within. The chapter wants you to do something difficult: look up.

The root is in a different direction.

Then comes the instrument. *Asanga-shastrena drdhena chittva.* With
the weapon of non-attachment, having firmly cut. *Asanga* means
non-clinging — not coldness, not detachment in the depressive sense,
but the freedom from being owned by what you have or want or fear to
lose. The instruction is to take this weapon and cut the tree down.
Then to seek the place from which those who reach it do not return.

You are not being asked to abandon your life. You are being asked to
loosen its grip on you so you can see which way it is pointed.

## What the chapter is doing with "higher than the imperishable"

Hold the image and turn to the chapter's metaphysical hinge. There
are two persons in this world, Krishna says at 15.16: the *kshara*,
the perishable, and the *akshara*, the imperishable. The perishable
is all beings; the imperishable is what stands at the summit. And
then, at 15.17–18, the move that the whole chapter is built around:
there is another. A third. Higher than both. He is *uttamah purusha*,
the Supreme Person, *purusottama*. He enters the three worlds and
sustains them. *That,* Krishna says, *is who I am.*

Whatever *purusottama* is, he is not the most exalted item on a list.
That is the structural claim, and it survives the inter-school dispute
about how to describe him. Sankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Aurobindo, and
Prabhupada have spent twelve centuries disagreeing about what exactly
the third person is, and the disagreement does not have to be settled
for the structural claim to land. Sankara reads him as the non-dual reality under personal
designation — the Supreme beyond the very category of imperishability,
beyond the frame that opposes perishable and imperishable in the
first place. Ramanuja reads grammatically, as the chapter's surface
invites: two persons (the soul bound in matter, the soul released from
matter) and a third (the Lord who is neither). Aurobindo refuses to
choose, holding the personal and impersonal as registers of a higher
synthesis. The chapter's grammar leans personalist; the impersonalist
tradition does real interpretive work to soften that surface; the
Gita as a whole does not close the question. What all three readings
share is the refusal of inventory. The chapter's own move at 15.18 —
*I transcend the perishable and am higher even than the imperishable*
— says the highest is not the highest item. It is not on the list
at all.

That is what the strange image of "higher than the imperishable"
finally means. The "toward which" of a life is not on the inventory
of things you can buy, achieve, or accumulate. It is not the highest
branch. It is the direction in which the branches do not grow.

## What you have probably mistaken for the root

Paul Tillich, writing in a Christian register two and a half millennia
later, made the same structural move when the Vedantic vocabulary was
not in front of him: God is being-itself, not a being. Not a being
beside others, not even the highest one. Different idioms, the same
insistence — what is finally real is not on the list. The recognition
you need is not Vedantic. It is structural. Whatever you point at,
when you point at the highest thing you can name, you are not pointing
at a thing on the inventory.

This matters because most of what you may have mistaken for your root
is on the inventory. Career is on the inventory. Status is on the
inventory. Accomplishment is on the inventory. These are all branches
— sometimes magnificent ones — and the chapter's most subtle warning
is that branches do not finally hold.

Career is the most defensible candidate, because it gives back so much
that is real: structure, purpose, competence, an answer to the
question *what do you do.* When the work aligns with your gifts and
the years take shape, it feels like the floor. It is not. It will
end — by retirement, by industry collapse, by the slow private
discovery that the work no longer means what it used to — and whoever
made it the root is, on that day, unmoored.

Status hides better. You rarely use the language of status about
yourself, but the phenomenology gives it away: the frequent checking,
the disproportionate hit when a metric falters, the particular
restlessness that follows a slight. The architecture of the modern
attention economy makes status-rootedness nearly the default, which is
not your fault but is your problem, because the attention economy is
not designed for your flourishing.

Accomplishment flatters: the shelf of finished projects, the book
written, the company built, the children raised. What accumulates also
depletes. Whoever has made accomplishment the root is one major
failure away from a kind of unrootedness harder to recover from than
the failure itself.

None of these is bad. Some are very good. The chapter is not
contemptuous of branches. It is asking you to know which is which.

## The Tuesday-morning question

Here is the most compact form of the chapter, for you, in 2026:

You are at your kitchen counter. The coffee is made. The first email
has not yet been answered. There is an hour, maybe forty-five
minutes, before the day's noise begins. And a question rises on its
own, the way questions do when the noise is briefly held off:

*What is my life pointed at?*

If you can answer cleanly, you are rare — and not for the reasons
you would expect. The cleanly-answering person is rare because most
of the candidate answers, on inspection, do not survive the test.
The job is a candidate. So is the partnership, the political project,
the next purchase, the next vacation, the next promotion, the
children's education, the savings goal, the half-finished novel, the
side project, the home renovation. Real things. Things that matter.

The test is small and brutal. Imagine the candidate gone. The job —
gone, by layoff or honest resignation. The partnership — gone, by
death or by distance. The project — finished, shelved, shown to be
unworthy. Is there still something to point at?

If yes — *there is something underneath that this branch was an
expression of and not a substitute for* — your relation to the
branch is healthy. If no — *my life would be uprooted* — the chapter
has named a problem. The rootedness was, all along, in a branch.

This is not an argument against love, vocation, parenthood, art, or
service. It is an argument *for* them, as they actually are: branches
in a real tree. A career held under the axe is a freer career. A
love held under the axe is a freer love. The axe does not diminish
the branches. It lets them be what they are without asking them to be
what they are not.

The Tuesday-morning question is not, in the end, a metaphysical
question. It is a directional one. *What is my life pointed at?* The
chapter does not require you to settle whether the toward-which is
finally personal or impersonal, Vaishnava or Advaitic, Christian or
secular. It requires you to have one — or to notice that you do not,
and to take the noticing seriously enough to do something about it.

## Two cases, sharpened

A promotion arrives. It pays better, requires a move, or absorbs most
of your discretionary attention for several years. Before you
calculate pay and scope, the chapter asks one question first: *what
would this decision do to my rootedness?*

The new role is a branch. So is the old one. The issue is not which
branch is taller. The issue is whether the new role lets your life
remain pointed at what you would, in a quiet hour, call its root —
or whether it is the kind of role that quietly conscripts your
attention until the role itself becomes what you take to be the root.

People answer this badly all the time. They take a role for good
local reasons — more pay, a worthy challenge — and discover, four
years in, that they no longer remember what the root was supposed to
be. The colonization is rarely dramatic. The calendar fills. The
mental space the job occupies gradually exceeds what you have
without it. Family is less because the job is more. Friendships are
maintained at the level the job permits. The personal projects are
deferred until they stop being deferred because they are no longer in
your head as candidates.

The chapter is not telling you to refuse the promotion. Sometimes the
role is the right one. What it asks is that you make the decision
*under* the discipline of the axe, not *as* a branch.

The axe is not a metaphor I want to leave abstract. It is a practical
discipline, and you can take it up tomorrow.

The discipline is *anasakti*, *vairagya*, *asanga* — non-attachment,
the freedom from being owned by what you have or do or want. Not
coldness. Not the spiritual-bypass move of pretending not to care
about things you in fact care about deeply. The axe is the practiced
capacity to set down the result of an action while you are performing
it. It is what chapter 2 teaches as a stance and chapter 15 hands
you as a tool.

In the daily texture of your life, the axe takes humble forms. The
breath taken before opening the application. The unprogrammed stretch
of time held back from being filled. The willingness to be bored
momentarily, to remember that boredom is the trailhead toward
something the algorithm cannot reach. The honest moment of asking,
about a current preoccupation, *root or branch?* — and the
willingness to live with the answer.

The phone deserves a paragraph of its own, because the inverted tree
is almost embarrassingly literal there. Your attention, eight hours
a day, is routed through a vast canopy of branches that have nothing
to do with your root, designed by skilled and well-paid people who
know more about how to hold your attention than you do about how to
hold your own. The chapter is not anti-technology. It is
anti-conscription — anti the silent, gradual takeover of attention
by what passes. Ask of your relation to the phone what you ask of
every relation: *which way is this pointed?*

The axe also takes weightier forms. The willingness, in a
relationship that has gone hollow, to name the hollowness rather than
to perform around it. The practice of, on hearing of someone's death,
refusing to defer the question — *if it were me, what would still be
there?* — until later, when later does not come.

These are not religious practices in the sectarian sense. They do not
require a theology. They do not require resolving the dispute
between Sankara and Ramanuja about what *purusottama* finally is.
They require the willingness to ask, the discipline to ask again
tomorrow, and the honesty to live with the answers.

Done once, a moment of clarity. Done daily, for months and years —
the slow felling of the great tree.

## The pointing

The chapter ends on a word.

*Purusottama.* The Supreme Person. Higher than the perishable, higher
than the imperishable, the third. He enters the three worlds and
sustains them. *That,* Krishna says, *is who I am. Whoever knows me
thus, undeluded, knows all.*

The inter-school question stays open. The chapter's grammar leans
personalist; the book holds both registers; the unsettledness is
deliberate. What is not unsettled is the operational claim.
*Purusottama* names a relation, not a place on the inventory. The
relation is one of derivation and direction — the world you are
inside is not its own root, and your life is not its own ground. To
live well, you need to know which way is "toward the root," and the
chapter's pastoral burden is to make sure you do.

The inverted tree shows you, in image, that the visible foundation is
not the foundation. The axe shows you, in instruction, what to do
about it. *Purusottama* shows you, in the chapter's final word, the
direction the axe is being swung — toward, not away from. The axe is
not nihilism. It is the clearing of a sight-line.

Take the personalist register if it serves you. Take the apophatic
register if that one does. Whatever you point at, point at the
highest thing you can name. Then live as if the pointing is real. The
image of "highest" the chapter offers is *purusottama*. If the word
lands for you, use it. If it does not, the chapter does not insist.
What the chapter insists on is that there be a pointing, and that the
pointing be at something the local pulls cannot, on a bad day,
substitute for.

Your life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. People
who do not point are not free; they are simply being shaped by the
strongest local pull. The career colonizes. The phone takes. The
status hierarchy organizes the day's emotional weather. The nearest
disappointment becomes the thing the day is about. None of this
requires malice. It requires only that you not be pointing at
something else.

The ones who point — at *purusottama*, at Tillich's being-itself, at
whatever language their tradition has given them, or at no language
and only the silent direction the axe is swung in — are not immune to
the pulls. They feel them. The pulls do not stop being real. But the
pulls do not, finally, *shape* them, because the shaping is being
done by something that is not local, that does not wax or wane with
the day's weather, and that the local cannot finally substitute for.

If the inverted tree, the axe, and the pointing have made any sense
at all, you already know, in some part of yourself, what you point
at. The career is *for* something. The relationships are *for*
something. The hours given to a craft, to a child, to an old friend,
to a piece of work that may never finish — these are *for* something,
and the something is rarely the visible foundation. The chapter's
gift is not to give you the something. The chapter's gift is to
teach you to recognize it as the something, and to keep the axe near
at hand for the days when the branches start to feel like the floor.

If you can still see what your life is pointed at, the day is yours.

If you cannot, the day belongs to the branches.

Pick up the axe.
