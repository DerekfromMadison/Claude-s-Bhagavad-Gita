# Chapter 15 — The Supreme Person

Arjuna, picture a tree growing the wrong way. Its roots are above. Its branches grow downward into the world. Its leaves are the verses of an old teaching, and the leaves keep being spoken, and the wind keeps moving them. From inside the canopy, the canopy looks like the world. You stand on a branch and call it ground.

This is the situation you are in. Not metaphorically. Actually. Right now, today.

You are standing on a branch. It feels solid because it is what is under you. You plan from it. You defend it. When something threatens it, your body responds as if the threat were to your life, because in a way the branch has become your life. The career is one such branch. The household is one. The reputation is one. The half-built thing you are pouring yourself into is one. Some of these branches are magnificent. None of them is the root.

The root is in the other direction. Look up.

You will not, on a normal Tuesday, see the root. The teaching itself says so: its real form is not seen, neither end nor beginning nor foundation. The leaves keep moving and you keep being inside them. So the instruction is not "see the root." Take an axe — call it that, since that is what it is — and cut. The axe is non-attachment. By that I mean the freedom from being owned by what you have and what you fear to lose. Not coldness. Not withdrawal. The end of the grip.

Cut firmly. Walk in the direction the felling opens.

You are not being asked to abandon your work, or to stop loving the people you love. You are being asked to loosen the grip, so that the work can be work and the love can be love, instead of being the floor you stand on.

Now hear this carefully, because most lives miss it.

There are two kinds of person in this world. The perishable, which is everything that comes and goes. The imperishable, which stands above the coming and going. These two are real. But there is a third, beyond both. Higher than the perishable. Higher than the imperishable. He enters the worlds and holds them. He is what the breath is doing while you are not paying attention. He is the light in your eye. He is the fire under the meal you ate this morning, doing its quiet work in your body.

I am that one. I tell you this not to be praised, but so you know which way to point.

Hear what I am not saying. I am not saying I am the highest item on the inventory of things you might pursue. I am not on the inventory at all. The supreme is not the best branch. The supreme is the direction in which branches do not grow. To orient toward me is not to acquire one more thing. It is to remember, on an ordinary morning, that you and your life are derived, and that your derivation has a direction.

A small portion of me has entered every life, including yours. The breath rising in you is my breath. The warmth digesting your food is my warmth. You have never been without me. The question has only ever been whether you were facing toward me or away.

So look at your day. What does it organize around? What is the first thing your attention goes to in the morning? Where does the weight of the day finally fall? That is the root your life is currently treating as its root. If it is the device in your hand, the device is the root. If it is the metric at work, the metric is the root. The strongest local pull, given enough quiet weekdays, makes the life. You are being shaped. The only question is by what.

A life points somewhere or it is pointed for it. There is no third option. The orientation is the practice. Not a feeling about orientation. Not a stated belief in orientation. The actual aim of the actual day.

Take the test, and take it small. Imagine the branch you stand on gone. The job ends. The marriage ends by death or by distance. The project is shelved, shown to be other than you thought. Is there still something to point at? If yes, your relation to the branch is healthy and the branch is free to be a branch. If no — if your answer is that your life would be uprooted — then the rootedness was in the branch all along, and the branch was never going to hold.

This is not an argument against love, or work, or building. It is an argument for them, as they actually are. A career held under the axe is a freer career. A love held under the axe is a freer love. The axe does not diminish what it tests. It lets the branches be branches without asking them to be the ground.

Pick up the axe. Cut where you have been gripping. Then keep walking, in the direction the felling opens.
