# Chapter 15 — The Supreme Person

A life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. There is
no third option. If you cannot say what your days are pointed at,
your days are being pointed for you, by whatever is closest at hand —
the promotion, the phone, the mood your spouse came home in, the
disappointment that arrived in this morning's email. The strongest
local pull will, given enough Tuesdays, make the life. The question
is not whether you are being shaped. The question is by what.

That is the chapter's claim. The strange image at its opening and
the instrument it puts in your hand serve that claim — to get it
into your bloodstream so it changes how you spend Wednesday.

## The tree growing the wrong way

Picture a tree growing the wrong way. Roots above. Branches growing
downward. A sacred fig inverted, as if you were standing on the sky
looking down at a world that grows toward you.

The chapter calls it imperishable, then in the same breath
undermines that solidity: its real form is not seen, neither end nor
beginning nor foundation. It looks rooted to those caught in the
leaves. From inside the canopy, the canopy is the world.

This is your life on a normal day. You are inside the leaves. The
rustle around you — your career, your relationships, the project
that ended hollow, the one underway — feels like the floor. You
stand on it, plan from it, defend it against threats. You experience
it as solid because you experience it from within. The chapter wants
you to do something difficult: look up. The root is in a different
direction.

Then comes the instrument. With the weapon of non-attachment, cut
firmly. Non-attachment is not coldness. It is the freedom from
being owned by what you have or want or fear to lose. Take this
weapon and cut the tree down — walk in the direction the felling
opens.

You are not being asked to abandon your life. You are being asked
to loosen its grip on you so you can see which way it is pointed.

## Higher than the imperishable

There are two persons in this world: the perishable, which is all
beings, and the imperishable, which stands at the summit. Then the
move the whole chapter is built around — there is another. A third.
Higher than both. He enters the three worlds and sustains them.
*That,* the chapter says, *is who I am.*

Whatever the highest is, it is not the most exalted item on a list.
It is not the highest item. It is not on the list at all.

The "toward which" of a life is not on the inventory of things you
can buy, achieve, or accumulate. It is not the highest branch. It
is the direction in which the branches do not grow.

## What you have probably mistaken for the root

Career is on the inventory. Status is on the inventory.
Accomplishment is on the inventory. These are branches — sometimes
magnificent ones — and the chapter's most subtle warning is that
branches do not finally hold.

Career is the most defensible candidate. It gives back so much that
is real: structure, purpose, competence, an answer to *what do you
do.* When the work aligns with your gifts, it feels like the floor.
It is not. It will end — by retirement, by industry collapse, by the
slow private discovery that the work no longer means what it used
to — and whoever made it the root is, on that day, unmoored.

Status hides better. The phenomenology gives it away: the frequent
checking, the disproportionate hit when a metric falters, the
restlessness that follows a slight. The attention economy makes
status-rootedness nearly the default.

Accomplishment flatters: the shelf of finished projects, the book
written, the company built, the children raised. What accumulates
also depletes. Whoever has made accomplishment the root is one
major failure away from an unrootedness harder to recover from than
the failure itself.

None of these is bad. Some are very good. The chapter is asking you
to know which is which.

## The Tuesday-morning question

Here is the most compact form of the chapter, for you, in 2026:

You are at your kitchen counter. The coffee is made. The first email
has not yet been answered. There is forty-five minutes before the
day's noise begins. And a question rises on its own:

*What is my life pointed at?*

The job is a candidate. So is the partnership, the political project,
the next promotion, the children's education, the half-finished
novel. Real things. Things that matter.

The test is small and brutal. Imagine the candidate gone. The job —
gone, by layoff or honest resignation. The partnership — gone, by
death or by distance. The project — finished, shelved, shown to be
unworthy. Is there still something to point at?

If yes — there is something underneath that this branch was an
expression of and not a substitute for — your relation to the branch
is healthy. If no — *my life would be uprooted* — the chapter has
named a problem. The rootedness was, all along, in a branch.

This is not an argument against love, vocation, parenthood, art, or
service. It is an argument *for* them, as they actually are:
branches in a real tree. A career held under the axe is a freer
career. A love held under the axe is a freer love. The axe does not
diminish the branches. It lets them be what they are without asking
them to be what they are not.

## The career decision

A promotion arrives. It pays better, requires a move, or absorbs
most of your discretionary attention for years. Before you
calculate pay and scope, ask one question first: *what would this
decision do to my rootedness?*

The new role is a branch. So is the old one. The issue is not which
branch is taller. The issue is whether the new role lets your life
remain pointed at what you would, in a quiet hour, call its root —
or whether it is the kind of role that quietly conscripts your
attention until the role itself becomes what you take to be the
root.

People answer this badly all the time. They take a role for good
local reasons and discover, four years in, that they no longer
remember what the root was. The calendar fills. Family is less
because the job is more. Personal projects are deferred until they
are no longer in your head.

The chapter is not telling you to refuse the promotion. Sometimes
the role is the right one. What it asks is that you make the
decision *under* the discipline of the axe, not *as* a branch.

## The axe as practical discipline

The axe is the practiced capacity to set down the result of an
action while you are performing it. Not coldness. Not the
spiritual-bypass move of pretending not to care about things you in
fact care about deeply.

In daily life, the axe takes humble forms. The breath before opening
the application. The unprogrammed stretch of time held back from
being filled. The willingness to be bored momentarily, to remember
that boredom is the trailhead toward something the algorithm cannot
reach. The honest moment of asking, about a current preoccupation,
*root or branch?* — and the willingness to live with the answer.

The phone deserves a paragraph, because the inverted tree is almost
embarrassingly literal there. Your attention, eight hours a day, is
routed through a canopy of branches that have nothing to do with
your root, designed by people who know more about how to hold your
attention than you do about how to hold your own. Ask of your
relation to the phone what you ask of every relation: *which way is
this pointed?*

The axe also takes weightier forms. The willingness, in a
relationship gone hollow, to name the hollowness rather than perform
around it. The practice of, on hearing of someone's death, refusing
to defer the question — *if it were me, what would still be there?*
— until later, when later does not come.

Done once, a moment of clarity. Done daily, for months and years —
the slow felling of the great tree.

## The pointing

The highest names a relation, not a place on the inventory — a
relation of derivation and direction. The world you are inside is
not its own root, and your life is not its own ground. To live well,
you need to know which way is toward the root.

A life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. People
who do not point are not free; they are simply being shaped by the
strongest local pull. The career colonizes. The phone takes. The
status hierarchy organizes the day's emotional weather. The nearest
disappointment becomes the thing the day is about. None of this
requires malice. It requires only that you not be pointing at
something else.

The ones who point are not immune to the pulls. They feel them. The
pulls do not stop being real. But the pulls do not, finally, *shape*
them, because the shaping is being done by something that is not
local, that does not wax or wane with the day's weather.

If the inverted tree and the axe have made any sense, you already
know, in some part of yourself, what you point at. The career is
*for* something. The relationships are *for* something. The hours
given to a craft, to a child, to an old friend, to a piece of work
that may never finish — these are *for* something, and the something
is rarely the visible foundation. The chapter's gift is to teach
you to recognize it, and to keep the axe near at hand for the days
the branches feel like the floor.

If you can still see what your life is pointed at, the day is yours.

If you cannot, the day belongs to the branches.

Pick up the axe.
