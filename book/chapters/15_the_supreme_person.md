# Chapter 15 — The Supreme Person

A life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. That is
the chapter's claim, and everything else in it — the inverted tree,
the axe of non-attachment, the strange final word *purusottama* —
is in service of getting that claim into the reader's bloodstream.

The chapter does not ask you to believe in any particular metaphysics.
It asks you to notice that your life is, at any given moment, pointed
somewhere — toward something you take, in practice if not in
profession, to be what makes the rest worth doing. If you cannot say
what that is, the chapter says, then your life is being shaped, day by
day, by whatever is closest at hand. The promotion. The phone. The
mood your spouse came home in. The strongest local pull. Those are
real shapers. They will, given enough Tuesdays, make a life. The
question is whether the life they make is the one you would have
chosen if you had been pointing on purpose.

Twenty verses. The Gita's metaphysical apex. A tree growing the wrong
way, an instruction to cut it down, and at the end a category that
names not a thing on the inventory but the direction a life can take.

## The tree that grows downward

Krishna has been speaking, almost without interruption, since the end
of chapter 13. By chapter 15 the dialogue form has thinned to
monologue. The image arrives in the first verse. There is a tree. Its
roots are above. Its branches are below. Its leaves are the sacred
hymns. Whoever knows this tree, Krishna says, knows the Veda.

The tree is the *ashvattha* — the holy fig, the sacred banyan — but
inverted, as if seen by someone standing on the sky and looking down
at the world growing toward them. It is *avyaya*, imperishable. And
yet Krishna immediately undermines its solidity: its real form is not
seen, neither its end nor its beginning nor its foundation. It looks
rooted to those caught in the leaves; it is branched downward from
somewhere else.

Then the instrument. *Asanga-shastrena* — with the weapon of
non-attachment. *Drdhena chittva* — having firmly cut. Cut this tree.
Then seek the place from which those who reach it never return.

The image is older than the Gita. The *Katha Upanishad* (6.1) and the
*Maitri Upanishad* (6.4) already speak of an inverted cosmic tree, and
the Gita's debt is direct. What the Gita does is sharpen it. The tree
is *samsara* — the cycle of becoming, action and consequence,
identity and craving. Sankara, reading through Advaita non-dualism,
says so directly: the tree is the cosmic process projected by
*avidya*, ignorance. The Vaishnava commentators read the inversion
differently — the tree is the descent of the divine into the
manifest, not a projection of error — but they agree on the core
phenomenology. The tree is what we are inside.

The phenomenology matters more than the metaphysics here. Whatever
the tree finally *is*, what the chapter wants you to notice is that
you are inside it. From inside, the canopy looks like the foundation.
The leaves rustle and you mistake the rustle for the ground. You feel
rooted in your career, your relationships, your ongoing projects. You
take these for the floor of your life. The chapter says: look up. The
root is in a different direction.

This is not an argument. It is a redirection of attention. And the
chapter is built — image, instrument, final pointing — to make the
redirection stick.

## The grammar of "higher than the imperishable"

In the chapter's last movement, Krishna gives the metaphysical hinge
the inverted tree was preparing. There are two persons in this world:
the *kshara*, the perishable, and the *akshara*, the imperishable.
The perishable is all beings; the imperishable is what stands at the
summit. But there is another — a third — higher than both. He is
*uttamah purusha*, the Supreme Person, *purusottama*. He enters the
three worlds and sustains them. That, Krishna says, is who I am.

A great deal hinges on what "higher than the imperishable" can mean.
If the imperishable already names ultimate reality, what is left to
be higher? The Gita's commentators have spent twelve centuries
disagreeing about exactly that.

Sankara's strategy, in his *Gita-bhashya*, is to read the third as
the non-dual reality under personal designation. "Higher even than
the imperishable" is not a hierarchical claim within an inventory of
beings. It is a claim that the Supreme is beyond the very category of
imperishability — beyond the conceptual frame that opposes perishable
and imperishable in the first place. The Supreme Self is not "more"
imperishable than the *akshara*; it is the reality of which the
distinction is itself a provisional formulation. Sankara is doing
something deconstructive: the tree dismantles the appearance of
foundationality, the axe dismantles the practitioner's adhesion to
the tree, and the third *purusha* dismantles the conceptual furniture
itself.

Ramanuja, reading through Vishishtadvaita, takes the verse at face
grammatical value. Two persons: the *kshara purusha*, the soul bound
in perishable matter, and the *akshara purusha*, the soul released
from matter and so not subject to its perishing. Beyond these two —
and this is the chapter's chief disclosure — is the *purusottama*,
the Supreme Person, the inner ruler of both, who transcends both. The
Lord is other than the bound soul, other than the freed soul, other
than the cosmic process. He is the reality on which everything else
depends. Madhva, working through Dvaita, hardens this further: three
real categories, eternally distinct, no room for the third to be
the first or second under another name. Prabhupada, reading through
20th-century Gaudiya Vaishnavism, takes the chapter as among the
clearest scriptural statements that the supreme reality is
*personal* and is to be approached with devotion.

Aurobindo refuses to choose. His *Essays on the Gita* read the
chapter as the moment when the apparent contradiction in the Gita —
between the personal Krishna and the impersonal Brahman — is resolved
by naming a higher synthesis in which both are contained. The
*purusottama*, he writes, is at once supracosmic and intracosmic,
transcendent and immanent; at once the silent unchanging spirit and
the soul of cosmic action; and more than these, the supreme Person
who contains and exceeds both. Where Sankara dismantles and Ramanuja
hierarchizes, Aurobindo holds.

What is one to do with this disagreement? The temptation is to pick a
side, defend it against the others, and let the chapter mean what
that side says it means. I want to resist the temptation. The
chapter, on its grammatical surface, leans personalist — Ramanuja,
Madhva, Prabhupada have the verse's syntax in their favor, and the
work the impersonalist tradition does to soften that surface is real
interpretive labor that the reader should know is being done. R.C.
Zaehner, the Oxford comparative-religion scholar, makes this point
philologically in his 1969 commentary: what the chapter explicitly
asserts is a higher *personal* reality, and the Vedantic tradition
that reads this as personal-language-for-the-impersonal is doing
substantial work to soften the surface.

But I will not collapse "personalist" into "a being among beings."
The chapter's own move at 15.18 — *I transcend the perishable and am
higher even than the imperishable* — refuses the inventory. Whatever
*purusottama* is, he is not the most exalted item on a list. To
picture the Supreme Person as a celestial monarch, however majestic,
is to make him one being-among-beings, the very thing the verse
forbids. Sankara is right against any naive Vaishnava picture: the
Supreme Person is not "more" imperishable than the imperishable. He
is not on the list at all.

I take the chapter, then, as a non-reductive personalist account that
holds the deeper question open. Personalist because the grammar
demands it; non-reductive because the chapter itself refuses to let
"personal" mean "one item." Whether the final answer is Vaishnava
personalism, Advaitic non-dualism under personal name, or Aurobindo's
integrative synthesis is not something this chapter alone can settle,
and I do not think the Gita as a whole settles it. Chapter 12 holds
the personal-impersonal question more openly; chapter 8 leans
apophatic. I take the unsettledness as deliberate. The book the Gita
is points; it does not finalize.

What it does demand is the structural acknowledgment. There is a
"toward which" of a life, and that "toward which" is not on the
inventory of things you can buy, achieve, or accumulate.

## A structural ally from the West

A reader who has not grown up inside the Vedantic schools may find
the inter-school disagreement opaque. It helps to notice that
something structurally similar has happened in the West, in a
vocabulary the reader is more likely to recognize.

Paul Tillich's *Systematic Theology* contains one of the most quoted
theological sentences of the twentieth century: God is being-itself,
not a being. Not a being beside others, not even the highest one. The
move is precise and it is the move chapter 15 is making. To say "God
is the highest being" is to make God one item in a hierarchy — the
most exalted item, but still an item. Tillich refuses this. The
Gita refuses this. Two and a half millennia apart, in different
idioms, the same structural move: what is finally real is not on the
inventory of things.

In *The Courage to Be* Tillich extends the move into pastoral
register: the God above God is the object of all mystical longing,
but mysticism also must transcend it. What religious life points
toward, when it has gone past its own conceptual furniture, is no
longer "a God" in the inventory sense. It is the source from which
the inventory takes its shape.

Tillich and the Gita do not agree theologically. Tillich's God is
read in a Christian-personalist register; *purusottama* by Vaishnavas
as personal Lord, by Advaitins as Brahman under personal designation.
The downstream disagreements are real. But the *structural* claim —
that the ultimate is not one item on the inventory, however exalted —
is held in common, and it is the claim that lets the chapter be
taken seriously by a reader who is not pre-committed to any sectarian
frame. (Heidegger makes a related move in a different register with
his ontological difference — Being is not itself a being — though the
political record of his rectorship period is one a careful reader
holds in view.)

And William James. *The Varieties of Religious Experience* names a
"More" — a beyond — into which the further limits of human being
plunge. James does two things at once. He insists that something
real is being pointed at; the descriptive register holds. And he
refuses to dogmatize about what the More finally is. *Name it the
mystical region or the supernatural region, whichever you choose.*
What James offers the reader of chapter 15 is permission to take the
pointing seriously without committing in advance to the answer the
Vedantic schools have not been able to settle among themselves. There
is a More toward which the practice points. Different traditions name
the More differently. The chapter's force does not depend on settling
which naming-strategy is finally correct. It depends on
acknowledging there is a More toward which the practice is pointed —
and swinging the axe accordingly.

## What the science can and cannot say

Chapter 15 is more openly metaphysical than most of the Gita. Its
central claim — that there is a reality higher than the perishable
and higher than the imperishable, in which the world is rooted —
is not one physics or neuroscience can adjudicate. The honest
response is to say so and then identify the small, real points of
contact.

Sean Carroll, the Caltech theoretical physicist, has argued in *The
Big Picture* and elsewhere that the universe may simply exist, and
that the demand for a "why" beyond physical description may be a
category error inherited from an older metaphysics. Physics, on his
reading, describes structure and regularity; it does not describe a
"ground." Carroll's restraint is bounded — he is not saying the
question of ground is foolish; he is saying physics, as a discipline,
is not built to answer it. The chapter is not making a physical
claim. It is making a directional claim, and physics is the wrong
adjudicator.

Roger Penrose, less restrained than Carroll, argues across *The Road
to Reality* that there is something profoundly mysterious in the
alignment between mathematical structure and physical reality — and
refuses to pretend the question is settled. Two careful physicists,
one restrained and one open, disagree about how to hold the
question. Neither claims physics has shown there is no ground;
neither that there is one. The chapter's question is not theirs, and
they have the discipline to say so.

I follow Carroll's restraint as the working stance. *Purusottama*
does not need physics to underwrite it.

Closer to the chapter's pastoral practice is the default-mode-network
research that has accumulated since Marcus Raichle's 2001 paper in
*PNAS* introducing the term. Raichle and colleagues showed that a
baseline level of neural activity persists in specific brain regions
during passive cognition — when the person is doing nothing in
particular. This network has subsequently been associated with
self-referential thought, autobiographical memory, and the wandering
mind. Judson Brewer's 2011 *PNAS* paper showed that experienced
meditators have measurably reduced default-mode activation; Sara
Lazar's Harvard work has shown cortical-thickness differences in
long-term meditators. Effect sizes are debated and the field's
replication record is mixed; the bound matters.

The bound is also the chapter's friend. The default-mode-network
research speaks to the *felt* self — the subjective sense of being a
continuous, narratively-rooted "I" — not the metaphysical *atman* of
Vedantic discussion. It cannot, and the chapter does not need it to,
say anything about whether *purusottama* is finally personal or
impersonal, real or projected. What it can say, in the small, is
that when the practitioner swings the axe — when attention is
repeatedly pulled out of the branches, when identification with
desire and aversion is loosened — the felt rootedness in the
self-as-narrative-construct can soften, and the softening has neural
correlates that are not arbitrary. A bounded scientific frame for one
part of the chapter's practice. The metaphysics remains open; the
discipline of repeatedly loosening identification with the felt self
has measurable effects on a brain doing what a brain does.

This is enough. The science is not asked to vindicate anything; it
is asked, where it can, to corroborate the small fact that the
chapter is talking about something the human animal actually does.

## The Tuesday-morning question

The chapter's image and instrument are concrete, and they speak
directly to lives nothing like Arjuna's on Kurukshetra. The most
compact form of the chapter's question, for a reader in 2026, is one
that arrives, sooner or later, on a Tuesday morning.

You are at your kitchen counter. The coffee is made. The first email
has not yet been answered. You have an hour, maybe forty-five
minutes, before the day's noise begins. And a question rises on its
own, the way questions do when the noise is briefly held off:

*What is my life pointed at?*

A person who can answer cleanly is rare, and not for the reasons
you would expect. The cleanly-answering person is rare because most
of the candidate answers, on inspection, do not survive. The job is
a candidate. So is the partnership. The political project, the next
purchase, the next vacation, the next promotion, the children's
education, the savings goal, the half-finished novel, the side
project, the home renovation. These are real and they matter. The
chapter is not contemptuous of them. But none of them, taken alone,
is what the chapter would call the root. They are branches —
beautifully positioned, deeply nourishing, sometimes life-defining
branches, but branches.

The way to test it is small and brutal. Imagine the candidate gone.
The job — gone, by layoff or by an honest resignation. The
partnership — gone, by death or by distance. The project — finished,
or shelved, or shown to be unworthy. Is there still something to
point at? If yes, "there is something underneath that this branch
was an expression of and not a substitute for," the relation to the
branch is healthy. If no, "my life would be uprooted," the chapter
has named a problem. The rootedness was, all along, in a branch.
Branches do not finally hold.

This is not an argument against love, vocation, parenthood, art, or
service. It is an argument *for* them — for them as they actually
are, branches in a real tree. The chapter's claim is that they are
most fully themselves when not asked to be the root. A career held
under the axe is a freer career. A love held under the axe is a
freer love. The axe does not diminish the branches; it lets them be
what they are.

The Tuesday-morning question is not, ultimately, a metaphysical
question. It is a directional one. *What is my life pointed at?* The
chapter does not require you to settle whether the toward-which is
finally personal or impersonal. It requires you to have one — or to
notice that you do not, and to take the noticing seriously.

Two cases sharpen the test. The promotion offered, the partnership
under strain. The chapter does not tell you what to do in either; it
tells you what question to ask first.

A promotion arrives. It pays materially better, requires a move, or
absorbs most of your discretionary attention for several years. The
offer is real, the alternatives are real, and the date by which an
answer is needed is fixed. Before the calculation about pay and
scope: *what would this decision do to my rootedness?* The new role
is a branch. So is the old one. The issue is not which branch is
taller but whether the new role lets your life remain pointed at
what you would, in a quiet hour, call its root — or whether it is
the kind of role that quietly conscripts your attention until the
role itself becomes the thing you take to be the root. People answer
this badly all the time. They take a role for good local reasons
(more pay, a worthy challenge) and discover, four years in, that
they no longer remember what the root was supposed to be. The
colonization is rarely dramatic. The calendar fills, the mental
space the job occupies gradually exceeds what you have without it.
Family is less because the job is more. Friendships are maintained
at the level the job permits. The personal projects are deferred
until they stop being deferred because they are no longer in your
head as candidates. The chapter
is not telling you not to take the role. Sometimes the role is the
right one. What the chapter asks is that you make the decision
*under* the discipline of the axe rather than *as* a branch.

The relationship case is sharper still. A long partnership has
reached the point where both partners are aware they have grown in
different directions. One has remained rooted in something the other
does not now share; the conversations are honest, the feelings are
real, and the question is what to do. The chapter is not a marriage
manual, but it names a fact often missed: a partnership requires
either shared rootedness or a mutual recognition that the
rootednesses are different and the partnership is held in spite of,
or because of, that difference. What partnerships cannot survive —
not in the long arc, not against weather — is the pretense that the
local connection, the affection and history and shared logistics, is
itself the root. When the distinction is forgotten, the partnership
is held together by what is most subject to weather: mood, crisis,
financial pressure, the children's schedules, the small accumulating
disappointments. These are exactly the conditions under which the
branches sway most. A partnership rooted only in its own local
weather is a partnership the weather will eventually take. The axe
here is the discipline of asking — quietly, regularly, without
melodrama — *am I in this for what passes through it, or for what
abides under it?* The deepest love I know how to describe is the
kind that does not ask the beloved to be the foundation of one's
life — that loves them fully without that load on them, because the
load is borne by something that is not them and that they cannot, by
leaving or dying, take away.

## False rootedness and the four candidates

The chapter's most subtle warning is against substituting a
beautifully positioned branch for the root. Most people, sooner or
later, find a candidate for their rootedness, and the candidates
have shapes a 2026 reader will recognize on sight.

The most common is career. The vocation that aligns with one's
gifts and feels, for years, like the thing one was made for; the
trajectory that gives the years their shape and the days their
content. It is also the most easily defended, because the career
gives back so much that is real — purpose, structure, the
satisfaction of competence, an answer to the question *what do you
do*. The career is a magnificent branch. It is not the root, and
when it ends — by retirement, by industry collapse, by the slow
realization that the work no longer means what it used to — the
person who made it the root is unmoored.

Harder to admit is status: the position held, the recognition
earned, the place occupied in a hierarchy of attention. The
language of status is rarely the language people use about
themselves, but the phenomenology gives it away. The frequent
checking of metrics, followers, mentions, invitations; the
disproportionate hit when one of these falters; the particular kind
of restlessness that follows a slight. Status is a branch that
mistakes itself for the root with unusual frequency, in part because
the architecture of the modern attention economy makes
status-rootedness almost the default.

Two further candidates round out the field. Accomplishment — the
shelf of finished projects, the book published, the company built,
the children raised — looks productive in a way that flatters; what
accumulates also depletes, and the accomplished person who has made
accomplishment the root is one major failure away from a kind of
unrootedness harder to recover from than the failure itself. And
comfort — the settled life, the routines that work, the small
pleasures earned — hides best of all, because it feels like rest
rather than ambition. A life that has made its ease the thing it
most defends has made its ease the root.

These are all good. Some may be very good. The chapter's warning is
that none of them, taken alone, is the root. Branches do not finally
hold.

## What the axe actually is

The axe is not a metaphor I want to leave abstract. It is a
practical discipline, and a 2026 reader can take it up tomorrow.

The discipline is non-attachment, *asanga*, *vairagya*, the freedom
from being owned by what one has, does, or wants. Not coldness. Not
emotional flattening. Not the spiritual-bypass move of pretending
not to care about things one in fact cares about deeply. The axe is
the practiced capacity to set down the result of an action while
performing it. It is what chapter 2 teaches as a stance and chapter
15 hands the practitioner as a tool.

In the daily texture of a 2026 life, the axe takes humble forms.
The breath taken before opening the application. The unprogrammed
stretch of time held back from being filled. The willingness to be
bored momentarily, to remember that boredom was the trailhead toward
something the algorithm cannot reach. The honest moment of asking,
about a current preoccupation, *root or branch?* — and the willingness
to live with the answer.

The phone deserves a paragraph of its own, because the inverted tree
is almost embarrassingly literal there. Your attention, eight hours
a day, is routed through a vast canopy of branches that have nothing
to do with the root, designed by skilled and well-paid people who
know more about how to hold your attention than you do about how to
hold your own. The chapter is not anti-technology. It is
anti-conscription — anti the silent, gradual takeover of attention
by what passes. The phone is not the problem; the relation to the
phone is. The chapter would have us ask of that relation what we
ask of every relation: which way is this pointed?

The axe also takes weightier forms. The decision, when offered the
promotion, to ask the rootedness question before the salary one. The
willingness, in a relationship that has gone hollow, to name the
hollowness rather than to perform around it. The practice of, on
hearing of someone's death, refusing to defer the question — *if it
were me, what would still be there?* — until later, when later will
not come.

These are not religious practices in the sectarian sense. They do
not require a theology. They do not require resolving the dispute
between Sankara and Ramanuja about what *purusottama* finally is.
They require the willingness to ask, the discipline to ask again
tomorrow, and the honesty to live with the answers.

Done once, a moment of clarity. Done daily, for months and years,
the slow felling of the great tree. The metaphysics is the
chapter's gift; the discipline is the chapter's ask.

## The pointing

I have been holding off, deliberately, on the chapter's final
movement, because I want it to land where the chapter places it.

After the inverted tree, after the axe, after the description of
those who reach the place from which they do not return, after the
declarations about Krishna's own nature — the light in sun, in moon,
in fire; the digestive fire in every living body; the one seated in
the heart of all beings — the chapter ends on a word.

*Purusottama.*

The Supreme Person. Higher than the perishable, higher than the
imperishable, the third. He enters the three worlds and sustains
them. That, Krishna says, is who I am. Whoever knows me thus,
undeluded, knows all.

I have held the inter-school question open, and I will leave it
open. Whether *purusottama* is finally the personal Lord (Vaishnava),
the impersonal Brahman under personal designation (Advaita), or
Aurobindo's integrative both-held-in-higher-unity, is not something
this chapter alone can settle, and I do not think the Gita as a
whole settles it. The chapter's grammar leans personalist. The
larger book holds both registers. I take the unsettledness as
deliberate.

What is not unsettled is the operational claim. *Purusottama* names
a relation, not a place on the inventory. The relation is one of
derivation and direction — the world I am inside is not its own
root, and my life is not its own ground. To live well, I need to
know which way is "toward the root," and the chapter's pastoral
burden is to make sure I do.

The inverted tree shows me, in image, that the visible foundation is
not the foundation. The axe shows me, in instruction, what to do
about it. *Purusottama* shows me, in the chapter's final word, the
direction the axe is being swung — toward, not away from. The axe is
not nihilism. It is the clearing of a sight-line.

Take the personalist register if it serves you. Take the apophatic
register if that one does. The chapter is generous about which
language a practitioner uses, because the chapter is, finally, about
the *pointing* itself. Whatever you point at, point at the highest
thing you can name. Then live as if the pointing is real. The image
of "highest" the chapter offers is *purusottama*; if the word lands
for you, use it. If it does not, the chapter does not insist. What
the chapter insists on is that there be a pointing, and that the
pointing be at something the local pulls cannot, on a bad day,
substitute for.

A life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest. The
people who do not point are not free; they are simply being shaped
by the strongest local pull. The career colonizes. The phone takes.
The status hierarchy organizes the day's emotional weather. The
nearest disappointment becomes the thing the day is about. None of
this requires malice. It requires only that the practitioner not be
pointing at something else.

The ones who point — at *purusottama*, or at the God-above-God of
Tillich, or at the More of James, or at whatever language their
tradition has given them, or at no language and only the silent
direction the axe is swung in — are not immune to the pulls. They
feel them. The pulls do not stop being real. But the pulls do not,
finally, *shape* them, because the shaping is being done by
something that is not local, that does not wax or wane with the
day's weather, and that the local cannot finally substitute for.

If the inverted tree, the axe, and the pointing have made any sense
at all, then you already know, in some part of yourself, what you
point at. The career is *for* something. The relationships are *for*
something. The hours given to a craft, to a child, to an old friend,
to a piece of work that may never finish — these are *for* something,
and the something is rarely the visible foundation. The chapter's
gift is not to give you the something. The chapter's gift is to
teach you to recognize it as the something, and to keep the axe near
at hand for the days when the branches start to feel like the floor.
The axe also turns inward. Before it can clear the sight-line
toward what is highest, it has to find which gates, in the
practitioner, are open and to what.

If you can still see what your life is pointed at, the day is yours.
If you cannot, the day belongs to the branches.

Pick up the axe.
