## Chapter 2 — *Sāṃkhya Yoga*: The Eternal Reality

*The chapter where Krishna, in answer to Arjuna's collapse, lays down
in compressed form what the rest of the Gita will unfold — a
metaphysics of the imperishable, an ethic of action without
self-grafting, and a portrait of the steady-minded sage.*

---

### § 1. The dialogue

Arjuna has fallen. He sat down between the armies, dropped his
bow, and refused the work he came for: he would rather be killed
unarmed than kill his teachers, his cousins, his elders. The
obvious right action, he has concluded, breaks every loyalty that
shaped him.

Chapter 2 opens with Krishna looking at this. His first reproach in
2.2 is uncomfortable: he calls the despair *anārya-juṣṭam*,
"unworthy," and asks where this weakness has come from at this hour.
There is contempt in 2.3 — get up, do not be a coward. He is not
yet teaching; he is rebuking. Arjuna pushes back in 2.4–2.8 and
finally, in the line that opens the rest of the Gita, says he is
Krishna's student and asks to be taught.

The teaching begins.

Krishna's first move, in 2.11, refuses the premise of Arjuna's
grief: the wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead.
From 2.12 to 2.30 he lays out the metaphysical claim — the embodied
self does not begin at birth or end at death; it passes from body
to body the way a person passes from childhood through youth into
age. Unborn, eternal, unchanging. Weapons cannot cleave it. Fire
cannot burn it. Water cannot wet it. Wind cannot dry it. What dies
is the body; the body's death is not the kind of loss that warrants
collapse.

At 2.31 the metaphysics turns into ethics. There is no greater duty
for a warrior than a righteous war, Krishna says; to refuse this is
to lose both this world and the next. The self does not die, and
your role here is not to abandon your station.

At 2.39 Krishna marks the transition: he has been teaching from the
standpoint of *sāṃkhya* (analytic knowledge); he now turns to *yoga*
(disciplined practice). The first account of *karma yoga* begins.
An action begun with the right inner posture cannot be lost; even a
little of this discipline protects from great fear.

Then, at 2.47, the most-quoted line of the Gita: your right
extends to action; it does not extend to its fruits. Do not act
for the fruit. Do not retreat into inaction either. Act, established
in yoga, with success and failure held evenly. Equipoise of mind,
2.48 says, is what yoga is.

From 2.54 onward, Arjuna asks the question that opens the chapter's
final movement: how does one of steady wisdom — the *sthitaprajña* —
speak, sit, walk? Krishna answers across nineteen verses. The
steady-minded one has abandoned the desires of the mind, content in
the self by the self. They are unmoved by sorrow and unhungering
for joy. Their senses, like a tortoise's limbs, withdraw from
sense-objects when collected. They watch the slow chain by which
dwelling on objects becomes attachment, attachment becomes desire,
desire frustrated becomes anger, anger becomes delusion, delusion
becomes loss of memory, loss of memory becomes ruin of intelligence
— and at the bottom of the chain, the person is destroyed. The sage
has stepped off this chain. What is night for ordinary beings is
the time of waking for the *sthitaprajña*; what they take for
wakefulness is the sage's night. Desires enter such a person as
waters enter a sea brimful and unmoving — the sea is not disturbed.

The chapter ends at 2.72 with Krishna naming the state: *brāhmī
sthiti*, the state of *Brahman* — the threshold beyond which
suffering of the kind Arjuna is suffering does not have purchase.

In a single chapter Krishna has put on the table what the rest of
the Gita will spend sixteen more chapters elaborating: the eternal
self, the ethic of action without self-grafting, and the portrait
of the integrated practitioner. The classical commentators have
called the chapter "the Gita in miniature." The phrase is right.

What it does not contain is the answer Arjuna asked for. He asked
*what to do*; he has been given a metaphysics, a redefinition of
action, and a portrait of who he might become. The order to fight
or to leave is not yet here. The chapter is not an instruction; it
is a foundation.

### § 2. The core concepts

Four concepts carry the chapter.

**(1) The imperishable self.** Krishna's term is *ātman* — the
self, the innermost, the deathless — used in tension with *deha*
(body) and *dehin* (the embodied). The teaching of 2.11–2.30: there
is in each being something that is not the body, not the senses,
not the personality formed by experience — something that does not
begin at birth, does not end at death. What is grieved at a funeral
is the body's absence. The other thing that was loved is not what
funerals can take.

This is not an obvious claim. The classical commentators argue
about *what* the *atman* is — whether it is a single non-dual
reality appearing as many, or many real individual selves — and
modern thought largely refuses the premise. Those disagreements
come in §3 and §4. The concept itself: there is, at the center of a
human life, something the body's mortality does not reach.

**(2) Non-attachment to fruits.** *Karma-phala-tyāga* —
relinquishment of the fruits of action — or, in Gandhi's
formulation, *anāsakti-yoga*. Non-attachment is not indifference. It
does not mean half-hearted performance or that the outcome "doesn't
matter." It means something more precise: the *self* doing the
action is not the self that needs the outcome. Effort can be total.
Caring about the outcome can be present. What is forbidden is the
*grafting of selfhood onto the result* — the move by which a person
becomes the action's success or failure rather than the action's
author.

Verse 2.47 condenses this. It is the most-translated line of the
Gita, partly because it lifts out of its context and lands in
modern life with little adjustment, partly because the disagreement
among interpreters has kept it in productive contention for
centuries. §3 treats it from five angles.

**(3) Equanimity in opposites — *sthitaprajña*.** *Sthita* (steady)
plus *prajñā* (wisdom). The chapter's last movement is the portrait
of this person — concrete (how they speak, how they sit, what moves
them) and strange (the *sthitaprajña* is awake when others sleep
and asleep when others think they are awake). The inversion is
meant: the person of steady wisdom is awake to a different layer of
what is happening than the ordinary person is.

The portrait is easily misread as someone who has stopped caring,
gone numb, retreated into pleasant dissociation. The text is
explicit that this is not what is meant. The *sthitaprajña* is not
numb — they are *self-collected*. The senses are withdrawn the way
a tortoise withdraws its limbs: deliberate composure, not injury.

**(4) Yoga as skill in action.** At 2.50 Krishna defines *yoga*:
*yogaḥ karmasu kauśalam* — "yoga is skill in action," in the
rendering nearly every translator converges on. *Yoga* here is not
meditative withdrawal but a quality of acting in the world — acting
from the self that is not grafted onto the fruit, with equanimity
in opposites. *Yoga* in 2.50 is the operative word for the whole
ethical program.

The four pillars — the imperishable self, non-attachment to fruits,
the steady-minded one, yoga as skill in action — are what the
classical and modern commentators argue about.

### § 3. The traditions speak

Mascaró's rendering of 2.20, Krishna's first declaration of the
imperishable self:

> "He is never born, and he never dies. He is in Eternity: he is
> for evermore. Never-born and eternal, beyond times gone or to
> come, he does not die when the body dies." (Gita 2.20, trans.
> Mascaró 1962, Penguin Classics)

Radhakrishnan reproduces *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* I.2.18 alongside the
Gita's verse — nearly identical wording. The Gita is restating an
Upanishadic inheritance with surgical compression.

Seven readings follow: five from the Indian commentary tradition,
two academic-translational.

#### § 3.1. Advaita Vedānta — Ādi Śaṅkarācārya

Sankara, reading through Advaita: there is finally one reality;
multiplicity is real at the empirical level (*vyāvahārika*) and
resolves, on the highest analysis (*pāramārthika*), into the
non-dual. Standard editions: Mahadeva Sastri's *Bhagavad-Gita with
the Commentary of Sri Sankaracharya* (Madras 1897, Samata Books
reprint) and Swami Gambhirananda's *Bhagavad Gita with the
Commentary of Sankaracarya* (Advaita Ashrama, 1984).

Sankara's controlling moves in Chapter 2:

- 2.11–2.30 is the *jñāna* section. The *atman* is the imperishable
  substrate, and Krishna's argument is that *the warrior does not
  finally exist as a warrior* — the social role is real at the
  empirical level but does not reach the *atman*.

- 2.39 onward, the *karma-yoga* section, is instruction for the
  *adhikari* (qualified student) not yet ready for direct *jñāna*.
  *Karma yoga* prepares the mind; it is not the end-point.

- For 2.47, the reading turns on *kartṛtva-abhimāna*, the conceit
  "I am the doer." The verse does not prohibit acting; it prohibits
  the agent's *identification* as doer in the way that makes the
  agent liable for fruits. The action is performed; the doer-conceit
  is dropped.

A verbatim Sankara sentence is not offered here — the substance is
attributed pending edition verification. Sankara's contribution: the
diagnosis that what attaches the agent to the fruits is not the
fact of action but the agent-conceit.

Sankara's reading is powerful. The two-level distinction
(*vyāvahārika*/*pāramārthika*) becomes the architecture of all
later Advaitic commentary. It also has to work to make the Gita's
repeated address to "you, Arjuna" — the persistence of named selves
in 2.12 — sit comfortably with non-duality.

#### § 3.2. Viśiṣṭādvaita — Rāmānujācārya

Ramanuja, reading through Vishishtadvaita: divine, individual
selves (*jīvas*), and material world are all real, with selves and
matter together as the body of the divine — inseparability, not
identity. Standard editions: Swami Adidevananda's *Sri Bhagavad
Gita Bhasya of Ramanuja* (Ramakrishna Math, 1991) and M. R.
Sampatkumaran's *Gitabhasya of Ramanuja* (Bombay, 1969).

Ramanuja's moves:

- The *atman* of 2.20 is real, individual, and eternal — but it is
  not *Brahman*. It is *jīva*. Selves are distinct from each other
  and from God. Krishna's claim in 2.12 that "I, you, and these
  kings" have always existed is, for Ramanuja, a positive defense
  of the eternity of plural selves.

- 2.47 is instruction to act *as worship*. The fruits belong to the
  Lord because the *jīva* is the Lord's instrument. The prohibition
  on attachment is the prohibition on self-aggrandizement: the
  *jīva* is not the proprietor of the action and therefore not of
  its fruits.

Sankara and Ramanuja converge on the practical instruction (act, do
not attach) but diverge on why. For Sankara, attachment presupposes
a doer-conceit that is metaphysically illusory. For Ramanuja, it
presupposes a proprietary relationship between *jīva* and action
that is theologically misplaced. The *jīva* is the Lord's; its
actions are the Lord's; the fruits are the Lord's.

Ramanuja's is the most theologically committed reading of the
chapter: a real plurality of selves in eternal relation to a real
personal divine, with *atman*-*Brahman* identity cleanly denied.

#### § 3.3. Activist *karma yoga* — Mahatma Gandhi

Gandhi, reading the Gita as a manual for nonviolent action — the
battlefield as inner moral life and ordinary social action, not (or
not only) literal war. He wrote his own translation and commentary,
*Anāsakti Yoga*, in Gujarati in 1929, serialized in *Navajivan*,
translated by Mahadev Desai as *The Gita According to Gandhi*
(Navajivan, 1946). He acknowledges the literal reading and chooses
the allegorical because it serves the ethical work.

Gandhi's chosen verse is 2.47. *Anāsakti* — non-attachment — is the
Gita's central ethical teaching and the foundation of nonviolent
action. *Young India* (6 August 1925) carries the widely-cited
passage about going to the Gita "for solace" through every crisis;
substance is paraphrased pending stable wording.

For 2.47: the right to work is real, full, unconditioned; the right
to the fruits is unreal — it never existed. The verse is not adding
a discipline; it corrects an error about what the agent ever had.
Acting for the fruits confuses what one controls (the action) with
what one does not (the result). Withdrawing from action is the
opposite error. The middle path — act fully, do not own the fruit —
is *anāsakti*.

The political projection: act for justice without needing victory
to know one acted rightly. *Satyagraha* depends on this. If victory
were the criterion of right action, the strategy would have
collapsed at every reversal. The Gita gives it inner durability.

Gandhi reads the battlefield allegorically and centers the
ethical-practical core of 2.47, with the metaphysics deferred.
Gandhi is candid that his is *a* reading, not the only one; the
candor matters.

#### § 3.4. Integral Yoga — Sri Aurobindo

Aurobindo, reading through Integral Yoga — a synthesis of action,
knowledge, and devotion within an evolutionary spiritual framework
in which the Gita's internal tensions are stages of an unfolding,
not contradictions. *Essays on the Gita*, serial in *Arya*
(1916–1920), now in *Complete Works*, Vol. 19 (Sri Aurobindo
Ashram, 1997).

Aurobindo's moves:

- The metaphysics of the imperishable self in 2.11–2.30 is the
  *foundation* of the Gita's teaching, not its conclusion. The
  acharyas are right that the self is real and not the body. They
  are wrong to make this the highest truth; the highest truth is
  the integral one in which the individual soul, having recognized
  its imperishable ground, returns to action as a conscious
  instrument of the divine working.

- For 2.47, the verse does not prohibit desiring outcomes; it
  redirects the desire. The agent does not attach selfhood to the
  result because the action is no longer the agent's private
  project — it is the divine working using the agent as instrument.
  The fruits belong to that working. The agent's task is to remain
  the instrument and not collapse back into the proprietary self.

Substance attributed; verbatim wording paraphrased pending edition
verification.

Aurobindo reads the *synthesis* of the three yogas as the Gita's
controlling thesis and inhabits either Advaita or Vishishtadvaita
as the spiritual purpose requires. The reading is developmental and
evolutionary: the imperishable self is the necessary first ground
for a much larger working.

#### § 3.5. Comparative-philosophical academic — Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan

Radhakrishnan, reading the Gita comparatively, cross-referencing
Plato, Plotinus, Christian mystics, Stoics, and Buddhists — *The
Bhagavadgita* (Allen & Unwin, 1948). The bias is harmonization;
the defense is that he was teaching a generation of Anglophone
readers how to engage a tradition the British academy had often
patronized.

Radhakrishnan's moves:

- For 2.20, he reproduces *Katha Upaniṣad* I.2.18 alongside the
  verse: nearly identical language. 2.20 is not the Gita's
  invention; it is drawing on an Upanishadic inheritance.

- For 2.47, the verse generalizes a Mimamsa ethical principle. The
  earlier tradition had defended ritual action for the sake of its
  promised fruits — heaven, sons, cattle, longevity. The Gita
  generalizes: the agent has authority over the effort, not the
  result; the proper object of discipline is therefore the effort.
  This is the early articulation of *niṣkāma karma*, action without
  desire-for-fruit.

Radhakrishnan reads the Gita in long-tradition conversation,
softening sectarian particularity in favor of the
comparative-philosophical method itself. The charge of dilution is
fair; the rejoinder is that for many readers it is the only
available door.

#### § 3.6. Literary-universalist — Juan Mascaró

Mascaró, reading through mid-twentieth-century literary
universalism — the conviction that the great wisdom texts speak in
related registers — translated the Gita for Penguin Classics in
1962. The rendering is sometimes more poetry than philology.

His 2.22:

> "As a man leaves an old garment and puts on one that is new, the
> Spirit leaves his mortal body and then puts on one that is new."
> (Gita 2.22, trans. Mascaró 1962, Penguin Classics)

The garment image is in the Sanskrit. Mascaró's contribution is the
cadence — the parallel construction and the choice of "Spirit"
where others render "the embodied" or "the dweller in the body."
"Spirit" is theologically loaded; it imports a Christian-inflected
sense of the inner person that the Sanskrit *dehī* does not carry.
A real cost — and also why the line has carried.

Mascaró trades the precision of the Sanskrit and the metaphysical
commitments of any commentator for literary memorability and
hospitality to many traditions — a translation that speaks to a
reader who has never opened a Sanskrit grammar.

Mascaró's renderings appear where the literary register matters,
not as a basis for technical philosophical argument.

#### § 3.7. Contemplative-pastoral — Eknath Easwaran

Easwaran, reading the Gita as instruction in the slow training of
attention — a manual of practical psychology for ordinary lay
practitioners. His Gita (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed. 2007 cited here) is
designed for daily use, with the three-volume *Bhagavad Gita for
Daily Living* (1975–1984, revised) as expansion. The softening
flattens the text's edges; for many contemporary readers his is the
door that opened.

For 2.47:

> "You have the right to work, but never to the fruit of the work.
> You should never engage in action for the sake of reward, nor
> should you long for inaction. Perform work in this world, Arjuna,
> as a man established within himself — without selfish attachments,
> and alike in success and defeat. For yoga is perfect evenness of
> mind." (Gita 2.47–2.48, trans. Easwaran, Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed.,
> 2007)

Two earlier English forms show what each translator chooses. Edwin
Arnold's *Song Celestial* (Trübner, 1885) — what Gandhi first
encountered as a young law student in London — gives 2.47 in
Victorian blank verse:

> "Let right deeds be / Thy motive, not the fruit which comes from
> them. / And live in action! Labour! Make thine acts / Thy piety,
> casting all self aside…" (Gita 2.47–2.48, trans. Arnold 1885,
> *The Song Celestial*)
> *(Arnold's original verse division differs slightly from later
> translations; the lines above span 2.47 into 2.48.)*

And Mascaró's 1962 Penguin Classics rendering, in his characteristic
literary register:

> "Set thy heart upon thy work, but never on its reward. Work not
> for a reward; but never cease to do thy work." (Gita 2.47, trans.
> Mascaró 1962, Penguin Classics)

For 2.55, Easwaran:

> "When a man gives up all desires that emerge from the mind, and
> rests contented in the Self by the Self, then is he called a man
> of steady wisdom." (Gita 2.55, trans. Easwaran, Nilgiri Press,
> 2nd ed., 2007)

For 2.62–2.63, the famous chain, Easwaran:

> "When you keep thinking about sense objects, attachment comes.
> Attachment breeds desire, the lust of possession that burns to
> anger. Anger clouds the judgment; you can no longer learn from
> past mistakes. Lost is the power to choose between what is wise
> and what is unwise, and your life is utter waste." (Gita
> 2.62–2.63, trans. Easwaran, Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed., 2007)

For 2.70 — the image of the sea, which closes the *sthitaprajña*
portrait — Easwaran:

> "As rivers flow into the ocean but cannot make the vast ocean
> overflow, so flow the streams of the sense-world into the sea of
> peace that is the sage." (Gita 2.70, trans. Easwaran, Nilgiri
> Press, 2nd ed., 2007)

Easwaran sets aside the classical metaphysical disputes for the
proposition that what the Gita teaches can be lived, incrementally,
through ordinary discipline. The classical commentators tend to
think the *sthitaprajña* a high and rare attainment. Easwaran's
response: the portrait describes a posture, the posture is
trainable, the metaphysics follows as the posture matures.

The chapter leans on Easwaran for many verse-quotations: he is the
most accessible English translator here, the renderings are honest
about being aimed at practice, and the edition is widely available
for verification.

### § 4. Modern counterpoints

Three voices outside the Indian commentary tradition. Each pushes
on the Gita's claims in a way that sharpens what the Gita commits
to.

#### § 4.1. Stoicism — Epictetus on the dichotomy of control

The Stoic tradition shares with the Gita a central interest in the
inner posture from which a person acts. The opening of Epictetus's
*Enchiridion* (compiled by Arrian from his discourses, c. 125 CE),
in Nicholas P. White's translation (Hackett, 1983):

> "Some things are up to us and some are not up to us. Up to us are
> opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a word, whatever
> is our own doing; not up to us are our body, our property,
> reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not our own
> doing." (Epictetus, *Enchiridion* §1, trans. White 1983)

The parallel with 2.47 is obvious: the agent's authority extends
over their own activity, not over what follows from it in the
world. The agent who treats outcomes as something they can demand
of reality has misunderstood the basic shape of the human
situation.

The differences sharpen the Gita. Epictetus is more procedural — a
*technique*, the discipline of separating impressions that arise
(not up to us) from the assent we give them (up to us). The Gita
in 2.47 is more ontological: it names what is yours and what is
not, leaving the procedural detail to the *sthitaprajña* portrait.
Epictetus is more individualist; the Stoic's project is the
well-formed inner life with social good downstream. The Gita is
more relational: the inner posture matters because action is
inescapable and consequential for others.

The deepest difference: Epictetus's frame is *cognitive* — by
reordering judgments, giving assent only to true impressions, the
wise person becomes free of disturbance. The Gita's frame, in
2.55–2.72, is *constitutive*: the *sthitaprajña* has not just
gotten their judgments right; they have undergone a reorganization
of the place from which judgments are made. The seat of identity
has shifted.

A reader with only Epictetus has the technique. A reader with only
the Gita has the orientation. Read together, they sharpen each
other.

#### § 4.2. Personal identity — Derek Parfit

Derek Parfit, in *Reasons and Persons* (Oxford: Clarendon, 1984),
advances a reductionist account of personal identity. Personal
identity is not a "further fact" beyond the physical and
psychological facts about a person. It is constituted by relations
of psychological continuity and connectedness — chains of
overlapping memories, intentions, beliefs, character — and there is
no separately existing self that *has* these relations.

Parfit's most-cited conclusion, from Part Three (chapters "What We
Believe Ourselves to Be" and "How We Are Not What We Believe"), is
that personal identity is not what matters in survival. What
matters is psychological continuity and connectedness "with any
cause"; once those are present, the further question of whether
the person who results "is the same person" is empty. (Region of
page 281 in the 1984 edition; paraphrased pending stable wording.)

The Gita appears the maximal opposite. It asserts the *atman* —
imperishable, real beyond and beneath the psychological-continuity
relations Parfit takes as constitutive of person-talk. If Parfit is
right, the Gita is wrong about metaphysics.

Two qualifications complicate the opposition.

First, Parfit's claim that "personal identity is not what matters"
is, in its lived implications, surprisingly close to 2.47. If
personal identity in the strict sense is not what matters, then
relentless attachment to the fruits of *their* actions, *their*
career, *their* reputation rests on an inflation of something that,
on Parfit's analysis, is not the right object of that level of
investment. Parfit's reductionism could ground the Gita's ethics of
non-attachment by removing the metaphysical premise on which
excessive self-investment rests. Similar practical recommendation
from opposite metaphysical directions.

Second, the Gita's *atman* is not robustly substantialist on close
reading. It is described overwhelmingly in negative terms (unborn,
undying, uncleavable, unburnable). What it positively is, the Gita
does not say with the clarity the classical commentators bring. The
gap between the Gita's *atman* and Parfit's reductionism may be
narrower than it first appears — though the gap does not disappear.

What is at stake when the Gita asks the agent to relinquish
attachment to fruits is, partly, what we mean by the agent in the
first place.

#### § 4.3. Buddhist counterpoint — *anattā* and Bhikkhu Bodhi

The Pali tradition's *anattā* (Sanskrit *anātman*) — "not-self" —
is explicitly anti-Gita on the metaphysics of the self. The
Buddha's analysis of the person into five aggregates (*khandhas* —
form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness) is meant to
show that none of them, singly or together, is the substantial,
abiding self Gita 2.20 asserts. There is the flow of aggregates;
there is no *atman* underneath.

Bhikkhu Bodhi, contemporary American Theravada monk and
scholar-translator (*The Connected Discourses*, *Middle Length
Discourses*, *In the Buddha's Words*, Wisdom Publications, 2005),
is careful: *anattā* is not a denial of all selfhood — the Buddha
does not say "there is no person." It is the precise denial that
any *khandha*, singly or together, is the substantial abiding self
the non-Buddhist traditions of the time were positing. The Gita's
*atman* is exactly that. Substance paraphrased pending stable page.

The pressure here is sharper than Parfit's. Parfit makes a
contemporary analytic argument; Bodhi names a tradition older than
Sankara that sat next to the Gita's for many centuries and refused
exactly the metaphysical commitment the Gita makes. The
disagreement is internal to the Indic philosophical landscape.

The Buddhist counterpoint asks: do you really need the *atman*? If
what the Gita's ethics requires is the *posture* of acting without
self-grafting (2.47) and the *phenomenology* of equanimity
(2.54–2.72), can these be delivered without the metaphysical claim?

Partly. The posture of non-attachment can be cultivated in a
non-substantialist frame; the early Buddhists demonstrated that for
centuries. But the Gita's *consolation* — its answer to grief — is
harder to deliver without it. The Gita can tell the bereaved that
what they loved is not utterly gone; the Buddhist tradition offers
a different consolation (grasping is what made the loss this
painful), not the same one. §6 returns to this.

### § 5. Modern science

Two strands of research speak to Chapter 2's concepts. Both claims
are bounded; researchers are named.

#### § 5.1. Self-determination theory — Deci and Ryan

Intrinsic motivation predicts greater persistence, creativity, and
well-being than extrinsic; under some conditions, introducing an
extrinsic reward to an intrinsically motivated activity *reduces*
the intrinsic motivation. Self-determination theory (SDT) has been
developed by Edward L. Deci and Richard M. Ryan since the 1970s
(*Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior*,
Plenum, 1985; canonical article: *American Psychologist* 55(1),
2000, pp. 68–78).

Intrinsic: engaging in an activity because the activity itself is
interesting or meaningful. Extrinsic: engaging for the sake of an
outcome external to it (reward, avoidance of punishment, approval).
The reduction effect — the overjustification effect — is the
classic Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett finding (*Journal of
Personality and Social Psychology* 28, 1973, pp. 129–137).

The bound: SDT does not claim outcomes are unimportant. The frame
is not "don't care about results"; it is that the *quality of
motivation* — the degree to which motivation is internal to the
activity rather than yoked to external reward — predicts sustained
engagement and well-being.

This puts precise empirical pressure on a misreading of 2.47. The
misreading: "the Gita teaches indifference to outcomes." The
chapter, on Aurobindo's and Easwaran's readings, teaches something
narrower: the agent should not graft selfhood onto outcomes. SDT
sharpens this: the structure of caring that predicts sustained
engagement and well-being is caring rooted in the activity rather
than yoked to external reward. Different from indifference; closer
to what the *sthitaprajña* portrait describes from the inside.

#### § 5.2. The neuroscience of equanimity — Davidson and the cognitive control of emotion

Trained contemplative practice alters "affective style" —
characteristic individual differences in emotional response
patterns. Richard J. Davidson at Wisconsin has documented, with
Antoine Lutz and others, what the training does (*American
Psychologist* 55(11), 2000, pp. 1196–1214; updated in Lutz, Dunne,
and Davidson, *Cambridge Handbook of Consciousness*, 2007).

A parallel line: *cognitive reappraisal* — re-describing the
meaning of an affect-eliciting stimulus — engages prefrontal
regions and modulates amygdala response (Kevin Ochsner and James
Gross, *Trends in Cognitive Sciences* 9(5), 2005, pp. 242–249).

Combined picture: equanimity in the face of difficult affect is not
a fixed temperament. It is, in part, a trainable capacity, with
identifiable neural mechanisms. Long-term contemplative
practitioners show altered baseline activity in regions associated
with self-referential processing (the default-mode network —
Raichle, *PNAS* 98(2), 2001, pp. 676–682) and in the insula and
anterior cingulate cortex.

The bound: a small set of replicated findings. *Not* the claim
that meditation produces enlightenment, or that the *sthitaprajña*
is an empirically demonstrated category. What it supports: the
equipoise the Gita describes is in important respects trainable,
with identifiable mechanisms. The classical commentators who saw
the *sthitaprajña* as a rare and high attainment were partly right:
full training is rare. They may have underestimated the degree to
which *partial* training is available to ordinary people through
ordinary discipline.

This is consonant with Easwaran's pastoral reading and cuts against
the strong-Advaita reading that the *sthitaprajña* is identical
with the *jñānin* of realized non-duality: on the neuroscience, the
relevant capacities are continuous, not discontinuous. Either
Sankara is right that there is a discontinuous metaphysical
attainment the science cannot see (possible — neuroscience makes no
claim about metaphysical realities), or Easwaran is right that the
portrait is of a posture trainable in degrees (consistent with what
the science can see). The science does not adjudicate, but it sets
a floor: the trainable capacities the *sthitaprajña* displays are
real and reachable.

### § 6. Synthesis — the path forward

The chapter teaches three things. A position on each.

**First: on the imperishable self (2.11–2.30).** The metaphysical
claim: in a being, something the body's mortality does not reach.
Sankara reads this as the non-dual *Brahman* appearing as the
*atman*; Ramanuja as a real plurality of *jīvas* distinct from God
and from one another; Aurobindo as the foundation on which an
integral spiritual evolution proceeds; the Buddhist tradition
refuses the claim; Parfit refuses it in different terms.

The science I have cited speaks to trainable capacities of attention
and emotion regulation. It does not speak to whether there is,
beneath the constructed self neuroscience studies, a substantial
imperishable self of the kind 2.20 asserts. That is not a question
neuroscience can answer.

I commit to the structural claim that there is something in the
human person that grief, ambition, and loss do not finally reach.
The chapter offers a *posture toward selfhood* with consequences
for how grief, fear, ambition, and loss are held. The metaphysical
floor this book stands on is named in front matter — Ch 7's
qualified non-dualism: real world, real divine, real relation,
real-but-not-final small self. Ch 2's contribution sits underneath
that floor: the chapter's *practical* claim is agency without
self-grafting, which the rest of the manuscript will unfold into a
life.

From the rejected readings: from Sankara, the diagnosis that grief
presupposes a misidentification with the perishable, and that
sustained ethical practice requires a relocation of the seat of
identity. From Ramanuja, the insistence that the self is real and
not absorbed without remainder into the divine — that the
relationality between agent and acted-toward is not a fiction. From
the Buddhist counterpoint, the warning that any positive
metaphysical claim about the self must work hard not to become an
inflation. From Parfit, the question of what we are actually
attaching to when we attach to outcomes.

**Second: on 2.47.** What does the verse prohibit? I find
Aurobindo's framing most useful, with Sankara's *kartṛtva-abhimāna*
underneath, Gandhi's *anāsakti* as its ethical-political projection,
and Easwaran's contemplative-pastoral reading as its practical
projection. What 2.47 prohibits is not the desire for outcomes but
the *grafting of selfhood onto outcomes*. The agent can want a
thing to happen, work for it with full effort, prefer success to
failure — and still, structurally, not be the thing the result
decides.

Where I depart from Aurobindo: I do not need a "divine working" to
make the prescription cash out practically. The posture of
agency-without-self-grafting can be cultivated in a secular
register. Epictetus gives its procedural shape; Deci and Ryan give
empirical support — the *quality* of motivation, specifically its
non-outcome-dependence, predicts what the *sthitaprajña* portrait
describes (sustained engagement, equanimity, well-being).

I read 2.47 as a prohibition on the collapse of agency into result
— on letting the self that does the acting be identical with the
self that needs the outcome to be intact. Effort can be total.
Caring can be present. What is forbidden is the move by which a
person becomes the success or failure of the action rather than its
author. The verse opens a space in which one can act fully, want
what one wants, and not be destroyed when the result does not come.

This absorbs what each rejected reading gets right. From
strict-Advaita, the philosophical core: attachment presupposes a
metaphysically inflated doer-conceit. From strict-Vishishtadvaita,
the relational: attachment confuses ownership of the action with
proprietorship of its outcomes. From Gandhi, the political: action
under unjust conditions is sustainable only when the agent can bear
reversal without abandoning the action. From the Stoic, the
procedural: distinguishing what is up to us from what is not is a
discipline. From Deci and Ryan, the empirical: the structure of
motivation matters for performance and well-being.

The metaphysical commitments any one commentator makes to ground
the practical instruction are not what the instruction depends on.
The instruction is more durable than any single metaphysical frame.

**Third: on the *sthitaprajña* (2.54–2.72).** The portrait is of a
trained posture. Easwaran's pedagogical framing is most useful for
a contemporary reader: the steady-minded one is not an unreachable
ideal but a portrait achievable in degrees through training.
Aurobindo's reading — the *buddhi* (discriminating intelligence)
becoming the seat of will rather than the desire-storm of the lower
nature — sits underneath as mechanism. The science is consonant.

The strong-Advaita reading — that the *sthitaprajña* is identical
with the *jñānin* of realized non-duality — makes the portrait
inaccessible to anyone who has not had the realization. The verses
describe a posture; the posture is what the contemporary reader can
train; the metaphysics, if there is one, will follow as the posture
matures.

What I take from Sankara even so: the *sthitaprajña* is not just
well-regulated. They have undergone a real shift in the *locus* of
identification. The training is not just behavioral; it is, finally,
a recognition. Easwaran's pastoral framing risks understating this.
The sage in 2.69 is awake to a layer the ordinary person is not
awake to; that wakefulness is more than attentional discipline.
Both training and recognition are required.

**Condensed:** the chapter is a *phenomenology of equipoise* held
on the structural commitment that something in the human person is
not finally reached by what happens to it, with 2.47 as the central
practical instruction. The metaphysical commitments of the
classical commentators are not required for the practical teaching
to land; the teaching is durable across multiple metaphysical
interpretations, including some the classical commentators would
not accept.

A reader who finds the *atman* a live metaphysical claim can
inherit the chapter's full sweep. A reader who finds the *atman*
suspect can still inherit the practical teaching: the posture
toward selfhood that makes acting and grieving possible without
annihilation, the discipline of agency-without-self-grafting, the
trainable equipoise of the *sthitaprajña*. The chapter's power
depends on the practitioner taking up the posture and undertaking
the training.

This is a more cautious reading than any of the classical
commentators would offer. Sankara would say I have not gone far
enough — the practical teaching is preparatory; the real work is
the recognition of non-duality. Ramanuja would say I have evacuated
the relationality — without the personal divine, the prohibition on
attachment loses its theological motor. Aurobindo would say I have
stopped at the foundation rather than proceeding to the synthesis.
Each is a serious objection. The choice I am making is to write a
reading a contemporary reader unsure of the metaphysics can
inhabit, with the metaphysical readings preserved in the citations
for any reader who wants to go further.

The commentators were arguing about real things. I have not settled
those arguments; I have chosen the reading of the *passage* that
best meets this book's criteria. A reader who follows the citations
can disagree precisely.

### § 7. The chapter for a modern life

**The job interview at the company you wanted.** You prepared
for weeks. You walked in and did your work. You came out with the
sense that some answers were strong and others were not. For three
days you cannot stop checking your email. Your sleep is broken. You
catch yourself in the kitchen running a phrase through your head,
finding the better version you should have given.

2.47's instruction is not "stop wanting the job." Wanting the job
is fine — in SDT terms, a healthy expression of real interest in
the work. The instruction is not to let the self that prepared and
walked in and did the work be the same self that needs the offer
to be intact. The work was real; the work is over. What follows
belongs to a process you do not control — the committee's
deliberation, the budget, what the other candidates did, what
happened in the company yesterday you cannot know about. Your
contribution has been made. The fruit is not yours.

The chemical pull toward the email refresh is real. The Gita is
not telling you to feel something different; it describes a posture
from which the wait becomes bearable. The *sthitaprajña* portrait
is what equanimity in this exact wait looks like from the inside.

**The hard conversation with a parent.** Something has been
unsaid for a long time. You know what you want to say. You also
know the conversation could open something or close it, make the
problem better or worse, change the relationship or change nothing.
You sit with your phone in your hand for an hour without dialing.

What is yours is the saying. What is not yours is what your parent
does with what you say. Most of the resistance is not the
difficulty of saying the thing; it is the unwillingness to hold the
outcome at the distance the situation requires. The temptation is
to wait until you can be sure it will go well, which is to wait
forever.

The Gita's instruction is to dial. Not "dial and don't care." Dial
without demanding the outcome from the conversation. The outcome
will be what it is. Your task ends when you have said the thing.

**The wave of attention coming for your day.** You sit down to
work. Within minutes you are somewhere else — a thread of news, a
side argument, three messages, a video that played on its own. You
were going to write a paragraph; you have not.

The chain in 2.62–2.63 names this with terrible precision.
Easwaran's translation above traces it through, and what sits at
the end is "your life is utter waste" — strong language, applied
to a process that, conducted across years, produces years of life
that have not landed anywhere in particular.

The instruction is the slow training of attention the
*sthitaprajña* portrait describes. Notice the senses pulling.
Withdraw, deliberately. Return. Davidson and Ochsner have shown
this is a trainable capacity. The training is boring and slow, and
most people do not undertake it because it is boring and slow, and
the cost of not undertaking it is the slow erosion of the day.

**The work that matters to no one.** You are doing the report
no one will read, taking the meeting that produces no decision,
writing the email that will be archived unanswered. The work has
to be done; the work has no audience that cares.

2.47 is not "do it badly because nobody will notice." It is to do
it with the same effort you would bring to more visible work,
without the self-grafting that would make the lack of recognition
feel like a wound. SDT predicts: work done from intrinsic
motivation — because the work itself meets a standard you hold —
is sustainable across many such reports. Work done for visibility
or career return collapses under absence of those returns. The
work can be done well, indefinitely, when the agent is not
contingent on the work being seen.

**The loss of someone you loved.** Someone has died. You are
in the first weeks. The instruction "do not grieve" is a thing
Krishna says in 2.11 and a thing no functioning person can do at
will.

The Gita's instruction is finer than "do not grieve." On my chosen
reading: what you loved is not identical with the body that is
gone. What you loved had a real existence, and the existence —
whatever its precise metaphysical status — is not what funerals
can take. You can grieve the body's absence and the irrecoverable
particular relationship the body made possible, without collapsing
into the view that what was loved is utterly gone.

This is a thinner consolation than Sankara's or Ramanuja's. It is
also one that survives if the metaphysics turns out otherwise. What
it gives is permission to grieve without grieving as if everything
that mattered has been annihilated. The grief is real; the
annihilation is not the right shape of the loss.

Whether the consolation is finally true is a question I leave open.
Whether it is *available* as a posture in which to grieve is not in
question. It is. It has been used by hundreds of millions of human
beings for over two thousand years. It is still in use.

The discipline the chapter asks for — agency without self-grafting,
the slow training of attention, equipoise holding opposites without
collapsing — is operative in nearly every part of an adult life. It
becomes operational the first time a person, having walked out of
an interview or a difficult conversation, refuses to graft their
selfhood onto the result and returns to the work in front of them.
That is what *sthitaprajña* looks like under contemporary
conditions.

(See Ch. 3 on *karma yoga* as a way of life; Ch. 6 on the
meditative discipline that trains the *sthitaprajña* portrait; Ch.
13 on the metaphysics of field and knower; Ch. 18 on the final form
of *karma-phala-tyāga*.)
