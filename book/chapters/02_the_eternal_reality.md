## Chapter 2 — *Sāṃkhya Yoga*: The Eternal Reality

Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood onto the result.

That is the practical center of the Bhagavad Gita, condensed into one
instruction. The chapter that delivers it is the one the classical
commentators have called the Gita in miniature. Everything later is
an unfolding of what gets set down here. A claim about what in you is
imperishable. An ethic of action without self-grafting. A portrait of
a person whose composure no longer depends on how things turn out.
Three pieces, one teaching: there is something in you the result does
not reach, and from that something, you can act fully without becoming
whatever the action becomes.

The instruction is harder than it looks. It is not *stop caring about
outcomes*. It is not *act half-heartedly so the disappointment is
small*. It is not the Stoic shrug, exactly, and not the Buddhist
emptying, exactly. It is closer to this: there is a self that does
the work, and there is a self that needs the work to have gone well.
The Gita is asking you to tell them apart. The first one is yours to
train. The second one is what destroys you when results do not come.

This chapter is about how to do the telling-apart, why the telling-
apart is possible at all, and what your life looks like once you have
practiced it for a while.

### The collapse Krishna is answering

The teaching begins from collapse. Arjuna has dropped his bow.
The previous chapter ended with him sitting between the armies,
refusing the work he came for. He has reasoned through the
wreckage — he would rather be killed unarmed than kill his
teachers and cousins, the obvious right action breaks every
loyalty he has — and concluded with the kind of clarity that is
also collapse.

Krishna's first move is uncomfortable. He calls the despair
*anārya-juṣṭam*, unworthy, and asks where this weakness has come
from at this hour. There is contempt in the line. He is not yet
teaching; he is rebuking. Only when Arjuna pushes back, exhausts his
arguments, and says *I am your student; teach me*, does the teaching
begin.

What Krishna says first is not what Arjuna asked for. Arjuna asked
*what to do*. Krishna gives him a metaphysics. Then a redefinition
of action. Then a portrait of a person. The order to fight or to
leave is not in this chapter. The chapter is foundation.

The metaphysics is laid down in twenty verses. Your embodied self
does not begin at birth or end at death; it passes from body to body
the way you pass from childhood through youth into age. Unborn,
eternal, unchanging. Weapons cannot cleave it. Fire cannot burn it.
Water cannot wet it. Wind cannot dry it. The wise grieve neither
for the living nor for the dead. What dies is the body; the body's
death is not the kind of loss that warrants the collapse Arjuna is
in. *Atman* — the imperishable self — is the term. There is in you,
the chapter is claiming, something the body's mortality does not
reach.

Then, at 2.39, the chapter pivots. Krishna names the turn: he has
been teaching from the standpoint of *sāṃkhya*, analytic knowledge;
he now turns to *yoga*, disciplined practice. Eight verses later he
gives you the line for which the Gita is most known.

The Sanskrit is *karmaṇy evādhikāras te mā phaleṣu kadācana / mā
karma-phala-hetur bhūr mā te saṅgo 'stv akarmaṇi*. Easwaran's
rendering of 2.47–2.48: "You have the right to work, but never to
the fruit of the work. You should never engage in action for the
sake of reward, nor should you long for inaction. Perform work in
this world, Arjuna, as a man established within himself — without
selfish attachments, and alike in success and defeat. For yoga is
perfect evenness of mind."

Two verses, four instructions. Your authority extends to the work,
not its result. Do not act *for* the result. Do not retreat into
inaction. Act, established in yoga, with success and failure held
evenly. *Yogaḥ samatvam ucyate* — yoga is equipoise. Two verses
later: *yogaḥ karmasu kauśalam* — yoga is skill in action. The two
definitions are the same teaching from inside and outside. The
internal posture is equipoise; the external expression is skill.

The chapter then asks the question that opens its final movement.
Arjuna wants to know what such a person looks like. How does one of
steady wisdom — the *sthitaprajña*, the steady-minded one — speak,
sit, walk? Krishna spends nineteen verses answering. The portrait is
concrete and strange: the steady-minded one is awake when others
sleep and asleep when others think they are awake. Their senses,
like a tortoise's limbs, withdraw deliberately. They have watched
the chain by which dwelling on objects becomes attachment, attachment
becomes desire, desire frustrated becomes anger, anger clouds
judgment, judgment clouded loses memory of what mattered, and at the
bottom of the chain the person is destroyed. They have stepped off
the chain. Desires enter such a person as rivers enter a sea brimful
and unmoving — the sea is not disturbed.

The chapter ends at 2.72 by naming the state: *brāhmī sthiti*, the
state of *Brahman* — the threshold beyond which the kind of
suffering Arjuna is in does not have purchase.

That is what Chapter 2 puts on the table. Now your work of reading
it begins.

### What 2.47 prohibits

The most-translated line in the Gita has been read in incompatible
ways for over a thousand years. The disagreement is productive; it
is also where the chapter's practical force lives or dies.

Gandhi reads the battlefield allegorically — your inner moral life
and ordinary social action, not only literal war — and centers 2.47
as the Gita's ethical core. His formulation is *anāsakti*,
non-attachment. The right to work is real, full, unconditioned; the
right to the fruits is unreal — it never existed. Acting for the
fruits confuses what you control (the action) with what you do not
(the result). Withdrawing from action is the opposite error. The
middle path — act fully, do not own the fruit — is the verse.

Gandhi's stake here was political as much as spiritual. *Satyagraha*
depends on this reading. If victory had been the criterion of right
action, the strategy would have collapsed at every reversal. The
Gita gives nonviolent action its inner durability — the capacity to
keep acting under conditions in which the outcome cannot be made
the measure.

Sankara, reading through Advaita, locates the prohibition deeper, in
what he calls *kartṛtva-abhimāna* — the conceit *I am the doer*. The
verse does not prohibit acting; it prohibits the agent's
identification as doer in the way that makes you liable for fruits.
The action is performed; the doer-conceit is dropped. What attaches
you to the result, on this reading, is not the fact of action but
the metaphysical mistake of taking yourself to be the substantial
agent of it.

Ramanuja and Aurobindo extend the verse along their own lines —
Ramanuja making action *worshipful* (the *jīva* as the divine's
instrument; the fruits never yours to own), Aurobindo redirecting
desire rather than abolishing it (the action is no longer your
private project but the divine working through you). Different
metaphysical frames, the same practical floor. Sankara and Ramanuja
disagreed on nearly every metaphysical question one can put to the
Vedanta. They agreed on this. The instruction is more durable than
any single frame.

What the instruction prohibits, on the reading I am committing to,
is the *collapse of agency into result* — the move by which the self
that does the acting becomes identical with the self that needs the
outcome to be intact. The work was real; the work is done. What
follows belongs to a process you do not control. Your contribution
has been made. The fruit is not yours.

You can want a thing to happen, work for it with full effort, prefer
success to failure — and still, structurally, not be the thing the
result decides.

### The self the result does not reach

The structural commitment underneath 2.47 is the chapter's earlier
claim: there is something in you that grief, ambition, and loss do
not finally reach.

I do not pretend to settle the metaphysics here. The classical
commentators argue about *what* the *atman* is — non-dual *Brahman*
appearing as individuals (Sankara), a real plurality of *jīvas* in
eternal relation with a personal divine (Ramanuja), strictly
distinct (Madhva), foundation for an integral spiritual evolution
(Aurobindo). Modern philosophy, on the whole, refuses the premise.
Parfit has argued that personal identity is constituted by relations
of psychological continuity — chains of overlapping memories,
intentions, character — with no separately existing self that *has*
those relations. The early Buddhists, the Gita's nearest
philosophical opponents, worked with *anattā*, not-self, as the
precise denial that any of the five aggregates is the substantial
abiding self the Gita's *atman* names.

The position I commit to, named in front matter and given fuller
defense in Chapter 7, is this: there is something in you that grief,
ambition, and loss do not finally reach. The world is real. The
relation between you and what you act in is real. The small self —
your daily, particular, named self — is real, but not final. Real
enough that loss reaches into it; not so final that identification
with it is the wisest stance.

What this gives Chapter 2 is the structural ground for 2.47. If the
self that does the acting is finally identical with the self the
acting affects, the result reaches all the way down — and there is
no posture from which acting-without-self-grafting is available. If
the self is not so reached, the instruction becomes possible. There
is a place from which the work can be done that the work's outcome
does not penetrate. That place is what *atman* names, on this
reading: not a thing you can point to in a brain scan, but a
structural feature of the self the chapter is asking you to commit
to.

If you find this metaphysics live, you can inherit the chapter's
full sweep. If you find it suspect — if you read with Parfit or with
the Pali tradition — you can still inherit much of the practical
teaching. The posture of acting without self-grafting can be
cultivated in a non-substantialist frame; the early Buddhists
cultivated it for centuries on exactly such a frame.

### The Stoic parallel and where it ends

The verse has been compared to Epictetus often enough that the
comparison risks becoming mechanical. Worth doing once, well.

Epictetus's *prohairesis* — the faculty of choice, the part of you
that gives or withholds assent to impressions — is what is up to
you. Body, property, reputation, what other people do: not up to
you. The wise person does not invest themselves in what is not up
to them.

The parallel is real. Your authority extends over your own
activity, not over what follows from it. If you treat outcomes as
something you can demand of reality, you have misunderstood the
basic shape of the human situation.

The difference sharpens the Gita. Epictetus's frame is cognitive.
By reordering judgments, giving assent only to true impressions,
you become free of disturbance. The Gita's frame, in 2.55–2.72, is
*constitutive*. The *sthitaprajña* has not just gotten their
judgments right; they have undergone a reorganization of the place
from which judgments are made. The seat of identity has shifted.
The Stoic gives you a technique. The Gita gives you an orientation.

### What the science can show

The empirical literature on motivation puts pressure on the easiest
misreading of 2.47.

The misreading: the Gita teaches indifference to outcomes. Deci and
Ryan's self-determination theory distinguishes intrinsic motivation
— engaging in an activity because the activity itself is meaningful
— from extrinsic motivation, engaging for the sake of an outcome
external to the activity. The classic finding: introducing an
external reward to an intrinsically motivated activity can *reduce*
intrinsic motivation. People who used to draw because they liked
drawing draw less when they are paid for it and the payment stops.

The frame is not *do not care about results*. It is that the
*quality* of caring — rooted in the activity rather than yoked to
external reward — predicts sustained engagement.

This sharpens 2.47. Caring that lasts is caring rooted in the work
itself, not in what the work generates. Different from indifference.
Closer to what the *sthitaprajña* describes from the inside: a
person whose work is sustained because the work meets a standard
they hold, not because the work generates returns they need.

### The interview and the diagnosis

Concrete, then. Two situations in which the chapter is not abstract.

**The interview.** You prepared for weeks. You walked in and did
the work. You came out with the sense that some answers were strong
and others were not. For three days you cannot stop checking your
email. Your sleep is broken. You catch yourself in the kitchen
running a phrase through your head, finding the better version you
should have given.

What 2.47 instructs is not *stop wanting the job*. Wanting the job
is fine. The instruction is not to let the self that prepared and
walked in and did the work be the same self that needs the offer to
be intact. The work was real. The work is over. What follows belongs
to a process you do not control — the committee's deliberation, the
budget, what the other candidates did, what happened in the company
yesterday you cannot know about. Your contribution has been made.
The fruit is not yours.

The pull toward the email refresh is real. The Gita is not telling
you to feel something different. It is describing a posture from
which the wait becomes bearable.

**The medical decision under uncertainty.** A parent's diagnosis
lands. The decision is yours, or yours together with siblings, and
the information is incomplete. Two specialists disagree. The
treatment your mother's friend swore by has weak evidence; the one
the oncologist recommends has stronger evidence and worse side
effects. You are choosing in the dark, and what you choose will be
the thing the next year is built on.

The Gita does not say *the right answer is available if you look
hard enough*. It does not promise that effort will produce
certainty. What it says is that the work — the gathering of
information, the asking of questions, the weighing — is what is
yours. What is not yours is whether the chosen treatment will work,
whether the body will tolerate it, whether the disease will respond
the way the trials suggested. You will make the call with what you
have. The result will not be a referendum on your love. The result
will be what bodies do when treated, which is partly available to
medical knowledge and partly not.

Self-grafting on this kind of decision is what destroys the
caregiver. The decision becomes the thing your love is being
measured against. The result becomes the test of whether you chose
right. A year of caregiving becomes an interrogation of a choice
made in incomplete information. The Gita's instruction is finer. You
acted with the love and the information you had. The acting was
real and full. The result is not the verdict on the acting.

The same posture answers the longer cases. The hard conversation
you have been avoiding for months: yours is the saying; theirs is
what they do with it. The year-long project waiting on a market or
a funder: *karma yoga*, the yoga of action which the next chapter
unfolds, names the posture from which long work becomes survivable
— effort total, attachment to outcome forbidden. The self that
built the thing is not the self that needs the thing to land.

This is what *sthitaprajña* looks like under contemporary
conditions. Not the saint in the cave. You, walking out of a
difficult conversation and refusing to graft your selfhood onto the
result. You, closing the laptop after a long project shipped without
needing the world to confirm the labor. You, the caregiver, choosing
a treatment with the information available and not requiring the
result to vindicate the love.

### What grief does, and what it does not

The chapter's hardest claim is also its first. Krishna in 2.11: the
wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead.

This is offensive on a literal reading. Grief is the human animal's
response to the loss of something it loved, and the response is not
optional. *Do not grieve*, said to a person whose mother died last
week, is cruelty.

The instruction is finer. What you loved is not identical with the
body that is gone. What you loved had a real existence, and that
existence — whatever its precise metaphysical status — is not what
funerals can take. You can grieve the body's absence and the
irrecoverable particular relationship the body made possible,
without collapsing into the view that what was loved is utterly
gone.

This is a thinner consolation than Sankara's or Ramanuja's, but it
survives if their metaphysics turn out otherwise than they thought.
It gives you permission to grieve without grieving as if everything
that mattered has been annihilated.

The grief is real. The annihilation is not the right shape of the
loss.

### Where the chapter leaves you

The chapter is foundation. Krishna does not yet tell Arjuna to
fight. He puts on the table the three things any later instruction
will need to rest on: the imperishable self, the ethic of action
without self-grafting, the portrait of the integrated practitioner.

What you carry out of it depends on whether you do the work the
chapter is asking for. The work is not a single decision. It is a
discipline practiced across years. You will walk out of an
interview, a difficult conversation, a hospital corridor, a project
that ended hollow, and you will catch yourself reaching for the self
that needs the outcome to be intact. You will recognize the reach.
You will subtract it. The work was real. The fruit is not yours.

Do this often enough, and you will not become the saint in the
cave. You will become someone whose composure has roots the day
cannot reach. You will be able to act fully in a world whose results
are not in your gift, and to grieve fully when what you love is
taken, without either action or grief swallowing the place inside
you from which both are done.

Pick up the work. Do not pick up the result.
