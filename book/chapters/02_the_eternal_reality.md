## Chapter 2 — *Sāṃkhya Yoga*: The Eternal Reality

Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood onto the result.

That is the practical center of the Bhagavad Gita, condensed into one
instruction. The chapter that delivers it is the one the classical
commentators have called "the Gita in miniature" — and the reason for
the name is that everything later is an unfolding of what is set out
here. A metaphysics of the imperishable. An ethic of action without
self-grafting. A portrait of a person whose composure no longer
depends on the way things turn out. Three pieces, one teaching: there
is something in you that the result does not reach, and from that
something, you can act fully without becoming whatever the action
becomes.

The instruction is harder than it looks. It is not "stop caring about
outcomes." It is not "act half-heartedly so the disappointment is
small." It is not the Stoic shrug, exactly, and not the Buddhist
emptying, exactly. It is closer to this: there is a self that does
the work, and there is a self that needs the work to have gone well.
The Gita is asking you to tell them apart. The first one is yours to
train. The second one is what destroys you when results don't come.

This chapter is about how to do the telling-apart, why the telling-
apart is possible at all, and what life looks like once it has been
practiced for a while.

### The collapse Krishna is answering

Arjuna has dropped his bow. The previous chapter ended with him
sitting down between the armies, refusing the work he came for. He
has been reasoning through the wreckage — he would rather be killed
unarmed than kill his teachers and cousins, the obvious right action
breaks every loyalty he has — and he has concluded with the kind of
clarity that is also collapse.

Krishna's first move is uncomfortable. He calls the despair
*anārya-juṣṭam*, "unworthy," and asks Arjuna where this weakness has
come from at this hour. There is contempt in the line. He is not
yet teaching; he is rebuking. Only when Arjuna pushes back, exhausts
his arguments, and says — in the line that opens the rest of the
text — *I am your student; teach me*, does the teaching begin.

What Krishna says first is not what Arjuna asked for. Arjuna asked
*what to do.* Krishna gives him a metaphysics. Then he gives him a
redefinition of action. Then he gives him a portrait of a person.
The order to fight or to leave is not in this chapter. The chapter
is foundation.

The metaphysics is laid down in twenty verses (2.11–2.30). The
embodied self does not begin at birth or end at death; it passes
from body to body the way a person passes from childhood through
youth into age. Unborn, eternal, unchanging. Weapons cannot cleave
it. Fire cannot burn it. Water cannot wet it. Wind cannot dry it.
The wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead. What dies
is the body; the body's death is not the kind of loss that warrants
the collapse Arjuna is in.

Mascaró renders 2.20 in the cadence that has carried the verse into
twentieth-century anthologies: "He is never born, and he never dies.
He is in Eternity: he is for evermore." The Sanskrit underneath is
nearly identical to *Kaṭha Upaniṣad* I.2.18 — Radhakrishnan prints
the parallel passages side by side. The Gita is restating an
inheritance, not inventing one. There is, in each being, something
the body's mortality does not reach.

Then, at 2.39, the chapter pivots. Krishna names the turn: he has
been teaching from the standpoint of *sāṃkhya*, analytic knowledge;
he now turns to *yoga*, disciplined practice. And it is here, eight
verses later, that he gives the line for which the Gita is most
known.

The Sanskrit is *karmaṇy evādhikāras te mā phaleṣu kadācana / mā
karma-phala-hetur bhūr mā te saṅgo 'stv akarmaṇi*. Easwaran's
rendering of 2.47–2.48: "You have the right to work, but never to
the fruit of the work. You should never engage in action for the
sake of reward, nor should you long for inaction. Perform work in
this world, Arjuna, as a man established within himself — without
selfish attachments, and alike in success and defeat. For yoga is
perfect evenness of mind."

Two verses, four instructions. Your authority extends to the work,
not to its result. Do not act *for* the result. Do not retreat
into inaction either. Act, established in yoga, with success and
failure held evenly. *Yogaḥ samatvam ucyate* — yoga is equipoise.
Two verses later, the second formula: *yogaḥ karmasu kauśalam* —
yoga is skill in action. The two definitions are the same teaching
from inside and outside. The internal posture is equipoise; the
external expression is skill.

The chapter then asks the question that opens its final movement.
Arjuna wants to know what such a person looks like. How does one of
steady wisdom — the *sthitaprajña* — speak, sit, walk? Krishna
spends nineteen verses answering. The portrait is concrete and
strange: the steady-minded one is awake when others sleep and
asleep when others think they are awake. Their senses, like a
tortoise's limbs, withdraw deliberately. They have watched the chain
by which dwelling on objects becomes attachment, attachment becomes
desire, desire frustrated becomes anger, anger clouds judgment,
judgment clouded loses memory of what mattered, and at the bottom
of the chain the person is destroyed. They have stepped off the
chain. Desires enter such a person as rivers enter a sea brimful and
unmoving — the sea is not disturbed.

The chapter ends at 2.72 by naming the state: *brāhmī sthiti*, the
state of *Brahman* — the threshold beyond which the kind of
suffering Arjuna is in does not have purchase.

That is what Chapter 2 puts on the table. Now the work of reading
it begins.

### What 2.47 prohibits

The most-translated line in the Gita has been read in incompatible
ways for over a thousand years. The disagreement is productive; it
is also where the chapter's practical force lives or dies.

Sankara, reading through Advaita, locates the prohibition in what
he calls *kartṛtva-abhimāna* — the conceit "I am the doer." The
verse does not prohibit acting; it prohibits the agent's
*identification* as doer in the way that makes the agent liable for
fruits. The action is performed; the doer-conceit is dropped. On
this reading, what attaches the agent to the result is not the fact
of action but the metaphysical mistake of taking oneself to be the
substantial agent of it.

Ramanuja reads differently. The *jīva* — the individual self — is
real, distinct from God, distinct from other selves, but is the
divine's instrument. Action is *worshipful* when it recognizes this.
The fruits belong to the Lord because the agent never owned them in
the first place; the prohibition on attachment is the prohibition on
*self-aggrandizement*, the move by which an instrument confuses
itself with the artisan.

Gandhi takes a third path. He reads the battlefield allegorically —
the inner moral life and ordinary social action, not (or not only)
literal war — and centers 2.47 as the Gita's ethical core. His
formulation is *anāsakti*, non-attachment. The right to work is
real, full, unconditioned; the right to the fruits is unreal — it
never existed. Acting for the fruits confuses what one controls
(the action) with what one does not (the result). Withdrawing from
action is the opposite error. The middle path — act fully, do not
own the fruit — is what the verse teaches.

Gandhi's stake here is political as much as spiritual. *Satyagraha*
depends on this reading. If victory were the criterion of right
action, the strategy would have collapsed at every reversal. The
Gita gives nonviolent action its inner durability — the capacity to
keep acting under conditions in which the outcome cannot be made
the measure.

Aurobindo, reading through Integral Yoga, takes the reading further.
The verse does not prohibit *desiring* outcomes; it redirects the
desire. The agent does not graft selfhood onto the result because
the action is no longer the agent's private project — it has become
the divine working using the agent as instrument. The fruits belong
to that working. The agent's task is to remain the instrument and
not collapse back into the proprietary self.

Four readings, one verse. They disagree about the metaphysics — Is
the agent finally non-dual? Distinct? An instrument of a personal
divine? An instrument of an evolving spiritual working? — and they
converge on the practice. Act. Do not graft selfhood onto the
result.

The convergence is the chapter's strongest evidence. Sankara and
Ramanuja agreed about almost nothing else; they disagreed about
nearly every metaphysical question one can put to the Vedanta. They
agreed on this. The instruction is more durable than any single
metaphysical frame.

What the instruction prohibits, on the reading I am committing to,
is the *collapse of agency into result* — the move by which the
self that does the acting becomes identical with the self that
needs the outcome to be intact. The work was real; the work is
done. What follows belongs to a process you do not control. Your
contribution has been made. The fruit is not yours.

The agent can want a thing to happen, work for it with full effort,
prefer success to failure — and still, structurally, not be the
thing the result decides.

### The self that doesn't get reached

The structural commitment underneath 2.47 is the claim of
2.11–2.30: there is something in the human person that grief,
ambition, and loss do not finally reach.

This is where the chapter's metaphysics gets contested most
sharply. The classical commentators argue about *what* the *atman*
is. Sankara reads it as the non-dual *Brahman* appearing as
individual selves. Ramanuja reads it as a real plurality of *jīvas*
in eternal relation to a personal divine. Madhva reads it as
strictly distinct from the divine, eternally so. Aurobindo reads
the imperishable as the foundation on which an integral spiritual
evolution proceeds.

Modern philosophy, on the whole, refuses the premise. Derek Parfit,
in *Reasons and Persons* (1984), argues that personal identity is
not a "further fact" beyond the physical and psychological facts
about a person. It is constituted by relations of psychological
continuity and connectedness — chains of overlapping memories,
intentions, beliefs, character — and there is no separately
existing self that *has* these relations. Parfit's most-cited
conclusion is that personal identity, in the strict sense, is not
what matters in survival; what matters is continuity and
connectedness with any cause. Once those are present, the further
question of whether the resulting person "is the same person" is
empty.

The Pali Buddhist tradition's *anattā* — not-self — is older than
the classical Vedantic commentators and was the Gita's nearest
philosophical opponent in its own century. Bhikkhu Bodhi, the
contemporary American Theravada monk and translator, is careful to
note that *anattā* is not a denial of all selfhood: the Buddha does
not say "there is no person." It is the precise denial that any of
the five aggregates — form, feeling, perception, formations,
consciousness — singly or together, is the substantial abiding self
the non-Buddhist traditions of the time were positing. The Gita's
*atman* is exactly that. There is the flow of aggregates; there is
no *atman* underneath.

This book does not pretend it has settled the metaphysics. It has
read across the traditions, weighed the readings, and committed to
a position. The position is this: there is something in the human
person that grief, ambition, and loss do not finally reach. The
world is real. The relation between the self and what it acts in
is real. The small self is real, but not final — real enough that
loss reaches into it, not so final that identification with it is
the wisest stance.

This is qualified non-dualism as the metaphysical floor — the
position the book stands on, named in front matter, given fuller
defense in Chapter 7. It is closer to Ramanuja than to Sankara on
the world's reality and the relationality between self and divine.
It is closer to Sankara than to Madhva on how strictly the small
self should be held distinct.

What it gives Chapter 2 is the structural ground for 2.47. If the
self that does the acting is finally identical with the self that
the acting affects, then the result reaches all the way down — and
there is no posture from which acting-without-self-grafting is
available. If the self is not so reached, the instruction becomes
possible. There is a place from which the work can be done that the
work's outcome does not penetrate. That place is what *atman*
names, on this reading: not a thing you can point to in a brain
scan, but a structural feature of the self the chapter is asking
you to commit to.

A reader who finds this metaphysics live can inherit the chapter's
full sweep. A reader who finds it suspect — who reads with Parfit
or with Bodhi — can still inherit much of the practical teaching.
The posture of acting without self-grafting can be cultivated in a
non-substantialist frame; the early Buddhists cultivated it for
centuries on exactly such a frame. What is harder to cultivate
without the metaphysics is the *consolation* of 2.20 — the answer
to grief that says what was loved is not utterly gone. The Buddhist
tradition offers a different consolation (grasping is what made the
loss this painful), not the same one.

I will return to grief at the end. For now: the metaphysics is here
because the practice depends on it, and the metaphysics is the
metaphysics this book is committed to. Naming it once is sufficient.
The chapters that follow can lean on it.

### The Stoic parallel and where it ends

The verse 2.47 has been compared to Epictetus often enough that the
comparison risks becoming mechanical. It is worth doing once, well.

The opening of Epictetus's *Enchiridion*, in Nicholas P. White's
1983 translation: "Some things are up to us and some are not up to
us. Up to us are opinion, motivation, desire, aversion, and, in a
word, whatever is our own doing; not up to us are our body, our
property, reputation, office, and, in a word, whatever is not our
own doing." The Stoic technical term is *prohairesis* — the
faculty of choice, the part of us that gives or withholds assent
to impressions. *Prohairesis* is what is up to us. Everything else
— health, wealth, reputation, what other people do — is not up to
us, and the wise person does not invest themselves in what is not
up to them.

The parallel with 2.47 is real. The agent's authority extends over
their own activity, not over what follows from it in the world. The
agent who treats outcomes as something they can demand of reality
has misunderstood the basic shape of the human situation.

The differences sharpen the Gita. Epictetus is more procedural —
the Stoic discipline is the technique of separating impressions
that arise (not up to us) from the assent we give them (up to us).
The Gita in 2.47 is more ontological. It is not primarily teaching
a technique for managing impressions; it is naming what is yours
and what is not, leaving the procedural detail to the
*sthitaprajña* portrait. Epictetus is more individualist; the
Stoic's project is the well-formed inner life, with social good
downstream. The Gita is more relational — the inner posture matters
because action is inescapable and consequential for others, and
the Gita's prescription is delivered to a man on a battlefield, not
to a philosopher in a study.

The deepest difference: Epictetus's frame is *cognitive*. By
reordering judgments, giving assent only to true impressions, the
wise person becomes free of disturbance. The Gita's frame, in
2.55–2.72, is *constitutive*. The *sthitaprajña* has not just
gotten their judgments right; they have undergone a reorganization
of the place from which judgments are made. The seat of identity
has shifted.

A reader with only Epictetus has the technique. A reader with only
the Gita has the orientation. Read together, they sharpen each
other — and both, working at the level of practice, converge with
something the chapter's portrait describes from inside.

### What the science can show

The empirical literature on motivation and emotion regulation puts
some pressure on a misreading of 2.47, and some support under a
better one.

The misreading: "the Gita teaches indifference to outcomes." Self-
determination theory, developed by Edward L. Deci and Richard M.
Ryan since the 1970s, distinguishes intrinsic motivation —
engaging in an activity because the activity itself is interesting
or meaningful — from extrinsic motivation, engaging for the sake of
an outcome external to the activity. The classic finding is the
overjustification effect (Lepper, Greene, and Nisbett, 1973):
introducing an external reward to an intrinsically motivated
activity can *reduce* intrinsic motivation. People who used to draw
because they liked drawing draw less when they are paid for it and
the payment stops.

The bound is important. SDT does not claim outcomes are
unimportant. The frame is not "don't care about results"; it is
that the *quality of motivation* — the degree to which motivation
is internal to the activity rather than yoked to external reward —
predicts sustained engagement and well-being.

This sharpens 2.47 considerably. The structure of caring that
predicts sustained engagement is caring rooted in the activity
rather than yoked to external reward. Different from indifference.
Closer, in fact, to what the *sthitaprajña* portrait describes from
the inside: a person whose work is sustained because the work
itself meets a standard the person holds, not because the work is
generating returns the person needs.

A second strand. Richard J. Davidson's work at Wisconsin has
documented what trained contemplative practice does to "affective
style" — characteristic individual differences in emotional
response patterns. Long-term practitioners show altered baseline
activity in regions associated with self-referential processing
(the default-mode network) and in the insula and anterior cingulate
cortex. A parallel line, Kevin Ochsner and James Gross on cognitive
reappraisal, shows that re-describing the meaning of an
affect-eliciting stimulus engages prefrontal regions and modulates
amygdala response.

What this combined literature supports: equanimity in the face of
difficult affect is not a fixed temperament. It is, in part, a
trainable capacity, with identifiable neural mechanisms. The
classical commentators who saw the *sthitaprajña* as a rare and
high attainment were partly right — full training is rare. They
may have underestimated the degree to which *partial* training is
available to ordinary people through ordinary discipline.

The science does not vindicate the metaphysics. It shows the
chapter is talking about something the human animal actually does.
That is the role science plays in this book — constraint, not
proof. What it constrains here is the older interpretive move that
made the *sthitaprajña* an unreachable saint. The portrait is of a
trained posture, the posture is trainable in degrees, and the
degrees are the lived form of the chapter for a contemporary
reader.

### The interview, the conversation, the diagnosis

Concrete, then. Four situations in which the chapter is not
abstract.

The job interview at the company you wanted. You prepared for
weeks. You walked in and did the work. You came out with the sense
that some answers were strong and others were not. For three days
you cannot stop checking your email. Your sleep is broken. You
catch yourself in the kitchen running a phrase through your head,
finding the better version you should have given.

What 2.47 instructs is not "stop wanting the job." Wanting the job
is fine — in SDT terms, a healthy expression of real interest in
the work, a sign of intrinsic motivation that the offer would
extend rather than create. The instruction is not to let the self
that prepared and walked in and did the work be the same self that
needs the offer to be intact. The work was real; the work is over.
What follows belongs to a process you do not control — the
committee's deliberation, the budget, what the other candidates
did, what happened in the company yesterday you cannot know about.
Your contribution has been made. The fruit is not yours.

The pull toward the email refresh is real. The Gita is not telling
you to feel something different; it is describing a posture from
which the wait becomes bearable. The *sthitaprajña* portrait is
what equanimity in this exact wait looks like from the inside.

The hard conversation with a parent. Something has been unsaid for
a long time. You know what you want to say. You also know the
conversation could open something or close it, make the problem
better or worse, change the relationship or change nothing. You sit
with your phone in your hand for an hour without dialing.

What is yours is the saying. What is not yours is what your parent
does with what you say. Most of the resistance is not the
difficulty of saying the thing; it is the unwillingness to hold the
outcome at the distance the situation requires. The temptation is
to wait until you can be sure it will go well, which is to wait
forever.

The Gita's instruction is to dial. Not "dial and don't care." Dial
without demanding the outcome from the conversation. The outcome
will be what it is. Your task ends when you have said the thing.

The medical decision under uncertainty. A parent's diagnosis lands.
The decision is yours, or yours together with siblings, and the
information is incomplete. Two specialists disagree. The treatment
your mother's friend swore by has weak evidence; the one the
oncologist recommends has stronger evidence and worse side effects.
You are choosing in the dark, and what you choose will be the thing
the next year is built on.

The Gita does not say *the right answer is available if you look
hard enough.* It does not promise that effort will produce
certainty. What it says is that the work — the gathering of
information, the asking of questions, the weighing — is what is
yours. What is not yours is whether the chosen treatment will work,
whether the body will tolerate it, whether the disease will respond
the way the trials suggested. You will make the call with what you
have. The result will not be a referendum on your love. The result
will be what bodies do when treated, which is partly available to
medical knowledge and partly not.

Self-grafting on this kind of decision is what destroys the
caregiver. The decision becomes the thing your love is being
measured against, the result becomes the test of whether you chose
right, and a year of caregiving becomes an interrogation of a
choice made in incomplete information. The Gita's instruction is
finer. You acted with the love and the information you had. The
acting was real and full. The result is not the verdict on the
acting.

The project whose outcome is not in your hands. You have been
building something for a year — a piece of writing, a software
product, a research direction, a small business. The work is
nearly done. Whether it will land depends on a market, an audience,
a funder, a turn of events you cannot predict. You wake up at 3 a.m.
running scenarios in which it goes badly.

The work has been real. The fruit is not yours. *Karma yoga* —
which Krishna is announcing in this chapter and which the next
chapter unfolds — names exactly the posture from which long
projects become survivable. Effort total. Attachment to outcome
forbidden. The self that built the thing is not the self that needs
the thing to land.

This does not make the 3 a.m. anxiety stop. It locates it. The
anxiety is a habit of self-grafting that the work itself did not
ask of you. The work asked for the work. The grafting was added by
a self that confused authorship with success. The chapter's
discipline is to do that subtraction, repeatedly, until the
subtraction becomes easier than the grafting.

This is what *sthitaprajña* looks like under contemporary
conditions. Not the saint in the cave. The person who walks out of
a difficult conversation and refuses to graft their selfhood onto
the result. The person who closes the laptop after a long project
shipped and does not need the world to confirm the labor. The
caregiver who chose a treatment with the information available and
does not require the result to vindicate the love.

### What grief does, and what it doesn't

The chapter's hardest claim is also its first. Krishna in 2.11:
the wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead.

This is offensive on a literal reading. Grief is the human animal's
response to the loss of something it loved, and the response is not
optional. To say "do not grieve" to a person whose mother has died
last week is to say something cruel that no functioning person can
do at will.

The chapter's instruction is finer than that. On my reading: what
you loved is not identical with the body that is gone. What you
loved had a real existence, and that existence — whatever its
precise metaphysical status — is not what funerals can take. You
can grieve the body's absence and the irrecoverable particular
relationship the body made possible, without collapsing into the
view that what was loved is utterly gone.

This is a thinner consolation than Sankara's promise that the
*atman* is non-dual *Brahman*, or Ramanuja's promise that the
*jīva* persists in eternal relation with the Lord. It is also one
that survives if those metaphysics turn out otherwise than their
authors thought. What it gives is permission to grieve without
grieving as if everything that mattered has been annihilated.

The grief is real. The annihilation is not the right shape of the
loss.

Whether the consolation is finally true is a question this chapter
does not settle. Whether it is *available* as a posture in which to
grieve is not in question. It has been used by hundreds of millions
of human beings for over two thousand years. It is still in use.

### Where the chapter leaves you

The chapter is foundation, not instruction. Krishna does not yet
tell Arjuna to fight. He puts on the table the three things any
later instruction will need to rest on: the imperishable self, the
ethic of action without self-grafting, the portrait of the
integrated practitioner.

What you carry out of it depends on whether you do the work the
chapter is asking for. The work is not a single decision. It is a
discipline practiced across years. You will walk out of an
interview, a difficult conversation, a hospital corridor, a project
that ended hollow, and you will catch yourself reaching for the
self that needs the outcome to be intact. You will recognize the
reach. You will subtract it. The work was real. The fruit is not
yours.

Do this often enough, and you will not become the saint in the
cave. You will become someone whose composure has roots the day
cannot reach. You will be able to act fully in a world whose
results are not in your gift, and to grieve fully when what you
love is taken, without either action or grief swallowing the place
inside you from which both are done.

That place is what *atman* names. Whether it is *Brahman*, or
*jīva*, or aggregates moving in the appearance of a self, the
classical commentators will keep arguing for as long as the human
mind keeps trying to think these things through. The instruction
does not wait for the argument to settle.

Pick up the work. Do not pick up the result.
