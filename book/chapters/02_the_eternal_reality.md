## Chapter 2 — Krishna's First Teaching

Arjuna. Look at me.

You grieve for those who do not need your grief. You have learned to speak the language of wisdom — duty, restraint, consequence — and you are using it tonight to dress up something simpler. You are afraid. You are tired. You have looked at the people in front of you and what they ask of you, and you have decided you would rather be the kind of person who refuses than the kind of person who acts. The wise do not grieve for the living. They do not grieve for the dead. You are doing both, and calling it conscience.

I am not going to tell you the situation is small. It is not small. The faces across from you are real faces. What you do tonight will mark the rest of your life. I am going to tell you something else, which is harder.

There has never been a time when you were not. There has never been a time when I was not, or when these warriors in front of you were not. There will not come a time when any of us cease. The body you are sitting inside passes from childhood through youth into age, and you do not mourn the child you were when you became the youth, or the youth you were when you became the man. The same passage carries the body out of this life into whatever comes next. The one who watches the passage — the one who is watching me speak to you right now — that one is not what the passage takes.

Weapons cannot cleave it. Fire cannot burn it. Water cannot wet it. Wind cannot dry it. It is not produced and it does not end. Knowing this, why are you grieving?

ARJUNA: Because I do not know it. I hear you say it. I do not know it from the inside. I know the bodies. I know the faces.

KRISHNA: Then begin where you are. The bodies are real and they will end. Even by the reasoning that says only the body is real, grief at what must end is a kind of confusion. What is born must die. What dies must be born. You are weeping over the shape of the wheel.

But that is the small answer. The larger answer is the one you will have to live into. For now, take this much as a working ground: there is something in you that the day cannot reach. Something the result of tonight will not finally touch. Stand on it.

Now I am going to tell you what to do with your hands.

You have a right to the work. You do not have a right to its fruit. The effort is yours. The outcome is not. Do not act for the sake of the reward. Do not collapse into doing nothing because the reward is not guaranteed. Both of those are the same mistake — both are the self bargaining with a process it does not control.

Hear me carefully. I am not telling you to stop caring. I am not telling you to act half-heartedly so the disappointment will be small. I am telling you that there is a self which does the work, and there is a self which needs the work to have gone well, and these are not the same self. The first is yours to train. The second is what destroys you when results do not come.

When you walk out of the conversation that did not go your way, what walks out with you? The one who did the work, or the one who needed the work to have gone well? When you close the laptop on the project that shipped to silence, who is sitting there? When the diagnosis comes back and the treatment you chose was the wrong one — who hears it? If it is only the second self, the one whose existence depends on the outcome, then every loss is annihilation. That is the life you are trying to refuse tonight by refusing to act at all. It will not work. Inaction is also a result. Sitting on this chariot is also a choice the world will judge.

ARJUNA: Then how do I act without acting for the result? The result is why anyone acts.

KRISHNA: It is why most act. It is not why the steady-minded act. The steady-minded act because the work is what is in front of them, and they have a right to it, and they bring themselves to it without bargaining. Success and failure they hold the same. Not because they cannot tell them apart — they can — but because the place inside them from which they act is not the place a result can rearrange.

Let me show you that person. Desires enter such a person the way rivers enter the sea — the sea brimful, unmoving, taking in all of them without becoming them. The person who chases after every river is not at peace. The person who has dammed every river is not at peace either. The one who lets the rivers come, full and steady, neither grasping nor refusing — that one is at peace. Their composure has roots the day cannot reach.

You will not be that person tomorrow. You may not be that person in this lifetime. Begin anyway. Each time you act and let the result go, you take one step. Each time you act and graft yourself onto the result, you take one step in the other direction.

Watch what happens when you do not. I want you to see this clearly, because it is the wheel you are sitting on right now. You dwell on something — a face, a phrase, an outcome you wanted. Dwelling becomes attachment. Attachment becomes desire. Desire denied becomes anger. Anger confuses the mind. The confused mind loses memory of what actually mattered. And the one who has lost memory of what mattered is destroyed — not by an enemy, but by the chain they walked down step by step while telling themselves they were thinking clearly.

You are partway down that chain right now. That is what the dropped bow is. You dwelt on the faces. The dwelling became the wish that this not be asked of you. The wish became the conviction that to act would be wrong. The conviction has clouded the memory of what you actually owe — to the people behind you, to the work you trained for, to the self that is not the self of this evening's grief.

Step off the chain.

Pick up the work. Not the outcome. The work.

I have given you the ground. Tomorrow I will give you the path.
