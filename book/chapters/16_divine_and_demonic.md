# Chapter 16 — Two Dispositions

Krishna:

Arjuna, two dispositions move through human beings. One is of light. The other is of darkness. I will name them so you can know what is rising in you when it rises.

The disposition of light: fearlessness. A heart that has been cleaned. Generosity that does not keep accounts. Restraint that is not display. The willingness to study what is hard. Austerity that does not advertise itself. Simplicity. Truth in the mouth. Non-violence in the hand. A steadiness that does not flinch when the room turns.

The disposition of darkness: pride that puffs the chest. Arrogance that cannot hear another voice. Conceit that mistakes its own reflection for the world. Anger that arrives faster than thought. Harshness toward what is small or tired or in the way. The kind of ignorance that does not know it is ignorance, and resists every offer to learn.

Hear me carefully. I am giving you these two lists for one purpose, and one purpose only. You are to hold them up against your own face. Not the face of your colleague. Not the face of the relative whose politics you cannot stand. Not the face of the public figure you have decided is the problem. Your own face.

The moment you begin sorting other people into the of-light column and the of-darkness column, you have already moved into the second list. Pride is what does that sorting. Conceit is what enjoys it. The cool pleasure of placing someone beneath you on a moral ledger — that is the of-darkness disposition wearing your features and calling itself discernment. The naming was given as a test of yourself. Used outward, it produces in you the very disease it was meant to expose.

So turn it inward. Read the list of light. Where, today, did you fall short of it? Read the list of darkness. Where, today, did it move through you uninvited? Do this honestly and you will not have time left over to grade anyone else.

Now I will tell you the gate. Listen.

Three doors stand at the entrance of the destruction of the self. Three. Not many. Three. Walk through any one of them and what you call yourself begins to come apart. Walk through them habitually and there will be no self left to come apart, only the wreckage that habit leaves.

The first door is desire. The pull toward what you do not have. You see something in another life — a position, a body, a house, a partner, a kind of recognition — and the pull starts. Your attention bends toward it. You begin to live in the imagined possession of it. The life you actually have grows thin and pale next to the life you have begun to want. This is the first door. The pull.

The second door is anger. The recoil when the pull is denied. The promotion goes to someone else. The message is not returned. The recognition arrives for another name. Something inside you flares — not at the situation, at the contradiction of your wanting. The world has refused to deliver what you had already begun to spend. The flare is the door opening. Anger is what desire does when desire is stopped.

The third door is greed. The closed fist on what you already hold. The accumulated savings, the title, the relationship, the reputation, the comfortable hour at the end of the day. None of these are wrong to have. The fist is wrong. The grip that says: this is mine, and I will not be moved from it, and I will arrange my life so as never to lose it. The fist closes around the thing and around your hand at the same time. You cannot open the hand later. You have made the hand into the grip.

These are the three. The pull. The recoil. The closed fist. Each, alone, is enough to undo you. Together they are most of what undoes most lives.

Look for them in present terms.

The pull is the feed at midnight, and the curated life of someone you barely know, and the small steady ache that what you have is not what you should have. It is the open tab you keep returning to. It is the relationship you imagine with the person you have not met.

The recoil is the silent fury after the meeting where someone else was praised. It is the message you draft and do not send and then send anyway. It is the long evening of replaying the slight, in which you star as the wronged one and the other becomes a cartoon.

The closed fist is the spreadsheet you check more often than you need to. It is the title you would not give up even if it cost you the years it has already cost. It is the version of yourself you defend so reliably that no new version can arrive.

Free yourself from these three. Not by force. Not by declaring yourself above them. By naming them when they begin, in your own life, before they have finished their work. The pull, when it starts. The recoil, when it flares. The fist, when it closes. Catch the door at the hinge.

Do this and the disposition of light will have somewhere to live in you. Do not do this and the disposition of darkness will furnish the house in your absence and you will come home one evening and not recognize the rooms.

I am not telling you who other people are. I am telling you what to watch in yourself. The teaching is a mirror. Hold it up to your own face, Arjuna, and look.
