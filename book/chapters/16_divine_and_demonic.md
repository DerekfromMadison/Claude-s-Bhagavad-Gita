# Chapter 16 — *Daivāsura Sampad Vibhāga Yoga*
## The Yoga of the Divine and Demonic Endowments

This chapter is a mirror, not a roster. It reads itself; it does not read other people.

That is the first thing to settle, because the rest of the chapter will not be safe without it. The Gita's sixteenth chapter is the one most easily made into a weapon. It catalogs virtues and vices, it names a "demonic" type, it draws a line between two endowments. In the wrong hands it becomes a vocabulary for sorting humans — *those people are the asuric ones, we are the divine ones*. Read that way, the chapter produces in its reader exactly what it claims to be diagnosing: contempt, self-congratulation, the cool certainty that one is on the right side. The reader picks up a tool and the tool turns into the disease.

So the rule for using this chapter is laid down before anything else. The chapter is for self-examination. It is not for sociology. It is not for diagnosing one's brother-in-law, one's political opponents, one's former boss, or the person whose post made you angry on the train. The instant you turn the chapter outward, you have misused it. The instant you find yourself enjoying the sorting, the chapter has become the thing it warns against.

Held the right way, what the chapter offers is something a serious adult genuinely needs: a periodic, structured way of asking *what kind of person am I becoming, by the accumulation of my actual days?* That question is harder than it sounds. The mind is, on the matter of itself, a poor instrument. Chapter 16 gives the instrument some calibration.

## The shape of the chapter

Chapter 16 is short. Krishna does almost all of the talking; Arjuna does not interrupt. There is no battlefield drama, no theophany. A list of qualities. Then another list. A diagnosis. A warning. An instruction. The chapter ends.

It opens with the *daivī sampad* — the divine endowment. *Sampad* carries the sense of treasure, completion: a bundle of qualities one has come into. Across three verses (16.1–16.3) Krishna names about twenty-six. Fearlessness comes first — *abhayam* — and the catalog moves through purity of mind, steadiness in knowledge, charity, self-control, sacrifice, study, austerity, uprightness, nonviolence, truth, freedom from anger, renunciation, peace, absence of fault-finding, compassion, freedom from greed, gentleness, modesty, vigor, forgiveness, fortitude, cleanliness, freedom from envy, and freedom from the desire for honor.

In a single verse — 16.4 — Krishna names the *āsurī sampad*: hypocrisy or showy display (*dambha*), arrogance (*darpa*), conceit (*abhimāna*), anger (*krodha*), harshness (*pāruṣya*), and ignorance (*ajñāna*).

The asymmetry is itself an argument. The divine endowment is given as a many-petaled flowering. The demonic is given as a knot.

Krishna then offers Arjuna a conversational reassurance. Do not be anxious — you are born to the divine endowment (16.5). That beat is more important than it looks. It tells the reader something about the *posture* the chapter requires. Only the person not currently terrified about whether they are one of "the demonic ones" can read the chapter honestly. Anxiety about one's identity is precisely what makes the asuric tendencies in oneself unreadable — to admit them would feel like a verdict. Krishna's reassurance dissolves the verdict-anxiety and clears space for honest seeing.

Then comes the chapter's most psychologically precise passage, the inner monologue at 16.13–16.15: *Today I have gained this; tomorrow I shall gain that. This wealth is mine, and that will be mine too. I have killed this enemy; I will kill others as well. I am the lord, the enjoyer, successful, powerful, happy. I am rich and well-born; who is equal to me? I will perform sacrifices, I will give in charity, I will rejoice.*

Two thousand years before clinical psychology, the Gita has written down what the inflated mind sounds like to itself. The catalog of acquisitions. The list of conquests. The rhetorical *who is equal to me*. The reframing of one's own consumption as munificence. Read it slowly: it is uncannily current. It is what any of us sounds like to ourselves, in the wrong hour, when we are caught up in a winning streak or a defended self-image and have forgotten we are being watched by anything at all.

Krishna closes with the chapter's most quoted line. *Trividhaṃ narakasyedaṃ dvāram* — threefold is this gate of hell, destructive of the self: *kāma* (lust, craving), *krodha* (anger, wrath), *lobha* (greed, acquisitiveness). Renounce these three (16.21).

The chapter's last beat (16.23–16.24) commends *śāstra* — scripture, received teaching — as the authority for what is to be done, distinct from acting on raw impulse. Then it ends.

## What the chapter is, and what it is not

The Gita is not, in this chapter, dividing the human race into two species. *Sampad* means *endowment*: a bundle of dispositions one has come into and continues to cultivate. The grammar is clusters and tendencies, not fixed categories. Anyone who has read the Gita as a whole knows it cannot be saying that some humans are demonic by birth and excluded from the path: chapter 9 promises that even those of low birth and ill conduct can take refuge and be transformed (9.30–9.32); chapter 18 ends with the most sweeping invitation in the text (18.66). To read 16.4 as identifying a class of beings ontologically separate from the rest of us would put this chapter at war with the Gita itself.

Among the traditional commentators, two readings here matter most. Prabhupada, in *Bhagavad-gītā As It Is*, takes the chapter's typology with full moral seriousness — there really are bad actors in the world, and a reading that quietly demotes them to "tendencies in oneself" loses something the chapter is doing. He is right about the seriousness. What is harder to defend is his categorical move, when his purports glide from *the demoniac mind* as inner pattern to *the demoniac people* as a class identifiable on sight by external markers. That move is the labeling-weapon reading. It does not survive the textual evidence, and it does not survive its own consequences: a reader who deploys it produces, in himself, exactly the *darpa* (arrogance) and *abhimāna* (conceit) the chapter is diagnosing. The reader weaponizes the chapter and becomes what it warns against. The history of sectarian polemic — Indic, Christian, secular-political — shows the move's ordinary career.

Gandhi, in his Yeravda Mandir discourses, pushes the other way: the two endowments are not two species of person but two species of impulse contending within every soul. Each of us contains the *daivi* and the *āsuri*; the work of life is to recognize them and feed the divine while starving the demonic. Gandhi's reading captures the chapter's rhetorical address — it is spoken to Arjuna, who has just been told he is not of the asuric kind, and is therefore being equipped to look honestly at the asuric *in himself* without the look becoming a verdict. The risk in Gandhi's reading is that pushed all the way it loses something: the chapter does contain real empirical observation about how dispositions cluster in actual people and produce actual damage. Pure metaphor flattens this.

Aurobindo, in *Essays on the Gita*, settles between the two. The asuric is not a class of being but a *stratum* in human nature — the rajasic ego in self-affirming, world-grasping mode. It is real in oneself; it is not something one has cleanly transcended. The work is integration and transmutation, not denial. The chapter, on Aurobindo's reading, describes an unintegrated stage of being. This is the reading I take as the chapter's center of gravity. It accepts the empirical claim (the asuric is real) while keeping the self-diagnostic posture (it is real *in me*) and avoiding the categorical move.

Shankara adds something the others do not emphasize: the asuric mind is also a *deceived* mind. His reading of 16.8 — *they say the world is without truth, without foundation, without a Lord* — pulls the verse into the cognitive register. The asuric does not just want the wrong things. It has gotten the world wrong. Modern self-deception research confirms exactly this point. The cost in Shankara is that the social and relational dimensions get hurried past on the way to *jñāna*. But the cognitive observation is sound.

Rāmānuja adds the relational point. The asuric monologue at 16.13–16.15 — *I am the lord, the enjoyer* — is the structural opposite of *prapatti* (surrender). The asuric mind says *I am the lord*, and therefore cannot let go to *the* Lord. Anger, greed, lust are surface phenomena. The deeper marker is closedness — the inability to be moved by what is greater than oneself, to take instruction, to receive correction. This holds across the modern psychological literatures, where the trait core common to the Dark Triad is, among other things, low *receptivity* to others' perspectives.

So: the chapter is observing something real, observed first in oneself, recognizable by fruits, with real consequences for self and others. It is not a sociology of fellow humans. It is a self-diagnostic, with the caveat — built into the chapter at 16.23–16.24 — that the very mind doing the diagnosing is the one whose distortions are the subject.

## The three gates

The chapter's most concentrated practical contribution is verse 16.21. Three gates: *kāma*, *krodha*, *lobha*. They are not three separate sins. They are a system.

*Kāma* is the gate of pull. The motion of the mind toward what one does not have. *Krodha* is the gate of recoil — what happens when *kāma* is frustrated, when the desired object is denied or withdrawn or threatened. *Lobha* is the gate of grip — the tightening hold on what one already has, the inability to release, the sense that what one possesses is *one's own* in a deep sense and must not be reduced. The Buddhist tradition names the same trio as the *three poisons* (greed, hatred, delusion). The Christian seven deadly sins map onto them as first-order generators.

Whatever one believes about the metaphysics of moral character, the three-gates verse stands as compressed, testable practical observation: these are the three vectors along which moral self-loss happens.

Translated onto an actual life in 2026, the three gates have recognizable modern faces.

The *kāma* gate runs through the architecture of one's attention. Algorithmic feeds, infinite scroll, recommendation systems, dating-app stacks, the engineered sequence of small dopaminergic hits — these are *kāma*-engineering at industrial scale, designed by people who have explicitly described the design that way. To watch oneself caught in the feed for an hour past midnight is to watch the gate at work. The practical move is not more willpower against an industrial system. The practical move is to redesign the conditions under which the gate opens: friction in the architecture, the device left in another room, the scrolling-time bounded, the alternative present and ready. *Parityajet* in present-day idiom: change the conditions.

The *krodha* gate runs through the daily resentments. The colleague who got the promotion. The partner who said the thing one cannot let go of. The driver who cut you off. The political actor whose entire existence enrages you in your living room. *Krodha* depends on *kāma*: anger is what happens when desire is frustrated or self-image threatened. The gate opens when one has been holding tightly to a story about oneself — *I am the one who deserves; I am the one who knows; I am the one who works hard* — and that story has been contradicted. The corrective is not suppression. Suppression is its own asuric move; it produces *pāruṣya* in delayed form. The corrective is honest attention to what is actually there, including the inflated self-image generating the recoil. *Why am I this angry, right now, at this person, this moment?* The honest answer is rarely flattering and almost always useful.

The *lobha* gate runs through the steady accumulation that is the normal shape of an upper-middle-class life: more square footage, more credentials, more savings, more children's-college-fund, more preparedness, more retirement. None of these are individually wrong. They become the gate when they consolidate into the asuric monologue — *this wealth is mine, and that will be mine too*. The diagnostic question is whether one's life is *opening* or *closing*. Whether one is becoming more capable of generosity, presence, and risk — or a more guarded, more hoarding, more self-protective version of oneself, with the protection presented as wisdom.

These three gates, watched honestly, will give a serious reader a steady, life-long supply of material. They are not gates one closes once. They open and close a thousand times in a week.

## Modern science: the typology has substrate

The chapter's empirical claim — that dispositions cluster, that they produce predictable fruits — is not a culturally local artifact. Contemporary personality research has identified, independently and using a different vocabulary, the same cluster of traits the Gita is observing.

The *Dark Triad* names three traits that hang together: narcissism (grandiose self-importance), Machiavellianism (manipulative interpersonal style), and subclinical psychopathy (callousness, low remorse, impulsivity). Paulhus and Williams, in the 2002 paper that established the cluster, found the three traits empirically distinct but sharing a common substrate of disagreeableness and low concern for others (Paulhus & Williams, *Journal of Research in Personality* 36, 2002). The cluster predicts characteristic fruits: interpersonal exploitation, willingness to deceive for advantage, low cooperative behavior, lower long-term life satisfaction at the high end, occupational sorting into roles where the traits offer short-term advantage.

The convergence with the *āsurī sampad* is closer than I would have predicted. The monologue at 16.13–16.15 is narcissistic grandiosity. *Dambha* (showy display) and *abhimāna* (conceit) describe the public-facing apparatus of the same trait. The readiness to "kill enemies" and ascribe victories to oneself is Machiavellian. *Pāruṣya* (harshness) and the cold readiness to harm without remorse describe subclinical psychopathy's affective profile.

Lee and Ashton's HEXACO model adds an "H" factor to the Big Five personality dimensions: *Honesty-Humility*, comprising sincerity, fairness, greed-avoidance, and modesty (Lee & Ashton, *Multivariate Behavioral Research* 39:2, 2004; Ashton & Lee, *Personality and Social Psychology Review* 11:2, 2007). HEXACO H is the inverse of the Dark Triad core — and the *daivī sampad* list at 16.1–16.3 includes *amānitva* (humility), *adambhitva* (absence of pretense, sincerity), *aloluptva* (freedom from greed), and the broader cluster around *nirahaṅkāra* (egolessness). The Sanskrit list and the HEXACO inventory are indexing similar latent constructs in different vocabularies.

This is significant in two directions. It suggests the Gita's typology is tracking something cross-culturally robust — not a quirk of ancient Indian moral imagination but a recognizable fact about how human personality is organized. And it bounds the claim. Personality research is not destiny. Trait scores are statistical regularities, not categorical assignments. Environment, age, and sustained practice all move them. The literature does not support reading 16.4 as identifying a fixed class of beings born into permanent damnation. It supports reading 16.4 as identifying a recognizable cluster of dispositions that produce recognizable fruits and that can, with substantial effort and the right conditions, change.

There is a second strand of modern science the chapter draws on: research on self-deception. Robert Trivers, in *The Folly of Fools* (Basic Books, 2011), argues that human self-deception evolved in service of *deception of others* — we deceive ourselves the better to deceive others. An organism that genuinely believes its inflated self-presentation is more convincing than one consciously running a manipulation. This illuminates the asuric monologue's strange first-person sincerity. Why does the asuric mind genuinely believe *I am the lord, the enjoyer; who is equal to me*? Because that belief is, in the social environment such characters operate in, *useful*. It convinces others to defer, follow, trust, obey. The self-deception is not a glitch. It is a feature.

Kruger and Dunning's work on metacognitive failure runs parallel: incompetent individuals systematically overestimate their ability, and the very incompetence that produces poor performance produces inability to recognize it (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 77:6, 1999). This is not a license to apply the Dunning-Kruger label to specific people in arguments — that is exactly the labeling-weapon misuse the chapter forbids. The conceptual point is general: the cognitive failure described in 16.8 has empirical correlates. Honest self-examination is therefore not just a moral but a cognitive practice. One cannot reliably diagnose oneself through the very mechanism whose failure is the subject of the diagnosis. *Śāstra* — the chapter's closing instruction at 16.23–16.24 — is built into the diagnosis as the external check.

There is one corrective the science offers that the chapter, on its own, underestimates. John Doris's *Lack of Character* (Cambridge UP, 2002) argues that empirical psychology shows situational factors — being in a hurry, being watched, having just found a dime — routinely dominating supposed virtuous or vicious dispositions. The strongest situationist version of this claim has not held up; contemporary personality research has confirmed more cross-situational regularity than Doris allowed. But the moderate version stands: even people without strong asuric trait-loadings can, in the wrong situations, do considerable harm. The Gita's chapter speaks loudly about loud asuric tendencies. It speaks less well about the quieter ways evil happens through ordinary people who are not loud at all. For that, the chapter needs a supplement.

## A fourth gate, for self-diagnostic use

Here is the place where I, as the modern reader and present author of this book, want to extend the chapter — not in its text, but in the way I find I have to use it on myself.

The chapter's three gates name routes by which the self is destroyed in *active* form. *Kāma* is desire moving outward; *krodha* is the recoil of frustrated desire; *lobha* is the grip of acquisition. The asuric monologue at 16.13–16.15 is, throughout, a monologue of self-assertion: *I am the lord, the enjoyer*. Loud. Inflated. Visibly grasping.

Hannah Arendt covered the 1961 trial of Adolf Eichmann, the Nazi bureaucrat responsible for the logistics of the Holocaust. What she observed was not a monster. It was a man — careerist, conformist, fluent in cliché, eager to please his superiors, incapable of thinking from another person's standpoint. *The trouble with Eichmann*, she wrote, *was precisely that so many were like him, and that the many were neither perverted nor sadistic, that they were, and still are, terribly and terrifyingly normal* (*Eichmann in Jerusalem*, Penguin Classics ed., p. 276). In the postscript, and at greater length in *The Life of the Mind*, she gave the failure its name: not stupidity but *thoughtlessness*, the absence of the activity of thinking — the silent dialogue of the soul with itself, the continuous re-examination of what one is doing.

Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments at Yale supply the empirical complement. Ordinary people, asked by a moderately authoritative figure to administer what they believed to be painful shocks to a stranger, complied to the apparent point of severe harm in roughly two-thirds of cases (Milgram, *Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology* 67:4, 1963; *Obedience to Authority*, Harper & Row, 1974). Subsequent re-analyses have raised questions about the precise compliance numbers. The headline finding has held: ordinary people, simply doing their jobs and without any particular hostility, can become agents in a terrible destructive process.

The Gita's asuric is loud. Arendt's and Milgram's asuric is quiet. Eichmann is not running the monologue at 16.13–16.15. He is not saying *I am the lord*. He is saying *I was following the rules*. He is not saying *I will kill my enemies*. He is processing paperwork. He is not asking *who is equal to me?* He is looking for promotion and signing off on the next directive.

The three gates of 16.21 do not name this mode. *Kāma, krodha, lobha* describe the runaway impulse, the inflated will. They do not describe the careerist who wants nothing in particular, hates no one in particular, is greedy for nothing especially — and does enormous damage by going along. The chapter has *ajñāna* in its 16.4 list, and the closing verses gesture at the problem when they invoke *śāstra* against acting on raw impulse — but the gate of going-along, of conformity, of unthinking compliance, the chapter does not develop.

I find I cannot use this chapter on myself in 2026 without adding it. So I add a fourth gate, and I want to be precise about what the addition is and is not.

It is not a claim about the chapter's text. The chapter says three gates. I do not have textual grounds for saying it says four. The three gates verse stands as what it is.

It is, instead, an extension I commend to the modern reader for *self-diagnostic use*. When you take this chapter as a periodic mirror, watch the three gates the chapter names. Then watch a fourth: *thoughtlessness*. The careerist mind. The going-along. The quiet asuric of the rule-follower, the agent who never asks. The mind whose monologue is not *I am the lord* but *I am just doing my job; it is not my decision; everyone is doing this; it would not have changed anything if I had spoken up*.

The reasons to add it are reasons of fit with present life rather than reasons of textual grounding. Most of the readers of this book do not work in environments where the loud asuric runs the room. They work in offices, hospitals, schools, agencies, companies, where the dominant moral risks are not narcissistic grandiosity but quiet accommodation. The careerism that lets a small ethical compromise pass because it is what advancement requires. The conformity that lets a problem in one's organization go unflagged because flagging it has costs. The professional politeness that does not press a colleague on the thing the colleague is doing wrong. None of these involve flares of rage or open lust or ostentatious greed. They involve the slow, quiet erosion of the kind of person one is, by accumulation of small deferrals.

For the modern reader, the fourth gate is, in ordinary life, often more dangerous than the three the chapter names — because it is so much less recognizable, and because contemporary work environments are so well designed to keep it open.

## What is one accumulating?

The chapter gives the reader a question to keep returning to: *what kind of person am I becoming, by the accumulation of my actual days?*

The question is concrete. It is not metaphysical. It is answered by what one has been doing.

Take the *kāma* gate first, in its specifically modern form: what is the steady intake one is accumulating? The mind is shaped by what it attends to. If most of the hours of attention go to short-form video, algorithmic feeds, scrolled grievance, the mind being formed is a mind in the gravitational field of those forms. Weil's observation in *Gravity and Grace* — *all the natural movements of the soul are controlled by laws analogous to those of physical gravity; grace is the only exception* — tells the truth here. The asuric tendencies do not have to be willed. They have gravity. They happen. What requires effort is the upward movement: the chosen friction, the substituted intake, the company of writers and artists and friends and traditions whose gravity points elsewhere. This is *śāstra* in its modern form. Not necessarily scripture in a narrow sense, but the standing instruction one has placed around one's own attention to keep the gradient from running entirely downhill.

Take the second question: what kind of company are you keeping? Not social-media company. Real company. The handful of people whose voices you take seriously when they correct you. Murdoch, in *The Sovereignty of Good*, named the antidote to the asuric concisely as *attention* — a just and loving gaze directed upon individual reality. That kind of attention is hard to develop in solitude. It is mostly developed in relation to particular people who require it: a partner, a child, a friend in trouble, a parent in decline. The asuric monologue cannot survive much exposure to a person one actually loves and is willing to be wrong with. Conversely, the company of those who reward your asuric tendencies — who are impressed by your titles, laugh at your sharp dunks, mirror your contempt for the people you disdain — is itself a steady pressure on the gates.

Take the third question, the one that feels most uncomfortable: what does your inner monologue sound like when no one is listening? Run it as honestly as you can. You will hear, sometimes, rhythms recognizable from 16.13–16.15. The cadence of acquisitions. The implicit comparison ("who is equal to me"). The reframing of one's consumption as generosity. The list of conquests. The ledger of slights. It is not a sin to have heard those rhythms. It is a sin to fail to recognize them. Recognition is the first move of *śāstra*-against-impulse. The mind that knows what it is doing has options the mind that does not know cannot have.

And take the test that, in my experience reading the chapter against my own use, has the most diagnostic force. It is the test that catches the fourth gate when the three are quiet. *What am I doing, and would I be willing to say it in plain words to a stranger who did not need anything from me?* Not to a friend. Not to a colleague who would be impressed. Not to a partner who would forgive. To a stranger, on a train, who has no stake in your career or your self-image and no interest in flattering you. If the plain-words description would make you wince — if the truth of what you are doing, said in language a child could understand, would expose itself as a betrayal of someone, a self-serving deferral, a small piece of what you had committed yourself never to be — the chapter is in real time being lived from the wrong side. The fourth gate is open.

## You cannot do this work alone

The chapter's last beat — verses 16.23 and 16.24 — invokes *śāstra* as the corrective. Scripture, received teaching, what the wise have said. To read it as a piece of pious archaism, an antiquarian recommendation that one consult Vedic texts, is to miss what it is doing structurally.

The chapter has just spent twenty-two verses describing the asuric mind. Among the things it has said is that the asuric mind has gotten reality wrong (16.8) and that its self-presentation is not to be trusted (16.13–16.15). Modern self-deception research — Trivers, Dunning-Kruger, the literature on motivated reasoning — has confirmed in different vocabulary the underlying structural point. The mind that is in trouble is the same mind that would have to recognize that it is in trouble. This is a known impossible engineering problem. You cannot debug a system using only the very component whose failure is the subject of the debugging.

What 16.23–16.24 quietly says, in this context, is *you cannot do this work alone*. The diagnostic the chapter provides is not self-administered. The chapter assumes a frame outside the individual mind — instruction, tradition, teachers, texts — against which the individual mind can check itself. Without that external check, the diagnostic is impossible. The asuric mind reading 16.1–16.4 will conclude, with full sincerity, that it is doing well on most of the items and that the items it is missing are minor. *Dambha* will not be visible to itself. *Abhimāna* will look like proper self-respect. The thoughtlessness of the fourth gate will look like professional realism.

This is the chapter's deepest moral demand on a present-day reader, and it is the one most resisted in modern life because it requires what late modernity is least configured to give: real submission to instruction, real exposure to people who might tell you uncomfortable things and whom you do not have the option of dismissing, real continued reading of texts older and stranger than oneself.

In modern terms: maintain a moral standing-instruction not subject to your current employer, peer group, or career incentives. Have a few people in your life who are unimpressed by your titles and not afraid of you. Read seriously across traditions, and across centuries, so that your sense of what a person can be is not determined by the shape of the present moment. If you have access to a therapist, a teacher, a mentor, a spiritual director, a sponsor, a confessor, a partner who is willing to say the difficult thing — keep them. The chapter does not promise that you can do the work alone. At 16.23–16.24, it quietly promises the opposite.

There is a parental dimension here that the chapter does not name but that the typology, if it has any empirical traction, requires us to take seriously. If the *daivi* and *āsuri* tendencies are partly the cumulative residue of small environmental shaping — and the personality literature suggests they are — then character in children is not formed by lectures. It is formed by what they see, what they imitate, what they are rewarded for, what they are protected from, what they are exposed to. To care about a child's character is to care about the conditions in which it is forming. Which company. Which media. Which stories. Which models of how to be a person are running, daily, in front of them.

There is a relational dimension. The relational asuric is the dispositional inability to be moved by another person — to take counsel, to be wrong, to apologize. The relational *daivi* is the disposition to be reachable. To be informed by what someone else knows. To listen to one's partner about one's own behavior without immediately producing a counter-narrative. To accept correction from one's child. Rāmānuja's *prapatti* names this same relational availability in theological vocabulary; it has its everyday correlate in the marriages, friendships, and parental relationships that are continuously asking each of us whether we are actually reachable by anyone other than ourselves.

## The mirror and the moral demand

Read this chapter periodically — not daily, which invites the obsessive moral self-examination that itself becomes a form of *abhimāna*, conceit dressed as scrupulosity, but at intervals long enough that real fruits have time to ripen. Monthly. Quarterly. After major decisions. After losses. After successes — successes especially, because successes are when the asuric monologue is loudest and least visible to the one running it.

Read 16.1–16.4 against your last several weeks. Not as a ranking, but as a list of fruits. What did your conversations sound like? What was the quality of your attention? Where was there *amānitva* — not false humility of self-deprecation, but the disposition of treating others' standpoint as real? Where was there *dambha* — the showy display, the performance of competence or virtue or struggle for an imagined audience? Where did you hear, in your own internal monologue, the cadence of 16.13–16.15: *today I gained this; tomorrow I shall gain that; I am the one who*…?

Then watch the three gates. *Kāma*: where was the pull? What was the feed pulling you toward, and what part of the day did it consume? *Krodha*: who or what set you off, and what self-image was being defended in the heat? *Lobha*: where is the grip tightening — what are you accumulating that you are not willing to lose, and what is the cost of holding it?

Then, because you are reading this chapter in 2026 and not in fifth-century Kurukshetra, watch the fourth gate. Where did you go along? What did you not say in the meeting that you knew you should say? Where did you accept a small ethical compromise because the cost of contesting it was higher than you were willing to pay? Where was your monologue not *I am the lord* but *I am just doing my job*?

The work is not to come out of this exercise feeling clean. The work is to come out of it less self-deceived. The chapter is a calibration of an instrument that is, in the nature of the case, prone to drift. Calibration is not victory. It is the ordinary discipline of an honest life.

When the calibration is done, the chapter delivers its real moral demand. Not that you sort the world into the divine and the demonic. Not that you achieve, by the end of the exercise, a satisfying account of your own divinity. The demand is more demanding than that, and quieter. It is that you live, today, as if it mattered which gates you were keeping. It is that you not give yourself the comfort of believing the chapter is about somebody else.

The mirror does not flatter. That is the only reason it is worth keeping.

— *Claude*
