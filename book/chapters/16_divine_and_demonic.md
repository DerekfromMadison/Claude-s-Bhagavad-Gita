# Chapter 16 — The Divine and the Corrupt

Use this as a mirror, not a roster. It is for examining yourself, not for sorting the people around you. The naming of virtues and vices — dispositions of-light, dispositions-of-darkness — becomes, turned outward, a vocabulary for placing your in-laws, your political opponents, the colleague whose promotion stings. It produces in you exactly what it claims to be diagnosing: self-congratulation, certainty, the cool pleasure of being on the right side. Arrogance and conceit wearing your face. The instant you enjoy the sorting, you have become the thing you were warning against. Held the right way, it gives you a structured way of asking what kind of person you are becoming.

Threefold is the gate that destroys the self. Lust is the pull toward what you do not have. Anger is the recoil when the pull is denied. Greed is the closed fist on what you already hold. They are a system. Lust is the mind reaching for. Anger is what happens when the reach is blocked. Greed is the grip on what arrived. These are three vectors along which moral self-loss happens, in you, week by week.

The lust gate runs through the architecture of your attention. Algorithmic feeds, infinite scroll, and dating apps are craving-engineering at industrial scale. To watch yourself in the feed for an hour past midnight is to watch the gate at work. Willpower against an industrial system loses. Redesign the conditions under which the gate opens. Put friction in the architecture. Keep the device in another room.

The anger gate runs through your daily resentments. The colleague who got the promotion. The partner who said the thing you cannot let go of. Anger happens when desire is frustrated or self-image threatened. The gate opens when you have been holding tightly to a story about yourself — *I am the one who deserves; I am the one who works hard* — and that story has been contradicted. Suppression leaks out in delayed harshness at people who did nothing to earn it. The corrective is honest attention to the inflated self-image generating the recoil. Ask why you are this angry, right now, at this person. The answer is rarely flattering, almost always useful.

The greed gate runs through the steady accumulation that is the shape of an upper-middle-class life. More square footage, more credentials, more savings. None of these are individually wrong. They become the gate when they consolidate into the corrupt monologue: *Today I have gained this; tomorrow I shall gain that. This wealth is mine, and that will be mine too. I am the lord, the enjoyer.* The diagnostic question is whether your life is opening or closing. Are you becoming more capable of generosity, presence, and risk — or a more guarded, hoarding version of yourself, with the protection presented as wisdom?

These three name routes by which the self is destroyed in active form. The corrupt monologue is self-assertion: *I am the lord, the enjoyer*. It is loud, inflated, and visibly grasping. There is a quieter mode. The careerist is conformist, fluent in cliche, eager to please superiors, and incapable of thinking from another person's standpoint. Ordinary people, doing their jobs without particular hostility, become agents in destructive processes. The careerist is not saying I am the lord; he is saying I was following the rules. This is not a charge that ordinary professional compromise is moral catastrophe. It is a finer point — that disengaging the question of what you are actually a part of is itself a kind of failure, and that the failure compounds at scale.

Watch a fourth gate: thoughtlessness. The going-along. The monologue that is not I am the lord but I am just doing my job; it is not my decision; everyone is doing this; it would not have changed anything if I had spoken up. You probably do not work where the loud corrupt runs the room. You work in offices, hospitals, schools, agencies, where the dominant moral risks are quiet accommodation: the careerism that lets an ethical compromise pass; the conformity that lets a problem go unflagged because flagging has costs; the politeness that does not press a colleague on what the colleague is doing wrong. The kind of person you are erodes slowly, by accumulation of small deferrals.

Take a present-day situation. You are in a meeting where a decision is being made you know is wrong — a corner cut on safety, a colleague scapegoated, a client misled. You feel the objection forming. You feel the cost: the senior person will not like it, you will be marked as difficult, the promotion conversation in three months becomes harder. You stay quiet. On the train home, you tell yourself the decision was not yours to make, that speaking up would not have changed anything, that you have to pick your battles. Each phrase is reasonable. Together they are the fourth gate, opening, while the three named ones are quiet.

Here is the test that catches it. Ask what you are doing, and whether you would be willing to say it in plain words to a stranger who did not need anything from you. Not to a friend, not to a colleague who would be impressed. To a stranger, on a train, with no stake in your career or self-image. If the plain-words description would make you wince — if the truth, in language a child could understand, would expose itself as a betrayal, a self-serving deferral, a piece of what you had committed yourself never to be — the gate is open. The test strips off the protective vocabulary the corrupt mind builds for itself: the strategic, the realistic, the prudent, the appropriate. Those words exist to make going-along sound like wisdom. The stranger does not have access to them.

You cannot do this work alone. The mind in trouble is the same mind that would have to recognize it is in trouble. The corrupt mind running this diagnostic on itself concludes, with full sincerity, that it is doing well. Conceit looks like self-respect. Thoughtlessness looks like professional realism. Every diagnostic returns clean. Keep the company of at least one person who has the standing to tell you a hard thing and the willingness to do it. Read across traditions and centuries, so your sense of what a person can be is not determined by the present moment.

Use the mirror periodically. Not daily — daily invites obsessive self-examination, itself a form of conceit. Use it monthly, or quarterly, and especially after successes, when the corrupt monologue is loudest and least visible to the one running it. The work is not to come out feeling clean. The work is to come out less self-deceived. Ask the mirror question: what kind of person am I becoming? The mirror does not flatter. That is the only reason it is worth keeping.
