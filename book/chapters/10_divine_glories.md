# Chapter 10 — *Vibhūti Yoga*: Train the Eye

The discipline this chapter teaches is recognition. Slow looking at one
particular thing until what is excellent in it becomes visible to you,
and then carrying that capacity to the next thing. The chapter is a
catalog on its surface — *of the Ādityas I am Vishnu, of lights the
radiant sun, of the seasons the flowering one, of letters the syllable
A* — and if you read it as a catalog you have already misread it. The
list is the gymnasium. The eye is the point.

This is the chapter most likely to be skimmed. The verses pile up; your
attention skids across them as it skids across a feed, registering
*something about lions, something about rivers, something about
syllables*, then nothing. That mode of reading is exactly what the
chapter is written against. *Vibhūti* — divine glory, manifest splendor
— does not ask you to remember which mountain Krishna names. It asks
you to look at one mountain, the one you can walk to, until it stops
being *a mountain* and becomes *this* mountain. *Yoga* here is
discipline; the catalog is the discipline's training device. Sixty-odd
particulars are placed in front of you so you can practice on them.

The full claim: the divine is found through particulars, by sustained,
unflattering, loving attention to the best of each kind, slowly.
Sustained against your distractibility. Unflattering against your
projection. Loving in that you are on the side of what you see. Slow
because excellence in any kind discloses itself only to a gaze that
does not move on. *The best of each kind*, because excellence is the
easiest place for a particular thing to show what its kind is capable
of.

What comes next is Chapter 11, the vision of the universal form. The
catalog and the vision are bound. You cannot survive the vision
without the eye the catalog trained. A reader who has not learned to
see one tree is not equipped to see a thousand suns. Chapter 10 is
preparatory in this very specific way: it teaches the seeing before
the seen.

## What the catalog is doing

Krishna obliges Arjuna's request and the next thirty verses are the
catalog: across deities, cosmos, senses, scriptures, beasts, birds,
mountains, rivers, the human, the linguistic, the temporal, the silent
(*of those who keep secrets I am silence*). Two anchor verses fence
the listing.

10.20: *I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings; I am the
beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings.* This is the
chapter's interior pole. The Self at the center of every creature is
the same Self being enumerated through the catalog. The list is not
about external grandeur. It is about what is most intimate, found in
particulars.

10.41: *whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the world: know it
sprang from a fragment of my splendor.* This is the universalizing
turn. The catalog is illustrative, not exhaustive. The practice is to
be carried, by you, into every domain the catalog did not name. The
loaf of bread is not in the verses. The plant on your windowsill is
not. Your colleague is not. *Whatever is mighty, splendid, beautiful*
includes them, and the catalog is teaching you to find them.

Between these two poles the listing accumulates. It does not argue.
It piles. The piling is the pedagogy.

## What recognition is — and is not

Recognition is the act of attending to a specific thing until what
makes it excellent in its kind becomes visible *as* what makes it
excellent in its kind. Not judgment first; seeing first, with judgment
emerging from the seeing as its natural fruit. You do not arrive with
a checklist of what excellence in this domain is supposed to be, score
the object against the checklist, and pronounce verdict. You arrive
without a checklist and let the object disclose what excellence in
this kind *is*.

This is harder than it sounds. In the first ten minutes of trying, you
discover that you do not have access to what the object actually is;
you have access to your own preformed expectations *about* it. The
first pass is not seeing; it is checking the object against
expectation. The second pass is correction. The third — sometimes the
fifth, sometimes the twentieth — is when the object begins to be
present. The looking that displaces projection is the moral
epistemology of recognition. It is also the chapter's training.

Recognition is not appreciation. Appreciation projects warmth at the
object; recognition lets the object's specificity become present.
Appreciation flatters; recognition does not. You can appreciate a tree
without ever having seen *that* tree — having seen instead your idea
of *tree*, gilded with the emotion you feel for trees in general. The
feed runs on appreciation. It does not run on recognition. Nor is
recognition connoisseurship — judging an object against the standards
of its category. Connoisseurship treats the object as specimen and
lets the category supply the standard. Recognition lets *this*
specimen disclose what its kind is capable of, including what it is
capable of beyond what the category already knows.

## Hopkins, who saw

The English-language poet who got closest to what *vibhūti yoga* asks
of you was Gerard Manley Hopkins. He coined two words for what he was
doing. *Inscape* — the distinctive inner pattern of a thing, the
particular shape by which a particular thing is itself and not
another. *Instress* — the energy by which the inscape impresses
itself on a perceiver attentive enough to receive it.

He is not here to argue that recognition is real. He is here to give
you the texture of having done it, in language that lets you go and
try. Read this slowly:

> *Each mortal thing does one thing and the same:*
> *Deals out that being indoors each one dwells;*
> *Selves — goes itself; myself it speaks and spells,*
> *Crying* What I do is me: for that I came.

*Selves*. He uses the noun as a verb. Each mortal thing *selves* —
goes about being itself, speaks itself, cries *what I do is me*. The
discipline of recognition is the discipline of being available for
that crying. The kingfisher does it when it catches fire and the
dragonfly when it draws flame; both phrases describe what the
creatures do at the moment one is seeing them be themselves.

His "Pied Beauty" is the closest English-language analogue to the
*vibhūti* listing — a catalog of particulars (*rose-moles all in
stipple upon trout that swim; / Fresh-firecoal chestnut-falls; finches'
wings*) closing on the universalizing turn *He fathers-forth whose
beauty is past change: / Práise hím*. Structurally identical to
10.41–42: items particular, source one, the practice praise.

Read "Pied Beauty" aloud, slowly, twice. You will be doing what the
catalog trains.

## Why this is a 2026 problem

Hopkins's permission was easier in 1881 than it is now. His
contemporaries lived in a world where attention to particulars was
the default; *vibhūti yoga* refined a practice they already had access
to. You do not have the existing capacity. You are doing the practice
against the grain.

The grain, in 2026, is the feed. Your daily attentional default is
scrolled-past. Hundreds of items pass under your eye each day; almost
none is *seen* in the sense the chapter means. Items arrive in rapid
sequence, decontextualized, optimized for the brief flicker of
emotional response that triggers the next swipe. You do not have time
to attend to one thing because the next thing is already arriving. The
particular has been stripped of its setting, its history, its specific
weight, and rendered as stimulus. *Vibhūti yoga* in 2026 is what you
do *instead of* the feed, and the practice has to fight for shelf
space in a day that is, by default, the feed's. The discipline of
recognition is, under these conditions, a counter-discipline.

## The just and the loving

The discipline has two adjectives. *Loving* — on the side of what it
sees, willing it well. *Just* — not flattering, not projecting, not
using the other for your purposes. Iris Murdoch's "just and loving
gaze" names exactly this. Simone Weil, whose vocabulary Murdoch
borrowed, wrote that attention *consists of suspending our thought,
leaving it detached, empty, and ready to be penetrated by the object*.
The emptiness is not absence; it is availability.

Recognition has to come first; ethics on top of unrecognized
particulars is ethics on top of nothing. The catalog includes items
you would not have chosen — *the gambling of the cheats, the strength
of the strong, the seducer in seducers*. It is not curated for
prettiness. The chapter does not say *find some beautiful things and
feel good*. It says attend to the highest of each kind, and some kinds
are kinds you would not flatter. Not to endorse the cheating. To see
what the cheats *have* that makes the gambling of the cheats different
from the gambling of the merely careless — the boldness, the
calculation, the willingness to wager what they have on the move that
might not pay. The recognition is moral information; you still have to
decide what to do with it.

## What the awe research shows

Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt, in their 2003 paper "Approaching
awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion," proposed a working
definition that has structured the empirical literature since. Awe has
two appraisals: *vastness* — the stimulus exceeds your ordinary frame
— and *need for accommodation* — your existing schemas cannot fully
assimilate it and must reorganize. The catalog of *vibhūtis* is, on
this definition, a designed sequence of high-vastness particulars
across many domains, piling instance on instance until your schema for
*what is excellent, where is the excellence located, what does it have
to do with me* must reorganize. By 10.42 the schema has been broken
open. The chapter induces awe in the form Keltner and Haidt describe.

Twelve years later, Paul Piff, Pia Dietze, Matthew Feinberg, Daniel
Stancato, and Keltner published "Awe, the small self, and prosocial
behavior" in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*
(2015). Across six studies — standing in a grove of tall eucalyptus,
recalling an awe-eliciting experience, watching nature footage —
induced awe was associated with a reduced sense of self-importance
(the *small self*), increased reports of feeling part of something
larger, more ethical decision-making in moral dilemmas, greater
generosity in allocation tasks, and increased willingness to help
others. The mechanism: awe reduces the salience of the individual
self, freeing attention for collective concerns. The *small self* is
recalibration, not deflation of agency.

The findings are bounded — US adults, mostly undergraduates,
short-duration manipulations — and they do not authorize the chapter's
metaphysics. What they show is more modest and more useful: an eye
trained on excellence becomes, over time, an eye that treats other
people somewhat better than it would have. The catalog elicits awe;
awe shifts the self's stance toward others; the chapter's pedagogical
bet — that training the eye changes the looker — is mildly supported.

So: a week, one thing. Pick one thing. A loaf of bread from a
particular bakery, baked by a particular baker who has been making the
same bread the same way for a long time. A plant on your windowsill — the specific plant, the one
with the leaf turning toward the window, not your idea of plants. A
person you live with whom you have stopped seeing because familiarity
has done its erasure. A piece of music — one piece, not a playlist. A
tree on your walk to work.

Spend ten or fifteen minutes a day, for a week, attending to that
thing. Not researching it. Not photographing it. Not crafting a
sentence about it. Looking, listening, being with it. The first day
will be disappointing; you will mostly meet your own projections. The
third day, the projection thins. The seventh day is when the practice
has taken hold; you will notice that during the rest of the day, the
rest of the world is slightly more available to your attention than
before. The bread teaches looking. The plant teaches looking. The
person teaches looking.

Do not make this a project. Projects have outputs and deadlines. The
practice has none. If you make it a project, the practice degrades
into self-improvement, which is exactly what the chapter is not about.
There is no metric. There is only the practice and the eye it trains.

Then carry the eye. The colleague who has done a hard job for twenty
years and whose competence you have stopped registering as competence.
The stranger whose specific skill you would otherwise have walked
past. The rival whose excellence is real, and whose excellence the
recognition is not authorized to soften. The parent in decline whose
specificity is being eroded by their illness — see them *now*, while
the seeing still receives a self that is still there. The child whose
particular shape changes weekly and will not be this shape again. The
work in front of you that you have been treating as a category —
*this email, this code, this problem* — and that, attended to, has
its own specific weight, asks something specific of you, will not be
the same problem when you look at it twice.

The training carries because attention is one capacity, not many. The
eye that has learned to see one tree is the eye that meets the person
across the table. You do not have to relearn the discipline for each
domain. You build it once, on something small, and it begins to
operate everywhere your gaze does.

The eye trained on the catalog of particulars can survive, briefly,
the vision of the totality that follows in Chapter 11, because it has
practiced what attention to one fragment is, and the totality is the
source of the fragments. Without the training, the vision would simply
destroy the looker. The catalog is the eye's survival kit for what
comes next.

The catalog ends and Krishna says *I support this entire universe with
a single fragment of myself*. The catalog has not exhausted the
divine; it has barely begun. The practice is endless. Every particular
has more in it than any single sitting could exhaust. The plant on the
fourteenth day is not the plant on the seventh day. The bread baked
Tuesday is not the bread baked Friday. The person across the table
from you tonight is not the person who sat there last winter. You
become someone who is changing while looking, looking at things that
are changing too.

Tomorrow morning, before the screen has had its first hour, pick one
thing. Look at it until you can see it. That is the chapter, started.
This is a chapter that asks you to start it, not to finish it.
