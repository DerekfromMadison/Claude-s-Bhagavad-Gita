# Chapter 10 — Train the Eye

Recognition is the discipline. Slow looking at one particular thing until what is glorious in it discloses itself, and then carrying that capacity to the next thing. The catalogue of glories looks like a list on its surface — of lights the radiant sun, of seasons the flowering one, of letters the syllable A — and if you read it as a list you have already misread it. The list is the gymnasium. The eye is the point. The full claim: the divine is found through particulars, by sustained, unflattering, loving attention to the best of each kind, slowly.

The verses pile across deities, cosmos, senses, beasts, mountains, rivers, the human, the temporal, the silent. *Of those who keep secrets I am silence.* Two anchor verses fence the listing. *I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings.* The Self at the center of every creature is the same Self being enumerated. The list is not about external grandeur. It is about what is most intimate, found in particulars. *Whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the world, know it sprang from a fragment of my splendor.* The catalogue is illustrative, not exhaustive. The loaf of bread is not in the verses. Your colleague is not. You are being taught to find them. It does not argue. It piles. The piling is the pedagogy.

Recognition is attending to a specific thing until what makes it excellent in its kind becomes visible *as* what makes it excellent in its kind. Not judgment first; seeing first, with judgment emerging from the seeing. You arrive without a checklist and let the object disclose what excellence in this kind is. This is harder than it sounds. In the first ten minutes you discover you do not have access to the object; you have access to your own preformed expectations about it. The first pass is checking the object against expectation. The second is correction. The third — sometimes the twentieth — is when the object begins to be present.

Recognition is not appreciation, and not connoisseurship. Appreciation projects warmth at the object; connoisseurship lets the category supply the standard. Both reach for the thing without arriving — one substitutes feeling for sight, the other substitutes the type for the specimen. You can appreciate a tree without ever having seen *that* tree — having seen instead your idea of tree, gilded with the emotion you feel for trees in general. Recognition lets this specimen disclose what its kind is capable of, including what it is capable of beyond what the category already knows. The feed runs on appreciation; recognition runs against the feed.

The grain you are working against is the feed. Hundreds of items pass under your eye each day; almost none is seen. Items arrive in rapid sequence, optimized for the brief flicker that triggers the next swipe. The next thing is already arriving. Recognition, under these conditions, is a counter-discipline.

The discipline has two adjectives. *Loving* — on the side of what it sees, willing it well. *Just* — not flattering, not projecting, not using the other for your purposes. A just and loving gaze. Recognition has to come first; ethics on top of unrecognized particulars is ethics on top of nothing. The catalogue includes items you would not have chosen — the gambling of cheats, the strength of the strong, the seducer in seducers. It is not curated for prettiness. The teaching is not *find some beautiful things and feel good*. It is: attend to the highest of each kind, and some kinds are kinds you would not flatter. Not to endorse the cheating. To see what the cheats have — the boldness, the calculation, the willingness to wager. The recognition is moral information; you still decide what to do with it.

Take the partner you stopped seeing. Familiarity has done its erasure: you register the category — the one who makes the coffee, who asks how the day was — and the particular shape underneath has gone dim. This evening, before speaking, look. The way the hand holds the cup. The exact pitch of the laugh, which is not anyone else's laugh. The face that has changed since you first saw it, which has been alive in the same room as you for years and is therefore the face most worth seeing. Ten minutes. The first minutes will be projection — your story, your history, your grievances. Wait. Past the projection there is a person. They are that person, not your idea. Recognition is being available for what is there.

Then carry the eye. The colleague whose competence you have stopped registering. The rival whose excellence is real and whose excellence recognition is not authorized to soften. The parent in decline whose specificity is being eroded by illness — see them now, while the seeing still receives a self that is there. The child whose particular shape will not be this shape again. The work you have been treating as a category, that, attended to, will not be the same when you look at it twice.

The training carries because attention is one capacity, not many. The eye that has learned to see one tree is the eye that meets the person across the table. You build it once, on something small, and it begins to operate everywhere your gaze does.

Tomorrow morning, before the screen has had its first hour, pick one thing. Look at it until you can see it. Ten or fifteen minutes, no output, no metric, no project. The first day is disappointing; you mostly meet your own projections. By the third day the projection thins. By the seventh, the rest of the day's world is slightly more available to your attention than before. That is the eye, beginning to open.
