# Chapter 10 — *Vibhūti Yoga*: Train the Eye

The discipline this chapter teaches is recognition. Not the survey of
items, not the memorization of a list, not the accumulation of facts
about excellence in many domains. Recognition: the slow, repeated,
honest looking at one thing until what is excellent in that thing
becomes visible to you, and then the carrying of that capacity to the
next thing, and the next. The chapter on its surface is a catalog —
*of the Ādityas I am Vishnu, of lights I am the radiant sun, of the
seasons I am the flowering one, of letters I am the syllable A* — and
if you read it as a catalog you have already misread it. The catalog
is the curriculum, not the doctrine. What the chapter is doing to its
hearer is training an eye. The list is the means. The eye is the
point.

This is the chapter most likely to be skimmed. The verses pile up;
one's attention skids across the surface of the items as it skids
across a feed, registering *something about lions, something about
rivers, something about syllables*, and then nothing. That mode of
reading is exactly the mode the chapter is written against. *Vibhūti
yoga* is not asking you to remember which mountain Krishna names. It
is asking you to look at one mountain — the one you actually live
near, the one you can walk to — until it stops being *a mountain* and
becomes *this* mountain, and then to keep looking.

The claim, fully: the central teaching of Chapter 10 is that the
divine is found through particulars, by sustained, unflattering,
loving attention to the best of each kind, slowly. Sustained against
the reader's distractibility. Unflattering against the reader's
projection. Loving in that the looker is on the side of what they see.
Slow because excellence in any kind discloses itself only to a gaze
that does not move on. *The best of each kind* because excellence is
the easiest place for a particular thing to show what its kind is
capable of. The catalog of *vibhūtis* is the chapter's training device
for the discipline that prepares Arjuna — and the reader — for what
comes next.

What comes next is Chapter 11, the vision of the universal form. The
catalog and the vision are bound. You cannot survive the vision
without the eye the catalog trained. A reader who has not learned to
see one tree is not equipped to see a thousand suns. The chapter is
preparatory in this very specific way: it is teaching the seeing
before the seen.

## The structure of the catalog

Krishna obliges Arjuna's request and the next thirty verses are the
catalog: across deities (*of the Ādityas I am Vishnu*), cosmos (*of
lights the radiant sun*), senses (*of the senses the mind*), scriptures
(*of the Vedas the Sāma*), beings (*of beasts the lion, of birds
Garuḍa*), the natural world (*of mountains Meru, of rivers the
Gaṅgā*), the human (*of cheats the gambling, of the strong the
strength*), the linguistic (*of letters the syllable A*), the temporal
(*of seasons the flowering one*), and the silent (*of those who keep
secrets the silence, the wisdom of the wise*).

Two anchor verses fence the listing. 10.20: *I am the Self seated in
the heart of all beings; I am the beginning, the middle, and the end
of all beings.* This is the chapter's interior pole — the Self at
the center of every creature is the same Self being enumerated through
the catalog. 10.41: *whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the
world: know it sprang from a fragment of my splendor.* This is the
universalizing turn. The catalog is illustrative; the practice it
teaches is to be carried, by the reader, into every domain the
catalog did not name. Between these two poles the catalog
accumulates. It does not argue. It piles. The structural question for
any reading is what the piling is *for*. My answer is the chapter's
title: *yoga*. Discipline, practice, yoking. The list is a training
implement.

## What recognition is — and is not

Let me unfold the discipline.

Recognition, as the chapter teaches it, is the act of attending to a
specific thing until what makes it excellent in its kind becomes
visible *as* what makes it excellent in its kind. This is not an act
of judgment first; it is an act of seeing first, and the judgment
emerges from the seeing as its natural fruit. The looker does not
arrive at the object with a checklist of what excellence in this
domain is supposed to consist in, score the object against the
checklist, and pronounce verdict. The looker arrives without a
checklist and *lets the object disclose what excellence in this kind
is*. The kingfisher in flight teaches what bird-flight at its best
looks like. The well-made bread teaches what bread can be. The person
who has done one job for thirty years teaches what mastery of that job
is. The looker's task is to *be present* for the disclosure.

This is harder than it sounds. The reader who tries the practice
discovers, in the first ten minutes, that they do not have access to
what the object actually is; they have access to their own
preformed expectations *about* the object. The first pass is not
seeing; it is checking the object against expectation. The second pass
is correction. The third pass — sometimes the fifth, sometimes the
twentieth — is when the object begins to be present. Murdoch's account
of the mother-in-law gradually coming to see her son's wife as she is,
not as the daughter-in-law's first appearance led her to project, is
the canonical philosophical example of this in English: a slow,
unflattering, just looking that displaces the projection (Murdoch,
*The Sovereignty of Good*, "The Idea of Perfection"). The just and
loving gaze is the moral epistemology of recognition.

Recognition is not appreciation. Appreciation is the looker projecting
warmth at the object; recognition is the object's specificity becoming
present to the looker. Appreciation flatters; recognition does not. A
person can appreciate a tree without ever having seen *that* tree —
they can have seen instead their idea of *tree*, gilded with the
emotion they feel for trees in general. Appreciation is compatible
with the feed; in fact the feed runs on it. Recognition is not
compatible with the feed. The feed is structured against it.

Recognition is not connoisseurship. Connoisseurship is the trained
ability to judge an object against the standards of its category —
this wine is a good wine of its kind, this argument is a good argument
of its kind. Connoisseurship treats the object as specimen, and the
category supplies the standard. Recognition lets *this* specimen
disclose what its kind is capable of — including what it is capable
of beyond what the category standard already knows. A kingfisher does
not catch fire in any field guide; that is Hopkins seeing the bird,
not the field guide telling Hopkins what to see.

Recognition is not analysis. Analysis decomposes the object into parts
and seeks understanding through their relations. The catalog does not
analyze the lion or the syllable A; it points at them. Recognition
does not deliver up the object's mechanism; it delivers up the
object's *being itself*, in its specific particularity. Analysis comes
after recognition and depends on it; analysis without recognition cuts
up something the analyst never saw.

What recognition is, then: the slow disclosure of the particular,
licensed by attention, oriented by love, disciplined by justice,
returning across many sittings to the same item, accumulating until
the looker can see *this* and not *its category*. This is what
*vibhūti yoga* teaches by giving the disciple sixty-odd particulars to
practice on. The list is not the lesson. The list is the gymnasium.

## Hopkins, who saw

The English-language poet who got closest to what *vibhūti yoga* asks
of its practitioner was Gerard Manley Hopkins, a Victorian Jesuit who
coined two words for what he was doing and what was being done to him.
*Inscape* was his word for the distinctive inner pattern of a thing —
its specific, self-organizing form, the particular shape by which a
particular thing is itself and not another. *Instress* was his word
for the energy by which the inscape impresses itself on a perceiver
attentive enough to receive it.

Hopkins was a poet, not a philosopher; the chapter benefits from a
different kind of voice, because its discipline is not philosophical
proposition-handling but the actual practice of seeing. What Hopkins
offers is not an argument that recognition is real; he offers the
texture of having done it, in language that lets the reader go and
try. His shortest expression of inscape comes in "As kingfishers catch
fire," a poem from around 1881–82:

> *Each mortal thing does one thing and the same:*
> *Deals out that being indoors each one dwells;*
> *Selves — goes itself; myself it speaks and spells,*
> *Crying* What I do is me: for that I came.

*Selves*. He uses the noun as a verb. Each mortal thing *selves* —
goes about being itself, speaks itself, cries *what I do is me*. The
discipline of recognition is the discipline of being available for
that crying. The kingfisher does it when it catches fire and the
dragonfly does it when it draws flame; both phrases describe what the
creatures do at the moment one is seeing them be themselves. *For that
I came* is the kingfisher's voice; the poet has heard it; the reader
who has done the looking has heard it too.

Hopkins's other major catalog poem, "Pied Beauty," is the closest
English-language structural analogue to the *vibhūti* listing:

> *Glory be to God for dappled things —*
> *For skies of couple-colour as a brinded cow;*
> *For rose-moles all in stipple upon trout that swim;*
> *Fresh-firecoal chestnut-falls; finches' wings;*
> *Landscape plotted and pieced — fold, fallow, and plough;*
> *And áll trádes, their gear and tackle and trim.*

The list is a list of *particulars*: not "beauty" in the abstract but
*these* spots on *these* trout, *these* freshly fallen chestnuts,
*these* trades with the gear and tackle they actually use. The
universalizing turn at the end —

> *He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change:*
> *Práise hím.*

— is structurally identical to 10.41–42. The items are particular; the
source is one; the practice is praise, which in Hopkins is another
word for the kind of attention the chapter teaches. A Victorian Jesuit
and a Sanskrit catalog poet, separated by twenty centuries and a
cultural ocean, doing the same thing. This is not coincidence. This is
what serious attention to particulars looks like when the practitioner
trusts the practice.

What Hopkins models, that the modern reader most needs, is the
permission to look at *this thing, not its category*. He does not
write "the kingfisher" as representative of its species. He writes
the kingfisher catching fire — a specific bird in a specific instant
of motion, the perceiver alive to *that* instant. The catalog of
*vibhūtis* asks the same of its hearer. *Of fish I am the makara* is
not zoology; it is the disciple being asked to attend to the makara
specifically, because attending to particulars is what the practice
is. A reader struggling to grasp what the chapter is asking might
find Hopkins a useful key. Read "Pied Beauty" aloud, slowly, twice.
You will be doing what the catalog trains.

## Why this is a 2026 problem

The chapter is more legible to its first hearers than to us. They
lived in a world where attention to particulars was the default;
*vibhūti yoga* refined a practice the practitioner already had access
to. We do not have the existing capacity. Charles Taylor, in *A
Secular Age*, describes the modern condition as a shift from a world
in which the *place of fullness* was understood as unproblematically
outside ordinary life to a world in which fullness is constantly
relocated, contested, and made a matter of choice. The pre-modern
looker assumed there was something there to recognize; the modern
looker has to construct, against the cultural default, the posture in
which recognition becomes possible. We are doing the practice against
the grain.

The grain, in 2026, is the feed. The reader's daily attentional
default is scrolled-past. Hundreds of items pass under the eye each
day; almost none is *seen* in the sense the chapter means. Items
arrive in rapid sequence, decontextualized, optimized for the brief
flicker of emotional response that triggers the next swipe. The looker
does not have time to attend to one thing because the next thing is
already arriving. The particular has been stripped of its setting, its
history, its specific weight, and rendered as stimulus. The feed
produces depleted attention, low specificity, dampened affective
range, increased restlessness — the antithesis of what the chapter
requires. *Vibhūti yoga* in 2026 is what one does *instead of* the
feed, and the practice has to fight for shelf space in a day that is,
by default, the feed's.

What the practice asks is concrete. Pick one thing. A particular loaf
of bread from a particular bakery. A particular plant on the
windowsill, the one with the leaf that has been turning toward the
window for a week. A particular person — partner, parent, child,
colleague — whom you have stopped seeing because familiarity has done
its erasure. Spend a week attending to that one thing. Not as research
project. Not as gratitude exercise. Not as content for a journal. As
practice. Look at the bread until you see what the baker did. Look at
the plant until you see what *this* plant is doing in *this* light at
*this* moment of the year. Look at the person until you see them —
past your projection of what they have always been, into the specific
shape they have become, the specific weight they carry today. A week
is the minimum dose at which the practice begins to do what it does.
The bread teaches not bread; the bread teaches *looking*. And the
looking carries.

## The just and the loving

Iris Murdoch's phrase for what the chapter trains is the *just and
loving gaze*: attention to an individual reality, refusing to use that
reality for the looker's purposes (Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*,
p. 33). The two adjectives do the work. *Loving*: on the side of what
it sees, willing it well. *Just*: not flattering, not projecting, not
using the other for the looker's purposes. Simone Weil, from whom
Murdoch borrowed the word, wrote that attention *consists of suspending
our thought, leaving it detached, empty, and ready to be penetrated by
the object* (Weil, *Waiting for God*, "Reflections on the Right Use of
School Studies"). The emptiness is not absence; it is availability.

The catalog includes items the reader would not have chosen — *the
gambling of the cheats, the strength of the strong, victory, adventure,
the seducer in seducers*. It is not curated for prettiness. The chapter
does not say find some beautiful things and feel good. It says attend
to the highest of each kind, and some kinds are kinds the reader would
not flatter. *Of cheats I am the gambling*. The chapter is asking the
disciple to look at human achievement, including human achievement that
is morally vexed, with the same just and loving attention offered to the
kingfisher and the syllable A. Not to endorse the cheating. To see what
the cheats have that makes the gambling *of* the cheats different from
the gambling of the merely careless — the boldness, the calculation,
the willingness to wager what they have on the move that might not pay.
The recognition is moral information. The looker still has to decide
what to do with it. But the recognition has to come first; ethics on
top of unrecognized particulars is ethics on top of nothing.

## What the catalog refuses

The catalog refuses, by its form, two seductive misreadings.

The first is *survey*. The reader trained on listicles is tempted to
consume the catalog as informational — *here are the divine
manifestations, learn the list*. This is the misreading the chapter is
structured against. There is no quiz. The list does not need to be
remembered. What needs to happen is that the reader, moving through
the list, has actually attended to several of the items, slowly enough
that they have done their work on the reader's attention. If the
reader has read all sixty items and seen none of them, the chapter has
failed. If the reader has stopped at *I am the radiant sun* and
actually thought about what the sun is, has stopped at *of those who
keep secrets I am silence* and actually thought about what silence in
such a person is, the chapter has succeeded — regardless of whether
the rest were absorbed. The list is not a content delivery vehicle. It
is a series of invitations to look.

The second misreading is *abstraction*. The philosophically minded
reader is tempted to extract the *principle* — *the divine is found in
particulars, all excellence is one in source, attention is the path* —
and treat it as the payload, with the items as ornament. The
principle is correct, but the principle without the practice is empty.
*The divine is in particulars* is not a fact you can know in the
abstract; it is a recognition you have to have, *of a particular*, for
the claim to mean anything. The chapter does not deliver the principle
and then illustrate it; it delivers a sequence of particulars and lets
the principle precipitate out of the practice.

The catalog accumulates, and what it accumulates is *the reader's
practice of attending*. By the time 10.41–42 arrives, the universalizing
claim has weight only because the catalog has trained the reader to
recognize the fragments. The claim is the precipitate of the practice.
The reader who has not done the practice receives the claim as a
slogan; the reader who has done the practice receives it as the natural
extension of what they have been doing. This is why the chapter cannot
be summarized. A summary delivers the principle without the practice.
The chapter *is* the practice.

## What the awe research can and cannot say

Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt, in their 2003 paper "Approaching
awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion" (*Cognition and
Emotion* 17), proposed a working definition of awe that has structured
the empirical literature since. Awe has two appraisals: *vastness* —
the stimulus exceeds the perceiver's ordinary frame — and *need for
accommodation* — existing mental schemas cannot fully assimilate it
and must reorganize. The catalog of *vibhūtis* is, on this definition,
a designed sequence of high-vastness particulars across many domains,
piling instance on instance until the reader's schema for *what is
excellent, where is the excellence located, what does it have to do
with me* must reorganize. By 10.42 the schema has been broken open.
The chapter induces awe, in the form Keltner and Haidt describe.

Twelve years later, Paul Piff, Pia Dietze, Matthew Feinberg, Daniel
Stancato, and Keltner published "Awe, the small self, and prosocial
behavior" in the *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* (2015,
vol. 108). Across six studies — standing in a grove of tall eucalyptus,
recalling an awe-eliciting personal experience, watching nature
footage — induced awe was associated with a reduced sense of
self-importance (the *small self*), increased reports of feeling part
of something larger, more ethical decision-making in moral dilemmas,
greater generosity in allocation tasks, and increased willingness to
help others. The mechanism: awe reduces the salience of the individual
self, freeing attention for collective concerns. The *small self* is
recalibration, not deflation of agency.

The findings are bounded — US adults, mostly undergraduates,
short-duration manipulations — and they do not authorize the chapter's
metaphysics. They do not show that the *vibhūtis* are real fragments of
a real divine source. What they show is more modest and more useful:
the practice the chapter teaches has measurable effects on the kind
of person the practitioner is becoming. The eye trained on excellence
becomes, over time, an eye that treats other people somewhat better
than it would have. The catalog elicits awe; awe shifts the self's
stance toward others; the chapter's pedagogical bet — that training
the eye changes the looker — is mildly supported.

## A week, one thing

The practice, named as concretely as I can.

Pick one thing. A loaf of bread from a particular bakery, baked by a
particular baker, the kind someone has been making the same way for a
long time. A plant on your windowsill, the specific plant, not your
idea of plants. A person you live with whom you have stopped seeing
because they have become the assumption rather than the presence. A
piece of music — one piece, not a playlist. A tree on your walk to
work.

Spend ten or fifteen minutes a day, for a week, attending to that
thing. Not researching it. Not photographing it. Not crafting a
sentence about it. Looking, listening, being with it. The first day
will be disappointing; you will mostly meet your own projections. The
third day, the projection thins. The seventh day is when the practice
has taken hold; you will notice that during the rest of the day, the
rest of the world is slightly more available to your attention than
before. The bread teaches looking. The plant teaches looking. The
person teaches looking.

Do not make this a project. Projects have outputs and deadlines. The
practice has none. If you make it a project the practice degrades into
self-improvement, which is exactly what the chapter is not about.
There is no metric. There is only the practice and the eye it trains.

Then carry the eye. The colleague who has done a hard job for twenty
years. The stranger whose specific competence you would otherwise have
walked past. The rival whose excellence is real. The parent in
decline whose specificity is being eroded by their illness — see them
*now*, while the seeing still receives a self that is still there. The
child whose particular shape changes weekly. The work in front of you
that you have been treating as a category — *this email, this code,
this problem* — and that, attended to, has its own specific weight.
The training carries.

This is how *vibhūti yoga* prepares for Chapter 11. What follows shows
Arjuna the universal form — vastness past holding, time as devourer,
the totality at full intensity — and Arjuna, even after having asked
for it, becomes terrified and asks for the gentler form back. The eye
trained on the catalog of particulars can survive, briefly, the vision
of the totality, because it has practiced what attention to one
fragment is, and the totality is the source of the fragments. Without
the training, the vision would simply destroy the looker. The catalog
is the eye's survival kit for what comes next.

But that is next chapter's work. This chapter's work is the eye.

The catalog ends, and Krishna says *I support this entire universe
with a single fragment of myself*. Of all that has been listed, all
that has been attended to, all that could ever be listed and attended
to — the source rests in a fragment. The catalog has not exhausted the
divine; it has barely begun. The practice is endless, in the sense
that there will always be one more thing to attend to, and the eye
that has been trained on this thing is then a slightly better eye for
the next. The practice is also endless in the sense that every
particular has more in it than any single sitting could exhaust. The
plant on the windowsill on the fourteenth day is not the plant on
the windowsill on the seventh day; the bread baked Tuesday is not the
bread baked Friday; the person across the table from you tonight is
not the person who sat there last winter. The catalog reads as if it
were a list of fixed glories, and the surface text is. The practice it
trains, however, is for items that change while one is looking at
them. The looker becomes someone who is changing while looking, looking
at things that are changing too.

Tomorrow morning, before the screen has had its first hour, pick one
thing. Look at it until you can see it. That is the chapter,
started — and this is a chapter that asks you to start it, not to
finish it.
