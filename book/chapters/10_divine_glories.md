# Chapter 10 — *Vibhūti Yoga*: The Yoga of Divine Glories

*Theophany through particulars; the divine as the "best of" each kind; recognition as a discipline.*

---

## § 1. The dialogue

*Tell me the glories by which you pervade the world, and how I should meditate upon you*: Arjuna's request opens Chapter 10. The Sanskrit term throughout is *vibhūti* — manifestation, splendor, power, the consecrated ash of a deity's presence; in this chapter, the specific particular through which the divine shines.

Krishna's response is the longest enumeration in the Gita. *Of the Ādityas I am Vishnu; of lights I am the radiant sun; among the stars I am the moon. Of the Vedas I am the Sāma Veda; of the senses I am the mind, and in living beings I am consciousness.* The list runs through deities, sages, animals, weapons, mountains, rivers, trees, meters, syllables, seasons, and human excellences. *Of the seasons I am the flowering one*. *Of letters I am the syllable A*. *Of those who keep secrets I am silence, and the wisdom of the wise.* Most arrestingly: *I am the gambling of the cheats, and the splendor of the splendid; I am victory, I am adventure, I am the strength of the strong.*

Arjuna is silent through the catalog; the chapter is formally a near-monologue. He speaks at the start (10.12–10.18) to ask for the listing; Krishna ends (10.40–10.42) by saying no exhaustive enumeration is possible, and concludes with two universalizing statements: *whatever being is glorious, prosperous, beautiful, mighty in the world: know that to be a manifestation of a fragment of my splendor*, and *I support this entire universe constantly with a single fragment of myself*.

Two verses anchor the chapter. 10.20: *I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings; I am the beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings.* This sets the ground — the Self at the center of every creature is the same Self being enumerated through the catalog. 10.41: the universalizing claim that whatever is excellent in any kind is from a fragment of the divine splendor. The catalog stands between, fenced by interiority on one side and by enumeration's necessary inadequacy on the other.

The chapter does not argue. It lists. At the wave's far end, two framing statements say *no list could be enough; the source is not in any item but in the fact that the items are excellent at all*. The catalog is the training. Chapter 11's vision of the divine form is what the training has been for.

## § 2. The core concepts

Three concepts organize the chapter.

**Vibhūti.** The Sanskrit word names a particular through which something larger is disclosed. In ordinary religious usage it can mean the consecrated ash from a sacred fire — a small physical residue that carries the presence of what was burnt — or any manifestation, splendor, glory, power, or excellence. In Chapter 10 a *vibhūti* is not merely an instance but a *qualifying* instance: an item that, by its excellence within its kind, points beyond itself. The chief of the Ādityas, the radiant of the lights, the strongest of the strong. The structure is *the best of X*. After first use, *vibhuti* is unitalicized.

The listing's point is pedagogy, not classification. The disciple is being taught a way of attending: see what is highest in a kind, repeat the looking, let it become recognition. Recognition is the chapter's actual goal.

**Theophany through particulars.** The chapter advances a claim about how the divine is found — not by abstracting from the world to some remainder, but *in* the specific, by recognition of what each kind, at its best, discloses. The technical term in Christian theology is *theophany*: the showing-forth of the divine through visible forms. The strangeness of the Gita's catalog — *Vishnu, sun, mind, consciousness; spring, the syllable A, silence, the strength of the strong* — is meant to break the assumption that theophany happens only through certified religious objects. *Of the cheats I am the gambling*: theophany through what one would not have looked at.

The chapter holds immanence and transcendence together. 10.41 says everything excellent is from a fragment of the divine splendor — pure immanence. 10.42 says the entire universe is sustained by a *single fragment* of the divine — pure transcendence. The chapter does not resolve the tension; it lets the two statements stand.

**Recognition as discipline.** The third concept appears as the chapter's implicit ask. The catalog teaches a *kind* of looking that, once learned, applies wherever the looker turns. *Whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the world*: not on a list, but everywhere the eye finds excellence. The catalog is illustrative — every commentator from Sankara to Gandhi makes this point — and its function is to school the seeker in a habit of recognition, so the habit travels.

This is why the chapter is *vibhuti yoga* and not *vibhuti shastra*. *Yoga* is discipline, practice, a yoking of attention. The list is the curriculum, not the doctrine.

## § 3. The traditions speak

Six lenses, taken together. The catalog accumulates by pressure across many domains; the readings here accumulate similarly, by their disagreements held in one frame.

Begin at the strict-non-dual end. Adi Shankaracharya (eighth century) reads the catalog as *upalakṣaṇa* — illustration: a finite set of pointers, the highest of each kind, chosen because the highest in any kind has the least obstruction and so most clearly indicates the one Self. Substance, in paraphrase pending edition verification: *the catalog is not a doctrine of identity between Krishna and the things listed but a teaching method; the seeker fixes the mind on what is most excellent in any kind and through that excellence is led toward the source of all excellence, the one Self. The list is upalakshana. The practice is preparatory: it prepares the mind to dissolve the very distinctions the list seems to draw.* Sankara's transcendence pole is decisive at 10.42 — *one fragment* sustains all — and the cost is that recognition in *this* tree or *this* musician risks being read as merely transitional.

Rāmānuja (eleventh–twelfth century) reads in the opposite key. The world's plurality is real, individual selves are real, the divine is real, and the relation among them is body to embodied self — the cosmos is the *śarīra* of *Bhagavān*. Substance, in paraphrase pending edition verification: *the chapter's verses affirm rather than subordinate the world. The vibhutis are real glories, the Lord real, the relation between them that of body and embodied self. To see the lion among beasts as Krishna's vibhuti is to see the lion as a real limb of the cosmic body, distinct and irreducible, while also perceiving the inner self that animates the whole.* 10.20 reads full-force; the cost is that the transcendental remainder of 10.42 quiets.

Madhvācārya (thirteenth century) preserves *bheda* — real distinction — at every step: *Of the Ādityas I am Vishnu* is not identification but the claim that supreme excellence of any kind discloses the supreme excellence of the supreme Lord, who remains hierarchically over all. Substance, in paraphrase: *the lion is not Vishnu. Each excellence is a real, distinct excellence through which the supreme Vishnu manifests — but the manifestation does not erase the distinction between the manifesting reality and the manifested medium. The vibhutis preserve, rather than dissolve, the hierarchy they indicate.* Hierarchy foregrounds; intimacy quiets, and 10.20's "I am the Self in the heart" must be glossed to protect distinction.

Sri Aurobindo's *Essays on the Gita* (Pondicherry: Sri Aurobindo Ashram Trust; first series 1922, second 1928) reads each vibhuti as *partial self-disclosure* of a Divine still unfolding through Nature. Substance, in paraphrase with attribution: *each vibhuti is a sign-post — a partial self-disclosure of the Divine, given as guidance toward the totality of which it is a fragment. The catalog is not a fixed list but a way of seeing; once the seeker is trained, every excellence becomes such a sign-post. The discipline is preparatory to the totalizing vision of Chapter 11.* The dynamic dimension foregrounds; what quiets is the literal-personal Krishna of Ramanuja and Madhva — Aurobindo's Divine is impersonal at the metaphysical floor.

Gandhi's discourses, delivered at the Satyagraha Ashram at Sabarmati in 1926 and translated by Mahadev Desai as *The Gita According to Gandhi*, read the chapter for its practical-ethical force, in paraphrase: *the chapter's purpose is practical: to teach the devotee to recognize divinity in whatever is good, beautiful, and excellent in the world, and through that recognition to grow in reverence and ahimsa. What matters is what the practice does to the practitioner.* In the same lineage, Eknath Easwaran's *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, second edition 2007) renders 10.41 for modern Western readers as a meditation aid: *"Wherever you find strength, or beauty, or spiritual power, you may be sure that these have sprung from a spark of my essence."* (Easwaran 2007; page locator pending verification.) The discipline foregrounds; doctrinal weight quiets, and the strict-metaphysical readings would say the practice without doctrinal frame is rootless. Gandhi and Easwaran reply that the practitioner's first task is recognition, not theological argument.

The literary-aesthetic readers — Juan Mascaró (Penguin Classics, 1962) and Barbara Stoler Miller (*The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in Time of War*, 1986) — treat the chapter as world-poetry whose effect is cumulative. Mascaró's 10.20: *"I am the soul, prince victorious, which dwells in the heart of all things. I am the beginning, the middle, and the end of all that lives."* (Mascaró 1962; page approximate.) Mascaró's 10.42: *"But of what help is it to thee to know this diversity? Know that with one single fraction of my Being I pervade and support this Universe, and know that I AM."* (Mascaró 1962.) The capitalized "I AM" is Mascaró's deliberate echo of the Hebrew divine name. Miller's 10.41: *"Whatever is powerful, splendid, or majestic in creation, know that it springs from a fragment of my brilliance."* (Miller 1986.) For both, the chapter works the way Whitman's "I Sing the Body Electric," the Psalms' enumerations, and Smart's *Jubilate Agno* work — by cumulative pressure. The rhetorical force foregrounds; theological commitments quiet, and for doctrinal weight one returns to the *acharyas*.

The six lenses are not equally weighted by this manuscript; the manuscript's choice against Sankara's strong non-dualism for the modern reader was made in Chs 5 and 7, and the working synthesis (named in front matter) is Ramanuja–Aurobindo–Easwaran–Gandhi. What the six together display is the chapter's interpretive bandwidth: Sankara at the transcendence pole, Ramanuja at immanence, Madhva at hierarchy, Aurobindo on the unfolding-disclosure axis, Gandhi-Easwaran on the practical-discipline axis, Mascaró-Miller on rhetorical structure. The catalog accumulates; so do these.

### § 3.7. Anchor verse — 10.41 in four translations

The chapter's universalizing claim, side-by-side, so the reader sees what the lenses argue about at the level of the rendered text.

- Easwaran: *"Wherever you find strength, or beauty, or spiritual power, you may be sure that these have sprung from a spark of my essence."*
- Mascaró: *"Whatever in this world is powerful, beautiful or glorious, that you may know to have come from a fragment of my splendor."*
- Miller: *"Whatever is powerful, splendid, or majestic in creation, know that it springs from a fragment of my brilliance."*
- Radhakrishnan: *"Whatsoever being there is, endowed with glory and grace and vigour, know that to have sprung from a fragment of My splendour."*

The translators agree on the structure and disagree mostly on diction (essence, fragment, spark, splendor, brilliance). The translation problem collapses to almost a single English shape; what the lenses argue is what to do with the result.

For 10.20, Miller's spare rendering is representative: *"I am the self abiding in the heart of all creatures; I am their beginning, their middle, and their end."* (Disagreement among translators turns on how to render *ātman* — true Self, soul, self, Supersoul — and whether the address feels mythic, philosophical, or personal.) For 10.42, Mascaró's *"with one single fraction of my Being I pervade and support this Universe, and know that I AM"* does the chapter's last gesture: a withdrawal, *of what use is so much listing? The whole rests on a fragment*. This is the chapter's hand-off to Chapter 11.

## § 4. Modern counterpoints

Modern voices, none interpreters of the Gita, help the contemporary reader hear what is at stake.

### § 4.1. Iris Murdoch on attention

Iris Murdoch's *The Sovereignty of Good* (Routledge, 1970) makes attention the central act of moral life. Against existentialist and utilitarian pictures of the moral self as free chooser or consequence-calculator, Murdoch argues morality lives in the patient look at a particular other.

> "I have used the word 'attention,' which I borrow from Simone Weil, to express the idea of a just and loving gaze directed upon an individual reality. I believe this to be the characteristic and proper mark of the active moral agent." (Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*, Routledge Classics edition, p. 33.)

The two adjectives do the work. *Just*: not flattering, not projecting, not using the other for the looker's purposes. *Loving*: on the side of what it sees, willing it well.

The catalog of vibhutis teaches just and loving attention. To recognize the chief of the Adityas, the silence of those who keep secrets — to recognize these *as such* — is to do what Murdoch describes. The Gita's pedagogy and Murdoch's moral epistemology point at the same act.

The lens to name: Murdoch is a Platonist moral realist — the Good has objective reality, attention to it is the moral life. Not religious in the doctrinal sense, but her Platonism is of a piece with the great religious traditions on what attention is for.

### § 4.2. Simone Weil on attention as prayer

Simone Weil — French philosopher, factory worker, Resistance courier, dead at 34 in 1943 — was the source from whom Murdoch borrowed the word *attention*. Her concentrated treatment is "Reflections on the Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God," in *Waiting for God* (trans. Craufurd, Putnam 1951; reissued Harper Perennial 2009).

> "Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer. It presupposes faith and love." (Weil, *Waiting for God*, Harper Perennial 2009, c. p. 57.)

> "Attention consists of suspending our thought, leaving it detached, empty, and ready to be penetrated by the object." (Weil, same essay, c. p. 56.)

Weil's account is more ascetic and theological than Murdoch's, but the structure is the same. To attend is to empty the will of preformed expectations and be available to what is there. The discipline is hard; most "attention" is something easier and more managerial.

Weil refines the chapter's practice in a specific direction. The vibhuti listing does not say *find what you already love and call it divine*. It says *attend to the highest of each kind*. "Highest" is a rigor term. The seeker is asked to look at what is excellent — including what they would not expect: *the gambling of the cheats*, *the strength of the strong*, the silence in those who keep secrets — and let the recognition land where it should, not where the seeker wishes.

The lens to name: Weil is a Christian mystic of an uncategorizable kind — Catholic catechumen never baptized, deeply read in Plato and the Upanishads, suspicious of institutional religion. The closeness of her account to the chapter's practice is convergent rather than derivative.

### § 4.3. Gerard Manley Hopkins on inscape

Gerard Manley Hopkins (1844–1889), Victorian Catholic poet and Jesuit, coined two terms for what *vibhuti yoga* is doing as aesthetic practice: *inscape* and *instress*. *Inscape* is the distinctive inner pattern of a thing — its specific, self-organizing form. *Instress* is the energy by which the inscape impresses itself on the perceiver.

His most accessible expression of inscape is "As kingfishers catch fire" (c. 1881–82, in *Poems*, 1918):

> "Each mortal thing does one thing and the same:
> Deals out that being indoors each one dwells;
> Selves — goes itself; *myself* it speaks and spells,
> Crying *Whát I do is me: for that I came*." (Hopkins, *Poems*, 1918; *The Major Works*, OWC, ed. Phillips, 1986/2002.)

Each thing *selves*. The transitive verb names exactly what the vibhuti recognition is for: attending to what each kind *does* when it most fully *is itself*. A kingfisher in full flight is one of the items the catalog could have named.

"Pied Beauty" (1877) is the closest English-language analogue to the catalog's rhetorical structure:

> "Glory be to God for dappled things —
> For skies of couple-colour as a brinded cow;
> For rose-moles all in stipple upon trout that swim;
> Fresh-firecoal chestnut-falls; finches' wings;
> Landscape plotted and pieced — fold, fallow, and plough;
> And áll trádes, their gear and tackle and trim.
>
> All things counter, original, spare, strange;
> Whatever is fickle, freckled (who knows how?)
> With swift, slow; sweet, sour; adazzle, dim;
> He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change:
> Praise him." (Hopkins, *Poems*, 1918.)

The parallel is striking. *Glory be to God for dappled things* is not a doctrine; it is a list. *He fathers-forth whose beauty is past change: praise him* is the universalizing turn, structurally identical to the Gita's 10.41–42: items particular, source one.

A Victorian Jesuit and *Mahābhārata* catalog poetry doing the same thing tells us something about what attention to particulars actually is — not a sectarian discipline but a way of looking the great traditions converge on when they get serious about theophany.

The lens to name: Hopkins's theology is explicitly Scotist — the haecceity of each created thing. His vocabulary (*inscape*, *instress*, *selves* as verb) is the precise English equipment for what the chapter teaches.

### § 4.4. Charles Taylor on the modern reader's predicament

Charles Taylor's *A Secular Age* (Belknap/Harvard, 2007) names the obstacle the modern reader brings to this practice.

> "We have moved from a world in which the place of fullness was understood as unproblematically outside of or 'beyond' human life, to a conflicted age in which this construal is challenged by others which place it 'within' human life." (Taylor, *A Secular Age*, c. p. 15.)

The Gita assumes a world in which fullness lies unproblematically outside ordinary life. The modern reader does not. Even the believer, Taylor argues, must now *choose* to construe the world religiously in a way the pre-modern believer did not. *Vibhuti yoga* in 2026 is not the simple recovery of a natural way of seeing; it is deliberate construction running counter to the cultural default. The catalog's first hearers learned to recognize a charge they assumed was there. We learn the recognition *and* keep faith with it against a background that would dissolve it.

The lens to name: Taylor is a Catholic philosopher describing rather than arguing for religious belief; the historical analysis stands independently of his commitments.

## § 5. Modern science

Two strands of empirical psychology speak directly to the practice. The science bounds rather than authorizes the chapter's metaphysics: it shows what the practice does to the practitioner.

### § 5.1. Dacher Keltner and the structure of awe

Awe has two appraisals: *vastness* (the stimulus exceeds the perceiver's ordinary frame) and *need for accommodation* (existing mental schemas cannot fully assimilate it and must reorganize). This is the working definition Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt laid down in their 2003 *Cognition and Emotion* paper, the foundation for the empirical research on awe that has followed.

> "We define awe as an emotional response to perceptually vast stimuli that overwhelm current mental structures, yet facilitate attempts at accommodation." (Keltner & Haidt, "Approaching awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion," *Cognition and Emotion*, 17(2), 2003, pp. 297–314.)

The paper distinguishes awe from related emotions (admiration, surprise, fear, elevation), identifies its prototypical elicitors (nature, art, exemplary moral action, perceived divinity), and grounds a research program extended by labs at Berkeley, Arizona State, and UC Irvine.

For Chapter 10, the structure illuminates two things. First, the catalog's items share high vastness within their kind: *the chief of the Adityas, the radiant of the lights, the silence of those who keep secrets* exceed ordinary frames. The catalog is, in effect, an empirically literate selection of high-vastness items from many domains. Second, *need for accommodation* matches the chapter's pedagogical pressure. The list piles instance on instance until the reader's schema for *what is the divine, where do I find it* must reorganize. By 10.42, the schema has been broken open.

The bound: Keltner and Haidt's paper is theoretical-observational synthesis, now backed by substantial empirical work on proximate psychological structure and behavioral effects. It does not adjudicate whether the awe-eliciting items are *actually* manifestations of a divine source.

The lens to name: Keltner is a careful empiricist with explicit philosophical interests; in *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder* (Penguin, 2023) he writes about practical implications while staying forthright about what the research can and cannot authorize.

### § 5.2. Piff, Keltner et al. on awe and the small self

Induced awe reduces the salience of the individual self and reliably increases prosocial behavior. Piff, Dietze, Feinberg, Stancato, and Keltner showed this across six experiments in their 2015 paper "Awe, the small self, and prosocial behavior" (*Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 108, no. 6: 883–899).

The inductions varied — standing in a grove of tall eucalyptus, recalling an awe-eliciting personal experience, watching nature footage, viewing an aesthetically powerful artifact. Across the six studies, awe induction was associated with:

- A reduced sense of self-importance (the "small self");
- Increased reports of feeling part of something larger;
- More ethical decision-making in moral dilemmas;
- Greater generosity in allocation tasks;
- Increased willingness to help others.

The proposed mechanism: awe reduces the salience of the individual self, freeing attention and motivation for collective concerns. The "small self" is recalibration, not deflation of agency.

The bound: studies were US adults, mostly undergraduates, short-duration. Effects are robust within the studies and replicated across six designs; long-term persistence and cross-cultural generality remain open.

For Chapter 10, the relevance is direct. The chapter teaches sustained attention to high-vastness particulars across many domains — a practice that reliably elicits awe — and awe has measurable effects on the self's relation to others. The chapter teaches a way of seeing with documented downstream effects on how the seer treats other people. The science supports the *pragmatic* claim; the metaphysical claim about *what* the awe is recognizing remains a matter of theological commitment.

## § 6. Synthesis — the path forward

The chapter, on my reading, teaches a discipline of recognition. The discipline has two parts. *First*, attend — slowly, repeatedly, with what Murdoch called a just and loving gaze — to the best of each kind. *Second*, recognize that the excellence you see partakes of, points toward, and is sustained by one source. The first part is a practice of attention. The second is a claim. Practice without claim is aestheticism; claim without practice is empty assertion. The chapter teaches them together.

Among the commentarial traditions, this synthesis is closest to Aurobindo's, with debt to Gandhi and Easwaran for the practical-discipline emphasis. From Mascaró and Miller comes the rhetorical understanding of how the listing works on the reader: cumulatively, by pressure, not by item-level proposition. From Murdoch and Weil, the English vocabulary for what *attention* is. From Hopkins, *inscape* and *instress*. From Keltner and the awe research, the empirical bounds: a real practice with measurable structure and documented effects. The strict-metaphysical readings — Sankara's strong non-dualism, Madhva's distinction-preservation, Prabhupada's literalist-personalist reading, the more elaborate forms of Ramanuja's body-of-the-Lord theology — are bracketed for this synthesis; the practice is intelligible from any of these standpoints and does not require them. (On the discipline of bracketing, see front matter.)

Why this synthesis? Briefly on each criterion.

*Textual fidelity.* The chapter lists. To read it as doctrinal treatise first and hymn second loses the rhetorical force of the listing. The synthesis takes the listing as the chapter's primary mode and reads the doctrine through it.

*Internal coherence.* The Gita repeatedly trains attention to the actual: the breath in Ch. 6, the leaf and flower in Ch. 9, the devotee in Ch. 12, the field in Ch. 13. Chapter 10 sits in that pattern, and structurally as the training stage for Chapter 11's overwhelming vision: the catalog teaches the eye what to look at; Chapter 11 then tears the eye open.

*Lived usefulness.* The synthesis gives a usable instruction: attend to the best of each kind; let the recognition land. Something a person can do today, in their kitchen.

*Honesty under science.* Keltner's awe research supports the practice's psychological reality without authorizing any particular metaphysical claim about its source. The synthesis commits to the practice while leaving the metaphysics open.

*Generosity to rejected readings.* The strict-metaphysical reading is right that the chapter makes claims, not suggestions. Krishna does not say "find some beautiful things and feel good"; he says *whatever is excellent is from a fragment of my splendor*. My synthesis absorbs this by holding practice and unifying claim together. Without the recognition of one source, the practice degrades into appreciation — which is not what the chapter teaches.

Two risks worth naming.

*Risk one: aestheticism.* "Find beautiful things and feel awe" is thin reading of a chapter that includes *the gambling of the cheats*, *the strength of the strong*, *victory*, *adventure*. The catalog is not curated for prettiness. The practice is recognition of what is excellent in *its* kind, including kinds where the excellence is hard to face. Murdoch's *just and loving* names the rigor: not flattering, not projecting, not using the item for the looker's purposes. Harder than appreciation.

*Risk two: privatized spirituality.* The catalog is pointedly social — the divine in the strength of the strong, in those who lead, in those who achieve. The practice has consequences for how one treats colleagues and rivals, how one spends money, how one regards human achievement. The synthesis is private only at the level of training; in its consequences, public.

## § 7. The chapter for a modern life

A reader picks this up on a Tuesday evening. They have spent the day on a screen, scrolled through hundreds of items, and almost none has been seen as itself. The flow has flowed past. The mind is full and attention is gone.

The chapter is the most direct intervention the Gita offers against this condition. The practice, in 2026:

**Pick a domain.** A category that matters to you. Music. Trees. People who have done one job well for forty years. Mathematical proofs. Old buildings. Animals you live with. Bread, well made. The art of one period in one place. Sports performed at the level where they become something else. Whatever the domain — provided clear excellence is findable within it.

**Attend to the best instances, slowly.** Choose one — a specific recording, a specific tree, a specific person. Sit with it. Watch, listen, walk around, reread. Go past first impression. The first is mostly your own projection; the second is correction; the third begins to be the thing itself. Murdoch's M did not arrive at fresh-and-spontaneous on her first pass. She arrived after work.

**Let the recognition land.** When the excellence becomes visible — the way the recording does what the music asks, the way the tree holds its shape against the weather, the way the person has become the work — let yourself recognize that *what you are seeing partakes of something larger than itself*. You do not have to believe the metaphysics; you have to do the looking. *Whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the world: a fragment of my splendor.*

**Repeat across domains.** Music today, trees this weekend, the person who fixes the stairs on Wednesday, the proof you have been trying to follow for a week. Different domains because excellence has different shapes.

**Notice the effects, but don't make them the goal.** The practice will, gradually, do specific things to you. Your sense of self-importance will become smaller. Your generosity of attention to others will increase. You will have more resistance to the scrolled-past mode (Piff & Keltner 2015 and successor work). These are by-products. If you make them the goal, the practice degrades into self-improvement, which is not the chapter's mode.

What the practice changes, medium-term, is how you read other people. The catalog lists *the strength of the strong*, *the splendor of the splendid*, *those who keep secrets*, the wisdom of the wise. These are people. Fully internalized, the practice makes you the kind of person who can see the colleague who has done a hard job for twenty years, the rival whose excellence is real, the parent who held something together for decades, the stranger whose specific competence you would have walked past. *This person, here, is excellent in their kind.* The recognition is moral as well as aesthetic.

What it changes about money: a person trained in recognition spends on the well-made and the long-lasting more than on the new and the fashionable — not as virtue-signaling but as side-effect of having learned to see what is excellent. The scrolled feed depletes the capacity that would be the bulwark against impulse buying; the practice rebuilds it.

What it changes about grief: a reader who has done this work for years, then loses someone close, can sometimes recognize *what was excellent in the specific person* in a way ordinary grief, fixated on absence, does not allow. The chapter's ground claim — the source of all excellence is one — gives the recognition somewhere to stand even when the specific item is gone. Not consolation but stability: a structure that does not collapse.

What the practice does *not* do, also worth naming. It does not exempt you from politics. The catalog includes *victory* and *strength* and the *gambling of the cheats* — human achievement that is morally complicated. Attention does not produce ethics on its own. A reader who attends fully to the excellence of a corrupt regime's exercise of power has done part of the practice — the seeing — and still has the *just* in *just and loving attention* to do.

It also does not exempt you from the rest of the Gita. Chapter 11 is coming. The catalog trains you to recognize the fragment; Chapter 11 demands that you face the totality, including time-as-devourer.

A reader puts this down on Tuesday evening. The week is what it is. Tomorrow morning, before the screen has had its first hour, they could pick one thing and attend to it as fully as it deserves. That is the chapter, started.
