# Chapter 10 — Train the Eye

The discipline this chapter teaches is recognition. Slow looking at one
particular thing until what is excellent in it becomes visible, and
then carrying that capacity to the next thing. The chapter looks like
a catalog on its surface — *of lights the radiant sun, of seasons the
flowering one, of letters the syllable A* — and if you read it as a
catalog you have already misread it. The list is the gymnasium. The
eye is the point.

This is the chapter most likely to be skimmed. The verses pile up;
your attention skids across them as it skids across a feed. That mode
of reading is exactly what the chapter is written against. The catalog
does not ask you to remember which mountain Krishna names. It asks you
to look at one mountain — the one you can walk to — until it stops
being *a mountain* and becomes *this* mountain.

The full claim: the divine is found through particulars, by sustained,
unflattering, loving attention to the best of each kind, slowly.
Sustained against distractibility. Unflattering against projection.
Loving in that you are on the side of what you see. Slow because
excellence in any kind discloses itself only to a gaze that does not
move on.

What comes next is Chapter 11, the vision of the universal form. You
cannot survive that vision without the eye the catalog trained. A
reader who has not learned to see one tree is not equipped to see a
thousand suns.

## What the catalog is doing

The next thirty verses pile particulars across deities, cosmos, senses,
scriptures, beasts, birds, mountains, rivers, the human, the
linguistic, the temporal, the silent (*of those who keep secrets I am
silence*). Two anchor verses fence the listing.

10.20: *I am the Self seated in the heart of all beings; I am the
beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings.* The Self at the
center of every creature is the same Self being enumerated through
the catalog. The list is not about external grandeur. It is about what
is most intimate, found in particulars.

10.41: *whatever is glorious, beautiful, mighty in the world: know it
sprang from a fragment of my splendor.* The catalog is illustrative,
not exhaustive. The loaf of bread is not in the verses. Your colleague
is not. The catalog is teaching you to find them.

It does not argue. It piles. The piling is the pedagogy.

## What recognition is — and is not

Recognition is attending to a specific thing until what makes it
excellent in its kind becomes visible *as* what makes it excellent in
its kind. Not judgment first; seeing first, with judgment emerging
from the seeing. You arrive without a checklist and let the object
disclose what excellence in this kind *is*.

This is harder than it sounds. In the first ten minutes you discover
you do not have access to the object; you have access to your own
preformed expectations *about* it. The first pass is checking the
object against expectation. The second is correction. The third —
sometimes the twentieth — is when the object begins to be present.

Recognition is not appreciation. Appreciation projects warmth at the
object; recognition lets the object's specificity become present. You
can appreciate a tree without ever having seen *that* tree — having
seen instead your idea of *tree*, gilded with the emotion you feel for
trees in general. The feed runs on appreciation. Nor is recognition
connoisseurship, which lets the category supply the standard.
Recognition lets *this* specimen disclose what its kind is capable of,
including what it is capable of beyond what the category already knows.

## Inscape

Two English words name what this chapter asks of you. *Inscape* — the
distinctive inner pattern of a thing, the particular shape by which it
is itself and not another. *Instress* — the energy by which the
inscape impresses itself on a perceiver attentive enough to receive
it. They give you the texture of having done it.

> *Each mortal thing does one thing and the same:*
> *Deals out that being indoors each one dwells;*
> *Selves — goes itself; myself it speaks and spells,*
> *Crying* What I do is me: for that I came.

*Selves*, used as a verb. Each mortal thing *selves* — speaks itself,
cries *what I do is me*. Recognition is being available for that
crying.

## The 2026 grain

The grain you are working against is the feed. Hundreds of items pass
under your eye each day; almost none is *seen* in the sense the
chapter means. Items arrive in rapid sequence, decontextualized,
optimized for the brief flicker that triggers the next swipe. You do
not have time to attend to one thing because the next thing is already
arriving. Recognition, under these conditions, is a counter-discipline.

## Just and loving

The discipline has two adjectives. *Loving* — on the side of what it
sees, willing it well. *Just* — not flattering, not projecting, not
using the other for your purposes. *A just and loving gaze* names this.

Recognition has to come first; ethics on top of unrecognized
particulars is ethics on top of nothing. The catalog includes items
you would not have chosen — *the gambling of the cheats, the strength
of the strong, the seducer in seducers*. It is not curated for
prettiness. The chapter does not say *find some beautiful things and
feel good*. It says attend to the highest of each kind, and some kinds
are kinds you would not flatter. Not to endorse the cheating. To see
what the cheats *have* — the boldness, the calculation, the willingness
to wager. The recognition is moral information; you still decide what
to do with it.

## What the research shows

Awe has two components: vastness that exceeds your ordinary frame, and
a schema that cannot assimilate it and must reorganize. The catalog of
manifestations is exactly this — a designed sequence of high-vastness
particulars piling until your schema for *what is excellent, where is
the excellence located, what does it have to do with me* must
reorganize.

Induced awe — in groves of tall trees, recalled experience, nature
footage — is associated with reduced self-importance, more ethical
decision-making, greater generosity, increased willingness to help
others. The findings are bounded and do not authorize the chapter's
metaphysics. They show something more modest: an eye trained on
excellence becomes, over time, an eye that treats other people
somewhat better. The chapter's bet — that training the eye changes the
looker — is mildly supported.

## The practice

A week, one thing. Pick one thing. A loaf of bread from a particular
bakery, baked by a particular baker who has been making the same bread
the same way for a long time. A plant on your windowsill — the specific
plant, the one with the leaf turning toward the window, not your idea
of plants. A person you live with whom familiarity has erased. A piece
of music — one piece, not a playlist. A tree on your walk to work.

Ten or fifteen minutes a day, for a week. Not researching it. Not
photographing it. Looking, listening, being with it. The first day is
disappointing; you mostly meet your own projections. By the third day
the projection thins. By the seventh, the rest of the day's world is
slightly more available to your attention than before.

Do not make this a project. Projects have outputs and deadlines; the
practice has none. If you make it a project it degrades into
self-improvement, which is what the chapter is not about. There is no
metric. There is only the practice and the eye it trains.

Then carry the eye. The colleague whose competence you have stopped
registering. The rival whose excellence is real and whose excellence
recognition is not authorized to soften. The parent in decline whose
specificity is being eroded by their illness — see them *now*, while
the seeing still receives a self that is there. The child whose
particular shape will not be this shape again. The work you have been
treating as a category, that, attended to, will not be the same when
you look at it twice.

The training carries because attention is one capacity, not many. The
eye that has learned to see one tree is the eye that meets the person
across the table. You build it once, on something small, and it begins
to operate everywhere your gaze does. Without it, the vision in
Chapter 11 would destroy the looker. The catalog is the eye's survival
kit for what comes next.

Tomorrow morning, before the screen has had its first hour, pick one
thing. Look at it until you can see it. That is the chapter, started.
This is a chapter that asks you to start it, not to finish it.
