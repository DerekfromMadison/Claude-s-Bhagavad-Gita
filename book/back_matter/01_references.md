# References

This book is a synthesis. The chapters do not carry inline
citations — the apparatus would interrupt the reading. This list is
the apparatus. It names the sources the book drew on, so a reader who
wants to follow an idea back to where it came from can.

## Translations of the Bhagavad Gita

The book draws on multiple English-language translations of the Gita.
The major ones, with the angle each tends to bring:

- **Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, various
  editions, including 2007). Contemplative-pastoral; the working
  rendering for daily-practice readings.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (Bantam, 1986). Literary scholar; foregrounds the
  *Mahabharata* dramatic context.
- **Juan Mascaró**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962).
  Mid-twentieth-century literary universalist; cadenced.
- **Graham Schweig**, *Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love
  Song* (HarperOne, 2007). Bhakti scholar; lyrical-devotional
  register.
- **W. J. Johnson**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics,
  1994). Precise academic translation, restrained interpretation.
- **Franklin Edgerton**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Harvard University
  Press, 1944). Foundational mid-twentieth-century philological
  rendering.
- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, *The Bhagavadgita* (George Allen &
  Unwin, 1948). Scholar-statesman; comparative philosophy.
- **A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
  (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1968 and later editions). Gaudiya
  Vaishnava devotional reading; the most widely circulated English
  Gita.
- **Laurie L. Patton**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 2008).
  Contemporary academic; foregrounds the dialogic frame.

## Classical and traditional commentators

- **Adi Shankaracharya** (eighth century). Non-dualism (Advaita).
  The world's plurality is finally not real apart from the absolute.
  His apophatic register carries the imperishable-beyond-the-
  unmanifest material in the book's eighth chapter, and his
  witness-distinction reading carries the book's thirteenth chapter.
- **Ramanujacharya** (eleventh–twelfth century). Qualified
  non-dualism (Vishishtadvaita). The world and individual selves
  are real and stand in relation to the divine. The book's
  metaphysical floor reads with him.
- **Madhvacharya** (thirteenth century). Strict dualism (Dvaita).
  The divine, individual selves, and matter are eternally distinct.
  Engaged honestly across the book; chosen against in most synthesis
  moves.

## Modern Indian interpreters

- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak**, *Gita Rahasya* (1915). Activist reading
  of the discipline of action.
- **Mahatma Gandhi**, *Anasakti Yoga* and various discourses on the
  Gita. Non-attachment as ethical practice; allegorical reading of
  the battlefield as the inner moral life.
- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita* (1916–1920) and *The
  Message of the Gita*. Integral Yoga — an evolutionary spiritual
  reading synthesizing action, knowledge, and devotion. The book's
  recurring metaphysical-cosmic horizon reads with him.

## Modern philosophers and contemplative writers

- **Iris Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good* (Routledge, 1970). The
  "just and loving gaze" as the moral capacity that runs through the
  book's chapters on attention and devotion.
- **Simone Weil**, *Waiting for God* and *Gravity and Grace*.
  Attention as prayer; attention as the rarest and purest form of
  generosity.
- **Hannah Arendt**, *Eichmann in Jerusalem* (1963) and *The Life of
  the Mind* (1971). Thoughtlessness as a moral category; the basis
  of the modern reader's "fourth gate" extension in the book's
  sixteenth chapter.
- **William James**, *The Varieties of Religious Experience* (1902)
  and *The Will to Believe* (1897). Faith as constitutive; the More
  the believing person stands in relation to.
- **Paul Tillich**, *Dynamics of Faith* (1957). Ultimate concern;
  the structural reading of faith the book uses.
- **Martin Heidegger**, *Being and Time* (1927). Being-toward-death
  as the structural category; engaged in the book's eighth chapter.
- **Viktor Frankl**, *Man's Search for Meaning* (1946). Meaning
  under unbearable conditions; the last freedoms.
- **Erich Fromm**, *The Art of Loving* (1956). Love as practice
  rather than feeling.
- **Bernard Williams**, *Moral Luck* (1981) and *Ethics and the
  Limits of Philosophy* (1985). Tragic dilemmas and moral residue.
- **Alasdair MacIntyre**, *After Virtue* (1981). Tradition and
  virtue ethics.
- **Michael Polanyi**, *Personal Knowledge* (1958) and *The Tacit
  Dimension* (1966). Tacit knowledge and apprenticeship.
- **Rudolf Otto**, *The Idea of the Holy* (1917). The
  *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* — the structural signature of
  the book's eleventh chapter on the universal vision.
- **Gerard Manley Hopkins**. *Inscape* and *instress*. The poet of
  recognition the book's tenth chapter is in conversation with.

## Modern science

These are the named researchers and findings the book draws on. Each
finding is bounded — the science is engaged as constraint on the
practice, not as authorization of the metaphysics.

- **Robert Sapolsky** — stress physiology and the freeze response
  under acute threat.
- **Daniel Kahneman** — System 1 / System 2 cognition under load.
- **Marcus Raichle** — default mode network research.
- **Judson Brewer** — meditation effects on default mode network
  activation; *The Craving Mind*.
- **Sara Lazar** — cortical thickness in long-term meditators.
- **Amishi Jha** — attention training under high-stress conditions.
- **Anil Seth** and **Karl Friston** — predictive processing
  accounts of perception and the constructed self.
- **Antonio Damasio**, **Lisa Feldman Barrett** — affect, emotion,
  and the embodied self.
- **Richard Davidson** — affective neuroscience of equanimity and
  cultivated states.
- **Edward Deci** and **Richard Ryan** — self-determination theory;
  intrinsic versus extrinsic motivation.
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi** — flow.
- **Steven Hayes** — Acceptance and Commitment Therapy; cognitive
  defusion; values clarification.
- **Dacher Keltner** and **Jonathan Haidt** — psychology of awe;
  the small self and prosocial behavior.
- **Robert Plomin**, **Mary Rothbart** — behavioral genetics and
  temperament; heritability of trait variance.
- **Russell Foster**, **Leon Kreitzman**, **Matthew Walker** —
  circadian biology and the cognition–sleep relationship (with the
  honest caveat that some popular framings of this literature have
  overstated effects).
- **Delwin Paulhus** and **Daniel Williams**; **Kibeom Lee** and
  **Michael Ashton** — Dark Triad and HEXACO research on character.
- **Robert Trivers** — self-deception research.
- **Stanley Milgram** — obedience studies.
- **Atul Gawande**, *Being Mortal* (2014) — medical practice at the
  threshold; the dying parent.
- **Christopher Kerr** — hospice cognition research.

## A note on selection

This list is partial. The book draws on more than what's named here.
What it names are the sources whose ideas are most legible in the
chapters. A reader who wants to follow a thread further should start
with the named source. A reader who wants the wider literature should
consult any of the academic Gita translations (Miller, Johnson,
Edgerton, Patton) for their respective bibliographies.
