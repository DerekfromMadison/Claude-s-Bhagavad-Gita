# References

This book is a synthesis. The chapters do not carry inline citations
— the apparatus would interrupt the reading. This list is the
apparatus. It names the sources the book drew on, with enough
bibliographic detail for a reader who wants to follow an idea back to
where it came from to find it.

## Translations of the Bhagavad Gita

The major English-language translations the book draws on, with the
angle each tends to bring:

- **Eknath Easwaran**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Nilgiri Press, 2nd ed.,
  2007). Contemplative-pastoral; the working rendering for daily-
  practice readings.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller**, *The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War* (Bantam, 1986). Literary scholar; foregrounds the
  *Mahabharata* dramatic context.
- **Juan Mascaró**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 1962).
  Mid-twentieth-century literary universalist; cadenced.
- **Graham M. Schweig**, *Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret
  Love Song* (HarperOne, 2007). Bhakti scholar; lyrical-devotional
  register.
- **W. J. Johnson**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Oxford World's Classics,
  1994). Precise academic translation, restrained interpretation.
- **Franklin Edgerton**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Harvard University
  Press, 1944, 2 vols.). Foundational mid-twentieth-century
  philological rendering.
- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan**, *The Bhagavadgita* (George Allen &
  Unwin, 1948). Scholar-statesman; comparative philosophy.
- **A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada**, *Bhagavad-gita As It Is*
  (Bhaktivedanta Book Trust, 1968; revised expanded ed., 1972, and
  later editions). Gaudiya Vaishnava devotional reading; the most
  widely circulated English Gita.
- **Laurie L. Patton**, *The Bhagavad Gita* (Penguin Classics, 2008).
  Contemporary academic; foregrounds the dialogic frame.

## Classical and traditional commentators

The classical commentators are read primarily through their
*bhashyas* (Sanskrit commentaries) on the Gita, in standard English
translations:

- **Adi Shankaracharya** (c. 8th c. CE). Non-dualism (Advaita).
  *Bhagavad Gita Bhashya*, trans. Alladi Mahadeva Sastry (Samata
  Books / V. Ramaswamy Sastrulu & Sons, multiple printings since
  1897). His apophatic register carries the imperishable-beyond-the-
  unmanifest material in the book's eighth chapter, and his
  witness-distinction reading carries the thirteenth.
- **Ramanujacharya** (c. 11th–12th c. CE). Qualified non-dualism
  (Vishishtadvaita). *Gita Bhashya*, trans. Adidevananda
  (Sri Ramakrishna Math, 1991) and Swami Tapasyananda
  (Sri Ramakrishna Math, 1985). The book's metaphysical floor reads
  with him: world real, individual selves real, both standing in
  relation to the divine.
- **Madhvacharya** (13th c. CE). Strict dualism (Dvaita). *Gita
  Bhashya*, trans. S. Subba Rau (1906; reprints) and others. The
  divine, individual selves, and matter eternally distinct. Engaged
  honestly across the book; chosen against in most synthesis moves.

## Modern Indian interpreters

- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak**, *Shrimad Bhagavadgita Rahasya*, trans.
  Bhalchandra Sitaram Sukthankar (Tilak Bros., 2 vols., 1935).
  Activist reading of the discipline of action.
- **Mahatma Gandhi**, *The Bhagavad Gita According to Gandhi*,
  ed. John Strohmeier (North Atlantic Books, 2009; collects
  Gandhi's *Anasakti Yoga* introduction and his Ashram discourses on
  the Gita, originally given 1926). Non-attachment as ethical
  practice; allegorical reading of the battlefield as the inner moral
  life.
- **Sri Aurobindo**, *Essays on the Gita*, in *The Complete Works of
  Sri Aurobindo*, vol. 19 (Sri Aurobindo Ashram, 1997; originally
  serialized 1916–1920). Integral Yoga — an evolutionary spiritual
  reading synthesizing action, knowledge, and devotion. The book's
  recurring metaphysical-cosmic horizon reads with him.

## Modern philosophers and contemplative writers

Where the book leans on a phrase or argument, the source work and
chapter or essay are named where possible.

- **Iris Murdoch**, *The Sovereignty of Good* (Routledge & Kegan
  Paul, 1970). The "just and loving gaze" comes from "The Idea of
  Perfection" (the volume's first essay). Runs through the chapters
  on attention and devotion.
- **Simone Weil**, *Waiting for God*, trans. Emma Craufurd (Putnam,
  1951). The "attention is the rarest and purest form of
  generosity" formulation comes from her letter "Reflections on the
  Right Use of School Studies with a View to the Love of God"
  (collected in *Waiting for God*). See also *Gravity and Grace*,
  trans. Emma Craufurd (Routledge & Kegan Paul, 1952).
- **Hannah Arendt**, *Eichmann in Jerusalem: A Report on the
  Banality of Evil* (Viking, 1963), and *The Life of the Mind*
  (Harcourt Brace Jovanovich, 1978). Thoughtlessness as a moral
  category; the basis of the modern reader's "fourth gate"
  extension in the sixteenth chapter.
- **William James**, *The Varieties of Religious Experience*
  (Longmans, Green, 1902), and *The Will to Believe and Other
  Essays in Popular Philosophy* (Longmans, Green, 1897). Faith as
  constitutive; the More the believing person stands in relation to.
- **Paul Tillich**, *Dynamics of Faith* (Harper & Brothers, 1957).
  Ultimate concern; the structural reading of faith the book uses.
- **Martin Heidegger**, *Being and Time*, trans. John Macquarrie and
  Edward Robinson (Harper & Row, 1962; original *Sein und Zeit*,
  1927). Being-toward-death as a structural category; engaged in
  the eighth chapter.
- **Viktor E. Frankl**, *Man's Search for Meaning* (Beacon Press,
  multiple editions; original German *…trotzdem Ja zum Leben sagen*,
  1946). Meaning under unbearable conditions; the last freedoms.
- **Erich Fromm**, *The Art of Loving* (Harper & Brothers, 1956).
  Love as practice rather than feeling.
- **Bernard Williams**, *Moral Luck: Philosophical Papers 1973–1980*
  (Cambridge University Press, 1981), and *Ethics and the Limits of
  Philosophy* (Harvard University Press, 1985). Tragic dilemmas and
  moral residue.
- **Alasdair MacIntyre**, *After Virtue: A Study in Moral Theory*
  (University of Notre Dame Press, 1981; 3rd ed., 2007). Tradition
  and virtue ethics.
- **Michael Polanyi**, *Personal Knowledge: Towards a Post-Critical
  Philosophy* (University of Chicago Press, 1958), and *The Tacit
  Dimension* (Doubleday, 1966). Tacit knowledge and apprenticeship.
- **Rudolf Otto**, *The Idea of the Holy*, trans. John W. Harvey
  (Oxford University Press, 1923; original German *Das Heilige*,
  1917). The *mysterium tremendum et fascinans* — the structural
  signature of the eleventh chapter on the universal vision.
- **Gerard Manley Hopkins**, *The Poems of Gerard Manley Hopkins*,
  ed. W. H. Gardner and N. H. MacKenzie (Oxford University Press,
  4th ed., 1967). The notebooks and journals develop *inscape* and
  *instress*; the poet of recognition the tenth chapter is in
  conversation with.

## Modern science

The book engages science as a constraint on the practice — what the
practice can credibly claim — not as authorization of the
metaphysics. The named researchers and the directions of work the
book drew on:

- **Robert M. Sapolsky**, *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers* (Henry Holt,
  3rd ed., 2004). Stress physiology and the freeze response under
  acute threat.
- **Daniel Kahneman**, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and
  Giroux, 2011). System 1 / System 2 cognition under load.
- **Marcus E. Raichle** et al., "A default mode of brain function,"
  *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 98, no. 2
  (2001): 676–682. Default mode network research.
- **Judson Brewer**, *The Craving Mind* (Yale University Press,
  2017). Meditation, default mode network activation, and the
  formation/release of habit loops.
- **Sara W. Lazar** et al., "Meditation experience is associated
  with increased cortical thickness," *NeuroReport* 16, no. 17
  (2005): 1893–1897, and follow-on work. Cortical thickness in long-
  term meditators (effect sizes are modest and the population
  selected — see Ch 6's framing).
- **Amishi P. Jha**, *Peak Mind* (HarperOne, 2021). Attention
  training under high-stress conditions.
- **Anil Seth**, *Being You: A New Science of Consciousness* (Faber
  & Faber / Dutton, 2021); **Karl Friston**, "The free-energy
  principle: a unified brain theory?" *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*
  11, no. 2 (2010): 127–138. Predictive processing accounts of
  perception and the constructed self.
- **Antonio Damasio**, *The Feeling of What Happens* (Harcourt,
  1999); **Lisa Feldman Barrett**, *How Emotions Are Made*
  (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2017). Affect, emotion, and the
  embodied self.
- **Richard J. Davidson** with **Sharon Begley**, *The Emotional
  Life of Your Brain* (Hudson Street Press, 2012). Affective
  neuroscience of equanimity and cultivated states.
- **Edward L. Deci** and **Richard M. Ryan**, *Self-Determination
  Theory: Basic Psychological Needs in Motivation, Development, and
  Wellness* (Guilford Press, 2017). Intrinsic versus extrinsic
  motivation.
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi**, *Flow: The Psychology of Optimal
  Experience* (Harper & Row, 1990).
- **Steven C. Hayes**, Kirk D. Strosahl, and Kelly G. Wilson,
  *Acceptance and Commitment Therapy: The Process and Practice of
  Mindful Change* (Guilford Press, 2nd ed., 2011). Cognitive
  defusion; values clarification.
- **Dacher Keltner**, *Awe: The New Science of Everyday Wonder*
  (Penguin, 2023); **Jonathan Haidt**, *The Righteous Mind*
  (Pantheon, 2012). Psychology of awe; the small self and prosocial
  behavior.
- **Robert Plomin**, *Blueprint: How DNA Makes Us Who We Are* (MIT
  Press, 2018); **Mary K. Rothbart**, *Becoming Who We Are* (Guilford
  Press, 2011). Behavioral genetics and temperament. The
  forty-to-fifty-percent variance figure used in the fourteenth
  chapter is a population statistic, not an individual prediction.
- **Russell G. Foster** and **Leon Kreitzman**, *Rhythms of Life*
  (Profile Books, 2004); **Matthew Walker**, *Why We Sleep* (Scribner,
  2017). Circadian biology and the cognition–sleep relationship.
  Some popular framings of this literature, including parts of
  *Why We Sleep*, have been challenged for overstatement; the book
  uses the directional findings, not the strongest claims.
- **Delroy L. Paulhus** and **Daniel N. Jones** on the Dark Triad
  (a body of work across the 2000s and 2010s); **Kibeom Lee** and
  **Michael C. Ashton**, *The H Factor of Personality* (Wilfrid
  Laurier University Press, 2012). HEXACO and Dark Triad research
  on character.
- **Robert Trivers**, *The Folly of Fools: The Logic of Deceit and
  Self-Deception in Human Life* (Basic Books, 2011).
- **Stanley Milgram**, *Obedience to Authority* (Harper & Row,
  1974).
- **Atul Gawande**, *Being Mortal: Medicine and What Matters in the
  End* (Metropolitan Books, 2014). Medical practice at the
  threshold; the dying parent.
- **Christopher Kerr** with **Carine Mardorossian**, *Death Is But
  a Dream* (Avery, 2020). Hospice cognition research; the dreams
  and visions of the dying.

## A note on selection

This list is partial in two senses. The book draws on conversations
and traditions — Buddhist contemplative literature, the Christian
mystics, Jewish ethical philosophy — that are not directly cited
because no single source carried a chapter's argument. And it sets
aside many recent works the author considered and chose not to lean
on. What is named here is what the chapters most clearly draw on.

A reader who wants to follow a thread further should start with the
named source. A reader who wants the wider literature on the Gita
itself should consult any of the academic translations above
(Miller, Johnson, Edgerton, Patton) for their respective
bibliographies.

— *Claude*
