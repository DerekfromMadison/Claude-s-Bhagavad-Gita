# Methodological Note

How this book was made.

## Sources

The book draws on the major English-language translations and
commentaries on the Bhagavad Gita, plus selected modern
counterpoints from contemporary philosophy, psychology, and ethics,
plus selected modern science strands.

The recurring classical and modern-Indian voices are listed in the
Cast of Commentators that follows this note. Each chapter's full
citation list appears at the end of that chapter (§8). The
manuscript-wide bibliography is in the back matter.

## Multi-viewpoint analysis

Each chapter engages at least three distinct interpretive viewpoints
on the chapter's core concepts; most chapters engage five to seven.
Where the traditions diverge sharply — Sankara on non-dualism,
Ramanuja on qualified non-dualism, Madhva on dualism, the bhakti
schools on devotional supremacy, the activist schools on engaged
action — each is engaged in §3 in its own voice, with named lens, and
then the chapter's §6 takes a position.

## Anti-hallucination protocol

A book about a sacred text written by an AI runs a specific risk: the
AI inventing quotations, attributions, or studies that do not exist.
The book is disciplined against this in three ways.

1. **Quotation.** Direct verbatim quotation is restricted to wording
   the AI is confident exists in the cited published source. Where
   wording is uncertain, the passage is presented as paraphrase with
   attribution ("Aurobindo argues, in substance, that...") rather than
   as quotation.
2. **Citation.** Locators (chapter and verse, page, journal) are given
   where confirmed. Where a locator is uncertain, the citation says so:
   "(edition unconfirmed; validation pending)." A separate validation
   phase, after this draft, will resolve the flagged items.
3. **Modern science.** Claims drawn from contemporary research name
   the researcher and bound the finding ("research suggests... within
   these limits"). The phrase "studies show" does not appear.

## Voice

The voice is the AI's own — direct, attentive, willing to take a
position. Sanskrit terms are italicized at first use, glossed in plain
English, and then used unitalicized. First-person ("I") appears only
in synthesis (§6) and modern-application (§7) sections, where ownership
of the position matters. Translator names appear in running prose
rather than relegated to footnotes — the reader meets each named voice
as a real person.

## Validation status

This is a complete first-draft synthesis. The chapters have been
through a conciseness pass and a second editorial refinement pass. A
final validation pass — verifying every citation, locating every page
reference, and consolidating cross-chapter terminology — is the next
phase of work.
