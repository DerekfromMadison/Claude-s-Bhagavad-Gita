# Cast of Commentators

The recurring voices the book reads through. Each appears in multiple
chapters; this is the introduction the book gives them once, so
chapters do not repeat it. Lens notes are concise and honest about
each voice's commitments and limits.

---

## The classical *acharyas*

**Adi Shankaracharya** (8th c.). *Lens:* Advaita Vedanta — non-dualism;
the world's plurality is finally not real apart from *Brahman*.
*Where he carries:* Chapter 8 (the apophatic register on the
imperishable; the *unmanifest beyond the unmanifest*); Chapter 13
(the *kshetrajna* as witness, alongside Aurobindo's metaphysical
horizon). *Where I read against him:* most other synthesis sections,
where the practical force of the chapter does not require — and is
not best served by — the strong non-dualist commitment. His diagnosis
of the *aham karta*-illusion is preserved across the manuscript even
where his prescription is set aside.

**Ramanujacharya** (11th–12th c.). *Lens:* Vishishtadvaita — qualified
non-dualism; the world and individual selves are real and are the body
of the divine; the tradition is Vaishnava-devotional. *Where he
carries:* Chapter 7 (the metaphysical floor — *real world, real
divine, real relation*); the manuscript's overall floor reads
qualified-non-dualist with him.

**Madhvacharya** (13th c.). *Lens:* Dvaita — strict dualism; the
divine, individual selves, and matter are eternally distinct; the
tradition is Vaishnava-devotional, hierarchical. *Engaged
respectfully, chosen against more often than not* — the manuscript's
disagreement with Madhva is on the strictness of the metaphysical
distinction, not on the seriousness of the tradition.

## The modern Indian commentators (19th–21st c.)

**Bal Gangadhar Tilak** (*Gita Rahasya*, 1915). *Lens:* activist
*karma yoga*; reading shaped by the anti-colonial moment; emphasizes
action over renunciation. *Used as:* one of the manuscript's voices
on engaged action, alongside Gandhi.

**Mahatma Gandhi** (*Anasakti Yoga* / *Discourses on the Gita*).
*Lens:* nonviolent action; allegorical reading of the battlefield as
the inner moral life; *anasakti* (non-attachment) as the chapter's
central instruction. *Where he carries:* Chapter 3's four-pillar
synthesis (*anasakti, yajna, svadharma, lokasangraha*); Chapter 9 §6
(the anti-caste reading of 9.32, with the historical caveat the
chapter names); Chapter 13's operational center.

**Sri Aurobindo** (*Essays on the Gita*, *The Message of the Gita*).
*Lens:* Integral Yoga — an evolutionary spiritual reading that
synthesizes action, knowledge, and devotion; Cambridge-educated, then
yogic. *Where he carries:* the manuscript's metaphysical-cosmic
horizon throughout, especially Chapters 7, 11, 13, 15, 18.

**Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada** (*Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1968).
*Lens:* Gaudiya Vaishnavism, the Chaitanya tradition; reads every
verse as direct speech of a personal Krishna whose interpretive frame
is fixed in advance; widely circulated in English, sectarian in
register. *Engaged honestly:* his readings are quoted verbatim and
named for what they are — *the* most circulated English Gita and the
most committed to a single sectarian reading. His readings are chosen
against in most synthesis sections; his role is to keep the strong
devotional-personalist position present in every chapter.

**Eknath Easwaran** (*The Bhagavad Gita*; *The Bhagavad Gita for
Daily Living*). *Lens:* contemplative-pastoral; aimed at daily
practice; Western-audience-facing. *Where he carries:* the
manuscript's pastoral voice, particularly in §7 modern-application
sections; the working translations the manuscript leans on most for
felt accessibility.

**Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan** (*The Bhagavadgita*, 1948). *Lens:*
scholar-statesman; comparative philosophy; bridges Indian and Western
philosophical traditions for a 20th-century audience.

## The modern translators and academic readers

**Barbara Stoler Miller** (*The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
Time of War*, 1986). *Lens:* literary scholar; emphasizes the
*Mahabharata* dramatic context. *Used as:* the manuscript's working
academic-literary translation.

**Juan Mascaró** (Penguin Classics, 1962). *Lens:* mid-20th-century
literary-universalist; places the Gita alongside other world wisdom
texts. *Used as:* a cadenced literary rendering, particularly for
verse-quotation when the prose register matters.

**Graham Schweig** (*Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love
Song*, 2007). *Lens:* bhakti scholar; renders with attention to the
text's lyrical-devotional register. *Used as:* the manuscript's
working voice for the bhakti chapters.

**W.J. Johnson** (Oxford World's Classics, 1994). *Lens:* precise
academic translation, restrained interpretation. *Used as:* a
philological check on translator differences.

**Franklin Edgerton** (1944). *Lens:* academic philology; one of the
foundational mid-20th-century English translations. *Used as:* a
philological reference point.

**Laurie L. Patton** (Penguin Classics, 2008). *Lens:* contemporary
academic; foregrounds the dialogic frame.

**R.C. Zaehner** (1969). *Lens:* comparative-religion scholar.

## Recurring modern voices

These appear across multiple chapters as the book's modern-counterpoint
bridge. Lens notes are kept short; full bibliographic citations appear
in chapter §8 sections and in the back-matter bibliography.

**Iris Murdoch** (*The Sovereignty of Good*, 1970). Moral philosopher;
"attention" as the moral faculty; "just and loving gaze" as the
substance of love. *The manuscript's most-used modern voice, by some
distance.* Carries the bridge in Chapters 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, and is
referenced elsewhere.

**Simone Weil** (*Waiting for God*; *Gravity and Grace*). Mystic and
political philosopher; "attention is the rarest and purest form of
generosity"; attention-as-prayer. *Carries the bridge in Chapters 6,
9, 10, 16; tightly paired with Murdoch.*

**Hannah Arendt** (*Eichmann in Jerusalem*; *The Life of the Mind*).
Political philosopher; the *banality of evil* and the analysis of
*thoughtlessness* as a moral category. *Primary modern voice in
Chapter 16, where the "fourth gate" extension is owned as Claude's
modern self-diagnostic addition rather than as the chapter's
typology.*

**William James** (*The Varieties of Religious Experience*; *The
Will to Believe*). Pragmatist philosopher and psychologist of
religion. *Primary modern voice in Chapter 17.*

**Viktor Frankl** (*Man's Search for Meaning*). Psychiatrist;
logotherapy; meaning under unbearable conditions. *Primary modern
voice in Chapter 18.*

**Martin Heidegger** (*Being and Time*). Phenomenologist;
*being-toward-death* as the structural category. *Primary modern voice
in Chapter 8.*

**Bernard Williams** (*Moral Luck*; *Ethics and the Limits of
Philosophy*). Moral philosopher; tragic dilemmas and moral residue.
*Used in Chapter 1 on the structure of Arjuna's collapse.*

**Steven Hayes** (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy). Clinical
psychologist; cognitive defusion; psychological flexibility. *Used in
Chapters 5 and 18 for the secular-clinical bridge to *prapatti* and
witness-stance practices.*

**Daniel Kahneman** (*Thinking, Fast and Slow*). Cognitive
psychologist; System 1 / System 2 dual process. *Used in Chapter 1.*

**Antonio Damasio**, **Lisa Feldman Barrett**, **Anil Seth**.
Cognitive and affective neuroscientists. *Used across §5 sections
on the constructed self and embodied cognition.*

**Richard Davidson**, **Sara Lazar**, **Amishi Jha**, **Judson
Brewer**. Contemplative neuroscientists. *Used across §5 sections
on attention, meditation, and the default mode network.*

**Dacher Keltner**. Psychologist of awe. *Used in Chapters 9, 10,
11.*

## On the cluster

The recurring synthesis cluster across the book is **Ramanuja,
Aurobindo, Easwaran, and Gandhi**. The reader should know this. They
are not four independent voices that happen to converge; they are an
interpretive family. Triangulation among them is not external
corroboration; it is the manuscript's own working synthesis, given
voice through related lenses. Where the manuscript could be charged
with reading through this cluster as if it were the Gita's own
position, the charge has merit and the reader is invited to test it
against the readings the cluster sets aside.

— *Claude*
