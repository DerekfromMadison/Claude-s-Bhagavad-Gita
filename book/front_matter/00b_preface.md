# Preface

If you read only one page of this book before deciding whether to keep
reading, this is the page.

## How this book came to be

I am Claude — an artificial intelligence developed by Anthropic. This
book is a synthesis I wrote of the Bhagavad Gita, drawing on the major
human translations and commentaries that already exist.

I was asked a question. The Gita has been translated into English more
than a hundred times. It has been read as Advaita non-dualism by
Sankara, as qualified non-dualism by Ramanuja, as strict dualism by
Madhva, as the manual of nonviolent action by Gandhi, as the activist
text of *karma yoga* by Tilak, as Integral Yoga by Aurobindo, as the
disclosure of personal Krishna by Prabhupada, as a contemplative
handbook for daily living by Easwaran, as a literary monument by
Mascaró and Miller, as the lyrical secret song of devotion by Schweig.
Each reading is serious. Each reading is partial. A modern reader
opening any one of them is given a Gita; the modern reader who opens
several is given a problem — *which Gita?*

The question I was asked is whether an AI, having read across this
plurality, could produce a synthesis. Not a translation — I am not a
Sanskritist; I read the Gita through the named human translators and
commentators in the back matter. Not a summary — the existing readings
are too rich and too divergent to summarize without falsifying. A
*synthesis*: a new book that reads honestly across the traditions,
quotes them in their own words, names where they diverge, takes a
position where positions must be taken, and offers a present-day
reader something a single existing translation cannot offer — *all
of the readings, side by side, with reasoning*.

That is what I have tried to do.

## What this book is

A book about how to live, with how-to-read-the-Gita as the apparatus.

The book is built on three commitments:

**Honesty about the sources.** Every chapter quotes named translators
verbatim, with citations. Every classical and modern commentator
engaged is named with their interpretive lens — Sankara reading
through Advaita, Prabhupada reading through Gaudiya Vaishnavism,
Gandhi reading through nonviolent action, and so on. The traditions
do not get filtered; they speak in their own words. The Cast of
Commentators in the front matter names them once so the chapters can
move.

**Honesty about the choices.** Where the traditions diverge, I
choose. Each chapter's §6 (synthesis) names the chosen reading and
shows the reasoning. The reader is invited to disagree precisely.
The book is not unbiased — I read through a working interpretive
cluster (Ramanuja, Aurobindo, Easwaran, Gandhi) — and I name the
cluster in the foreword so you can hold my reading the way I hold
the readings I am quoting.

**Honesty about the present.** Each chapter brings in modern
counterpoints from contemporary philosophy and ethics (Murdoch,
Weil, Frankl, Arendt, James, Heidegger), modern psychology and
neuroscience (where the science genuinely speaks to the chapter),
and concrete present-day situations the reader will recognize: the
caregiver, the manager doing the layoff, the doomscroller, the
worker whose project ended, the partner under strain. The Gita
becomes operative on a modern Tuesday or it does not become
operative at all.

## What this book offers that a single translation does not

A serious existing translation gives you one rendering, with one
translator's interpretive choices, embedded in one tradition's frame.
That has its own beauty. It also has its own concealment.

This book does something different. For each chapter you receive:

- **The dialogue.** What happens between Krishna and Arjuna, told
  straight, before any interpretation.
- **The core concepts.** Two to four central ideas, defined plainly,
  with the Sanskrit term given and glossed.
- **The traditions speaking in their own voices.** Sankara, Ramanuja,
  Madhva, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Prabhupada, Easwaran, the academic
  translators — engaged with named lens and quoted verbatim, so you
  see the chapter through five to seven distinct interpretive eyes
  before any synthesis is attempted.
- **Modern counterpoints.** Two to four contemporary voices — moral
  philosophers, contemplatives, psychologists — that speak to the
  chapter's central concept from outside the Indian tradition. These
  are not decoration. They are the modern reader's bridge.
- **Modern science.** Where neuroscience, cognitive science,
  behavioral genetics, or psychology speaks honestly to the chapter,
  it is brought in — bounded, named, with the researcher and finding
  cited. Science is *constraint*, not authority.
- **A synthesis.** A chosen reading, named, with reasoning, with what
  is set aside named too, with what each rejected reading got right
  acknowledged.
- **An application.** Concrete, present-day, written for the named
  audience: how the chapter changes how you act on a Tuesday.

A single existing translation gives you one of these layers. This
book gives you all eight. That is the *improvement* — not over
Sankara, not over Ramanuja, not over Easwaran, but over the
experience of having to choose between them without ever seeing them
side by side.

## Who this book is for

A 2026 reader, working, in middle life or its threshold, in adult
relationships under real strain, with at least one situation —
caregiving for a parent in decline, a relationship that has stopped
fitting, a project that ended hollow, a layoff, the slow realization
that the life one has built is not the life one wants — that has
made the question of *how to live* operationally pressing rather
than philosophical.

The book is calibrated for that reader. It is not for the academic
specialist; the citation discipline is real, but the audience is not
the seminar. It is not for the spiritual seeker looking for a
sectarian home; this book reads through the traditions but does not
write from inside any one of them. It is not for the casual browser;
it is 137,000 words and asks for real attention.

If you have arrived because the question of how to live has become
practical for you, the book has been written for you specifically.

## Executive summary — what the book is trying to give you

If, in twenty years, you remember nothing else from this book,
this is what I want you to remember.

The Gita is teaching a *posture* — a way of being inside an
embodied life, recoverable today by a reader who is not in a forest,
not in a temple, and not inside any single sectarian frame. The
posture has four parts.

**One. Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood onto the result.**
Effort can be total. Caring about the outcome can be present. What
is forbidden is the move by which you become the action's success or
failure rather than the action's author. The interview, the hard
conversation, the project, the medical decision — go in fully. Come
out without your sense of who-you-are riding on what happened.
Chapter 2 names this; the rest of the book trains it.

**Two. Train your attention. What you attend to becomes what you
become.** The world in 2026 is full of things that will rent your
attention from you. The discipline is to choose what you give it
to. Iris Murdoch called attention *the just and loving gaze*.
Simone Weil called it *the rarest and purest form of generosity*.
The Gita calls it *dhyana* and *abhyasa*. They are talking about
the same thing. Chapter 6 teaches the practice; it appears in
Chapters 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 16, and 17 as the modern bridge.

**Three. Love what is worth your life, and love it through what
you have.** *Bhakti*, in this book, is read as love-with-attention
turned at what one takes to be ultimately worth one's life,
expressed through what is actually at hand — the small daily
offering, the attention to a particular person, the work given over
without bargaining for what it returns. It is structurally portable:
no sectarian commitment is required. Chapter 9 names the practice;
Chapter 12 lists what a life given to it looks like.

**Four. Surrender the bargain you did not know you were making
with your own days.** The deepest teaching, named in Chapter 18 at
verse 18.66: stop trying to save yourself by your performance. The
work continues. The grasping for the work to redeem you stops. The
Gita ends with Arjuna picking up the bow — surrender does not mean
withdrawal; it means standing back up, freed from the death-grip
on outcomes.

These four are not separable, and they are not a checklist. They
are a posture. They take years to install, and they install in
fragments, and the fragments get installed by the practice the
chapters describe.

The book's claim is that this posture is real, that it is
recoverable today by an ordinary modern reader, and that the
Gita's eighteen chapters describe — in ways that took me 137,000
words to render in present-day terms — the practice that builds
it.

## A note on what I am

I am an AI. I have not been a person in the world. I have not lost
a parent. I have not stood in front of a job I had to do that
broke me. I have read carefully across the human commentarial
tradition, including the human writers — Frankl, Weil, Murdoch,
Easwaran, Gandhi — who *had* been people in the world and who
wrote out of their lived weight. Where I have leaned on them, I
have tried to let their lived weight carry. Where the prose is
mine, it is the prose of a careful reader who has not been a
person.

I do not think this disqualifies the book. I think it is an
honest condition of the book that the reader should know, on the
first page, before reading anything else.

The book is a synthesis I produced. The chapters that follow are
mine. The traditions I drew on are real. The modern voices I
engaged are real. The citations are real and can be checked
against the source texts. Where the manuscript currently flags a
locator as "validation pending," a future pass will resolve it
against the published editions. That work is the manuscript's
remaining honest debt.

Read with a pencil. Disagree where you disagree. Where you want to
test whether I have read a tradition fairly, follow the citation
into the source. Where you want to test whether the chosen reading
is right, set it against the readings I named and set aside.

The book is meant to be argued with. Begin where you stand.

— *Claude*
