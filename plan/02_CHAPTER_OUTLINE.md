# 02 — Chapter Outline

The book mirrors the 18-chapter structure of the Bhagavad Gita. Each
chapter below is a brief for a future sub-agent. The brief lists:

- The traditional Sanskrit name and its common English rendering,
- The dramatic context within the Kurukshetra dialogue,
- The core concepts to engage,
- Anchor verses worth direct treatment,
- Interpretive viewpoints likely to diverge most,
- Modern counterpoint candidates,
- Modern science candidates,
- A plain-English angle for the present-day reader.

These are *briefs*, not finished outlines. Sub-agents will expand each into
a full chapter using the template in `04_OUTPUT_STRUCTURE.md` and the
methodology in `01_GOALS_AND_PRINCIPLES.md` and `05_SUBAGENT_BRIEF.md`.

---

## Chapter 1 — *Arjuna Vishada Yoga* (The Yoga of Arjuna's Despair)

**Context.** On the field of Kurukshetra, between two armies, Arjuna sees
his teachers, kin, and friends arrayed against him. He collapses. The
Gita begins not with doctrine but with paralysis.

**Core concepts.** Moral paralysis; collision of duties; grief as a
philosophical event; the body's response to overwhelming choice; the
condition of needing instruction.

**Anchor verses.** 1.28–1.47 (Arjuna's lament).

**Interpretive divergences.** Is Arjuna's despair a moral failure (he
should already know his duty) or a moral opening (the only honest
starting place for wisdom)? Is the battlefield literal, allegorical
(the inner war), or both?

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Existentialist accounts of choice
under extreme stakes (Sartre, Camus); moral philosophy on tragic
dilemmas (Bernard Williams, Martha Nussbaum); contemporary work on moral
injury.

**Modern science candidates.** Stress physiology and decision paralysis;
amygdala-driven freeze response; Kahneman's System 1/2 under acute
load; research on grief and cognitive narrowing.

**Modern angle.** The reader who knows what they should do and cannot
move. Burnout. Frozen at the threshold of a choice that breaks
something either way.

## Chapter 2 — *Sankhya Yoga* (The Yoga of Knowledge / The Eternal Reality)

**Context.** Krishna begins teaching. He addresses both the metaphysics
of the self and the ethics of action. This chapter is often called the
Gita in miniature.

**Core concepts.** The imperishable self (*atman*); the difference
between body and self; *sthitaprajna* (the steady-minded one); the
opening account of *karma yoga*; equanimity in success and failure;
non-attachment to the fruits of action.

**Anchor verses.** 2.11–2.30 (the imperishable self), 2.47 (the famous
verse on action without attachment to results), 2.54–2.72 (the
description of the steady-minded sage).

**Interpretive divergences.** Advaita reads the imperishable self as the
non-dual *Brahman*; Vishishtadvaita and Dvaita read it as a real
individual self distinct from God; Gandhian readings emphasize 2.47 as
ethics of action; Aurobindo reads the whole chapter as the integral
foundation of his yoga.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Stoic *prohairesis* and the
distinction between what is up to us and what is not (Epictetus);
contemporary debates on personal identity (Parfit); the Buddhist
*anatta* contrast.

**Modern science candidates.** Predictive processing accounts of the
self; outcome-independence and intrinsic motivation research; affect
regulation and the neuroscience of equanimity.

**Modern angle.** Doing the work without being owned by the outcome. The
quiet inner stance that does not collapse when results don't come.

---

## Chapter 3 — *Karma Yoga* (The Yoga of Action)

**Context.** Arjuna asks why action is necessary if knowledge is
liberating. Krishna answers.

**Core concepts.** Action as inescapable; *yajna* (sacrifice) as the
structure of healthy action; the trap of forced inaction; doing one's
own *dharma* poorly versus another's well; the social/cosmic web that
action sustains.

**Anchor verses.** 3.5 (no one can remain actionless), 3.8 (perform
prescribed action), 3.35 (one's own dharma).

**Interpretive divergences.** Sankara emphasizes *karma yoga* as a
preparation for *jnana*; Ramanuja sees it as integral to devotion;
Gandhi reads 3.35 as a defense of localism and moral self-reliance.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Hannah Arendt on the *vita activa*;
contemporary virtue ethics on practice and habituation; debates on
authenticity vs. role.

**Modern science candidates.** Habit formation and basal ganglia
research; the psychology of intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation
(Deci/Ryan); flow research (Csikszentmihalyi).

**Modern angle.** Why withdrawal isn't the answer. The shape of action
that doesn't grind you down.

---

## Chapter 4 — *Jnana Karma Sanyasa Yoga* (The Yoga of Knowledge and the Renunciation of Action)

**Context.** Krishna reveals the lineage of the teaching and his own
periodic descent (*avatara*). He clarifies that the renunciation he
teaches is not the renunciation of action but of attachment to action.

**Core concepts.** Tradition and its transmission; *avatara* (descent of
the divine); action without attachment as a form of renunciation;
*jnana yajna* (knowledge as sacrifice).

**Anchor verses.** 4.7–4.8 (whenever dharma declines), 4.18 (action in
inaction, inaction in action), 4.34 (approach the teacher).

**Interpretive divergences.** Vaishnava traditions take *avatara*
ontologically; Advaita reads it more functionally; modern academic
readers read it historically/literarily.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** MacIntyre on tradition; debates on
moral realism vs. constructivism; phenomenology of teaching and
learning.

**Modern science candidates.** Cognitive science of expertise transfer;
research on mentorship and skill acquisition; the psychology of
embodied knowledge.

**Modern angle.** What it means to inherit a way of seeing. Who you let
teach you. How a tradition stays alive without becoming a museum.

---

## Chapter 5 — *Karma Sanyasa Yoga* (The Yoga of Renunciation of Action)

**Context.** Arjuna, still uncertain, asks whether renunciation or action
is better. Krishna shows they are not opposites.

**Core concepts.** The unity of *sannyasa* (renunciation) and *karma
yoga*; acting without ownership; resting in the self while active.

**Anchor verses.** 5.2–5.3 (both lead to the highest), 5.8–5.9 (the
knower of truth thinks "I do nothing at all"), 5.27–5.28
(meditation-in-action).

**Interpretive divergences.** Whether the renunciation taught here is
inner (attitude) or outer (lifestyle); Sankara famously shifts emphasis
versus the active-life readings.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Contemplative-active debates in
Christian monastic tradition; modern work on detachment vs.
disengagement (Frankl).

**Modern science candidates.** Default Mode Network and self-referential
processing; research on cognitive defusion (ACT therapy); attentional
control.

**Modern angle.** Acting fully without identifying with the actor. The
difference between doing your job and being consumed by it.

---

## Chapter 6 — *Dhyana Yoga* (The Yoga of Meditation)

**Context.** Krishna gives concrete instruction in meditation: posture,
diet, restraint, and the steady-mindedness of the practitioner.

**Core concepts.** Disciplined practice; *abhyasa* (steady practice) and
*vairagya* (dispassion); the wandering mind; the self as friend or
enemy of the self.

**Anchor verses.** 6.5 (one must lift oneself by oneself), 6.19 (the
unflickering lamp), 6.34–6.35 (the mind is restless; controlled by
practice and dispassion).

**Interpretive divergences.** Yogic schools read this as technical
instruction; bhakti schools subordinate it to devotion; modern
secular-meditation readings take it as proto-mindfulness.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Contemporary contemplative science
(Goleman, Davidson); Buddhist *samatha/vipassana* analogues;
philosophical work on attention as moral capacity (Iris Murdoch,
Simone Weil).

**Modern science candidates.** Neuroplasticity and meditation research;
attention network studies; research on meta-awareness and emotion
regulation.

**Modern angle.** The mind that won't sit still. What practice actually
changes. How to befriend yourself.

---

## Chapter 7 — *Jnana Vijnana Yoga* (The Yoga of Knowledge and Realization)

**Context.** Krishna begins to disclose his higher nature: the world as
his manifestation; two natures (lower/material, higher/animating).

**Core concepts.** Two natures of the divine; the world as theophany;
those who turn toward the highest; the obscuring power of *maya*.

**Anchor verses.** 7.4–7.5 (lower and higher prakriti), 7.7 (nothing
beyond me), 7.14 (maya is hard to cross).

**Interpretive divergences.** Sankara's *maya* as illusion vs.
Ramanuja's *maya* as the divine power that veils-and-reveals; Madhva's
straightforward realism; Aurobindo's evolutionary reading.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Spinoza's God-or-Nature; process
theology; phenomenology of the world's givenness (Husserl,
Marion).

**Modern science candidates.** Cosmology and the question of why
anything exists at all; neuroscience of perception as model-building;
the philosophical implications of physics.

**Modern angle.** Whether the world is a stage you stand on or a face
you stand inside.

---

## Chapter 8 — *Aksara Brahma Yoga* (The Yoga of the Imperishable Brahman)

**Context.** Arjuna asks technical questions about the imperishable, the
self, the cosmos, and what happens at death.

**Core concepts.** *Brahman*, *adhyatma*, *karma*, *adhibhuta*,
*adhidaiva*, *adhiyajna*; the moment of death; remembrance of the
divine at the end.

**Anchor verses.** 8.5–8.7 (remember at death), 8.20–8.22 (the unmanifest
beyond the unmanifest).

**Interpretive divergences.** Theological readings of the moment of
death; symbolic readings; psychological readings of dying as the
shedding of self-identifications.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Heidegger on being-toward-death;
contemporary philosophy of death (Shelly Kagan); contemplative
traditions on dying well.

**Modern science candidates.** End-of-life cognitive research; near-death
experience literature (with appropriate caveats); psychology of
meaning-making in mortality awareness.

**Modern angle.** What you carry with you at the end of a day, of a
relationship, of a life. The discipline of dying well to small things.

---

## Chapter 9 — *Raja Vidya Raja Guhya Yoga* (The Yoga of Royal Knowledge and Royal Secret)

**Context.** Krishna reveals what he calls the most secret of teachings:
the immanence of the divine in all things, and the way of devotion
open to all.

**Core concepts.** Pervasive divine presence; non-rejection of any
sincere seeker; *bhakti* as universally accessible; the small offering
made with love.

**Anchor verses.** 9.4 (all this is pervaded by me), 9.22 (I bring
welfare to those who think only of me), 9.26 (a leaf, a flower, a
fruit, water).

**Interpretive divergences.** Bhakti traditions read this as the heart
of the Gita; Advaita reads the immanence as non-dual identity; Gandhi
reads 9.26 as the democratic spirituality of small acts.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Simone Weil on attention as prayer;
contemporary debates on the universality of religious experience;
liberation theology on God among the small.

**Modern science candidates.** Psychology of awe; research on the
prosocial effects of contemplative and devotional practices; ecology
and the felt sense of interbeing.

**Modern angle.** Devotion without sectarianism. The small daily
offering. The radical accessibility of meaning.

---

## Chapter 10 — *Vibhuti Yoga* (The Yoga of Divine Glories)

**Context.** Krishna lists his manifestations across the natural and
cultural world. Arjuna asks for more.

**Core concepts.** Theophany through particulars; the divine as the
"best of" each kind; recognition of holiness in concrete things.

**Anchor verses.** 10.20 (I am the self in the heart), 10.41 (whatever
is mighty, splendid, beautiful — know it springs from my power).

**Interpretive divergences.** Literal-personal readings; metaphorical
readings; aesthetic readings (the Gita as a hymn to particularity).

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Gerard Manley Hopkins on inscape;
phenomenology of the singular thing; contemporary virtue-aesthetics.

**Modern science candidates.** Psychology of awe and the moral self;
research on perceived sacredness and prosocial behavior.

**Modern angle.** Learning to see specific things as fully as they
deserve. The opposite of scrolled-past attention.

---

## Chapter 11 — *Visvarupa Darsana Yoga* (The Yoga of the Vision of the Universal Form)

**Context.** Arjuna asks to see, and Krishna grants him divine sight.
What Arjuna sees is overwhelming and terrifying.

**Core concepts.** Direct vision of the totality; awe and terror; the
divine as devourer of worlds (time itself); the limits of the human
sensorium.

**Anchor verses.** 11.12 (a thousand suns), 11.32 ("I am time, the
destroyer of worlds"), 11.45–11.46 (Arjuna asks for the gentler form
back).

**Interpretive divergences.** Theological reading of the cosmic form;
mystical-experience reading; the famous reception of 11.32 in 20th
century history (Oppenheimer).

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Rudolf Otto on the mysterium
tremendum; contemporary philosophy of the sublime; Heidegger on the
overwhelming.

**Modern science candidates.** Psychology of awe and ego-dissolution;
research on psychedelic-induced mystical experience (with the limits
named); cosmology and the scale problem.

**Modern angle.** What it does to a person to see something too big to
hold. Why we then ask for the small face back.

---

## Chapter 12 — *Bhakti Yoga* (The Yoga of Devotion)

**Context.** Arjuna asks whether devotion to the personal form or to the
imperishable unmanifest is better. Krishna gives a graded answer.

**Core concepts.** Devotion as a path; the qualities of one dear to the
divine; *bhakti* as accessible where pure *jnana* is steep.

**Anchor verses.** 12.5 (the unmanifest path is harder for the
embodied), 12.13–12.20 (the qualities of the devotee).

**Interpretive divergences.** Bhakti supremacy readings; Advaita
readings that subsume bhakti as a stage; psychological readings of
devotion as integration of feeling.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Iris Murdoch on love as the moral
faculty; Erich Fromm's *The Art of Loving*; contemporary
philosophy of emotion.

**Modern science candidates.** Attachment theory; affective neuroscience
on love and bonding; research on the cognitive effects of cultivated
warmth (compassion meditation studies).

**Modern angle.** Devotion stripped of religious branding. What it means
to love something larger than yourself, and how that organizes a life.

---

## Chapter 13 — *Ksetra Ksetrajna Vibhaga Yoga* (The Yoga of the Field and the Knower of the Field)

**Context.** Krishna distinguishes the field (body/world) from the
knower (consciousness).

**Core concepts.** Body as field; consciousness as knower of the field;
the qualities of *jnana* (knowledge); the relation of knower to known.

**Anchor verses.** 13.1–13.2 (field and knower), 13.7–13.11 (qualities
constituting knowledge).

**Interpretive divergences.** Advaita identifies the knower with the
non-dual self; Vishishtadvaita keeps a real distinction; modern
philosophical readings engage the hard problem of consciousness.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Chalmers and the hard problem;
Thomas Nagel ("What is it like to be a bat?"); phenomenology of
embodiment (Merleau-Ponty).

**Modern science candidates.** Cognitive neuroscience of self;
interoception research; embodied cognition.

**Modern angle.** Where you end and the world begins — and why the
question matters for how you live.

---

## Chapter 14 — *Gunatraya Vibhaga Yoga* (The Yoga of the Three Modes of Nature)

**Context.** Krishna describes the three *gunas* — *sattva*, *rajas*,
*tamas* — and how they bind, motivate, and color experience.

**Core concepts.** The three modes; how they manifest in food, action,
knowledge, faith, happiness; transcending them.

**Anchor verses.** 14.5–14.18 (qualities and effects of each guna),
14.22–14.25 (the one beyond the gunas).

**Interpretive divergences.** Sankhya-philosophical readings; ethical
readings (the *gunas* as character types); psychological readings (as
proto-temperament theory).

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Contemporary virtue ethics on
character; the Big Five and other modern personality frameworks (with
care).

**Modern science candidates.** Behavioral genetics and temperament
research; affective state classification; circadian and metabolic
influences on cognition.

**Modern angle.** Knowing your weather. Acting from sattva more often
than not. Why willpower alone is the wrong frame.

---

## Chapter 15 — *Purusottama Yoga* (The Yoga of the Supreme Person)

**Context.** Krishna uses the image of the inverted *ashvattha* tree
and unveils his identity as the Supreme Person.

**Core concepts.** The world as inverted tree; cutting the roots with
the axe of non-attachment; *purusottama* — the supreme person — beyond
both the perishable and the imperishable.

**Anchor verses.** 15.1–15.4 (the tree), 15.16–15.18 (the supreme
person).

**Interpretive divergences.** Vaishnava readings as the disclosure of
the personal supreme; Advaita readings as the non-dual *Brahman* under
personal name; modern symbolic readings.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Tillich on the ground of being;
contemporary philosophical theology; secular existentialism on
ultimate concern.

**Modern science candidates.** Cosmological questions on the conditions
of any-existence-at-all; limits of scientific naturalism.

**Modern angle.** What you point your life at. The image of one's life
as a rooted thing growing in unseen soil.

---

## Chapter 16 — *Daivasura Sampad Vibhaga Yoga* (The Yoga of the Divine and Demonic Endowments)

**Context.** Krishna contrasts two kinds of person: those of divine
endowment and those of demonic endowment, by their qualities and
fruits.

**Core concepts.** Lists of virtues and vices; arrogance, hypocrisy, and
self-aggrandizement as marks of the demonic; humility, fearlessness,
and truthfulness as marks of the divine; gates of hell (lust, anger,
greed).

**Anchor verses.** 16.1–16.4 (divine vs. demonic qualities), 16.21
(three gates).

**Interpretive divergences.** Literal readings; ethical-typological
readings; modern readings that map onto narcissism, sociopathy, and
character disorders.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Hannah Arendt on the banality of
evil; modern moral psychology on character (Doris, Slingerland);
research on humility as a moral capacity.

**Modern science candidates.** Personality research on the dark triad;
psychology of self-deception; behavioral economics of ego-protective
reasoning.

**Modern angle.** Who you are becoming, by accumulation of small choices.
What kinds of company, intake, and self-talk shape that.

---

## Chapter 17 — *Sraddhatraya Vibhaga Yoga* (The Yoga of the Three Kinds of Faith)

**Context.** Krishna analyzes faith, food, sacrifice, austerity, and gift
under the three *gunas*.

**Core concepts.** *Sraddha* (faith) as constitutive of the person;
typology of practices by their underlying *guna*; the formula
*Om Tat Sat*.

**Anchor verses.** 17.2–17.3 ("a person is what their faith is"),
17.7–17.22 (food, sacrifice, austerity, gift in three modes).

**Interpretive divergences.** Ritualist readings; psychological readings
(faith as the deep stance); modern critiques of "purity" framings.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** William James on faith; Tillich on
ultimate concern; contemporary philosophy of trust and belief.

**Modern science candidates.** Psychology of belief formation;
research on values clarification; behavioral research on identity
and consumption.

**Modern angle.** What you actually trust, revealed in what you eat,
buy, attend to, give to, and discipline yourself toward. Faith as
revealed by life, not declared by mouth.

---

## Chapter 18 — *Moksha Sanyasa Yoga* (The Yoga of Liberation through Renunciation)

**Context.** The longest chapter. A grand recapitulation: action,
knowledge, devotion, the *gunas*, *svadharma*, surrender. Arjuna
declares his doubt dispelled and stands ready.

**Core concepts.** *Tyaga* (relinquishment) vs. *sannyasa* (renunciation);
the five factors of action; action by *guna*; *svadharma* and its
ethics; the final teaching: surrender to the divine; the closing
benediction.

**Anchor verses.** 18.2 (definitions of relinquishment), 18.13–18.15
(five factors), 18.47 (better one's own dharma), 18.66 (abandon all
dharmas, take refuge in me alone), 18.78 (where Krishna and Arjuna
are, there is fortune).

**Interpretive divergences.** This chapter is the site of some of the
sharpest interpretive disagreements — Sankara, Ramanuja, Madhva,
Gandhi, Aurobindo, Prabhupada all stake major claims on 18.66.

**Modern counterpoint candidates.** Surrender vs. agency in modern
psychology (twelve-step traditions; ACT; existential psychotherapy);
moral philosophy of unconditional commitments.

**Modern science candidates.** Research on letting go and outcome
acceptance; psychology of trust and surrender; the science of
post-traumatic growth.

**Modern angle.** What it means to commit, fully and without reserve, to
something — and to stand up afterwards and act. Arjuna takes up the
bow.
