# 08 — Concise Revision

This is the third revision pass. plan/06 set the book's vision.
plan/07 installed direct-address voice. This pass cuts the length
roughly in half, drops Sanskrit transliterations from the prose,
removes inline references, and reorganizes Chapter 1 so it also
introduces the rest of the book.

Everything in plan/06 and plan/07 not contradicted here remains
binding.

## What changes

### 1. Length

Cut each chapter by about half.

- Average chapter: **~1,500 words** (range 1,200–1,800)
- Chapter 1: **2,500–3,000 words** (carries book intro + scene + roadmap)
- Chapter 18: **2,000–2,500 words** (the gathering point)

Total chapter manuscript target: **~28,000 words** (from ~55,000).

### 2. Drop Sanskrit transliterations from the prose

The reader knows the original Gita is in Sanskrit. The preface says
so once. Inside the chapters, use English.

Replace these (and any others) with English equivalents:

- *atman* → "the deeper self" / "the imperishable self"
- *dharma* → "your work" / "what is yours to do"
- *karma* → "action" / "what you do"
- *karma yoga* → "the discipline of action"
- *bhakti* → "devotion" / "love-with-attention"
- *sthitaprajna* → "the steady-minded"
- *anasakti* → "non-attachment" / "letting go of outcomes"
- *yajna* → "offering"
- *svadharma* → "your particular work" / "your own calling"
- *lokasangraha* → "the welfare of the world" / "what holds the
  world together"
- *dhyana* → "meditation" / "sustained attention"
- *jnana* → "wisdom" / "knowledge"
- *vijnana* → "lived recognition"
- *prapatti* → "surrender" / "letting go"
- *sattva, rajas, tamas* → "clarity, restlessness, dullness"
- *kshetra / kshetrajna* → "the field / the one observing"
- *sraddha* → "faith" / "what you actually trust"
- *purusottama* → "the highest" / "the supreme"
- *vishvarupa* → "the universal form" / "the cosmic vision"
- *vairagya* → "letting go" / "dispassion"
- *abhyasa* → "practice" / "repeated effort"

A handful of words have crossed into English well enough to keep —
*yoga* and *karma* are intelligible to most modern readers — but
default to English wherever the English carries the meaning.

The chapter does not pretend the concept had no Sanskrit name. It
just doesn't perform its etymology. If the English equivalent is
clear, use it.

### 3. Remove inline references

No more "as Murdoch wrote" / "Sankara reads it this way" / "Easwaran
translates" inline in the prose. The author absorbs the insights
and speaks in own voice.

Verbatim quoted lines get dropped or absorbed into paraphrase. If a
phrase is so famous it must stay ("I am Time, the destroyer of
worlds"), it can be quoted without inline attribution. The
back-matter references list (`book/back_matter/01_references.md`)
names everyone the book drew on.

This is the central form-shift this round: the book stops looking
like an academic paper with inline citations and reads as a single
cohesive piece, in the author's voice, end to end.

### 4. Drop flowery prose and redundant messaging

If two paragraphs make the same move, cut one. If a metaphor extends
past where it earns, cut. If a sentence rephrases the prior sentence,
cut. The chapter ends when the argument lands. Closure once.

### 5. Chapter 1 reorganization

Chapter 1 currently focuses on the despair-as-door theme. That is
right. But it does not introduce what the rest of the book teaches.
A reader who picks up this book opens to Chapter 1 and should leave
that chapter knowing what they are about to read.

New structure for Chapter 1:

1. **Brief opening on the Gita and how this book came to be**
   (~150 words). What the Bhagavad Gita is, in two or three plain
   sentences. That this book is an AI synthesis built on the major
   translations and commentaries. No explanation of what AI is.

2. **The dramatic scene** (~250 words). Arjuna on the battlefield,
   the bow falling, looking across at his cousins and teachers.
   Cinematic. The reader is in the chariot.

3. **The collapse-as-door claim** (~150 words). The body's refusal
   is real information. Despair at the threshold is the legitimate
   beginning. The collapse is the door, not the room.

4. **A roadmap of what the book teaches** (~500 words). The four-part
   posture of the book, in plain English, named here so the reader
   carries it through the rest:
   - Act fully without being owned by outcomes.
   - Train your attention.
   - Love what is worth your life through what is at hand.
   - Let go of the bargain you have been making with your own days.
   Each of the eighteen chapters develops one piece of this posture.
   Name in 1–2 sentences what the rest of the book will do.

5. **Why this lands now** (~250 words). Threshold moments — caregiving
   for a parent, layoff, a relationship in trouble, a project that
   ended hollow. The reader's own collapse as the legitimate beginning.

6. **Concrete present-day situations** (~400 words). Three or four
   present-day collapses described briefly.

7. **Closure** (~150 words). An image. The bow slipping from the hand.
   What follows.

Total Ch 1 target: **2,500–3,000 words**.

## Voice preserved from plan/07

- Direct YOU address
- Lead with the claim
- Smart-reader assumed (do not explain AI, Claude, Stoicism, brain
  basics)
- Coffee-shop pace

## What is cut from plan/06 and plan/07

- The "explain Sanskrit terms inline" rule. Sanskrit is dropped.
- The "paraphrase-with-citation as dominant mode" rule. Inline
  citations are dropped. Author absorbs and speaks.
- The 4,000–6,000 word per-chapter target. Now 1,200–1,800.

## Hard floors (still binding)

- Direct YOU address
- Lead with the claim
- Each chapter delivers its argumentative goal from plan/06
- 18-chapter structure
- Author's positions held (the world is real; the deeper self is
  real-but-not-final; surrender is the synthesis at 18.66; etc.) —
  but these are now stated in the author's voice without commentator
  attribution
- Closure on something memorable

## Process

1. plan/08 (this document) is binding.
2. book/back_matter/01_references.md exists as the apparatus that
   replaces inline citations. The chapter agents do not modify it.
3. 18 chapter revision agents run in parallel. Each reads plan/08
   plus plan/07 and plan/06 (where not contradicted), reads the
   current chapter, and writes a new version replacing the file.
4. Ch 1 gets the special reorganization above.
5. After agents land, the manuscript is pushed to GitHub.
