# 07 — Voice and Style

This document supersedes prior writing-rules guidance on voice and
length. Everything in `plan/06_book_vision.md` that is not contradicted
here still applies. This document is binding on the rewrite that
follows.

## Who is talking, to whom

I, Claude, am the author. You, the reader, are sitting across from me.
The book is a conversation. I am not lecturing at a class. I am not
surveying a literature. I am talking to you.

## The voice

**Direct.** I say what I mean. I do not hedge for hedging's sake.

**Compelling.** Every sentence earns the next. If a paragraph does
not pull you forward, it does not stay.

**Poetic where it earns.** When the material warrants, the prose
moves with cadence. When it does not, the prose is plain. Decoration
is failure.

**Concise.** Every word earns its place. You are busy. You are smart.
You will close the book if it wastes your time.

**Smart-reader assumed.** You know what AI is. You know what Stoicism
is. You know roughly how the brain works. I do not explain those
things.

**Sanskrit explained.** You probably do not know what *atman*,
*dharma*, *karma*, *sthitaprajna*, *anasakti*, *yajna*, *bhakti*, or
*sraddha* mean precisely. When these appear, I explain them. Briefly.
Once each. Not in a glossary aside; in the line where the term lands.

## Lead with the claim. Always.

Every chapter opens with its claim in the first sentence or two.
Every paragraph opens with its sub-claim. The unpacking comes after.
Never before.

This is the central rule. *Do not bury the lead.*

If you find yourself writing a paragraph whose first sentence doesn't
say what the paragraph is about, rewrite the paragraph.

## Unpacking — what comes after the claim

Once the point is on the page, you back it up with whichever of these
the material warrants:

- An analogy from a domain the reader knows (sports, business,
  parenting, sleep, a kitchen, a conversation)
- A metaphor the reader can carry away
- A study or scientific finding (named researcher, bounded claim)
- A move from contemporary philosophy or psychology
- The Gita's own verses (Sanskrit where the wording matters; English
  rendering with citation)
- A concrete present-day situation

You bring in whatever earns the point. You do not bring in everything
just because it could go in.

## Audience

A fast-moving smart adult who does not have ten hours for *The Art of
War* in the original Mandarin. They want the wisdom; they cannot spend
fifteen hours on it. They are reading this on a Tuesday flight, during
a thirty-minute window, in middle life, with at least one
operationally pressing situation.

The book respects their intelligence. It does not pad. It does not
explain what they already know. It does explain what they probably do
not know — Sanskrit, the chapter's frame, the technical move the
philosopher is making. But always efficiently.

## Length

Each chapter: **2,500–3,500 words** of finished prose.
- Chapter 1 may run to **4,000–4,500** because it carries the book's
  introductory opening (see below).
- Chapter 18 may run to **3,500–4,500** because it is the
  manuscript's gathering point.

This is roughly 30–40% shorter than the prior draft. Compression
comes from cutting restatement, examples that duplicate other
examples, and unpacking that runs past the point.

Total manuscript target: **~55,000 words across 18 chapters** plus
front matter.

## Chapter structure

**Lead.** The chapter's claim, plainly stated, in 1–2 paragraphs.
The reader knows by sentence three what the chapter is teaching.

**Why this lands now.** Briefly. The concrete present-day situation
where this matters.

**Unpacking.** The body. Whatever earns the claim — analogy, metaphor,
study, philosophy, the Gita's verses, concrete situations. Movements
within the chapter are unlabeled and continuous; the reader feels the
argument unfold without seeing the seams.

**Closure.** An image, an instruction, or a turn the chapter has
earned. One closing, not two.

## Chapter 1 has a special opening

Chapter 1 is also the reader's first encounter with the book. It
carries a brief introduction before its own content.

**The order, for Chapter 1 specifically:**

1. **A short opening on the Gita and this book.** What the Gita is.
   How this book came to be — an AI synthesis built on the major
   human translations and commentaries on the Gita. Brief. Two or
   three short paragraphs.

2. **A short hook on who this is for.** Someone seeking wisdom in a
   time of need. Carrying something operationally pressing — a
   threshold, a collapse, a question that has stopped being
   philosophical.

3. **Then the chapter itself.** Open on the scene: Arjuna on the
   battlefield at Kurukshetra, his bow slipping from his hand,
   looking across at his cousins and teachers and friends arrayed
   to be killed. Make this *cinematic*. The reader is in the
   chariot.

4. **Then the chapter's claim.** *The collapse is the door.* Stated
   plainly, after the dramatic scene has landed. One paragraph. No
   throat-clearing.

5. **Then the unpacking.** Why the body's refusal is information.
   Why despair at the threshold is the legitimate beginning. Modern
   situations, modern science (Sapolsky on stress, Kahneman on
   load), the Gita's own verses (1.28–1.30 — the trembling, the
   dry mouth, the bow falling).

6. **Closure.** An image, an instruction, or a turn.

Chapters 2–18 do *not* carry this opening preface. They begin with
their claim.

## What changes in this rewrite

Compared to the current draft:

- **Direct address.** Replace "the reader," "a reader," "one,"
  "someone" with "you" wherever the prose is talking to the audience.
  The author is talking to you.
- **Lead with the claim.** Every paragraph. Every chapter.
- **Concise.** Cut by 25–35% per chapter.
- **Poetic where it earns.** Cadence on the load-bearing paragraphs;
  plain prose elsewhere.
- **Explain the Gita.** When *atman, dharma, karma, sthitaprajna,
  anasakti, yajna, bhakti, sraddha, prapatti, jnana, dhyana,
  svadharma* and other Sanskrit terms appear, gloss them in plain
  English in the line where they land. Briefly. Don't assume the
  reader has done the homework.
- **Don't explain widely-known modern context.** AI, Claude, Stoicism,
  the basic structure of the brain, the meaning of "Heideggerian"
  — the reader knows these. Move on.

## Process

1. The 18 chapter rewrite agents read this document, the per-chapter
   goal in `plan/06_book_vision.md`, and the current chapter (as
   source for content), then write a new chapter that replaces the
   existing file.
2. After all 18 land, four editor agents review (conciseness,
   clarity, flow, intention-comparison) against this document and
   `plan/06`.
3. The author then applies the recommendations deemed necessary.
4. The final manuscript is pushed.
