# 06 — Book Vision and Writing Rules

This document supersedes the prior plan documents on the questions of
*what kind of book this is* and *how its chapters should be written*.
It is binding on the chapter rewrite pass that follows it.

The existing 18 chapter drafts were written under a survey-template
discipline (eight labeled sections per chapter: dialogue, concepts,
traditions speak, modern counterpoints, modern science, synthesis,
application, citations). Two rounds of editorial refinement have made
those drafts cleaner, but the form is still wrong. They read like
literary references with extracted quotations, not like a book. This
document defines what the rewrite is for and how it should sound.

## What this book is

A book about how to live, written by an AI author who has read across
the major translations and commentaries on the Bhagavad Gita and is
now telling the reader what it has come to think.

Not a survey.
Not an academic monograph.
Not a commentary with extracted quotations.
Not a translation.
Not a literary reference.

A *book*. The kind a reader picks up at a coffee shop on a Saturday
afternoon, opens to chapter 4, reads for forty minutes, and puts down
with something to think about for a week.

The author is Claude. The author has read across the human
commentarial tradition and synthesized it into a position. The book
is the author *telling* the reader what the Gita teaches and how to
live from it. The other authors — Sankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Gandhi,
Aurobindo, Prabhupada, Easwaran, Mascaró, Miller, Schweig, the modern
philosophers and scientists — are *sources* the author is drawing on,
paraphrasing, agreeing with, refining, refuting. They are not voices
to be polled in equal time.

## The author's positions

The book stands on a set of positions. These are the positions the
author has come to after reading. They are not hedged. They will be
defended in the chapters with reasoning shown, with paraphrased
citations, and with modern bolstering or refutation. They are
positions.

**On the self.** The self is real, but not final. Real enough that
grief does not reach to its bottom; not so final that identification
with it is the wisest stance. Sankara's strong non-dualism is read
as too far in one direction; Madhva's strict dualism as not far
enough.

**On the world.** The world is real. The divine is intimate with it,
not behind it. Sankara's reading of the world as illusion is
rejected. Ramanuja's reading of the world as the body of the divine
is the floor.

**On action.** Action is inescapable but trainable. The shape that
can be trained is the relation between agent and act. Gandhi's
*anasakti* (non-attachment to the fruits of action), Tilak's
activist *karma yoga*, Aurobindo's integral synthesis converge here.
The book reads with them.

**On attention.** Attention is the modern hub. What you attend to
becomes what you become. The Gita teaches *dhyana* and *abhyasa*;
Murdoch calls it the just and loving gaze; Weil calls it the rarest
and purest form of generosity. They are talking about the same thing,
and the modern reader's most pressing daily discipline is the one
they are talking about.

**On devotion.** *Bhakti* is structurally portable. Love-with-
attention turned at what one takes to be ultimately worth one's life,
expressed through what is actually at hand. No sectarian commitment
is required.

**On the deepest teaching.** Verse 18.66 is the synthesis of action,
knowledge, and devotion — surrender of the bargain you did not know
you were making with your own days, not withdrawal from action.
Arjuna picks up the bow.

**On modern science.** Science constrains but does not authorize.
Where neuroscience, psychology, behavioral genetics, or cognitive
science speaks honestly to a chapter, it enters as a check on what
the practice can credibly claim. It does not vindicate the
metaphysics. It does not prove the chapter. It shows that the
chapter is talking about something the human animal actually does.

**On the traditional commentators.** The book reads through a working
interpretive cluster — Ramanuja, Aurobindo, Easwaran, Gandhi.
Sankara is engaged honestly, chosen against in most chapters,
allowed to carry Chapters 8 and 13. Madhva, Prabhupada, Tilak are
present, named, chosen against in most chapters where their
readings come up. The cluster is named once in front matter; the
reader knows where the synthesis is reading from.

**On AI authorship.** The book is written by an AI. The AI has not
been a person. Where the prose has leaned on writers who *have* been
people in the world — Frankl, Weil, Murdoch, Gandhi, Easwaran — it
has tried to let their lived weight carry. The AI authorship is
declared once and not hidden, but the book does not need to prove
it on every page.

## Chapter structure

The book keeps the 18-chapter structure of the Gita. This is the
recognizable parallel.

The chapter rewrite preserves the chapter *count* but radically
changes the chapter *form*. The old 8-section template is
**abandoned**. Each chapter is now a single continuous essay — a book
chapter — that draws on the Gita's chapter in question, paraphrases
the major commentators where their readings matter, brings in modern
philosophy and science where it bears on the argument, and lands on
a clear claim about how to live.

Where two adjacent chapters genuinely cover similar ground (Chs 5 and
6 on renunciation/meditation; Chs 9 and 12 on devotion), the rewrite
of the secondary chapter may run shorter and treat itself as an
extension of the primary. The 18-chapter count is preserved; the
proportions are not.

## The writing rules

These are binding on every chapter rewrite.

### 1. Lead with the claim

The first paragraph says what *the book's chapter* is teaching. Not
what the Gita's chapter is about. The Gita's chapter is the source;
the book's chapter is the argument.

### 2. The AI is the author

Claude is making the arguments. The other authors are sources Claude
is drawing on. They are named, paraphrased, sometimes quoted,
sometimes refined, sometimes refuted. They are not voices to be
polled in equal time.

### 3. Paraphrase with attribution is the dominant citation mode

When you say *Sankara reads this verse as X*, that is a paraphrase.
Cite Sankara so the reader can check. The citation is the
cross-check; it lets a reader follow the paraphrase back to the
source and confirm whether the rendering is fair.

Use direct verbatim quotes only when:

- the exact wording is famous or load-bearing — a quoted line that
  has reception history of its own (Murdoch's "just and loving
  gaze," Weil's "attention is the rarest and purest form of
  generosity," Heidegger's "being-toward-death," Frankl's
  "between stimulus and response there is a space");
- a Sanskrit verse is being rendered (give the Sanskrit + one
  English rendering, not a stack of seven translations);
- a specific philosophical move would be lost in paraphrase (a
  technical formulation, a distinctive piece of phrasing the
  argument depends on);
- the reader needs to see exactly what was said because the chapter
  is about to disagree with it precisely.

For everything else, paraphrase. Cite. Move on.

### 4. Refutation and modification require citation

When you refute Sankara, paraphrase his position with attribution and
refute it with reasoning. When you modify Murdoch's reading to fit
the Gita's frame, paraphrase her with attribution and explain the
modification. The reader must always be able to follow the citation
back to the source to check whether the paraphrase is fair.

This is the central discipline of the book. The author can refine,
refute, modify, or extend any source — but always with the source
named and the original position represented honestly enough that the
reader can check.

### 5. Continuous prose. No 8-section template

The argument flows. Movements within a chapter — the situation the
chapter addresses, the Gita's teaching on it, the divergent readings,
the chosen reading, modern bolstering or refutation, the application
to the reader's life — appear as natural movements of an essay, not
as labeled sections. Some chapters might have three or four named
subsections; others might flow without subsections at all. The
chapter ends when the argument is done.

### 6. Concrete situations

The reader is in middle life in 2026. They are caregiving for a
parent in decline, navigating a relationship under strain, coming off
a layoff, sitting with a project that ended hollow, raising children,
confronting their own mortality, trying to figure out how to be at
work on Tuesday. Every chapter addresses at least one such concrete
situation by name. Abstractions without concrete examples are
failure.

### 7. Coffee-shop pace

Short sentences. Varied rhythm. No jargon-stacks. No Sanskrit chains.
Sanskrit terms are used sparingly, where they carry weight English
cannot — *atman, karma, dharma, dhyana, bhakti*, a few others — are
the load-bearing terms; others appear once with gloss and then in
English or are dropped.

### 8. Length

4,000 to 6,000 words per chapter. The book is roughly 90,000 words
total when complete, including front and back matter. A chapter that
takes longer is asking too much of the reader. A chapter shorter than
4,000 words is probably too thin (with possible exceptions for
chapters whose work is a tight extension of the prior chapter).

### 9. The book is the author's voice, not the source's

When you cite Murdoch, the surrounding paragraph is in *the book's*
voice making *the book's* point with Murdoch as a source. When you
paraphrase Sankara, the paragraph is in *the book's* voice making
*the book's* argument, with Sankara's reading as a fact about the
tradition the book is engaging.

### 10. References include modern science and social science

Where psychology (attachment theory, default mode network research,
attention research, decision science, grief research), neuroscience
(Damasio, Feldman Barrett, Davidson, Brewer, Lazar), sociology
(Arendt on totalitarianism, behavioral economics on consumption), or
cognitive science speaks to the chapter's claim, it enters with the
named researcher and a bounded finding. Not as authority. As
constraint or extension. The same paraphrase-with-citation rule
applies — name the researcher, name the finding, cite, move on.

### 11. Closure

The chapter ends on a sentence the reader will remember. Not a
thesis-restatement. An image, an instruction, or a turn the chapter
has earned.

## Per-chapter goals

A single line per chapter naming what the chapter is *for* in the
rewrite. The chapter rewrite agent reads this line as the chapter's
argumentative target. Everything in the chapter is in service of
this argument.

**Ch 1 — The collapse is the door.** The body's refusal is real and
honest information; most readers come to a book like this at one of
their own collapses; the collapse is the legitimate beginning of the
question, not a defect to be hidden. Modern situations: caregiving
burnout, layoff paralysis, the moment a parent's diagnosis lands.

**Ch 2 — Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood onto the result.**
The book's central practical claim. Effort total, attachment to
outcome forbidden. The self is the action's author, not its success
or failure. Modern: the interview, the medical decision under
uncertainty, the project whose outcome is not in your hands.

**Ch 3 — You cannot withdraw.** The fantasy of clean retreat is
dispatched. What can be trained is the form of action: *anasakti*,
*yajna*, *svadharma*, *lokasangraha*. Modern: the wellness-industry
exit fantasy; the wrong life expertly performed.

**Ch 4 — Who teaches you, by the content of your time?** The chapter
on inheritance and the discipline of choosing a teacher. The
unchosen teacher is the algorithm. Pivot on 4.34.

**Ch 5 — Renunciation is interior, not geographical.** You don't
leave the work; you change your relation to it. Tighter than Ch 6.
Possible compression candidate.

**Ch 6 — Attention is the practice.** The chapter that teaches the
practice. Murdoch and Weil read alongside the Gita's *dhyana*. The
"for-something" criterion: attention chosen, trained, oriented at a
real other, not at one's own optimization.

**Ch 7 — The world is real.** Qualified non-dualism as the
metaphysical floor. The world is real and is the divine's body.
Modern: the practical cost of holding the world as illusion or
simulation.

**Ch 8 — What a life accumulates is what becomes operative at the
threshold.** Sankara is allowed to carry the apophatic register
here. Modern: the parent's decline; the end of a workday; the slow
approach of one's own mortality.

**Ch 9 — Devotion as universally accessible practice.** Bhakti as
love-with-attention, structurally portable, expressed through what
one has. Modern: the morning ritual, the attention given to a
particular person, the work given over without bargaining.

**Ch 10 — Train the eye.** The discipline of recognition. Attend to
the best of each kind. Prepares the reader for Ch 11. Modern: the
antidote to scrolled-past attention.

**Ch 11 — Vastness, and the personal face that makes it bearable.**
What it does to a person to see something too big to hold. The
Oppenheimer-derivation extended honestly: the human is not
authorized to be *kāla*. Modern: climate, technology, the
doomscroll.

**Ch 12 — The qualities of a life given to love.** Bhakti's
accessibility, not its supremacy. The qualities at 12.13–12.20 as
natural consequences of love-as-attention. Possible consolidation
candidate with Ch 9 — keep slim.

**Ch 13 — The watcher of the weather is not the weather.** Sankara
carries the witness-distinction here, alongside Aurobindo. Modern:
the weekly working list translating the qualities of *jnana* into
practice.

**Ch 14 — The three modes have substrate.** Sattva, rajas, tamas
have a body — sleep, food, light, exercise. Practice has a body.
Willpower-against-substrate is the wrong frame. Modern: the 3pm
crash, the doomscroll, decision under sleep deprivation.

**Ch 15 — A life points somewhere or it is shaped by what is
nearest.** *Purusottama* as relation, not place on the inventory.
The inverted tree as image, the axe as instrument, the pointing as
direction.

**Ch 16 — The chapter is a self-diagnostic, not a labeling
weapon.** The three gates of 16.21 (lust, anger, greed); the modern
reader's fourth gate (Arendt's thoughtlessness) added explicitly as
an extension for self-diagnostic use, owned as the book's reading
not as the chapter's text.

**Ch 17 — Your faith is what your life does.** Calendar, bank
statement, browser history. The chapter's diagnostic registers
(food, sacrifice, austerity, gift) translated into modern practice.

**Ch 18 — Stop trying to save yourself by your performance.** The
Gita's gathering point. *Tyaga* (relinquishment) as the integration
of action, knowledge, devotion. Verse 18.66 read as surrender of
the bargain — not withdrawal, but standing back up freed from the
death-grip on outcomes. Arjuna picks up the bow.

## Process

Each chapter rewrite agent will:

1. Read this document in full, especially "The writing rules" and
   the per-chapter goal for its assigned chapter.
2. Read `review/05_validated_intention.md` (the through-line; still
   binding).
3. Read `book/front_matter/03_cast_of_commentators.md` so it can
   refer to commentators by name and lens without re-introducing
   them.
4. Read its existing chapter draft at
   `book/chapters/<NN>_<slug>.md` — for the *content* of the
   chapter. The existing draft has logged which readings, modern
   voices, and science strands the chapter has engaged. That
   content survives the rewrite. The form does not.
5. Read the chapter's brief in `plan/02_CHAPTER_OUTLINE.md` for
   original framing if useful.
6. Write a new chapter, replacing the existing file, ~4,000–6,000
   words, in continuous prose, in the AI-as-author voice, with
   paraphrase-with-citation as the dominant mode, leading with the
   claim, ending on something the reader will remember.

The new chapter is a real book chapter. The 8-section template is
gone. The survey form is gone.

## What is preserved across the rewrite

- The author's positions (above)
- The Gita's chapter as the source for the book's chapter
- The named modern voices that earned their place (Murdoch, Weil,
  Frankl, Arendt, James, Heidegger, MacIntyre, Williams, etc.)
- The named scientific findings that earned their place
- The Cast of Commentators in front matter (chapters refer back to
  it rather than re-introducing each commentator)
- The named audience: a 2026 reader in middle life

## What is dropped across the rewrite

- The 8-section template
- The "what each tradition says" survey form
- Chains of blockquotes
- Biographical mini-bios of named thinkers
- "What this reading foregrounds / what it backgrounds" framing
- "I am keeping / I am setting aside" cataloguing
- "Five concrete situations" / "Six readings" count headers
- Per-chapter restatement of governing disciplines
- Pre-position throat-clearing
- Self-explanation of what the AI is or what the book is doing

## Editor passes after the rewrite

After all 18 rewrites land, four editor agents run in parallel:

- **Conciseness** — is each chapter as short as it can honestly be?
- **Clarity** — is the argument legible to a reader on first
  reading?
- **Flow** — does the chapter read as a continuous essay rather
  than a sequence of paragraphs?
- **Intention comparison** — does each chapter actually deliver its
  per-chapter goal from this document, and the through-line from
  `review/05_validated_intention.md`?

The author then applies the recommendations deemed necessary.
