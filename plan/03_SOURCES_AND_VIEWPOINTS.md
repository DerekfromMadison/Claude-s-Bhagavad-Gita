# 03 — Sources and Viewpoints

This catalogs the source corpus the project draws on. It is not a
bibliography (the bibliography is part of the back matter) — it is a
working map of *which voices*, *which lenses*, and *which biases*
sub-agents must hold in mind as they engage each chapter.

Every author listed here gets a one-line lens note. Sub-agents must
preserve this when they introduce the author in-text, and may extend it.

## Classical Indian commentators (the *acharyas*)

These set the major theological readings and remain the gravitational
centers around which most later commentary orbits.

- **Adi Shankaracharya** (8th c.). Lens: Advaita Vedanta — non-dualism;
  the world's plurality is finally not real apart from *Brahman*. Bias:
  reads passages on devotion and action through the lens of preparatory
  stages toward *jnana*.
- **Ramanujacharya** (11th–12th c.). Lens: Vishishtadvaita — qualified
  non-dualism; the world and individual selves are real and are the
  body of the divine. Bias: reads in a Vaishnava-devotional register
  with strong emphasis on *bhakti*.
- **Madhvacharya** (13th c.). Lens: Dvaita — strict dualism; the divine,
  selves, and matter are eternally distinct. Bias: reads against
  identity-theses; emphasizes hierarchy and devotion to the personal
  Lord.
- **Abhinavagupta** (10th–11th c., Kashmir Shaivism). Lens: tantric
  non-dualism. Useful as a counterweight to the Vedantic readings.
- **Jnaneshwar** (13th c., *Jnaneshwari*). Lens: Marathi-vernacular
  bhakti synthesis. Notable for making the Gita accessible in a
  regional language with a poetic register.

## Major modern Indian interpreters (19th–21st c.)

These shape how the Gita has been received in the modern era and tend
to be the most quoted in popular and political reception.

- **Bal Gangadhar Tilak** (*Gita Rahasya*, 1915). Lens: activist
  *karma-yoga* — action over renunciation; reading shaped by anti-
  colonial nationalism.
- **Mahatma Gandhi** (*Anasakti Yoga* / *Discourses on the Gita*).
  Lens: nonviolence and selfless action; allegorical reading of the
  battlefield as the inner moral life.
- **Sri Aurobindo** (*Essays on the Gita*, *The Message of the Gita*).
  Lens: Integral Yoga; an evolutionary spiritual reading that
  synthesizes action, knowledge, and devotion.
- **Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada** (*Bhagavad-gita As It Is*, 1968).
  Lens: Gaudiya Vaishnavism; reads through ISKCON's theological
  commitments. Bias: very strong sectarian framing; widely circulated
  globally.
- **Paramahansa Yogananda** (*God Talks With Arjuna*). Lens: Kriya
  Yoga, esoteric/psychological reading aimed at modern Western
  seekers.
- **Vinoba Bhave** (*Talks on the Gita*). Lens: Gandhian, but more
  interior; emphasizes simplicity and self-rule.
- **Swami Chinmayananda** (*Holy Geeta*). Lens: Vedantic teacher
  rendering for a broad modern Indian audience.
- **Swami Vivekananda** (selected lectures and writings on the Gita).
  Lens: practical Vedanta, missional and modernizing.
- **Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan** (*The Bhagavadgita*, 1948). Lens:
  scholar-statesman; comparative philosophy; a bridge to a Western
  philosophical audience.
- **Eknath Easwaran** (*The Bhagavad Gita for Daily Living*; *The
  Bhagavad Gita*, translation). Lens: contemplative-pastoral; aims at
  daily practice.
- **Krishna Warrier**, **Bhaskar G. Bhattacharya**, and other 20th c.
  Indian scholars: useful as additional academic-Indian voices.

## Major Western and academic translators / scholars

The Gita has had a vigorous scholarly and literary life in English.
These voices tend to differ in *what they take the text to be* —
literary, philosophical, historical document — as much as in what
they think it means.

- **Charles Wilkins** (1785). The first English translation; lens:
  Enlightenment Orientalism, audience the East India Company and
  British learned societies.
- **Edwin Arnold** (*The Song Celestial*, 1885). Lens: Victorian
  literary-poetic; widely read; influenced Gandhi's first encounter
  with the Gita.
- **Annie Besant** (1895). Lens: Theosophical; combines a real
  knowledge of Sanskrit with a Theosophical interpretive frame.
- **Franklin Edgerton** (1944). Lens: academic philology; aims at a
  text-critical reading.
- **Juan Mascaró** (Penguin Classics, 1962). Lens: literary-universalist
  mid-20th-century; introduction places the Gita alongside other
  world wisdom texts.
- **Christopher Isherwood and Swami Prabhavananda** (1944). Lens:
  Vedanta Society; literary collaboration aimed at a Western audience.
- **Barbara Stoler Miller** (*The Bhagavad-Gita: Krishna's Counsel in
  Time of War*, 1986). Lens: literary scholar; emphasizes the
  Mahabharata context and dramatic structure.
- **Winthrop Sargeant** (interlinear edition). Lens: word-by-word
  philological; useful for Sanskrit verification.
- **W.J. Johnson** (Oxford World's Classics, 1994). Lens: precise
  academic translation with restrained interpretation.
- **Laurie L. Patton** (Penguin Classics, 2008). Lens: contemporary
  academic; foregrounds the dialogic and cultural frame.
- **Graham Schweig** (*Bhagavad Gita: The Beloved Lord's Secret Love
  Song*, 2007). Lens: bhakti scholar; renders with attention to the
  text's lyrical-devotional register.
- **R.C. Zaehner** (1969). Lens: comparative-religion scholar;
  philosophically oriented commentary.
- **Stephen Mitchell** (2000). Lens: literary; not from the Sanskrit
  but worked through other translations; valuable as a poetic gloss
  but flagged as derivative.
- **Lars Martin Fosse** (2007). Lens: precise modern academic
  rendering.

## Western philosophical and literary reception

Use these where the modern reader benefits from how the Gita has been
*read into* the Western tradition — and how the Western tradition has
shaped its reception.

- **Henry David Thoreau** (*Walden*; reading notes). Lens: American
  Transcendentalism; the Gita as confirming a self-reliant
  contemplative ethic.
- **Ralph Waldo Emerson**. Lens: American Transcendentalism; the
  "Brahma" poem and journal references.
- **Aldous Huxley** (*The Perennial Philosophy*; introduction to
  Isherwood/Prabhavananda's Gita). Lens: perennialism.
- **T.S. Eliot** (allusions in *Four Quartets*). Lens: high-modernist
  literary reception of the Gita's view of action and time.
- **J. Robert Oppenheimer** (1945, on 11.32). Lens: 20th c. moral-
  scientific reception; useful as a single point in Chapter 11 only.
- **Carl Jung** (occasional remarks). Lens: depth psychology; useful
  with care.

## Schools of interpretation as analytic categories

When sub-agents structure the multi-viewpoint analysis in a chapter,
they should think first in *schools* and only then in named voices.
At least three of the following schools must be engaged per chapter:

1. **Advaita Vedanta** (Sankara and successors).
2. **Vishishtadvaita / Sri Vaishnava** (Ramanuja and successors).
3. **Dvaita** (Madhva and successors).
4. **Gaudiya / ISKCON Vaishnava** (Prabhupada).
5. **Activist / Karma-yoga readings** (Tilak, Gandhi).
6. **Integral / evolutionary** (Aurobindo).
7. **Contemplative-pastoral modernizers** (Easwaran, Yogananda,
   Chinmayananda).
8. **Comparative / academic philological** (Edgerton, Johnson,
   Patton, Schweig).
9. **Literary-universalist** (Mascaró, Mitchell, Arnold, Miller).
10. **Theosophical / esoteric** (Besant, Yogananda's esoteric layer).
11. **Western philosophical reception** (Emerson, Thoreau, Huxley,
    Eliot).
12. **Feminist and post-colonial readings** (where available — to be
    expanded as research surfaces specific scholars).

## Modern counterpoint sources (philosophy, ethics, psychology)

Modern counterpoints should be drawn from named, attributable
thinkers. Working candidate list (not exhaustive):

- **Stoicism**: Epictetus (*Discourses*, *Enchiridion*), Marcus
  Aurelius (*Meditations*), Seneca (*Letters*).
- **Existentialism / phenomenology**: Sartre, Camus, Heidegger,
  Merleau-Ponty.
- **Moral philosophy**: Bernard Williams, Martha Nussbaum, Iris
  Murdoch (*The Sovereignty of Good*), Alasdair MacIntyre (*After
  Virtue*).
- **Contemplative philosophy / theology**: Simone Weil, Paul Tillich,
  Thomas Merton.
- **Psychology of meaning**: Viktor Frankl, Erich Fromm, William
  James (*The Varieties of Religious Experience*).
- **Buddhist counterpoints**: secular and traditional voices on
  *anatta*, *upekkha*, mindfulness — Stephen Batchelor, Bhikkhu
  Bodhi, Thanissaro Bhikkhu.
- **Modern moral psychology**: Jonathan Haidt, Joshua Greene,
  Owen Flanagan.

## Modern science sources

Science citations must come from named researchers, named studies, or
established findings — never "studies show." Working candidate list:

- **Cognitive / affective neuroscience**: Antonio Damasio, Lisa
  Feldman Barrett, Joseph LeDoux, Richard Davidson.
- **Attention and meditation research**: Amishi Jha, Judson Brewer,
  Sara Lazar, Daniel Goleman.
- **Behavioral / decision science**: Daniel Kahneman, Amos Tversky,
  Cass Sunstein.
- **Self and identity research**: Marcus Raichle (Default Mode
  Network), Anil Seth (predictive self), Thomas Metzinger.
- **Habit and motivation**: Edward Deci & Richard Ryan
  (self-determination theory), Charles Duhigg (popular synthesis,
  cite carefully).
- **Flow research**: Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi.
- **Stoic-clinical bridge**: Aaron Beck (CBT origins), Steven Hayes
  (ACT), Marsha Linehan (DBT).
- **Awe and prosociality**: Dacher Keltner.
- **Death, meaning, and post-traumatic growth**: Richard Tedeschi,
  Roy Baumeister.
- **Physics / cosmology** (used sparingly and without overreach):
  thermodynamic time (Sean Carroll); the observer in quantum
  mechanics (treated with appropriate skepticism about misuse).

## Source bias matrix (illustrative)

For each cited author, the in-text introduction names:

- **Period.** When were they writing?
- **Tradition.** Which school or movement?
- **Audience.** Who were they writing for?
- **Methodology.** How did they read the text — devotionally,
  philologically, psychologically, polemically?
- **Known limits.** Where does responsible scholarship now correct or
  caveat them?

Example: *Prabhupada's Bhagavad-gita As It Is (1968) reads through the
Gaudiya Vaishnava tradition of Chaitanya, addresses an emerging Western
devotional audience, and treats every verse as direct speech of a
personal Krishna whose interpretive frame is fixed in advance. It is
one of the most circulated English Gitas in the world; it is also the
most sectarian.*

## Source acquisition discipline

The project does not invent quotations. To meet the anti-hallucination
protocol in `01_GOALS_AND_PRINCIPLES.md`, sub-agents must:

1. Use only translations and commentaries that they can identify
   precisely (author, title, edition/year).
2. Where Claude does not have verified text in working memory, treat
   the passage as paraphrase and flag for validation.
3. Maintain a per-chapter `research/source_notes/chXX.md` file that
   logs every quote pulled, with locator, so later validation can
   re-check.
4. Prefer multiple translations side-by-side for any anchor verse —
   to surface where translators diverge.
