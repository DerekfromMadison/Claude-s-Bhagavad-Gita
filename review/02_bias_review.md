# 02 — Bias Review of *Claude's Bhagavad Gita*

A per-chapter survey of where the manuscript's bias presses harder than the
project's own quality bar can support. The project explicitly takes positions
(see plan/00 "not unbiased, not biased — synthesized") and bias-in-service-of-
message is acceptable. The job here is to surface the moments where bias is
either crowding out a tradition the chapter's own brief named, smuggling
metaphysical weight without flagging it, leaning on modern science as a stamp,
or stating the synthesis with more certainty than the cited sources support —
so that a downstream human reader can decide which to keep and which to
soften.

## Executive summary (manuscript-wide picture)

**Schools weighted heavily across the 18 chapters.**

The synthesis voice consistently leans — across most chapters — on a
**Ramanuja-Aurobindo-Easwaran-Gandhi cluster**: qualified non-dualism for the
metaphysics, integral synthesis for the dynamics, contemplative-pastoral
framing for usability, and Gandhian *anasakti* for the moral-political bridge.
This cluster is named openly in several chapters as the "chosen reading,"
which is the right discipline. The bias is integral to the project: this is
the family of readings most usable for a 2026 lay reader, and the brief
authorizes the choice. The cost is structural rather than per-chapter: across
the manuscript, **Sankara is the most consistently rejected voice**, and
**Madhva, Prabhupada, and Tilak are the three most consistently softened
voices**.

**Schools systematically under-weighted.**

- **Strict Advaita / Sankara.** Engaged technically in every chapter, but
  almost every synthesis section "sets aside" Sankara's strong reading. The
  cumulative effect is that the most influential commentator in the Indian
  tradition functions in this book as the consistent foil. Each individual
  rejection is reasonable; the pattern is heavier than the individual
  rejections explain.
- **Gaudiya Vaishnavism / Prabhupada.** Engaged in nearly every chapter with
  the same rhetorical frame — "the most circulated, the most sectarian"
  before the reading is presented. The labeling is not wrong. It is
  mechanically repeated and starts to function as a frame that prejudges.
- **Madhva / Dvaita.** Treated as a technical correction whose larger
  metaphysical commitments are repeatedly described as "rigorist" or
  "unflinching" and set aside.
- **Tilak.** Granted a serious hearing on a few chapters but described
  recurrently as "flat," "thin on metaphysics," or as "missing the
  experiential weight." The activist reading is allowed to win the surface
  point and lose the synthesis.
- **Theosophical / Besant; literary-poetic / Mascaró; Mitchell.** Appear
  briefly, often as decoration or as caveat. Vinoba, Yogananda, Vivekananda,
  Chinmayananda, Jnaneshwar, Abhinavagupta — named in plan/03 as part of the
  source map — are largely absent or mentioned in passing.
- **Feminist and post-colonial readings.** Plan/03 specifically flagged these
  as "to be expanded as research surfaces specific scholars." They are still
  absent. This is, manuscript-wide, the largest unfilled lens.

**Synthesis sections.**

The synthesis sections are mostly *exposing* their reasoning, which is the
project's discipline. The recurring move "I take from X, I set aside Y, here
is why" is honest and traceable. The two patterns where exposure thins are
(1) when the chosen reading is presented as serving "the modern lay reader,"
where the reader's posture is assumed rather than argued; and (2) when "the
chapter says" is used to mean "the chapter, on the chosen reading, says,"
which collapses the distinction the synthesis sections elsewhere preserve.

**Modern science.**

Generally bounded responsibly. Each chapter pairs the science with explicit
caveats ("this is the structure, not the metaphysics"; "the framework is
contested in detail"). The patterns worth flagging:

- The **default-mode-network / Brewer / Lazar / Farb** cluster is called on
  in chapters 2, 5, 6, 8, 13, 15. The recurrence is reasonable; the risk is
  that the same finding starts to function as the science layer rather than
  as one finding among several.
- The **Keltner-Haidt awe research** carries chapters 9, 10, and 11, with
  small bound-statements but big rhetorical weight; in chapter 11 it
  underlies the synthesis's claim that Arjuna's experience is
  "phenomenologically real," which is more than the research warrants.
- The **psychedelic ego-dissolution** material in chapter 11 is the manuscript's
  most boundary-pressing science-layer move.

**Modern application.**

The §7 sections are uniformly well-written but uniformly land on a similar
profile: an educated, white-collar, English-speaking adult with a phone, a
career, a long marriage or a complicated family, perhaps a parent in
decline. This is consistent with the audience the project names. It does
mean the manuscript's "modern reader" is one specific modern reader; the
chapters do not address — and do not claim to address — readers in
materially different conditions.

**Highest-priority moments for human attention** (~5–10):

1. Chapter 1 §6's framing of "strict pole A" (Advaita) as making the reader
   feel their collapse is "a confusion they should be ashamed of." This
   over-states the affective cost of Sankara's reading.
2. Chapter 2 §6's treatment of the *atman* as "regulative" rather than
   "falsifiable." The metaphysical move is large and is done in two
   paragraphs.
3. Chapter 4 §6's choice to read 4.7–4.8 (the *avatara* doctrine) as
   "function rather than ontology." The chapter explicitly sidesteps a
   metaphysical question the Vaishnava traditions take as central.
4. Chapter 5 §6's flat statement that the active-life reading is "the better
   reading of *this chapter as it stands*." The chapter rhetorically wins
   the verse against Sankara.
5. Chapter 9 §3.3's repeated frame around Prabhupada ("the most sectarian";
   "locks the chapter into a single sectarian register"). The rhetorical
   labeling is consistent across chapters and starts to do work.
6. Chapter 11 §6's reading of Oppenheimer's gloss as "wrong about the verse
   in a productive way," and its derivation of a 2026 ethical constraint
   ("the chapter does not authorize the human to be *kāla*") that goes
   beyond what the chapter explicitly says.
7. Chapter 13 §6's commitment to "Aurobindo's reading carries the chapter's
   own internal coherence furthest" — a strong sectarian preference dressed
   as text-internal coherence.
8. Chapter 16 §6's addition of a "fourth gate" (thoughtlessness) to the
   chapter's three gates. The synthesis is explicit that it is *adding*; the
   move is editorial, not interpretive.
9. Chapter 17 §6's treatment of *Om Tat Sat* as "horizon, not magic
   formula." This silently demotes a ritual-theological dimension the
   classical tradition takes more literally.
10. Chapter 18 §6's setting-aside of Sankara's strong reading as a "small
    subset of the qualified" — the demographic argument against the
    Advaita reading is weaker than the textual one and is doing more work
    than it earns.

These are not errors. They are places where the manuscript's chosen reading
is doing more work than the chapter's surface text alone can underwrite, and
where a reader who knows the rejected tradition will feel the under-weighting
sharply.

## Chapter 1 — *Arjuna Viṣāda Yoga*: The Yoga of Arjuna's Despair

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Tradition overweighting** at §3.2 near "This is the reading I will lean
on most heavily in this chapter."
- What the bias is doing: The chapter announces in §3.2 that Ramanuja's
  reading will be the load-bearing one, then later (§6) absorbs Sankara,
  Aurobindo, Gandhi, Stoler Miller, and Easwaran as correctives. The
  hierarchy is openly stated, but it is stated *first*, before the other
  schools have been fully heard.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter argues that
  Arjuna's collapse is real moral collapse and Ramanuja best names that.
  Naming the choice early is honest.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's strong claim that
  "compassion that paralyzes" is real moral weight rather than category
  mistake.

(2) **Dismissive treatment** at §3.3 near "the strongest sectarian framing"
and "the cost of the strongest sectarian framing."
- What the bias is doing: Prabhupada's reading is engaged respectfully and
  then framed as the "cost" position. The frame *most circulated, most
  sectarian* recurs across chapters and starts to do prejudicial work.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The label is technically
  accurate. Its rhetorical placement (twice in close succession) starts to
  function as a verdict rather than a description.
- If softened, what would be lost: The reader's awareness that Prabhupada is
  reading from a fixed-in-advance theological frame; this is a fact the
  reader should know.

(3) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §2 near "It is also reporting, with clinical
accuracy, what happens to a human nervous system at a threshold of decision
it cannot meet."
- What the bias is doing: The chapter is shifting from literary description
  to clinical-naturalistic re-description, in a way that already prepares §5
  to read the chapter naturalistically. The shift is not flagged.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is arguing
  that the depiction lands today because it is clinically recognizable; that
  argument requires the framing.
- If softened, what would be lost: The bridge to the Sapolsky / Kahneman /
  O'Connor section.

(4) **Voice confidence outpacing evidence** at §6 near "Strict pole A makes
the reader feel their collapse is a confusion they should be ashamed of."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis section caricatures Sankara's
  position to motivate the rejection. Sankara's reading does not require
  shame; it requires a recognition that grief presupposes misidentification.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The point can be made
  without the affective characterization.
- If softened, what would be lost: Some rhetorical force; nothing
  substantive.

### Overall bias posture

The chapter's center of gravity is the Ramanuja-Williams-Nussbaum cluster:
the despair is real moral collapse, the conflict is real, the body is
information, the response is teaching not dismissal. The bias is openly
declared and carried honestly. The one place it carries weight it has not
quite earned is the framing of Sankara as the position that would shame the
reader, which is sharper than Sankara's actual texts demand. Otherwise the
chapter is well within the project's "named-and-defended" discipline.

## Chapter 2 — *Sāṃkhya Yoga*: The Eternal Reality

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §6 near "I take the Gita's metaphysics of
self as a *regulative* claim rather than a *falsifiable* one."
- What the bias is doing: The single largest metaphysical move in the
  chapter — and arguably in the manuscript — is the demotion of the *atman*
  doctrine from "claim about reality" to "regulative posture." This is a
  philosophical commitment with sharp consequences: it lets the chapter
  claim modern-science compatibility while declining to be evaluated against
  modern-science rivals.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The chapter is candid that it is
  doing this. The cost is that Sankara, Ramanuja, and Madhva all read the
  *atman* doctrine as a substantive claim, and the chapter's "regulative"
  reading does not engage their reasons for that.
- If softened, what would be lost: The bridge to Parfit / *anatta* /
  predictive-self neuroscience. A more cautious framing ("I bracket the
  metaphysical question") would do less of the same work.

(2) **Modern-science overreach** at §5.2 near "Either Sankara is right that
there is a discontinuous metaphysical attainment that the science cannot see
(entirely possible, since neuroscience makes no claim about metaphysical
realities), or Easwaran is right that the portrait is of a posture trainable
in degrees."
- What the bias is doing: The chapter sets up a forced choice between two
  readings on the basis of which is "consistent with what neuroscience can
  see." The neuroscience's silence is converted into a tilt toward the
  Easwaran reading.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The point that the
  *sthitaprajna* portrait is partially trainable does not require staking it
  against Sankara.
- If softened, what would be lost: Less rhetorical clarity, no substance.

(3) **Synthesis framed as obvious** at §6 near "the practical teaching is
recoverable from more than one of them."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis claims the practical instruction is
  "durable across multiple metaphysical interpretations." This is a
  significant philosophical claim — that ethics floats free of metaphysics —
  presented as if it were observation.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary, with care. The chapter is
  trying to make the practice usable across reader-positions; this is the
  project's stance.
- If softened, what would be lost: The accessibility argument that runs
  through the rest of the book.

(4) **Modern-counterpoint imbalance** at §4.3 near "The Buddhist
counterpoint asks the Gita."
- What the bias is doing: Bhikkhu Bodhi's *anatta* counterpoint is given the
  loudest voice among the modern counterpoints. The chapter then concedes
  that the consolation the *atman* offers is not equivalently available in
  the Buddhist frame. The conclusion ("partly," "the *atman* is doing more
  regulative than substantive work") is the chapter's pre-existing position
  smuggled into a charitable summary.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. Bodhi's reading is real
  pressure; the synthesis does engage it. But the resolution leans the
  Buddhist way without quite saying so.
- If softened, what would be lost: A genuinely engaged counterpoint moment.

### Overall bias posture

The chapter is doing a careful and ambitious philosophical move — reading
*atman* as regulative — and the move is the manuscript's foundational
commitment. The bias is integral. It is also the largest bias-in-need-of-
flagging in the book, because the move quietly makes the project's
metaphysics neutral-by-design, which the project elsewhere disclaims. The
synthesis section is honest that it is doing this; the human reviewer should
decide whether the candor is sufficient.

## Chapter 3 — *Karma Yoga* (The Yoga of Action)

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "the *Gandhian–Aurobindonian*
reading."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis names the chosen reading openly as a
  Gandhi-Aurobindo blend. The chapter is candid. The cost is that two
  20th-century modern readers carry the synthesis on a chapter where Sankara,
  Ramanuja, and Tilak each have substantial textual purchase.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter argues
  textually for the chosen reading and shows its work.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's specific contribution to
  the modern reader's life; this is the bias the project is built on.

(2) **Dismissive treatment** at §3.3 near "There is a real risk that Tilak
collapses the Gita's plurality."
- What the bias is doing: Tilak gets a fair reading, then a quick
  characterization that he "collapses the Gita's plurality" — a charge
  Tilak's own readers would dispute and the chapter does not defend.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. Tilak's activist reading is in
  fact more capacious than the line suggests; the chapter has reason to set
  it aside but the framing is sharper than the argument.
- If softened, what would be lost: Some rhetorical efficiency; nothing the
  argument required.

(3) **Modern-application politics** at §7 near "The 'lying flat' movement
among young workers in China and increasingly elsewhere — *tang ping* — is,
at one register, a refusal to participate in an extractive economic system.
At another register, it is the same fantasy Arjuna proposed in 2.5."
- What the bias is doing: Mapping a contemporary political-cultural
  movement (tang ping) directly onto Arjuna's 2.5 position is an
  interpretive stretch. The chapter is being ideologically definitive about
  a contested social phenomenon.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The example illustrates the
  point but the equation is editorial.
- If softened, what would be lost: A contemporary illustration; the
  argument's structure would be unaffected.

(4) **Synthesis framed as obvious** at §6 near "The four are not separable."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis presents *anasakti*, *yajna*,
  *svadharma*, *lokasangraha* as a four-part structure where each requires
  the others. This is the chapter's editorial construction; the four terms
  are not bound this tightly in the Sanskrit or in any single classical
  reading.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary as the chapter's
  contribution. Naming it as the chapter's construction (rather than as the
  Gita's) would be more honest.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's strongest practical
  takeaway.

### Overall bias posture

A confident chapter that wins its arguments and tells the reader it has done
so. The bias is the project's central bias — a Gandhian-Aurobindonian
practical synthesis — and the chapter carries it well. The two places to
watch are the *tang ping* mapping (over-eager modern-application politics)
and the dismissal-line on Tilak (sharper than necessary).

## Chapter 4 — *Jñāna Karma Sannyāsa Yoga*: The Yoga of Knowledge and the Renunciation of Action

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §6 near "Read the avatāra passage for its
function rather than for its literal ontology."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis explicitly sidesteps the
  metaphysical question (does the divine descend?) by reading the verse for
  what it does in the chapter. This is a major move on a verse that the
  Vaishnava traditions take as the locus classicus of avatara doctrine.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The chapter is candid that it is
  doing this. The cost is that the chosen reading lets a modern reader bypass
  what Ramanuja, Madhva, and Prabhupada all consider the chapter's load-
  bearing claim.
- If softened, what would be lost: The reader is asked to take the
  cosmological claim seriously even if not literally; softer framing would
  change the shape of the offer.

(2) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "Take the lineage and teacher
passage as the chapter's load-bearing instruction for the present-day
reader."
- What the bias is doing: The chapter pivots its synthesis onto 4.34
  (paramparā / approaching the teacher), making it the chapter's most
  important verse. This is one defensible reading; the Vaishnava and
  Advaita traditions place the weight on 4.7–4.8 instead.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter's case for
  4.34 as the chapter's most usable instruction is well-argued.
- If softened, what would be lost: The strong link to MacIntyre / Polanyi /
  Ericsson, which is the chapter's best modern-counterpoint and modern-
  science engagement.

(3) **Modern-science overreach** at §5 near "the procedure the chapter
prescribes is not a historical artifact but a working description of an
empirically observable mechanism."
- What the bias is doing: Ericsson and Collins-Brown-Newman are pulled in to
  underwrite the chapter's prescription about teacher-student transmission.
  The leap from "expertise transmission requires master-apprentice
  structures" to "the Gita's prescription is empirically vindicated" is
  larger than the science supports.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The science is bounded
  carefully ("not a claim of vindication-by-science"); the next sentence
  walks the bound back. The careful reader can navigate it; the casual
  reader will hear vindication.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's claim that the modern
  reader can take the chapter's instruction as practically defensible
  without the metaphysics.

(4) **Voice confidence outpacing evidence** at §3.6 near "Whether the chain
is historically real is a separable question."
- What the bias is doing: A strong claim about the *Mahābhārata*'s framing
  device that the chapter does not defend or footnote. The literary-academic
  tradition is more cautious about this than the chapter implies.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The chapter could state
  the literary-academic position without committing to the historical
  separability.
- If softened, what would be lost: A small bridge to the Polanyi /
  MacIntyre engagement.

### Overall bias posture

A philosophically ambitious chapter whose central move (read 4.7–4.8 for
function, not ontology) is the chapter's largest interpretive choice. The
bias is openly declared and well-defended for the modern lay reader; the
cost is that the chapter sets aside what the Vaishnava traditions take as
the verse's whole point. The science layer is the manuscript's most
ambitious and is carried with care, though the bound-statements are doing
work the prose around them sometimes undercuts.

## Chapter 5 — *Karma Sanyasa Yoga* — The Yoga of Renunciation of Action

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Synthesis framed as obvious** at §6 near "Where this chapter's
traditions diverge, and they diverge sharply, I judge that the active-life
reading — Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Ramanuja, Easwaran, in their different
keys — is the better reading of *this chapter as it stands*."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis declares Sankara's reading of *this
  chapter* not the better one. The argument is given (textual fidelity to
  5.2's *vishisyate*), but the line presents the choice as a verdict.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. 5.2's surface grammar
  does favor the active-life reading; the chapter is right to say so.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's decisive shape.

(2) **Tradition overweighting** at §3.6 near "the householder whose inner
life has been re-anchored, not the formal monk."
- What the bias is doing: Easwaran's "householder is the addressee" reading
  is taken as the chapter's pastoral default. The chapter explicitly says
  this aligns with how a 2026 reader will need the chapter; the bias is
  audience-driven.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is making the
  audience-fit explicit.
- If softened, what would be lost: The §7 application's specificity.

(3) **Modern-science overreach** at §5.1 near "the practitioner's experience
of 'I do nothing' during action is consistent with a shift away from
narrative self-reference… toward experiential self-reference."
- What the bias is doing: Norman Farb's two-mode framework is mapped onto
  the chapter's 5.8–5.9 with a "consistent with" hedge that nonetheless
  carries the synthesis's weight on the trainability claim.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The science is bounded. The
  rhetorical move that the *experience-type* the chapter names is
  empirically real does more work than the cited findings warrant.
- If softened, what would be lost: A direct bridge to 21st-century clinical
  practice.

(4) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §6 near "the operative phenomenology — the
trainable stance — is invariant under all three."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis claims that the trainable stance
  named in 5.8–5.9 is "invariant" under Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, and
  Vaishnava metaphysics. This is a substantive philosophical claim that
  most classical commentators would dispute.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is openly
  making the move; the claim is the chapter's contribution.
- If softened, what would be lost: The pluralism the chapter is offering
  the reader.

### Overall bias posture

The strongest "active-life-wins" chapter in the manuscript. The chapter
makes a textual case for the synthesis it commits to and shows its work.
The bias is consistent with the project's overall lean. The one place it is
sharper than necessary is the absorption of Sankara's insight into the
synthesis ("operationally meaningful illusion") which the chapter
characterizes as something Sankara would also accept; he would not.

## Chapter 6 — *Dhyāna Yoga* (The Yoga of Meditation)

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "I am setting aside, as the
*organizing* frame, the Gauḍīya reading that subordinates *dhyāna yoga* to
*bhakti yoga*. Prabhupada is right that 6.47 is not a footnote."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis explicitly declines to let
  Prabhupada's strongest textual claim (6.47 ranks the bhakti-yogi above
  others) organize the chapter. The chapter says he is "right that 6.47 is
  not a footnote" then sets aside his framing anyway.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is honest
  about the trade-off.
- If softened, what would be lost: A weaker stand on what *dhyāna* is for.

(2) **Modern-counterpoint imbalance** at §4.1 and §4.2 (Weil and Murdoch
together).
- What the bias is doing: Weil and Murdoch carry a great deal of the
  chapter's interpretive work, with Murdoch's "personal fantasy" diagnosis
  doing more work than the chapter's classical commentary.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The two voices fit the chapter
  beautifully. Their rhetorical weight is heavy enough that the modern
  philosophical reading risks displacing the classical one.
- If softened, what would be lost: One of the manuscript's most successful
  modern-counterpoint moments.

(3) **Modern-science overreach** at §5 near "The wind, in modern cognitive
terms, is largely the Default Mode Network's spontaneous narration."
- What the bias is doing: The DMN literature is mapped onto the chapter's
  "wind" image with a strong metaphor that does interpretive work. The
  chapter does name the bound ("the structural correlates of long-term
  practice are correlates of practice in general"), but the metaphor lands
  before the bound does.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The metaphor is rhetorical
  effect rather than load-bearing argument.
- If softened, what would be lost: A vivid science-prose bridge.

(4) **Synthesis framed as obvious** at §6 near "The yogi of 6.19 is a lamp.
Lamps are for seeing."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis closes with a forceful one-liner
  that turns 6.19's image into the chapter's whole point. The image
  supports the reading; the chapter is over-claiming the image's
  determinacy.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The closing line is
  rhetorically effective and substantively accurate; it is also doing
  rhetoric.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's memorable close.

### Overall bias posture

A well-constructed chapter that lets Murdoch and Weil carry the
modern-conceptual weight. The chapter's bias is integrative: the chosen
reading is "Aurobindo via Easwaran with Murdoch and Weil as the conceptual
hinge." This works but it is heavy on 20th-century European voices; a
reader looking for a more Indian-traditional reading of *dhyāna* will feel
the manuscript's pull. The science is bounded responsibly; the metaphor is
slightly out ahead of the bounds.

## Chapter 7 — *Jñāna Vijñāna Yoga*: The Yoga of Knowledge and Realization

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "I prefer this reading on textual
grounds: 7.5 says of the higher *prakriti* that *jagad dhāryate*… and
*jīva-bhūtām*."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis explicitly favors Ramanuja's reading
  over Sankara's, with a textual argument. The textual case is strong; the
  chapter is one of the manuscript's clearest commitments to qualified
  non-dualism.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter argues from
  the Sanskrit and from internal coherence with later chapters.
- If softened, what would be lost: The manuscript's metaphysical spine.

(2) **Dismissive treatment** at §3.5 near "the most sectarian widely
available; it is also the most pastorally direct."
- What the bias is doing: Prabhupada framing — recurring across chapters —
  appears here again. The line "the most sectarian widely available" is
  cumulatively weighty across the manuscript, less so within this chapter.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary as labeling, optional as
  recurring rhetoric. Single-instance characterization is honest; the
  manuscript's repetition is the issue.
- If softened, what would be lost: The reader's awareness of Prabhupada's
  frame; this should remain.

(3) **Modern-science overreach** at §5.1 near "The screen between the
perceiver and the perceived is partly the perceiver's own model."
- What the bias is doing: Predictive processing is mapped onto *māyā* with
  a careful bound, then the metaphor ("the screen between the perceiver and
  the perceived is partly the perceiver's own model") does work that
  conflates a perceptual-mechanism finding with the chapter's metaphysical
  claim.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The bound is in place; the
  metaphor is doing the interpretive lifting.
- If softened, what would be lost: A vivid contemporary frame for *māyā*.

(4) **Synthesis framed as obvious** at §6 near "The position that survives
is this: a Gita that teaches a real world held in real relation to a real
divine."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis presents qualified non-dualism as
  what "survives" the readings, smuggling Ramanuja's commitment into the
  language of survival.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is taking a
  position the project authorizes.
- If softened, what would be lost: The manuscript's clearest claim about
  what kind of world the Gita is in.

### Overall bias posture

The chapter where the manuscript's metaphysical lean is most explicit. The
bias is named and defended. The chapter is aware that this is one defensible
reading and not the only one; it does the work to show its reasoning. The
modern-science layer is carried with care but the prose around the bounds is
slightly more confident than the bounds themselves.

## Chapter 8 — *Aksara Brahma Yoga* (The Yoga of the Imperishable Brahman)

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §6 near "I take the first as describable in
present-day terms and the second as a *pointer* — a register of what the
practice points toward without asserting what science cannot adjudicate."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis distinguishes the chapter's claim
  about *life* (what attention shapes) from its claim about *after* (the
  supreme abode). The first is taken seriously; the second is reframed as
  "pointer." This is a major move on a chapter whose surface text is
  metaphysically committed.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The chapter is candid that it
  is doing this; the move is the manuscript's standard discipline. The
  cost is that Ramanuja and Prabhupada read the verses as substantive
  ontological claims, and the synthesis effectively rules that reading
  out of the modern application.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's address to a reader who
  cannot affirm a particular eschatology.

(2) **Modern-counterpoint imbalance** at §4.2 (Shelly Kagan).
- What the bias is doing: Kagan is brought in to represent the
  metaphysically deflationary position. The synthesis then argues the
  chapter "remains intelligible and arguably true" on Kagan's metaphysics —
  a strong claim that flattens what the Vaishnava and Advaita readings
  take to be the chapter's whole point.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary for the project's
  audience. The cost is that the secular-philosophical voice gets to
  stand for "what the chapter still says when stripped" and the
  stripping is itself the synthesis's choice.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's bridge to a non-religious
  reader.

(3) **Modern-application politics** at §7.5 near "The smartphone as the
modern threshold-shaping device."
- What the bias is doing: A specific cultural critique (algorithmic
  attention capture, the attention economy) is presented as the chapter's
  obvious modern application. The critique is well-attested; framing it as
  the chapter's natural extension overstates the chapter's reach.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The §7 application earns its
  contemporary specificity. The frame "what the chapter is talking about
  is the algorithm" presses harder than necessary.
- If softened, what would be lost: A vivid contemporary example.

(4) **Voice confidence outpacing evidence** at §6 near "the chapter's
*gesture* toward the supreme abode, as a pointer."
- What the bias is doing: Demoting a chapter-explicit claim to "gesture"
  is a strong move done lightly. The chapter does not present itself as
  gesturing; it presents itself as teaching.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The synthesis is openly
  doing this. The candor is what the project asks for.
- If softened, what would be lost: The reader's permission to use the
  chapter as a teaching about life.

### Overall bias posture

The chapter does the manuscript's standard reframing — read the metaphysical
content as functional, take the practical content as load-bearing — and is
honest about it. The Heidegger / Gawande / Frankl modern-counterpoint
sequence is one of the manuscript's strongest. The smartphone-as-
threshold-device application in §7 is the chapter's most editorially loaded
section.

## Chapter 11 — *Viśvarūpa Darśana Yoga*: The Yoga of the Vision of the Universal Form

- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter argues the
  case textually and against the major schools.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's strong stand against the
  literary-only and Advaitic-only readings.

(2) **Modern-application politics** at §6 and §7 near "the chapter does not
authorize the human to be *kāla*. It authorizes the human to be the
conscious instrument of a process whose source is not the human."
- What the bias is doing: A normative ethical claim about contemporary
  technology — *be merely an instrument is not a posture available to the
  *kāla*-role* — is derived from a verse that does not state it. The
  chapter is honest that "the Gita is silent about those positions
  because they were not on its horizon. We have to work out what the
  chapter is implying about them ourselves." But the synthesis treats the
  derivation as the chapter's reading.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The political move is one of

(3) **Modern-science overreach** at §5.2 (psychedelic ego dissolution).

  chapter stages is "real and recurring." The chapter bounds the claim
  carefully ("the chemical induction of ego dissolution does not
  'explain' the Visvarupa"), but the rhetorical effect of the section is
  to give Arjuna's experience a contemporary scientific footing. The
  bound and the effect are in tension.
- Whether the bias is integral: Borderline. The science is well-cited
  and bounded. The chapter's structural argument relies on it.
- If softened, what would be lost: One of the manuscript's most

(4) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "Prabhupada is right about the
chapter's denouement."
- What the bias is doing: Concedes Prabhupada's point structurally then
  refuses his sectarian content. This is the manuscript's standard move
  on Prabhupada and reaches its sharpest form here, where Prabhupada is
  named as right about a textual fact while his framework is set aside.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is honest
  about absorbing what Prabhupada gets right.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's careful generosity.

### Overall bias posture

The most ambitious chapter in the manuscript. The Otto / Burke / Kant /
Heidegger / Oppenheimer sequence is bold. The Oppenheimer reading is the
single most editorially loaded passage in the book — the chapter argues
that Oppenheimer's gloss was "wrong about the verse in a productive way"

gloss. The reasoning is exposed; the political weight is heavy. A reader
should expect this chapter to do more contemporary-application work than
its surface text alone could authorize, and decide whether that is the
right load to carry.

## Chapter 18 — *Mokṣa Sannyāsa Yoga*: The Yoga of Liberation through Renunciation

### Heavy-bias moments

(1) **Tradition overweighting** at §6 near "I am going to read 18.66 as a
synthesis of three of the readings — **Rāmānuja's *prapatti*, Aurobindo's
*integral surrender*, and Tilak's *coherence with 18.47***."
- What the bias is doing: The chapter explicitly chooses three
  20th-century-and-medieval voices and excludes Sankara and Prabhupada
  on the chapter's most-debated verse. The chosen synthesis is candid;
  the rejection of Sankara's reading on demographic grounds (a "small
  subset of the qualified") is weaker than the textual argument.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary at the synthesis level;
  the demographic argument is optional and slightly weaker than the
  rest of the reasoning.
- If softened, what would be lost: A small line; the chapter's main
  argument is unaffected.

(2) **Smuggled metaphysics** at §6 near "The 'me' is somebody. Whatever
the reader's reflexes about that 'somebody' — a personal Lord, the
indwelling self, the deepest possible reality, the ground of being — the
verse insists that surrender is not into nothing."
- What the bias is doing: The synthesis preserves the addressee of
  surrender while leaving the addressee's nature open. This is the
  chapter's most careful metaphysical move and is candid about its
  shape.
- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is making
  the verse usable across reader-positions; the project authorizes this.
- If softened, what would be lost: The chapter's central offer.

(3) **Modern-counterpoint imbalance** at §4.1 (Twelve Steps).
- What the bias is doing: The Alcoholics Anonymous Twelve Steps are
  brought in as the structural double of *prapatti*. The chapter is
  candid about the difference (the Steps' "as we understood Him" lets
  the addressee float; the verse names "me"). The pairing is bold and

- Whether the bias is integral: Judged necessary. The chapter is

- If softened, what would be lost: One of the chapter's strongest

(4) **Voice confidence outpacing evidence** at §6 (the demographic

- What the bias is doing: "For the modern reader, who is overwhelmingly

- Whether the bias is integral: Judged optional. The textual argument

(5) **Modern-application politics** at §7 (the five situations).

### Overall bias posture

The closing chapter does the manuscript's largest synthesis honestly. The
