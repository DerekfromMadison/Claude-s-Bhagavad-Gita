# 19 — Clarity Review (Voice Rewrite)

## Overall

The voice rewrite is largely legible to a fast-moving smart reader,
but unevenly. Two-thirds of the chapters lead cleanly with their
claim and let a busy reader name the teaching after one read: 1, 2,
3, 6, 7, 8, 10, 12, 13, 15, 17, 18. The other six fail. Chapters 4,
5, 9, 11, 14, and 16 bury the lead, perform the claim before stating
it, or let an architectural front-section delay the reader's grasp.

Where the manuscript fails, it fails in three patterns. First, some
chapters hand the reader a *what's-coming* preview ("two registers,"
"three movements") instead of the actual claim — the reader gets a
table of contents, not a thesis. Second, Sanskrit terms load faster
than gloss can carry them: 4.34's three terms, 13.7–13.11's
twenty-item list, 18.66's *sarva-dharman parityajya* in five
translators' versions. Smart readers survive any one density spike;
some chapters spike four times. Third, chapters engaging classical
commentators most heavily (5, 11, 13, 18) sometimes spend their best
paragraphs on inter-school disagreement before the reader knows why
the disagreement matters to their Tuesday. The fix in each case is
small and local. The book is close. It is not yet there.

## Per Chapter

### Chapter 1 — Arjuna's Despair

**Lead:** Yes. Cinematic Kurukshetra opening earns its way to "The
collapse is the door" at line 61. Voice-and-style explicitly
authorizes this structure for Chapter 1.

**Spine:** Yes. Body's collapse is honest information, not weakness;
the collapse is the door, not the room.

**Sanskrit:** Strong. *kṣatriya*, *śoka-moha*, *kṛpā*, *Anāsakti
Yoga* glossed inline.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 83–87: unannotated Sanskrit for two lines
before the English. Reverse. (b) Lines 105–135: Sapolsky, Kahneman,
O'Connor in three consecutive paragraphs. Cut Kahneman to one
sentence. (c) Line 244 (Prabhupada paragraph): reads as editorial
bookkeeping; compress to three sentences.

### Chapter 2 — The Eternal Reality

**Lead:** Yes. "Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood onto the
result" — line 11.

**Spine:** Yes. 2.47 as practical center; the self that does the
work is not the self that needs the work to have gone well.

**Sanskrit:** Mostly strong. *atman*, *sthitaprajña*, *brāhmī sthiti*
glossed.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Line 67 (*sāṃkhya/yoga* pivot): arrives without
preparation. (b) Line 72: full Sanskrit of 2.47–2.48 before
Easwaran's English; reverse. (c) Lines 113–164 (four readings of
2.47): lead with the punchline ("Four readings converge on the
practice; they diverge on the metaphysics").

### Chapter 3 — The Yoga of Action

**Lead:** Yes. "*You cannot withdraw.*" Four pillars named by line
23.

**Spine:** Yes. Four pillars as one structure; the diagnostic
question (*does the rewards focus produce focus, or anxiety?*).

**Sanskrit:** Strong. Each pillar glossed at first introduction.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 36–46: "structural rather than moral" reads
as jargon. (b) Lines 100–115: MacIntyre's internal/external goods
needs a half-sentence on what *practice* means in his technical
sense. (c) Line 195 (*svadharma* caste warning): lead with the
broader reading, not "I am not defending it."

### Chapter 4 — Who Teaches You

**Lead:** **Partially.** "Who teaches you?" is striking. But the
chapter's actual claim — the unchosen teacher is the algorithm, 4.34
is the chapter's center — is delayed until line 36. The reader does
not know what the chapter is teaching for twenty-two lines.

**Spine:** Yes, after the lead is found. Algorithm as unchosen
teacher; 4.34 (humble approach, honest inquiry, service); the hours
are finite; choose one teacher.

**Sanskrit:** Strong. *praṇipāta, paripraśna, sevā* glossed at first
appearance; *paramparā* glossed.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 11–32: twenty-two lines of mood-setting
before the claim. Compress to ten lines and surface the claim
earlier. (b) Lines 87–113 (the algorithm passage): "default mode
network" arrives without setup. Add one sentence linking the
algorithm to the DMN as substrate. (c) Lines 156–174 (Polanyi): the
jump from the algorithm to Polanyi-on-laboratory-science is abrupt.
Add a one-sentence bridge.

### Chapter 5 — Renunciation of Action

**Lead:** **Partially.** The first sentence is right. But the second
paragraph drops into Sanskrit etymology before the claim has had time
to register, and the chapter then says "This is a lighter chapter —
the porch of the next one" (line 27) — meta-commentary the reader
does not need.

**Spine:** Yes. The wrong question is *leave or stay*; the right
question is *how to stay without the agent-thought deforming the
staying*.

**Sanskrit:** Heavy in places. By line 337 the reader is doing
Sanskrit homework on *kartṛtva-abhimāna, aham karta, brahma-nirvāṇa,
sparśān kṛtvā bahir bāhyāṁś...*

**Hot-spots:** (a) Line 27 (the "porch" meta-commentary): cut. Let
the chapter prove it by being short. (b) Lines 105–121: six
commentators on 5.2 (Sankara, Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Ramanuja,
Easwaran). Compress to two clusters. (c) Line 335 (the Sanskrit
posture-and-breath run): English first, Sanskrit after.

### Chapter 6 — Meditation

### Chapter 8 — The Imperishable

**Lead:** Yes. "What your life accumulates is what becomes operative

**Spine:** Yes. Thresholds are where the long pattern becomes

### Chapter 9 — Royal Knowledge, Royal Secret

**Lead:** **Partially.** "Devotion has no entry fee" is sharp. But
the next sentence ("This is the chapter's claim, and the chapter
performs the claim...") is meta-commentary about how the chapter
operates — the reader gets the claim and is then told the chapter
will deliver it.

**Spine:** Yes. *Bhakti* is structurally portable; *patram pushpam
phalam toyam* names what anyone has; the leaf is structurally free.

**Sanskrit:** Mostly strong. *Bhakti*, *Raja vidya*, *Raja guhya*,
*Patram pushpam phalam toyam* all glossed. *yoga-kshema* and
*susukham kartum* given briefly.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 27–69 ("The architecture"): "three
movements" preview before the argument has been made. Cut the
preview; let the movements unfold. (b) Lines 119–181 (the 9.32
reading): the longest inter-school argument in this chapter,
delaying the practical claim. Compress by a third; consider moving
after the practical-application section. (c) Lines 263–285

weight. Compress one or the other.

### Chapter 10 — Divine Glories

**Lead:** Yes. "The discipline this chapter teaches is recognition."

**Spine:** Yes. The eye is the point; catalog is the gymnasium;

**Sanskrit:** Strong. *Vibhūti*, *Yoga* glossed in context.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 105–135 (Hopkins): *inscape/instress*

### Chapter 11 — The Universal Form

**Lead:** **Partially.** The opening is striking. But the chapter's
most important specific claim — *you are not authorized to be kāla*
— is held back as "an extension I will own as my reading and not
the chapter's text" (line 31). A smart reader keeps waiting for the
chapter's actual argument.

**Spine:** Yes, by the end. See what you must see, then return to
the face; *kāla/nimitta-mātra* distinction; the asking is right
response, not weakness.

**Sanskrit:** Mostly strong. *Kāla*, *nimitta-mātra*, *divya-cakṣus*,
*anāsakti*, *bhakti* all glossed. **But** *fascinans* and *mysterium
tremendum* are not glossed.

**Hot-spots:** (a) Lines 99–135 (three traditions): four readings
before the chapter has done its positive teaching. Compress to two
clusters; do positive teaching first. (b) Line 152 (*mysterium
tremendum et fascinans*): in Latin without English. Translate inline:
"the holy encountered as simultaneously terrifying and fascinating."
(c) Lines 249–296 (the silent territory section): the chapter's most
distinctive original move — buried behind a long preliminary reading.
Open the section with one sentence stating what the chapter is
silent on and why the reader needs the extension.

### Chapter 12 — Devotion

**Spine:** Yes. Qualities at 12.13–12.20 are diagnostic, not a

**Sanskrit:** Strong. *Bhakti*, *kleshah*, *abhyasa*, *priya*,

## Manuscript-Wide Clarity Patterns

1. **Architecture-preview meta-commentary.** Chapters 4, 5, 8, 9, 14,
   17 each contain a "the chapter has [N] movements / two registers"
   preview. The voice-and-style document says "Movements within the
   chapter are unlabeled and continuous; the reader feels the
   argument unfold without seeing the seams." Cut them.

2. **Inter-school disagreement before the chapter's own teaching.**
   Chapters 2, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15, 18 walk the reader through
   Sankara/Ramanuja/Gandhi/Aurobindo before the chapter's positive
   teaching has landed. The chapter's commitment should land first;
   the disagreement should be the reason for the commitment, not
   prologue to it.

3. **Sanskrit transliteration density spikes.** Chapters 1, 5, 7, 13,
   18 each contain passages where unannotated transliterated Sanskrit
   runs for two or more lines. Lead with English; offer Sanskrit for
   readers who want it; do not require the reader to scan
   transliteration to extract meaning.

4. **Untranslated technical Latin/Greek.** *Mysterium tremendum et
   fascinans* (11), *prohairesis* (2), *Dasein* (11). Smart-reader-
   assumed in religious-studies and continental philosophy but not
   all the book's smart busy readers have done that homework.

5. **Named scientists without minimal context.** Chapter 6 stacks
   Lazar/Brewer/Jha in three consecutive paragraphs; Chapter 13
   stacks Craig/Garfinkel/Seth. Compress to one researcher per claim.

6. **Closing instructions that compete with the claim.** Chapters
   4, 7, 8, 11, 12, 13, 14, 16, 17 each end with at least one
   paragraph that could be cut without loss. The voice-and-style
   document says "One closing, not two."

7. **The "I want to be careful here" move.** Chapters 4, 9, 11, 16
   each phrase carefulness as the topic. Replace with the careful
   move itself.
