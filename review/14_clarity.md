# 14 — Clarity Review (Post-Rewrite)

## Overall

The manuscript is largely legible to a first-time reader, and that is the first
honest thing to say. A coffee-shop reader picking up Chapter 2, 6, 13, or 18
will, on a single sitting, walk away knowing what each of those chapters claims
and roughly how the claim was earned. The book has a posture, a vocabulary, and
an audience, and they hold across chapters. The rewrite has done its biggest
job: the survey form is gone, and the prose moves.

Where clarity fails, it fails in patterned ways. Five recurring problems:
buried claims (the chapter announces what it is teaching three or four
paragraphs in, after a long approach); commentator-clusters (Sankara, Ramanuja,
Madhva, Aurobindo, Prabhupada, Tilak in dense succession that taxes a reader
who does not already know who these are); Sanskrit terms used as load-bearers
without re-gloss after long absence (a reader meeting *tyaga* for the first
time at Ch 18 needs a one-line gloss the chapter assumes from earlier); long
expository middles where the chapter's spine drops below the surface for two
to three thousand words at a time; and chapter endings that gather many
threads but sometimes leave the reader uncertain which thread was the spine.

The book's most legible chapters (1, 2, 8, 13, 15, 18) lead with a sentence
that is also the chapter's claim and re-state it in different lights through
the chapter. The least legible (4, 11, 12, 16) bury the claim, run multiple
parallel arguments, or rely on prior chapters to do glossing the chapter
itself needs.

## Manuscript-wide clarity patterns

Six recurring clarity problems, ranked by how often they cost the reader:

**1. Commentator-cluster fatigue.** The cluster of Sankara, Ramanuja,
Madhva, Aurobindo, Prabhupada, Tilak, Gandhi appears in nearly every
chapter, often with three to six commentators given a paragraph each
in succession. The cast of commentators in front matter helps, but
the per-chapter survey-of-readings format is the survey form
returning under different clothes. Where the chapter's claim could
be made by engaging two commentators, three or four are typically
deployed. Chapters 6, 11, 13, 18 are the most affected.

**2. Multiple-modern-voice piling.** Modern philosophers (Murdoch,
Weil, Williams, Nagel, Chalmers, Merleau-Ponty, Tillich, Heidegger,
James, Frankl, Hayes, Arendt, MacIntyre) appear in clusters,
typically two to four per chapter. Sometimes the chapter genuinely
needs all of them; often it does not. The pattern is most visible
in Chs 4 (Polanyi/Collins/Ericsson), 7 (Spinoza/Marion), 12
(Murdoch/Fromm/attachment theory/Singer/Fredrickson), 13
(Nagel/Chalmers/Merleau-Ponty), 15 (Tillich/Heidegger/James), 18
(Twelve Steps/ACT/Frankl).

**3. Science triple-stack.** Where one bounded finding would do,
the chapters often deploy two or three (Lazar + Brewer + Jha; TMT
+ DMN + hospice cognition; Dark Triad + HEXACO + Trivers +
Dunning-Kruger). The chapter typically uses each finding to make
the same structural point, and the cumulative effect is to dilute
rather than reinforce.

**4. Buried-claim openings (a smaller pattern, but real).**
Chapters 4, 11, 12, 16 each delay the chapter's actual proposal
to give a vivid scene-setter or warning. The scenes are good. They
should not be substituting for a clear early statement of what the
chapter is teaching.

**5. Sanskrit terms used as load-bearers without re-gloss.** Some
terms are introduced once and then deployed many times, sometimes
many pages later. Cases: *anasakti*, *vijñāna*, *jnana yajña*,
*paramparā*, *kṣānti*, *susukham kartum*, *param ayanam*. A
first-time reader who reads at coffee-shop pace may not retain a
gloss given thirty pages earlier.

**6. Recursive endings.** Several chapters (6, 9, 11, 13, 15, 17,
18) end strongly, with an image, instruction, or scene the reader
will remember. But the ending is sometimes preceded by a "what
this means in 2026" section listing four to six applications,
which itself follows the chapter's argument. The "argument →
applications → closing image" structure produces three closes per
chapter, and the reader is uncertain which one carried the spine.
