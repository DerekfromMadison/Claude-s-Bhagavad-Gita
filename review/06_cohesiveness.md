# 06 — Cohesiveness Review

A structural read of the manuscript as a single book, against the
through-line validated in `05_validated_intention.md`. The
conciseness pass has tightened many of the prior editor's flagged
muddles; this review tracks what now coheres, what wobbles, what
went silent, and what surfaced after the cut.

---

## Executive summary

The book holds together. The through-line — *posture (agency without
self-grafting; trained attention; love-with-attention; surrender of
the bargaining stance) recoverable today by a non-forest, non-temple,
non-sectarian reader* — is legible from cover to cover. After the
conciseness pass the chapters carry the spine more cleanly than they
did at review/04: Ch. 7's metaphysical commitment has been allowed
to set the floor, Ch. 11's *kala*-extension is now properly
attributed as Claude's reading, Ch. 16's fourth-gate move is
labelled as extension-for-self-diagnostic-use, and the manuscript-
wide compression of "bracketed metaphysics, kept practice" has
cleaned up the dilution problem the prior editor flagged.

**Top three cohesiveness issues that remain.** (1) The bhakti
register is not yet stabilised across Chs. 9, 12, and 18: Ch. 9
still reads bhakti as the chapter's *answer* (with sectarian-
universalist heat) where Ch. 12 reads it as accessibility-not-
supremacy and Ch. 18 reads surrender as synthesis-not-fourth-path;
the three sit awkwardly when read in sequence. (2) The metaphysical
floor is *declared* in Ch. 7 but several earlier and later chapters
still read as if Ch. 2's regulative posture were the manuscript's
floor — Ch. 2 §6, Ch. 8's "pointer" demotion, and Ch. 14's "I am
not asking the reader to commit to Brahman" each lean minimalist in
ways the Ch. 7 commitment has displaced. (3) The "Ramanuja-Aurobindo-
Easwaran-Gandhi cluster" is the manuscript's de facto load-bearing
synthesis but is never named in front matter and never named as
*pattern* inside the chapters — the reader who reads sequentially
feels a cumulative tilt without ever being told it is happening.

**Top three things working well.** (a) The Arjuna-arc has integrity:
Ch. 1's collapse, Ch. 2.10ff. teaching, Ch. 11's vision, Ch. 18.73's
standing-up form a dramatic spine the chapters honour without
labouring. (b) The attention-hub (Murdoch, Weil, the algorithmic
feed, the trained gaze) is the manuscript's strongest cross-chapter
thread, and the conciseness pass has not damaged it. (c) The §7
modern-application sections are the manuscript's most distinctive
prose — the kitchen, the bedside, the inbox, the layoff, the phone.
This is what the book *is*, and it is consistent across all 18
chapters.

## 1. Through-line preservation

The validated through-line is a posture: *agency without proprietary
self-grafting + attention trained against its captors + love-with-
attention oriented at what is worth one's life + surrender of the
bargaining stance*. Each chapter is supposed to contribute one piece
of it. Below is the chapter-by-chapter scan.

**Chapters where the through-line is sharp:**

- **Ch. 1.** The despair-as-door framing in §6 lands the through-
  line's stance toward suffering. The §7 thresholds (manager,
  caregiver, three-AM-engineer, ICU doctor, burnout) install the
  reader inside the address.
- **Ch. 2.** §6's three commitments (*atman* as ground, 2.47 as
  prohibition on grafting, *sthitaprajna* as trainable posture)
  carry the manuscript's practical centre. This chapter is the
  through-line's load-bearing engine on agency-without-self-grafting.
- **Ch. 3.** §6's four-pillar synthesis (*anasakti*, *yajna*,
  *svadharma*, *lokasangraha*) and §7's "rewards focus or anxiety,
  and which one delivers?" line — sharp through-line contributions.
- **Ch. 6.** §6's *dhyana is for something* claim, the attention-as-
  moral-act bridge through Murdoch and Weil, and §7's "self at six
  in the morning" — the through-line's modern hub installed.
- **Ch. 11.** §6's *nimitta-matra* / *kala* distinction and the
  return-to-the-personal-face structure carry the through-line's
  most ambitious moves. The Oppenheimer extension is now properly
  attributed to Claude.
- **Ch. 13.** §7's "working list for the week" translates the twenty
  qualities of *jnana* into modern practice. This is the through-
  line's clearest operational vocabulary.
- **Ch. 17.** §7's "calendar / bank statement / browser history"
  application is the through-line's sharpest single sentence in the
  manuscript.
- **Ch. 18.** §6 explicitly names which earlier chapters the
  synthesis is gathering (Ch. 2 no-self-grafting; Ch. 3 four pillars;
  Ch. 6 attention-as-discipline; Ch. 9 bhakti-as-accessible-practice;
  Ch. 13 witness-as-steady-point; Ch. 17 faith-as-constitution).
  This is the manuscript's clearest "here is what I have been
  doing."

**Chapters where the through-line goes quieter or pulls against
itself:**

- **Ch. 4.** §6's three moves (interior renunciation; *avatara*-as-
  function; 4.34 as load-bearing) are clean, but the chapter carries
  a heavy *avatara* and *paramparā* freight that does not bend
  toward the through-line as obviously as the §7 application
  ("audit who is teaching you, by the content of your time")
  promises. The through-line lives more clearly in §7 than in §6 of
  this chapter.
- **Ch. 7.** §6 *declares* the metaphysical floor (qualified non-
  dualism), which is exactly the through-line's spine — but the
  declaration sits in the metaphysical-cosmological register more
  than in the posture register. The §7 application recovers the
  through-line, but the chapter is the manuscript's most "about how
  to read the Gita" chapter. The through-line is present but quieter
  in §6 than in §6 of Chs. 2, 6, 11, 13, 18.
- **Ch. 8.** §6's "what a life accumulates is what becomes operative
  at the threshold" is sharply through-line. But the chapter's first
  half — the seven technical terms, the cosmology of cosmic days and
  nights, *aksara*-as-pointer — sits at a metaphysical altitude
  where the through-line goes silent. §7 (end of workday, end of
  relationship, smartphone-as-threshold-shaping-device) recovers it.
- **Ch. 10.** The through-line is the *discipline of recognition* —
  attend to the best of each kind, slowly, with Murdoch's gaze.
  Strong. But the §3 catalog (Sankara's *upalakshana*, Ramanuja's
  *sharira*, Madhva's hierarchy, Aurobindo's evolutionary self-
  disclosure) is doing more work than the through-line needs, and
  Hopkins's *inscape* in §4 is the through-line's most efficient
  modern voice but appears only here.
- **Ch. 14.** §6's three-layer practice (substrate / sattva / witness)
  is sharply through-line. But the chapter spends substantial space
  on the *guna* taxonomy that does not by itself land on posture;
  the through-line is doing structural work the surface text does
  not foreground. The reader has to wait until §7 to feel the
  through-line snap into focus on the 3pm-crash and the doom-scroll.
- **Ch. 15.** §6's *purusottama-as-relation-not-place-on-the-
  inventory* is sharp. The through-line lives in the axe and the
  pointing, not in the inverted tree. The chapter is among the
  manuscript's clearest moments of metaphysical-pastoral synthesis.
- **Ch. 16.** §6's self-diagnostic framing and Arendt's fourth gate
  are firmly through-line. But the chapter's most arresting passages
  (the Eichmann passage, the Dark Triad survey) are *about asuric
  others*, and the through-line's stance — *use this on yourself*
  — has to compete with the chapter's empirical material.

**Specific moments where the through-line wobbles or pulls against
itself, with locators:**

1. **Ch. 2 §6, on *atman*.** The chapter says: *"I do not claim to
   settle the metaphysical status of the atman. I commit to the
   structural claim that there is something in the human person that
   grief, ambition, and loss do not finally reach."* Good. But four
   paragraphs later it says: *"I am not claiming the atman exists in
   the way Sankara or Ramanuja claim. I am claiming the chapter's
   use of the imperishable — as the ground from which one can act and
   grieve without annihilation — is durable."* This is the
   regulative-posture frame the validated through-line specifically
   replaced with Ch. 7's qualified non-dualism. Either Ch. 2 should
   be read as *teaching the practical floor that Ch. 7 will name as
   metaphysical*, or Ch. 2 is genuinely committing less than Ch. 7,
   and the manuscript shifts register between Ch. 2 and Ch. 7. The
   conciseness pass has softened but not removed this. The through-
   line says Ch. 7 is the floor; Ch. 2's "the practical teaching is
   durable across multiple metaphysical interpretations" reads, at
   second reading, as the older minimalism.

2. **Ch. 7 §6, "the position I am committing to is this."** The
   editor 3.8 fix has landed cleanly — the language no longer
   smuggles "the position that survives." But the *register* of Ch.
   7 §6 is metaphysical-confessional ("a Gita that teaches a real
   world held in real relation to a real divine") in a way the rest
   of the manuscript's §6 sections are not. Ch. 7 §6 reads like the
   manuscript's *creed*; the other §6 sections read like its
   *practice*. The through-line is held but the register clashes
   slightly with the practice-register the rest of the book occupies.

3. **Ch. 8 §6, "what your life accumulates is what becomes operative
   at the threshold."** This is sharp through-line. But the *next*
   paragraph — *"On the metaphysical pointer: Sankara carries"* —
   pulls in the apophatic register the rest of the book does not
   share. The conciseness pass has signaled this is one chapter
   where Sankara is allowed to win, which is right, but the
   manuscript-wide reader feels Ch. 8 as a brief departure from the
   through-line and is not fully prepared for it. A single sentence
   in front matter naming "Ch. 8 and Ch. 13 are the chapters where
   I let Sankara carry" would resolve it (this is Part 4.4 of
   review/05).

4. **Ch. 11 §6, the kala-extension.** *"This extension is not the
   chapter's claim. It is my reading of the chapter's silent
   territory."* The fix has landed cleanly. The through-line is now
   sharp here. The remaining wobble is small: the §7 climate
   application says the chapter teaches that Arjuna *is* an
   instrument of *kala* — but the chapter's surface says Arjuna is
   *nimitta-matra* and the *kala* role is Krishna's. The through-
   line on climate (you are inside *kala* at planetary scale; act
   without ownership) is right, but the §7 prose drifts toward a
   reading the §6 has just chastised.

5. **Ch. 14 §6 vs. Ch. 7.** *"Whatever one's larger metaphysics, the
   experiential reality of the gunatita description in 14.22–14.25
   requires that there is a place from which the gunas can be seen
   as gunas. That place is not a fourth guna, and it is not nothing.
   I am not asking the reader to commit to Brahman."* This is
   metaphysical minimalism — the regulative-only position the
   validated through-line has displaced. After Ch. 7's commitment
   to qualified non-dualism, the explicit *"I am not asking the
   reader to commit to Brahman"* feels like a back-step. The
   through-line says: *the metaphysical floor has been declared in
   Ch. 7; subsequent chapters can reference it.* Ch. 14 § 6 reads
   instead as if the floor were still being held open.

6. **Ch. 9 §6 and §7 vs. through-line.** §6 says bhakti is *the
   chapter's working answer for the embodied reader on grounds of
   accessibility* — close to Ch. 12's stance, per the validated
   intention 4.5. But the chapter's earlier §3 and §4 still tilt
   toward bhakti-as-the-chapter's-naming, and §7's
   *"this-day-is-what-I-have"* prescriptive cue, although now framed
   descriptively ("a person who does this often says, inwardly,
   something like…"), still *sounds* like a teaching from inside a
   tradition. The through-line's "non-sectarian" stance and Ch. 9's
   pastoral warmth pull against each other slightly. The fix is
   probably another small descriptive move in §7's last paragraph.

## 3. Cross-chapter consistency on shared concepts

### Atman

Treated in Chs. 2, 6, 7, 13, 14, 15. The trajectory:

- Ch. 2 §6: regulative-posture register; "I do not claim to settle
  the metaphysical status of the atman."
- Ch. 6 §2: doubled atman of 6.5 (friend / enemy of the self) held
  ontologically open by design.
- Ch. 7 §6: qualified non-dualism declared as the floor; "a Gita
  that teaches a real world held in real relation to a real divine."
- Ch. 13 §6: the *kshetrajna* held as "perspective from inside the
  field, shaped by the field's organization, not separable from it
  but not reducible to it either."
- Ch. 14 §6: "I am not asking the reader to commit to Brahman."
- Ch. 15 §6: *purusottama* as relation-not-place-on-the-inventory.

**Inconsistency:** Chs. 2 and 14 read more minimalist than Chs. 7,
13, and 15. Ch. 7 is the declared floor; Ch. 2's older "regulative"
framing has been softened in the conciseness pass but not all the
way; Ch. 14's *"I am not asking the reader to commit to Brahman"*
sentence reads as the regulative-only stance the floor has displaced.
**Fix:** Ch. 14 §6 should reference Ch. 7's commitment ("the
metaphysical floor declared in Ch. 7 — qualified non-dualism — is
where the witness-distinction lives") rather than re-bracketing.

### Bhakti

Treated in Chs. 7, 9, 11, 12, 18. The trajectory:

- Ch. 7 §3.5 (Schweig): bhakti-literary register named.
- Ch. 9 §6: bhakti as universally accessible *practice* (post-
  conciseness; was *path*) — "on grounds of accessibility, while the
  metaphysical question of which path is finally higher is held
  open."
- Ch. 11 §6: bhakti as the mode of relation in which the cosmic
  vision becomes survivable.
- Ch. 12 §6: bhakti is more accessible for embodied beings, *not*
  metaphysically supreme.
- Ch. 18 §6: bhakti folded into the synthesis, not a fourth path.

**Inconsistency:** Ch. 9 §6 still reads with more rhetorical heat
than Ch. 12 §6. Ch. 9 says: *"I read with the bhakti traditions
— Rāmānuja, Aurobindo, the practical instinct of Easwaran — and
against strict Advaita on the question of whether bhakti is a path
among others or the universally accessible practice the chapter is
opening."* The *"against strict Advaita"* phrasing reads as polemic,
while Ch. 12 carefully declines metaphysical supremacy and walks
back the path-supremacy implication. **Fix:** Ch. 9 §6 should
reference the manuscript's settled stance (Ch. 12's accessibility-
not-supremacy) and trim the "against strict Advaita" framing. The
through-line is consistent only if Ch. 9 reads forward to Ch. 12,
not as a self-contained verdict.

### Sankara as foil

This was the prior editor's largest cumulative-pattern flag. The
conciseness pass has helped but not fully resolved it.

- Ch. 1 §6: Sankara absorbed cleanly without affective caricature
  (the editor's 3.1 fix).
- Ch. 2 §3.1: full engagement.
- Ch. 5 §6: "I am holding the operational shape of his diagnosis and
  grafting it onto the active-life synthesis — precisely the move his
  soteriology rules out. The reader should know the present synthesis
  borrows Sankara's diagnosis while declining his prescription."
  Clean.
- Ch. 7 §6: choice against strong-Advaita reading on this chapter.
- Ch. 8 §6: **Sankara carries** ("On the metaphysical pointer:
  Sankara carries"). This is the chapter's strongest move and works.
- Ch. 9 §6: "against strict Advaita" framing, see bhakti above.
- Ch. 10 §3.1: "the manuscript's choice against it for the modern
  reader was made in Ch. 5 and Ch. 7." Good, points back.
- Ch. 11 §6: choice against Sankara on cosmic form (preferring
  Ramanuja's reading of viśvarupa as real disclosure).
- Ch. 12 §6: more measured — "Sankara's caution against collapsing
  the absolute into a personal name remains defensible."
- Ch. 13 §6: **Sankara given substantial weight** ("on this
  chapter Sankara is doing real work, and I want to keep him close").
  Good — this is the second chapter where Sankara is allowed to
  carry, per the validated intention 4.4.
- Ch. 14 §3.1: Sankara's witness-distinction kept; full metaphysics
  set aside.
- Ch. 15 §6: Sankara's *deconstructive instinct* honoured but not
  taken as the whole story.
- Ch. 16 §3.1: Sankara's epistemic point absorbed.
- Ch. 17 §3.1: Sankara as preparatory diagnostic — not central.
- Ch. 18 §3.1: choice against strong Sankaran reading of 18.66 on
  textual grounds.

**Inconsistency:** Chs. 8 and 13 *do* let Sankara carry, per the
validated intention. The manuscript-wide pattern of "against strict
Advaita" still appears in Chs. 7, 9, 11, 18. The *aggregate* feels
less polemical than at review/04 because Chs. 8 and 13 actively
weight Sankara forward; the residual problem is Ch. 9, where the
"against strict Advaita" framing has not been softened enough.

### The witness / *kshetrajna* / *sthitaprajna*

Treated in Chs. 2, 5, 6, 13, 14. Cross-chapter consistency is good;
the manuscript reads these as one teaching in different vocabularies
(Ch. 14 §8 explicitly says "*gunatita* and *sthitaprajna* are the
same posture in different vocabulary"). The trajectory holds.

### Karma yoga / *anasakti* / *tyaga*

Treated in Chs. 2, 3, 5, 6, 18. Strong consistency. Ch. 3's four
pillars (*anasakti*, *yajna*, *svadharma*, *lokasangraha*) carry
through; Ch. 18 explicitly gathers them. The reader feels these as
one teaching, not four versions of one.

### Attention as discipline

Treated in Chs. 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 13, 16, 17. Strong consistency —
this is the manuscript's load-bearing modern thread. Murdoch and
Weil recur; the conciseness pass has not damaged this. The
diversification recommendation in 4.10 (Hopkins as another voice in
Ch. 10; Arendt as primary in Ch. 16) is partly implemented; Hopkins
is in Ch. 10 §4.3, Arendt is in Ch. 16 §4.1. Good.

### Devotion as orientation vs. devotion as path

Treated in Chs. 7, 9, 12, 18. The manuscript's settled stance
(per validated intention 4.5) is *bhakti as accessibility-not-
supremacy*. The Ch. 9 / Ch. 12 inconsistency above is the main
remaining problem.

### Specific concept-level inconsistencies

1. **Ch. 2 §6 vs. Ch. 7 §6 on metaphysics.** Already flagged.
   Severity: medium. The conciseness pass has softened but not
   displaced the regulative framing in Ch. 2.

2. **Ch. 4 §6 *avatara*-as-function vs. Ch. 11 §6 *visvarupa*-as-real-
   disclosure.** Ch. 4 brackets *avatara* metaphysics; Ch. 11
   commits to the cosmic form as real. The two are not contradictory
   but the reader feels different rules. The conciseness pass has
   not addressed this. Per the prior editor 3.4, the manuscript needs
   to name (in front matter, ideally) the discipline by which it
   sometimes brackets and sometimes commits.

3. **Ch. 9 §6 *bhakti as universally accessible practice* vs.
   Ch. 12 §6 *accessibility-not-metaphysical-supremacy*.** The
   conciseness pass has softened *path* to *practice* but not the
   "against strict Advaita" framing. **Fix:** Ch. 9 should explicitly
   point forward to Ch. 12 — "the metaphysical question of supremacy
   is held open and engaged at length in Ch. 12" — and the reader
   would feel the consistency.

4. **Ch. 11 §5.2 psychedelic discussion vs. its own bound.** The
   conciseness pass has compressed this substantially; the tension
   the prior editor flagged (the bound disclaimed the rhetorical
   effect) has been reduced. But the §5.2 still leans on the
   structural-psychology point through implicit comparison to drug-
   induced ego dissolution, even where it disclaims direct
   identification. Minor remaining wobble.

5. **Ch. 13 §6's "Aurobindo's reading carries the chapter's
   own internal coherence furthest" — fix landed.** Now reads "on
   the criterion of internal coherence with the Gita as a whole,
   Aurobindo's reading fits best, in my judgment." Clean.

6. **Ch. 14 §6 minimalism vs. Ch. 7 commitment.** Already flagged.

7. **Ch. 17 §6 "*Om Tat Sat* less as ritual invocation that
   sanctifies practice from outside it, and more as the chapter's
   reminder that any honest practice is implicitly oriented toward
   what it cannot itself name."** Fix has landed cleanly; the magic-
   formula strawman is gone.

8. **Ch. 18 §6 demographic argument removed, textual argument
   carries.** The fix has landed. Clean.

## 4. Cumulative pattern issues

### Pattern 1: The Murdoch-Weil-attention thread has become the manuscript's voice

Murdoch appears load-bearing in Chs. 6, 7, 9, 10, 12, 16. Weil in
Chs. 6, 9, 10, 14, 16. The validated intention (4.10) noted this
and called for some diversification. The conciseness pass has done
some of this — Hopkins has joined in Ch. 10, Arendt in Ch. 16, ACT
and Hayes in Chs. 5, 17, 18. But Murdoch and Weil still feel like
the manuscript's primary modern voice across more than half the
chapters.

**What the reader feels:** by Ch. 12 the reader can predict that
Murdoch's "just and loving gaze" will appear, and it does. By Ch. 16
the reader notices the structural recurrence and wonders whether
this is a 20th-century-Western-philosophy pipeline. The validated
intention's call for diversification (4.10) is the right diagnosis;
the conciseness pass has implemented it partially.

**Suggested acknowledgment:** front-matter naming of "attention is
the through-line's load-bearing modern vocabulary; Murdoch and Weil
are the working bridge between classical practice and 2026 life"
(per validated intention Part 1) would convert what reads as
*default* into *named choice*. The reader who knows the choice has
been made stops noticing the recurrence as drift.

### Pattern 2: The manuscript's "I do not adjudicate" / "I am not entitled to decide" / "I am not asking the reader to commit"

This appears in Chs. 6, 7, 8, 12, 13, 14. By Ch. 14 the reader can
hear it as a tic. The phrasings vary — "I leave the reader to" / "I
will not settle" / "the chapter does not adjudicate" — but the move
is the same. After the conciseness pass the manuscript has named the
metaphysical floor in Ch. 7; the residual "I do not adjudicate"
moves in subsequent chapters now read against the floor rather than
in the absence of one.

**What the reader feels:** in Ch. 7 the move feels honest; by Ch. 14
it feels like protective hedging. The reader is unsure whether the
manuscript is committing or holding-open, and the recurrence makes
the *commitment* in Ch. 7 feel less load-bearing.

**Suggested acknowledgment:** front-matter naming of the
metaphysical floor (per validated intention Part 1) would let later
chapters reference it rather than re-open it. The discipline
"floor declared in Ch. 7; subsequent chapters reference it; the
chapters that bracket are the chapters where the bracketing is
doing chapter-specific work" should be visible.

### Pattern 3: The "cosmic-form-and-the-personal-face" structure recurs across Chs. 11, 12, 15

This is a *good* recurring framing. Ch. 11 stages it (the *visvarupa*
is too much; Arjuna asks for the personal form back). Ch. 12 names
it (the embodied human relates to the divine through *priya*).
Ch. 15 generalises it (*purusottama* as relation, the axe pointed at
something larger than oneself). The reader benefits from the
structural rhyme.

**What the reader feels:** by Ch. 15 the reader hears the chapter
as the metaphysical climax of an arc Chs. 11 and 12 prepared.

**Suggested acknowledgment:** Ch. 15 §6 already gestures at this
("the chapter resolves what looks like a contradiction in the Gita
between the personal Krishna and the impersonal Brahman") but does
not name the Ch. 11–12 preparation. One sentence in Ch. 15 §6 would
make the structural rhyme visible.

### Pattern 4: The "this-is-Claude's-extension" discipline is now landed but unevenly

The validated intention specifies that Ch. 11's *kala*-extension
and Ch. 16's fourth-gate should be reframed as Claude's reading of

## 7. Front-matter recommendations

The validated intention specifies a "What this book is for" piece.
Here are 8 specific items the front matter must communicate, with
the chapter where each becomes a stumbling block if not communicated.

### 7.1. The book's core claim, in one paragraph in the book's own voice
This is the through-line stated as a single proposition. The reader
who reads cover-to-cover assembles it themselves; the reader who
reads non-linearly does not get it until late. **Without this:** by
Ch. 5 the reader is asking *what is this book trying to install in
me?* The validated intention's Part 1 paragraph is the right shape.

### 7.2. The audience named
Educated, English-speaking, urban-or-suburban working adult in 2026,
in adult relationships, in middle life, with a phone that runs more
of their day than they would admit, at a threshold (caregiving,
grief, layoff, relationship, parenting, vocational drift). **Without
this:** by Ch. 7 the reader notices the §7 sections are calibrated
for someone — and wonders if it is them. By Ch. 13 the reader who is
not in this audience feels the manuscript writes around them
without naming the choice. Naming the audience is generous to
readers it does *not* address.

### 7.3. The metaphysical floor declared
Qualified non-dualism per Ch. 7: real world, real divine, real
relation, real-but-not-final small self. **Without this:** Ch. 2 §6
reads as the floor and Ch. 7 §6 reads as a register shift; Ch. 8's
apophatic register reads as a departure; Ch. 14's *"I am not asking
the reader to commit to Brahman"* reads as a back-step. With the
floor declared, all three read as chapter-specific moves inside a
named commitment.

### 7.4. Attention named as the modern-reader hub
Murdoch's *just and loving gaze*, Weil's *attention is the rarest
and purest form of generosity*, Murdoch's *attention as prayer*.
**Without this:** the reader notices the recurrence by Ch. 9 and
reads it as drift; the conciseness pass cannot undo the cumulative
effect. With it named, the recurrence converts from default to
choice.

### 7.5. The governing disciplines named once
- Bracketed metaphysics, kept practice
- Science as bound, not as proof
- Generosity to rejected readings
- Synthesis-as-position, exposed

**Without this:** the reader meets each discipline restated chapter
by chapter; the conciseness pass has trimmed the per-chapter
restatements but the discipline still feels like an unstated rule.
With it named, every per-chapter restatement can come out and the
reader can read the §6 sections against the named criteria.

### 7.6. The center-of-gravity named
The working synthesis is the Ramanuja–Aurobindo–Easwaran–Gandhi
cluster, with Sankara, Madhva, Prabhupada, Tilak, and the
Theosophical strand engaged honestly and chosen against in some
chapters and chosen with in others. **Without this:** the reader who
notices the pattern (typically by Ch. 12) reads it as a manuscript-
wide tilt the manuscript has not acknowledged. With it named, the
reader knows what they are reading and can disagree precisely.

### 7.7. The Sankara discipline
Sankara is one of three load-bearing classical voices, set aside in
some chapters (Chs. 5, 7, 9, 11, 18) and let to carry in others
(Chs. 8, 13). The choice is named per chapter, not re-litigated.
**Without this:** the reader feels the manuscript reads as long
covert argument against non-dualism; the choice in Chs. 8 and 13
to let Sankara carry seems anomalous. With it named, the chapter-
by-chapter choice is legible as the manuscript's named pluralism.

### 7.8. The genre claim
The book is *about how to live*, with reading-the-Gita-well as the
apparatus. **Without this:** Ch. 7 leans toward how-to-read; Ch. 13
leans toward how-to-read; the reader is uncertain which is the
spine and which is the apparatus.

**Two further items the front matter could communicate (numbers 9–10):**

### 7.9. The Gita as a householder's text
Read as instruction for the working non-monastic life. The
renunciate readings are engaged in §3 sections but not the
manuscript's chosen audience. **Without this:** the reader who
encounters the manuscript's gradual choice against renunciate
framing across Chs. 5, 6, 12, 18 wonders whether the choice is
deliberate. With it named, every §3 engagement of renunciate
readings can be honest and unambiguous about who the manuscript is
for.

### 7.10. The book is an AI synthesis, declared as such
Per plan/00. **Without this:** the reader wonders by Ch. 1 §7 ("I
write this knowing I am an AI…") whether the AI-authored framing
is a one-time disclaimer or a sustained claim. The validated
intention has this in Part 1; the front matter should make it
plain.

The first 8 items are essential. Items 9 and 10 sharpen the read but
8 is the load-bearing minimum.
