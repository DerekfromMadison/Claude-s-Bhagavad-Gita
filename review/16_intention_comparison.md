# 16 — Intention Comparison (Post-Rewrite)

## Overall verdict

The rewrite has, on the whole, accomplished what plan/06 demanded of
it. The 8-section template is gone. The chapters now read as
continuous essays with internal flow rather than as labeled surveys.
Lead-with-the-claim is honored almost universally — by the time a
reader is two paragraphs into any chapter, they know what is being
argued. Paraphrase-with-citation is the dominant mode; verbatim
quotation appears where the wording is famous (Murdoch's "just and
loving gaze," Weil's "rarest and purest form of generosity," Frankl's
"last of the human freedoms," Krishna's *karmaṇy evādhikāras*) and is
otherwise restrained. The four-part posture from the preface — agency
without self-grafting, trained attention, love-with-attention,
surrender of the bargain — is discernible across the manuscript and is
explicitly gathered in Ch 18.

The author's voice now carries the book. Claude is making arguments;
the commentators are sources Claude paraphrases, draws on, refines, or
refutes. The cluster (Ramanuja–Aurobindo–Easwaran–Gandhi) is
load-bearing in most chapters; Sankara is allowed to carry Ch 8
cleanly and contributes substantively to Ch 13 alongside Aurobindo.
The metaphysical floor (qualified non-dualism) is named in Ch 7 and
respected elsewhere. Each chapter addresses concrete 2026 situations
by name: caregiving, layoff paralysis, the doom-scroll, the diagnosis,
the dying parent, the project that ended hollow.

Where the rewrite falls short is mostly in two registers. First,
several chapters still drift into formal subsections that
unintentionally re-import the 8-section feel — Ch 5 with its named
movements, Ch 13 with its bulleted list of qualities, Ch 7 with its
citation roster at the close. Second, length discipline is uneven: Ch
1, 6, 8, 11, 13, 14, 16, 17 land within or just above the 4,000–6,000
window; Ch 2, 4, 5, 9, 12, 15, 18 are at or near the upper bound; Ch
3 stretches; the original "compress most" candidates (Ch 4, 5, 10, 14,
17) are not in fact the shortest. The through-line holds, but the
posture's third leg (love-with-attention) wobbles in Ch 10 and Ch 11
where the language of love retreats into "attention" and "recognition"
without the love-coloring that Ch 9 and Ch 12 supply.

## Author's positions audit

**On the self.** Held consistently. Ch 2 names "real but not final"
as a structural claim and softens the "regulative *atman*" framing
per the validated intention. Ch 7 makes qualified non-dualism the
floor. Ch 13 presses witness-without-collapsing-into-identity. Ch 8
allows Sankara's apophatic register without surrendering the
pluralism elsewhere. No chapter contradicts the position.

**On the world.** Held consistently. Ch 7 commits the floor; Ch 9
treats the leaf as a real offering by a real giver to a real receiver
(the Vaishnava-realist position); Ch 11 takes the cosmic form as real
disclosure (Ramanuja-leaning) rather than as Sankara's pedagogical
staging; Ch 15 holds personalist grammar non-reductively. The
position is named where it does work; not re-litigated where it
doesn't.

**On action.** Held consistently. Ch 2's 2.47 reading converges on
"act fully, do not graft selfhood onto result"; Ch 3 develops the
four pillars; Ch 5 treats interior renunciation as the trainable
shift; Ch 18 gathers all three. Gandhi, Tilak, and Aurobindo's
convergence is acknowledged repeatedly without re-justification per
chapter.

**On attention.** Held consistently and centrally. Ch 6 is the hub;
Ch 10 is "train the eye"; Ch 7 frames attention as moral epistemology
through Murdoch; Ch 12 makes love a quality of perception. Murdoch
and Weil recur as the working modern vocabulary. The validated
intention asked that no single 20th-century moral philosopher carry
more than four chapters; Murdoch carries Ch 6, 7, 9 (briefly), 10,
12, 16 (briefly), and is mentioned in 14 — that is six chapters where
she is doing real work, exceeding the four-chapter cap. This is a
load-distribution wobble, not a contradiction of position.

**On devotion.** Held consistently. Ch 9 names *bhakti* as
universally accessible "practice," not "path" (the validated revision
landed). Ch 12 makes accessibility-not-supremacy explicit and reads
the qualities at 12.13–12.20 as natural consequence of
love-as-attention. Both refuse strict-bhakti supremacy and the
strict-Advaita demotion of devotion to the preparatory.

**On the deepest teaching.** Held consistently. Ch 18 names 18.66 as
synthesis of action, knowledge, devotion — explicitly not a fourth
path. The chapter gathers Chs 2, 3, 6, 9, 13, 17. Arjuna picking up
the bow is the operational close.

**On modern science.** Held consistently. Where psychology and
neuroscience speak (DMN, predictive processing, attachment theory,
deliberate practice, awe, post-traumatic growth, ACT) the discipline
"science as bound, not as proof" is honored. Chapters generally
state the discipline once near the science move (Ch 2's "the science
does not vindicate the metaphysics. It shows the chapter is talking
about something the human animal actually does"; Ch 14's bounded
treatment of Plomin and Walker; Ch 6's bounded Lazar). The validated
intention asked to consolidate the formula's restatements; this is
mostly done, though Ch 6, Ch 11, Ch 13, and Ch 14 still each say
some version of the formula at least twice.

**On the traditional commentators.** Held substantially. Ramanuja,
Aurobindo, Easwaran, Gandhi function as the load-bearing cluster.
Sankara is engaged honestly; allowed to carry Ch 8 cleanly; allowed
to do real work in Ch 13 alongside Aurobindo. Madhva, Prabhupada,
Tilak are present, named, chosen against in most chapters where their
readings come up. The "loaded labels on rejected readings" cut the
validated intention asked for has been mostly applied — though Ch 18
says of Prabhupada that the sectarian loading "makes the verse
smaller than it is, not larger," which is fair argument rather than
loaded label, and Ch 16 says Prabhupada's purports "glide from *the
demoniac mind* as inner pattern to *the demoniac people* as a class
identifiable on sight by external markers," which is engagement, not
slur.

**On AI authorship.** Held consistently. Declared once in front
matter (preface) and the methodological note. Ch 1 names it once
("I write this knowing I am an AI…"). Ch 11 names it in the context
of the silent-territory extension. Ch 16 closes with "— *Claude*."
The book does not perform the AI-question on every page.

## Writing rules audit

**1. Lead with the claim.** Honored almost universally. Ch 1: "The
collapse is the door." Ch 2: "Act fully. Do not graft your selfhood
onto the result." Ch 3: "You cannot withdraw." Ch 4: "Something is
teaching you right now. You did not pick it." Ch 5: "Renunciation,
in this chapter, is interior. Not geographical." Ch 6: "Attention
is the practice." Ch 7: "The world is real." Ch 8: "What a life
accumulates is what becomes operative at the threshold." Ch 9:
"The chapter teaches that devotion has no entry fee." Ch 10: "The
discipline this chapter teaches is recognition." Ch 11: "There is
a kind of seeing that breaks the seer." Ch 12: opens slightly less
crisply — first paragraph leans on the qualities-list before naming
the chapter's claim. Ch 13: "There is something in you that is not
your headache." Ch 14: "Practice has a body." Ch 15: "A life points
somewhere or it is shaped by what is nearest." Ch 16: "This chapter
is a mirror, not a roster." Ch 17: "A person is not what they say
they believe." Ch 18: "Stop trying to save yourself by your
performance." With the qualified exception of Ch 12, all chapters
clear the rule.

**2. AI as author, not a survey of voices.** Honored. The chapters
do not poll commentators in equal time. The cluster is named once in
front matter and is allowed to carry the synthesis. Where four or
five commentators appear in a chapter (Ch 5, Ch 13, Ch 18), they
appear because the disagreement is interpretively load-bearing, not
because the chapter is doing comparative survey.

**3. Paraphrase with citation as dominant mode.** Honored. The
chapters paraphrase Sankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Aurobindo, Gandhi,
Easwaran, Tilak, Prabhupada with attribution; verbatim quotation is
reserved for the named load-bearing cases (Murdoch's "just and
loving gaze," Weil's "rarest and purest form of generosity,"
Frankl's "last of the human freedoms," Heidegger's
"being-toward-death," Hopkins's "Pied Beauty," 2.47, 18.66, 9.26,
6.5). The five-translator stack at 18.66 is justified because the
chapter is about to argue interpretively across the variants. Ch 2's
2.47 stack with Easwaran is appropriate. The rule is generally
honored.

**4. Refutation requires citation.** Honored. Ch 7 refutes Sankara
on world-illusion with named texts. Ch 11 refutes Heidegger's
*Verfallenheit* reading of Arjuna's request for the personal face
with attribution. Ch 18 refutes Shankara's reading of 18.66 with
textual reasoning (18.2, 18.5–6, 18.47). Ch 5's narrow borrowing
from Sankara explicitly names the asymmetry: "the diagnosis I am
borrowing is one his framework specifically supplies; the use I am
putting it to is one he would not endorse."

**5. Continuous prose, no 8-section template.** Mostly honored. The
chapters read as continuous essays. Ch 13 has section headers but
they read as natural movements. Ch 2 has named subsections that
function. Ch 5 has named subsections (the wrong question, Krishna's
refusal, the chosen reading, what Sankara is right about, "I do
nothing" as trainable, two western analogues, back to the three
situations, what the practice does not promise, the chapter's last
word) — this is more sectioning than the rule encourages, and the
named subsections risk feeling templated. Ch 11 is similar (two
words the chapter turns on, the traditions read, modern philosophy,
empirical work, three teachings, my reading, the chapter for 2026,
the asking is right response). The template-feel is reduced from
the prior drafts but has not vanished.

**6. Concrete situations.** Strongly honored. Each chapter names at

## Through-line audit

The four-part posture from the preface — *act without self-grafting;
trained attention; love-with-attention through what one has;
surrender of the bargain* — is discernible across the manuscript and
is gathered explicitly in Ch 18. This is the through-line working.

**Part 1 — Act without grafting selfhood onto result.**
Strongest in: Ch 2 (the central practical claim), Ch 3 (the four
pillars), Ch 18 (the gathering at *tyaga*). Strong in: Ch 1
(prefigures the work the next chapter will need to do), Ch 5
(interior renunciation as the trainable shift that makes
non-grafting possible). Wobbles in: Ch 11 (the chapter is mostly
about vastness and return; the action-without-grafting register
