# Missing Viewpoints — A Per-Chapter Catalog

**Reviewer's role.** This document does not edit the chapters. It surveys each
of the 18 completed drafts and lists viewpoints, voices, schools, and
science strands the chapter did *not* engage but that were available within
the project's own source catalog (`plan/03_SOURCES_AND_VIEWPOINTS.md`) or
within the Gita's broader interpretive ecosystem. Each candidate is tagged
**include** (a strong gap-filling addition), **consider** (plausible but not
load-bearing), or **skip** (listed for completeness, probably not worth the
word count). The user makes the final call.

---

## Executive summary

The manuscript engages an unusually deep classical and modern dialogue
chapter by chapter, but the *distribution* of voices is uneven, and several
substantial regions of the source catalog never make it onto the page. The
ten observations below name the most consequential gaps.

**Manuscript-wide gaps.**

1. **Madhva's Dvaita appears substantively in only ~5 of 18 chapters
   (7, 10, 13, 15, 18).** It is the third great *acharya* tradition and the
   plan/03 catalog promises it as a baseline lens. Chapters where Madhva
   would have sharpened the reading — 2 (the imperishable self), 4
   (*avatara*), 8 (the moment of death), 9 (immanence), 12 (devotion) — go
   without it.
2. **Feminist and post-colonial readings are absent from all 18 chapters.**
   plan/03 explicitly lists this category (item #12) "to be expanded"; it
   was not. Veena Howard, Arvind Sharma, Vrinda Dalmiya, Laurie Patton's
   own gender-attentive scholarship, and Rita DasGupta Sherma are
   load-bearing absences, especially around 1.40 (women, mixed castes),
   3.35/18.47 (*svadharma*), 9.32, and 16's gendered virtue-list.
3. **Vivekananda, Yogananda, Vinoba Bhave, Chinmayananda, and Jnaneshwar
   appear nowhere — all five are zero-mentioned across every chapter.**
   These are five of the most-read Indian voices on the Gita worldwide.
   Annie Besant appears only in Ch. 6.
4. **Abhinavagupta / Kashmir Shaivism gets one mention (Ch. 9).** A
   non-dual tantric reading would have done specific work in Chs. 7
   (*prakriti* and *maya*), 10 (theophany), 11 (*visvarupa*), and 14
   (the gunas).
5. **Stoic counterpoints are properly developed only in Ch. 2.** Marcus
   Aurelius would have been natural in Chs. 8 (death) and 11 (cosmic
   form). Seneca on grief would have been natural in Ch. 1. Epictetus on
   role/duty would have been natural in Chs. 3 and 18.
6. **Existentialism and phenomenology are unevenly applied.** Sartre is in
   Ch. 1; Heidegger is in Chs. 8, 11, 15; Merleau-Ponty is in Ch. 13.
   Camus on the absurd is missing from Ch. 1 and Ch. 11. Jaspers on
   limit-situations is missing from Chs. 1 and 8.
7. **Buddhist counterpoints are uneven.** Bhikkhu Bodhi appears in Chs. 2,
   6, 14. Thanissaro and Batchelor are zero. The Gita-Buddhism contrast
   on *anatta* / *atman* and on *nirvana* / *brahma-nirvana* is therefore
   under-developed in Chs. 5, 8, 12, and 18.
8. **Affective neuroscience is under-used.** Damasio (somatic markers) and
   Lisa Feldman Barrett (constructed emotion) appear in zero chapters.
   They would have done specific work in Chs. 1, 2, 12, 13, 14, 16.
9. **Behavioral/decision science is under-used outside Ch. 1.** Kahneman
   sits in Ch. 1; Tversky and Sunstein/nudge are absent. Choice
   architecture is natively a Gita-3, Gita-16, Gita-17 conversation.
10. **The Stoic-clinical bridge (CBT/DBT) is missing.** ACT (Hayes)
    appears in Chs. 5, 17, 18; Beck (CBT) and Linehan (DBT) appear nowhere.
    The CBT-Gita parallel on cognitive reframing is precisely what Chs. 2,
    6, 14, and 16 are doing.

**Highest-priority cross-chapter additions (10).**

1. **Madhva's Dvaita reading via Subba Rao or Bannanje Govindacharya** —
   add to Chs. 2, 4, 8, 9, 12.
2. **Veena Howard / Arvind Sharma on gender and the Gita** — add to Chs.
   1 (1.40 on women), 3/18 (svadharma and varna), 16 (virtue/vice
   gendering), as a manuscript-wide thread.
3. **Vinoba Bhave's *Talks on the Gita*** — Gandhian-but-interior; add to
   Chs. 3, 5, 6, 9, 18.
4. **Jnaneshwar's *Jnaneshwari*** — Marathi vernacular bhakti; add to Chs.
   9, 12, 18 (the bhakti spine).
5. **Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*** — add to Chs. 8 (death) and 11
   (cosmic form, the smallness of personal stakes against time).
6. **Lisa Feldman Barrett's *How Emotions Are Made*** — add to Chs. 2
   (equanimity), 12 (cultivated love), 14 (the gunas as constructed
   affect), 16 (anger/lust/greed as habits, not entities).
7. **Antonio Damasio (somatic markers, *Descartes' Error*)** — add to Chs.
   1, 13, 17 (faith as embodied disposition).
8. **Aaron Beck / cognitive therapy** — add to Chs. 2, 6, 14 as the
   clinical analogue to *sthitaprajna* training.
9. **Abhinavagupta (Kashmir Shaiva non-dualism, via the Gitarthasangraha)**
   — add to Chs. 7, 10, 11, 14.
10. **Camus's *The Myth of Sisyphus*** — add to Ch. 1 (paralysis at the
    threshold) and Ch. 11 (the absurd of cosmic indifference).

Adding any subset of these would meaningfully strengthen the manuscript's
representativeness without diluting its synthesis.

## Chapter 1 — Arjuna's Despair

**What was engaged.** Sankara, Ramanuja, Prabhupada, Gandhi, Aurobindo,
Stoler Miller, Easwaran (7 schools); modern: Bernard Williams, Nussbaum,
Jonathan Shay, Sartre; science: Sapolsky on stress, Kahneman, O'Connor on
grief.

### Candidates not engaged

**A. Classical / interpretive schools**
- **Dvaita (Madhva)** — proposed voice: Madhva via Subba Rao's translation.
  Why it would fit: Madhva's strict realism would sharpen the case that
  Arjuna's grief is for *real* persons whose loss is not collapsible to
  *moha*. (recommend: consider)
- **Kashmir Shaivism (Abhinavagupta)** — proposed voice: Abhinavagupta's
  *Gitarthasangraha*. Why fit: a tantric non-dualism that takes the body's
  collapse as a real spiritual datum, not a confusion. (recommend: consider)
- **Theosophical (Annie Besant)** — Why fit: Besant reads 1.28–1.47 as the
  pre-initiatory crisis of the aspirant; relevant counterweight to
  Aurobindo's similar but more philosophical version. (recommend: skip)
- **Feminist / post-colonial** — proposed voice: Veena Howard on gender
  and Gandhi's Gita; Arvind Sharma on the marginal women in 1.40. Why fit:
  1.40's "women without husbands will produce mixed castes" is a load-
  bearing line that reads very differently with a feminist lens.
  (recommend: include)

**B. Named commentators/translators**
- **Vinoba Bhave, *Talks on the Gita*** — Why fit: Vinoba reads Chapter 1
  as the moment of moral clarity arrived at through grief; pairs naturally
  with Gandhi but more interior. (recommend: consider)
- **Christopher Isherwood / Prabhavananda** — Why fit: their introduction
  treats Arjuna's collapse devotionally; useful as a Vedanta-Society
  alternative voice to Easwaran's pastoral. (recommend: skip)
- **Edwin Arnold (*The Song Celestial*)** — Why fit: Gandhi's first
  encounter was through Arnold; quoting Arnold on 1.47 would let the reader
  see the Victorian register that shaped 20th-century Anglophone reception.
  (recommend: consider)
- **Laurie Patton (Penguin Classics 2008)** — Why fit: Patton's
  introduction emphasizes the *dialogic* frame, useful here where Krishna
  is silent. (recommend: include)

**C. Modern counterpoint voices**
- **Albert Camus, *The Myth of Sisyphus*** — Why fit: Camus's "judging
  whether life is worth living is the truly serious philosophical question"
  is a near-perfect twentieth-century echo of Arjuna's *I would rather be
  killed than do this*. (recommend: include)
- **Karl Jaspers on *Grenzsituationen* (limit-situations)** — Why fit:
  Jaspers names exactly the kind of moment Ch. 1 depicts; complements
  Sartre and Williams. (recommend: consider)
- **Seneca, *Letters to Lucilius* (esp. Letter 99 on grief)** — Why fit:
  Seneca's careful treatment of anticipatory grief and proportionate
  mourning would dialogue precisely with O'Connor on grief-prediction.
  (recommend: include)
- **Iris Murdoch on attention and "fat relentless ego"** — Why fit:
  Murdoch's account of moral perception under stress would frame what
  Arjuna *cannot stop seeing*. (recommend: consider)

**D. Modern science strands**
- **Stephen Porges / polyvagal theory** — Why fit: the chapter already
  invokes freeze and tonic immobility; one explicit polyvagal paragraph
  (with caveats) would help the reader. (recommend: skip — already nodded
  to with appropriate hedges)
- **Antonio Damasio, *Descartes' Error* (somatic markers)** — Why fit:
  Damasio's somatic-marker hypothesis is precisely what 1.28–1.30 depicts
  — the body's affective signal *as* moral cognition. (recommend: include)
- **Roy Baumeister / meaning-making after trauma** — Why fit: connects
  Arjuna's collapse to the post-traumatic-growth literature that Ch. 18
  uses. (recommend: consider)
- **Klaus Scherer / appraisal theory of emotion** — Why fit: appraisal
  theory is exactly what Arjuna's 35-verse argument is doing; would
  complement Kahneman. (recommend: skip)

### Top-3 priority adds
1. **Damasio's somatic markers** — load-bearing for the chapter's claim
   that the body's collapse is informational, not weakness.
2. **Camus on the serious question** — closes the existentialist-trio
   (Sartre, Williams, Camus) the chapter half-opens.
3. **Veena Howard / feminist reading of 1.40** — fills a manuscript-wide
   gap exactly where 1.40 invites it.

## Chapter 2 — The Eternal Reality

**What was engaged.** Sankara, Ramanuja, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Radhakrishnan,
Mascaró, Easwaran (7 schools); modern: Epictetus, Parfit, Bhikkhu Bodhi;
science: Deci/Ryan, Davidson on equanimity, DMN, cognitive control of
emotion.

### Candidates not engaged

**A. Classical / interpretive schools**
- **Dvaita (Madhva)** — proposed voice: Madhva via the *Gita Bhashya* of
  Madhva (trans. Bannanje Govindacharya). Why fit: chapter 2 is the
  imperishable-self chapter; Madhva's reading — selves are eternally
  distinct from each other and from God — is precisely the foil to
  Sankara's non-duality and Parfit's reductionism. (recommend: include)
- **Kashmir Shaivism (Abhinavagupta)** — proposed voice: Abhinavagupta's
  reading of the imperishable as *spanda* (vibratory consciousness). Why
  fit: gives a non-Vedantic non-dualism to the imperishable-self
  conversation. (recommend: consider)
- **Gaudiya Vaishnava (Prabhupada)** — Why fit: Prabhupada is the most
  widely-read English Gita and is conspicuously not in Ch. 2's traditions
  section. He has very specific things to say about 2.13, 2.20, 2.22,
  2.47. (recommend: include)

**B. Named commentators/translators**
- **Vivekananda's lectures on the Gita** — Why fit: Vivekananda's
  practical-Vedanta reading of 2.47 was the mass-market 19th-century
  Anglophone framing; the chapter cannot fully account for the modern
  reception of "act without attachment to fruit" without him.
  (recommend: include)
- **Vinoba Bhave** — Why fit: his commentary on 2.47 explicitly pairs
  inner *anasakti* with concrete material renunciation. (recommend: consider)
- **Chinmayananda's *Holy Geeta*** — Why fit: the most circulated
  contemporary Indian Vedantic exposition; would balance the academic and
  pastoral voices. (recommend: consider)
- **Stephen Mitchell** — Why fit: not from the Sanskrit but a literary
  paraphrase that is widely encountered; flagging it (as plan/03 itself
  notes) would help readers triangulate. (recommend: skip)
- **Graham Schweig** — Why fit: Schweig's bhakti-attentive rendering of
  2.55–2.72 would surface a devotional register that's hard to hear in
  Easwaran's pastoral voice. (recommend: consider)

**C. Modern counterpoint voices**
- **Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations* Book 4–7** — Why fit: Marcus on the
  "view from above" and the impermanence of self maps onto 2.13–2.22 in a
  way that complements Epictetus on 2.47. (recommend: include)
- **Galen Strawson on the narrative self** — Why fit: Strawson's argument
  that not everyone experiences a continuous narrative self is the modern
  philosophical counterpoint to *atman*-talk. (recommend: consider)
- **Iris Murdoch, *The Sovereignty of Good*** — Why fit: Murdoch's "unselfing"
  is the closest English-language analogue to *sthitaprajna*. (recommend: include)
- **Thomas Nagel, "Death" (in *Mortal Questions*)** — Why fit: 2.13–2.22's
  argument that "the self does not die" benefits from Nagel's careful
  account of why the deprivation of death seems bad even if the self does
  not survive. (recommend: consider)

**D. Modern science strands**
- **Antonio Damasio (somatic markers, "feeling of what happens")** —
  Why fit: complements Davidson on equanimity by showing how emotion
  *participates* in cognition rather than just being regulated. (recommend: include)
- **Lisa Feldman Barrett (constructed emotion theory)** — Why fit:
  Feldman Barrett's view that emotions are constructed predictions, not
  inbuilt entities, dialogues sharply with the *sthitaprajna*'s described
  imperturbability as a *trained* construction. (recommend: include)
- **Aaron Beck / cognitive therapy** — Why fit: the *sthitaprajna* portrait
  is, in modern clinical language, a cognitive-restructuring portrait;
  Beck's CBT is the explicit Stoic-derived analogue. (recommend: include)
- **Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi on flow** — Why fit: 2.47's outcome-independence
  is structurally what flow research finds about optimal performance.
  (recommend: skip — Csikszentmihalyi is in Ch. 3)
- **Anil Seth (predictive self)** — Why fit: predictive-processing accounts
  of the self complicate the *atman*/*anatman* contrast in productive
  ways. (recommend: consider)

### Top-3 priority adds
1. **Madhva's Dvaita reading on 2.13/2.20** — most under-represented
   classical school in the most metaphysically central chapter.
2. **Aaron Beck (CBT) and Lisa Feldman Barrett (constructed emotion)** —
   completes the clinical-scientific bridge to *sthitaprajna* training.
3. **Marcus Aurelius's *Meditations*** — completes the Stoic triangle the
   chapter's Epictetus section opens.

## Chapter 3 — The Yoga of Action

**What was engaged.** Sankara, Ramanuja, Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Miller,
Patton (7 schools); modern: Hannah Arendt, MacIntyre, Bernard Williams;
science: Deci/Ryan, Csikszentmihalyi flow, Graybiel basal ganglia/habit.

### Candidates not engaged

**A. Classical / interpretive schools**
- **Dvaita (Madhva)** — proposed voice: Madhva on 3.5/3.35 — that
  inescapable action arises from the *eternal* dependence of finite selves
  on God, not from Sankhya prakriti dynamics. Why fit: a third *acharya*
  voice on 3.5/3.35. (recommend: include)
- **Gaudiya Vaishnava (Prabhupada)** — Why fit: Prabhupada's reading of
  3.30 ("renouncing all action to me") is the Bhakti-yoga keystone for
  3's *karma yoga*. (recommend: include)
- **Theosophical (Besant)** — Why fit: Besant's reading of action in
  service of evolution; would interface with Aurobindo. (recommend: skip)
- **Contemplative-pastoral (Easwaran)** — Why fit: Easwaran's reading is
  load-bearing in adjacent chapters but the lens is thin here. (recommend:
  include)

**B. Named commentators/translators**
- **Vivekananda's *Karma-Yoga*** — Why fit: this is *the* book on this
  chapter for a generation of readers; missing it is a real gap.
  (recommend: include)
- **Vinoba Bhave** — Why fit: Vinoba's *Talks* on Ch. 3 is among the
  clearest interior-Gandhian readings of *yajna*. (recommend: include)
- **Yogananda (*God Talks With Arjuna*)** — Why fit: Yogananda's esoteric-
  psychological reading of *yajna* as inner sacrifice would be useful as
  the symbolic counterpart to Aurobindo's cosmic reading. (recommend: consider)
- **Edgerton** — Why fit: precise philological reading of *karma* and
  *yajna* that would balance the activist-modern weight. (recommend: consider)

**C. Modern counterpoint voices**
- **Hannah Arendt's *The Human Condition* on labor / work / action** —
  Why fit: the chapter cites Arendt on *vita activa* but does not
  distinguish Arendt's three categories (labor, work, action), each of
  which dialogues differently with *karma*. (recommend: include — even
  just expand the existing Arendt section)
- **Charles Taylor on social imaginaries and goods** — Why fit: 3.35's
  "one's own dharma" question is exactly the question of what social
  imaginary one acts within. (recommend: consider)
- **Epictetus on role ethics (*Discourses* I.2)** — Why fit: a Stoic
  *role* ethic alongside the Gandhian/Tilak readings of 3.35. (recommend:
  include)
- **Confucian ethics (Mencius on the four sprouts)** — Why fit: a non-
  Western, non-Indian counterpoint that takes "performing your role
  cultivates virtue" seriously. (recommend: skip)

**D. Modern science strands**
- **Wendy Wood, *Good Habits, Bad Habits*** — Why fit: contemporary habit
  science updates the Graybiel-basal-ganglia layer with 21st-century
  longitudinal data. (recommend: consider)
- **Roy Baumeister on self-regulation** — Why fit: although ego depletion
  has been challenged, the broader self-regulation literature is the
  natural science layer for *karma yoga as discipline*. (recommend: skip)
- **Cass Sunstein / Richard Thaler on choice architecture** — Why fit:
  3.35 / 18.47 *svadharma* questions are choice-architecture questions —
  what range of options the agent can in fact see. (recommend: consider)
- **Angela Duckworth on grit** — Why fit: useful but perhaps too thin;
  Duckworth's grit work has its own replication concerns. (recommend: skip)

### Top-3 priority adds
1. **Vivekananda's *Karma-Yoga*** — historically central, structurally
   missing.
2. **Madhva's Dvaita on 3.5/3.35** — third *acharya* on the chapter's
   core verses.
3. **Epictetus on role ethics** — makes Tilak's and Gandhi's *svadharma*
   readings dialogue with a non-Indian role ethic.

- **Robert Hogan / mentor research** — Why fit: 50 years of mentorship

- **Kahneman/Tversky on expert intuition** — Why fit: complements Ericsson

- **Albert Bandura on social learning theory** — Why fit: foundational

### Top-3 priority adds
1. **Jnaneshwar's *Jnaneshwari* opening on *paramparā*** — a vernacular

2. **Madhva on *avatara*** — completes the *acharya* triangle on 4.7–4.8.
3. **Carol Dweck on mindset / Murdoch and Weil on the disciple's

   teacher-disciple thread.

## Chapter 5 — Renunciation of Action

**What was engaged.** Sankara, Ramanuja, Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Easwaran,
Miller/Johnson/Edgerton/Sargeant, Prabhupada (8 schools); modern: Merton,
Eckhart, Frankl; science: Raichle/Brewer DMN, Farb two-mode self-reference,
Hayes ACT cognitive defusion.

### Candidates not engaged

**A. Classical / interpretive schools**
- **Dvaita (Madhva)** — proposed voice: Madhva on the *eternal* doer-status
  of the self even when actions are surrendered. Why fit: a sharp realist
  alternative to Sankara's "I do nothing" reading; the third *acharya*
  voice. (recommend: include)
- **Theosophical (Besant)** — Why fit: Besant on action without attachment
  as service to evolutionary intelligence; useful between Aurobindo and
  Easwaran. (recommend: skip)

**B. Named commentators/translators**
- **Vivekananda on work as worship** — Why fit: missing; Vivekananda on
  selfless service (*Karma-Yoga* lectures) is the canonical 19th-century
  framing of the chapter's central question. (recommend: include)
- **Vinoba Bhave** — Why fit: Vinoba's reading of 5.27–5.28 as continuous
  with householder life is close to the chapter's own synthesis.
  (recommend: include)
- **Schweig** — Why fit: bhakti-aware rendering of "resting in the self
  while active." (recommend: consider)

**C. Modern counterpoint voices**
- **Iris Murdoch on unselfing** — Why fit: precisely the operational stance
  the chapter names. The Murdoch material in Ch. 6 and Ch. 12 is exactly
  what Ch. 5 is asking for. (recommend: include)
- **Bernard Williams on integrity / "the absolute conception"** — Why fit:
  Williams's worry that "I do nothing" risks evacuating moral integrity is
  the strongest objection to the chapter's synthesis and deserves a
  hearing. (recommend: include)
- **Buddhist counterpoint (Bhikkhu Bodhi or Thanissaro on *anatta* and
  action)** — Why fit: the Gita's "I do nothing" is structurally close to
  Buddhist action-without-doer; the difference is *atman* vs *anatman*.
  (recommend: include)
- **Twelve-step "we are not the doer"** — Why fit: the Step 3 / Step 11
  literature on surrender is a contemporary lay version of 5.8–5.9. (Ch.
  18 uses this; it could appear briefly here.) (recommend: skip — already
  in Ch. 18)

**D. Modern science strands**
- **Csikszentmihalyi on flow** — Why fit: explicitly mentioned in passing
  in §7 but not engaged in §5 as a science strand. Self-loss-in-execution
  *is* what flow research describes. (recommend: include)
- **Jonathan Haidt on the rider and the elephant** — Why fit: dual-systems
  account of agent-as-self vs ground; useful complement to ACT's defusion.
  (recommend: consider)
- **Jud Brewer on craving and the posterior cingulate** — Why fit: the DMN
  paragraph cites Brewer 2011 but not Brewer's later targeted-intervention
  work showing how DMN-targeted training reduces craving. (recommend: consider)
- **Ellen Langer on mindfulness and effortless engagement** — Why fit:
  Langer's older "mindful work" research complements Brewer/Hayes.
  (recommend: skip)

### Top-3 priority adds
1. **Iris Murdoch on unselfing** — exact match for the chapter's central
   move; strange not to be there.
2. **Madhva's Dvaita** — completes the *acharya* spectrum on 5.8–5.9.
3. **Csikszentmihalyi on flow as the empirical face of "I do nothing"** —
   science strand promised by §7 but not delivered.

## Chapter 6 — Meditation

**What was engaged.** Sankara, Ramanuja, Prabhupada, Gandhi, Aurobindo,
Easwaran, Mascaró, Miller, Besant (9 voices); modern: Simone Weil, Iris
Murdoch, Bhikkhu Bodhi; science: Lazar cortical thickness, Brewer DMN,
Jha attention training.

### Candidates not engaged

**A. Classical / interpretive schools**
- **Dvaita (Madhva)** — proposed voice: Madhva on 6.5 ("lift oneself by
  oneself") as the structure of *prapatti* in graded form. Why fit: the
  third *acharya* voice on the chapter's central self-help verse.
  (recommend: include)
- **Kashmir Shaivism (Abhinavagupta)** — proposed voice: Abhinavagupta on
  meditation as recognition (*pratyabhijna*). Why fit: a non-Vedantic

## Closing note

The manuscript's chapter-by-chapter strength is real: most chapters
engage 7–10 distinct interpretive voices, with credible modern
counterpoints and named, citable science. The patterns above are not a
critique of the chapters' depth; they are a map of the source catalog's
unused capacity. The user can read this report as a menu and decide,
chapter by chapter, which absences are worth a paragraph and which are
worth letting stand.
