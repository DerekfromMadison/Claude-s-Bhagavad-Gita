# 12 — Over-Explanation Review

## The reader the manuscript currently writes for vs. the reader it should write for

The manuscript writes for two readers at once and never resolves the tension. The first is the brief's intended reader — a 2026 educated adult, philosophically literate, who recognizes Murdoch and Weil and Frankl by name, who has heard of the default mode network, who knows what existentialism is, who can hold a thesis across paragraphs, and who finds biographical mini-bios faintly insulting. The second, ghost reader is an undergraduate first-year encountering all of this for the first time: someone who needs the date of Murdoch's *Sovereignty of Good*, the institutional affiliation of every neuroscientist cited, a procedural reminder of where the chapter is in its own argument, and a glossary entry for *atman* in the seventh chapter that uses it.

The book's voice is calibrated to the second reader and apologizes to the first. Because of this it routinely (a) introduces recurring thinkers as if they had not appeared in the previous twelve chapters; (b) attaches lens-tags ("a Catholic theologian who taught at Union…") to figures whose lens has already been registered in the front matter; (c) defines terms — existentialism, the hard problem, the default mode network, the three poisons, the *vita activa* — at first appearance in each chapter that uses them, sometimes redefining them in adjacent chapters; (d) signposts the prose's own architecture ("First, attend to substrate. Second, strengthen sattva. Third, witness all three."); (e) recaps where the argument stands ("By chapter 12 the dialogue form has thinned to monologue"); (f) glosses its own usage ("By 'non-reductive personalist' I mean…"); and (g) hedges with chains of qualifying clauses where one would be enough.

The cumulative effect is paternalism. The reader the front matter promises — an adult who has come to the question of how to live operationally — does not need a paragraph on Iris Murdoch's institutional history every time she is cited. The reader currently being addressed needs that paragraph in chapter 6, again in chapter 7, again in chapter 9, again in chapter 10, again in chapter 12, again in chapter 13, again in chapter 16. Each individual instance is forgivable; the cumulative texture is over-helpful in a way that signals the book does not trust the reader it claims to write for.

The fix is mostly subtractive. The Cast of Commentators carries the lens-tagging once. Each in-chapter use of a recurring figure should be name + one-line lens (already standard in the better passages) and nothing else. Procedural framing should come out almost wholesale; section headings carry navigation. Self-glosses should be replaced by clearer prose. Hedges should be reduced to one per claim, the strongest one. Recaps should be assumed unnecessary; the reader following the argument is following it.

## Manuscript-wide patterns (top 8)

### 1. Mini-bios of recurring thinkers at every appearance — *must*

The single most pervasive pattern. The Cast of Commentators establishes Murdoch, Weil, Frankl, Arendt, Heidegger, Tillich, James, Williams, Aurobindo, Gandhi, Easwaran, Sankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Prabhupada, MacIntyre once, with lens-tags. In-chapter, the book repeatedly re-introduces them with new biographical sentences.

**Example.** Ch 6 §4.1: "Simone Weil, French philosopher and mystic who died in 1943, wrote about attention with the precision the Gita implies but does not articulate in those terms." Ch 9 §4.1: "Simone Weil (1909–1943) was a French philosopher, political activist, and mystic whose late writings on attention have become one of the central modern accounts of contemplative practice." Ch 10 §4.2: "Simone Weil — French philosopher, factory worker, Resistance courier, dead at 34 in 1943 — was the source from whom Murdoch borrowed the word *attention*."

Three different chapters, three different biographies of Simone Weil, none of which the reader needs after the front-matter introduction.

**Tighter alternative.** Use a one-line frame at first in-chapter use and nothing thereafter. "Weil's attention-as-prayer (see Cast)…" or simply "Weil writes…". The work of the front matter is wasted if every chapter rebuilds what it established.

### 2. Defining terms an educated reader knows — *must*

The manuscript stops to define existentialism, the hard problem of consciousness, the *vita activa*, *anatta*, the default mode network, attachment theory, terror management theory, the Big Five, the Stoic dichotomy of control, the *Mahabharata* dramatic frame, *theophany* as a Greek term ("*theos*, god; *phainein*, to show"), and many others. Educated adults of the named target audience know these.

**Example.** Ch 7 §2 stops to gloss "*Theophany* is Greek — *theos*, god; *phainein*, to show — and names the moment in which the divine appears through the world rather than from a sky beyond it." This is a glossary entry, not prose. Ch 8 §4.1: "Heidegger's *Being and Time* (1927) is the strongest secular counterpart to chapter 8 in Western philosophy. *Sein-zum-Tode* — being-toward-death — argues that *Dasein* is constituted by its end-toward-which…" — a smart reader knows what *being-toward-death* names; the chapter could just use it.

**Tighter alternative.** Use the term; trust that usage discloses meaning. If a term is genuinely unfamiliar within the manuscript's frame, gloss in a half-clause, not a paragraph.

### 3. Procedural framing / signposting — *must*

The prose announces what it is about to do, did, and will do. "What follows…", "I will now turn to…", "The chapter has done its work…", "The synthesis is not a vote count.", "Let me unpack this.", "What survives.". Section structure carries navigation; the prose should not narrate it.

**Example.** Ch 14 §6 is structured around the announced sequence "First, attend to substrate. Second, strengthen sattva. Third, witness all three." This works as a list but reads as schoolwork because it is preceded by paragraphs explaining that there are three layers and that they are being numbered. Ch 16 §6: "That sentence carries my position; the rest unpacks why I hold it." — meta-prose.

**Tighter alternative.** Cut the announcement. Number the three moves and let them stand. The reader can see the structure.

### 4. Self-glosses ("By X I mean Y") — *should*

The manuscript stops mid-claim to define its own coinages or usage choices. The result is a sentence that has done less work than its length suggests.

**Example.** Ch 15 §6: "By 'non-reductive personalist' I mean: I take the chapter's grammar at its word." Ch 12 §2: "Religious bhakti is a special case of this broader capacity, not a separate thing. The chapter reads more accurately as oriented love — with the divine as the proposed final object." (Glosses *bhakti* twice in adjacent sentences.) Ch 16 §6: "*Sampad* names an *endowment* — a bundle of dispositions one has come into and continues to cultivate." The self-gloss form is repeated dozens of times.

**Tighter alternative.** If a usage is opaque, rewrite the sentence to be unambiguous in itself. If it is clear, drop the gloss.

### 5. Recap-as-orientation — *should*

The book repeatedly opens chapters and synthesis sections with recaps of where the argument stands. "By chapter 12 the dialogue form has thinned to monologue." "Chapters 5, 6, 12, 18 give weight to renunciation, meditation, devotion, and surrender." "The chapter sits at one of the historical fault lines of Gita commentary." A reader who has reached chapter 12 has been there.

**Example.** Ch 11 §1 opens: "For ten chapters Krishna and Arjuna have been talking — charioteer and warrior in conversation. Krishna has explained the imperishable self (Ch. 2), the yoga of action (Ch. 3), meditation (Ch. 6), the two natures of the divine (Ch. 7), divine immanence (Ch. 9), and his glory disclosed through the best of each kind of being (Ch. 10)." Six chapters of recap before the chapter under review begins.

**Tighter alternative.** Cut. Or compress to one sentence: "The dialogue has been teacherly for ten chapters." A first-time reader will skim the recap; a reader following the argument will resent it.

### 6. Chains of chapter pointers — *should*

The book frequently points the reader at three to five other chapters in a single sentence, making the prose feel like a hyperlinked outline.

**Example.** Reading guide §4: "Action without proprietary self-grafting — Chapters 2, 3, 4, 5, 18. The nature of the self — Chapters 2, 13, 15. The nature of the divine — Chapters 7, 9, 10, 11, 15. Devotion as accessible practice — Chapters 9, 12, 18." Inside chapters, the same multi-pointer structure recurs (Ch 18 §6 ends with a list of seven cross-references; Ch 12 §6 names six chapters; Ch 14 §8 lists five). Once is fine; the manuscript chains them.

**Tighter alternative.** One pointer per topic per chapter. Index-level cross-referencing is for the back matter, not the prose.

### 7. Over-qualifying hedges — *should*

The book often runs three or four hedges in series — "may," "in some sense," "to a substantial extent," "within bounds," "approximately" — where one would be enough or none would be cleaner.

**Example.** Ch 13 §5.2: "There is no homunculus inside watching the show. There is the show, and the show contains a model of 'the one watching the show,' which is part of what is shown." (Two restatements where one would do.) Ch 14 §5.1: "Heritability is also a population-level statistic — it does not say any *individual's* personality is 50% genetic and 50% chosen, does not say which genes do which work, and does not say which traits sit at the high or low end of the heritable range. It does say…" (a four-fold disclaimer, then the claim).

**Tighter alternative.** Make the claim. State the strongest single qualification. Trust the reader to register that empirical claims are conditional.

### 8. Restating-what-you-just-said — *consider*

Many synthesis sections paraphrase a point in two adjacent sentences. The pattern is most visible in §6 and §7.

**Example.** Ch 7 §6: "Realization is recognizing the divine through the world. The chapter's character ideal, the *jñānī* of 7.16–7.18, is not the one who has dissolved out of the world; it is the one who has come to see what the world is showing." Two sentences, one point. Ch 17 §7: "*Śāstric* faith is not adopted by deciding to adopt it. It is something you move toward by repeatedly noticing what your conduct actually betrays…" — the second sentence does the work; the first is throat-clearing.

**Tighter alternative.** Pick the better one.
